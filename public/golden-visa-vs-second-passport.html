Golden Visa vs Second Passport: What Americans Need to Know

MS By The MigrateState Editorial Team · Reviewed by a licensed international real-estate attorney  ·  Updated June 2026  ·  9 min read
A golden visa buys residency; a second passport buys citizenship. A golden visa (from €250K) lets you live somewhere and renew, with citizenship years away. Citizenship by investment (from ~$230K) hands you a passport in 3–6 months. For an American, the key fact: a second passport is fully legal alongside your US one — but neither cuts your US taxes unless you renounce.

People use "golden visa" and "second passport" interchangeably. They are not the same — and for an American the difference carries real legal and tax weight. One makes you a resident who can be told to renew or leave; the other makes you a citizen who cannot be deported and can pass status to their children. Here's the clear-eyed comparison, ending with the US-specific catch that the glossy brochures bury.

Two different products

A golden visa is residency by investment: you invest (real estate, a fund, a business) and receive the right to live in a country, usually with Schengen or regional travel rights, renewable as long as you maintain the investment. Citizenship is a separate step you may reach years later through naturalization. A second passport via citizenship by investment (CBI) skips the wait: you make a qualifying contribution or investment and are granted full citizenship and a passport, typically in three to six months, with no residence requirement.

Rights: resident vs citizen

The gap is bigger than it looks. A resident can live, often work, and travel regionally — but their status is conditional: miss a renewal, fail to maintain the investment, or fall foul of the rules, and it can lapse. A citizen has the strongest possible footing: they generally cannot be deported even in legal trouble, they vote, they hold a passport with its own visa-free network, they access the country's healthcare, education and financial system as of right, and they transmit citizenship to their children. For a family building a multi-generational Plan B, that durability is the whole point.

Dual citizenship and US law

Good news for Americans: the United States permits dual and even multiple citizenship. Acquiring a second passport by investment does not automatically cost you your US citizenship — you keep your US passport, and your existing obligations, alongside the new one. You will still file US taxes, still report foreign accounts (FBAR, FATCA), and still travel on your US passport when entering and leaving the United States. A second passport adds options; it doesn't subtract American duties.

The US exit tax — the real catch

Here's the nuance that reframes the entire decision for a US citizen, and that almost no broker leads with. Neither a golden visa nor a second passport reduces your US taxes while you remain American — the US taxes citizens on worldwide income no matter how many passports they hold. The only way to end US worldwide taxation is to renounce US citizenship. And renouncing has a price: if you're a "covered expatriate" (broadly, high net worth or income, or non-compliant), the exit tax treats your worldwide assets as sold the day before you renounce, taxing unrealized gains above an exclusion of roughly $910,000 (2026, indexed — approximate; verify the exact figure with a cross-border tax adviser once the IRS publishes the annual adjustment) at capital-gains rates — plus a $450 renunciation fee and Form 8854. So the strategic truth is subtle: a second passport is the prerequisite that makes renunciation even possible (you can't render yourself stateless), but it is the renunciation — not the passport — that changes your taxes, and that step has its own toll. Most people who renounce owe no exit tax; the wealthy can owe a great deal. Model it before you romanticize it.

Which should an American choose?

If your goal is an EU lifestyle, a home abroad, or a low-cost Plan B while staying fully American, a golden visa is the cheaper, more flexible start — see our best golden visas for Americans. If your goal is maximum travel freedom, a durable family nationality, or the legal foundation to eventually renounce, a second passport earns its higher cost. Many Americans sequence it: start with a golden visa, live and invest, and naturalize into citizenship years later — reaching the same destination at lower upfront cost, if they can tolerate the wait.

A sensible sequence

  1. Define the objective — lifestyle/Plan B, travel freedom, or future renunciation. Each favors a different product.
  2. Check your US tax exposure with a cross-border CPA before investing — especially if renunciation is even a distant goal.
  3. Pick the jurisdiction on passport strength, stay rules, stability and cost — not on marketing.
  4. Engage an independent immigration lawyer in that country, separate from any sales agent.
  5. Complete the qualifying investment with full source-of-funds documentation.
  6. Maintain and renew (golden visa) or receive the passport (CBI); keep US filings current throughout.
  7. Only then weigh renunciation, with the exit tax fully modeled, if that was ever the aim.

If you're not American: what changes

The products are the same for everyone; the tax stakes are not. The golden-visa-vs-passport mechanics, costs and timelines above apply to any nationality. But the heavy US exit-tax warning is American-specific — most other countries tax on residency, not citizenship, so a non-American rarely faces an exit-tax toll for relocating.

If you're not American, you can largely ignore the renunciation and exit-tax discussion that dominates the US version of this decision. For most nationalities, simply changing tax residency — which a golden visa can enable — is enough to change your tax position, with no need to give up your original citizenship. Before assuming that, check three things in your country of residence: (1) whether your home country has its own exit tax or trailing-tax rules when you leave; (2) whether it taxes worldwide income and requires foreign-asset reporting; and (3) whether it even permits dual citizenship (some require you to renounce the original). Confirm with a tax and immigration adviser in your country of residence before committing.

The bottom line for 2026

A golden visa and a second passport solve different problems. Residency is cheaper, country-specific, and a fine Plan B for an American who's staying American. Citizenship is pricier, portable, durable, and the only door that — paired with renouncing — actually changes US taxation, at the cost of an exit-tax reckoning. Decide on the goal, price the US side honestly, and don't let anyone sell you a passport as a tax shortcut it isn't.

Frequently asked questions

What is the difference between a golden visa and a second passport?

A golden visa is residency by investment — the right to live somewhere, renewable, with citizenship years away. A second passport is citizenship by investment — full nationality, usually in 3–6 months. One makes you a resident; the other a citizen.

Can an American hold a second passport without losing US citizenship?

Yes. The US permits dual citizenship. A second passport by investment doesn't cost you your US citizenship — you keep it, and its obligations, including worldwide-income tax filing, unless you formally renounce.

Does a second passport reduce US taxes?

Not while you remain a US citizen. The US taxes citizens on worldwide income regardless of other passports. Only renouncing ends that — and it can trigger an exit tax. A second passport is the enabler, not the tax cut.

What is the US exit tax?

It applies when a "covered expatriate" renounces. It treats worldwide assets as sold the day before renunciation; for 2026 you exclude ~$910,000 of unrealized gain, with amounts above taxed at capital-gains rates. Most renouncers owe nothing; the wealthy can owe a lot.

Which is better for an American: a golden visa or a second passport?

It depends on the goal. A golden visa is cheaper and suits a Plan B while staying American; a second passport costs more but delivers travel freedom and the foundation to one day renounce. Many start with a golden visa and naturalize later.
