Colombia Real Estate & Residency Guides

Lawyer-reviewed guides for Americans buying property in Colombia — the legal and tax reality, not the dream.

Colombia is one of the few markets in the Americas where a foreigner owns real estate outright — 100% freehold, in their own name, with no residency or permit required to buy. Closing costs are strikingly low at roughly 1.5%–2.5% for the buyer, and a registered US$155,000+ purchase can open an investor-visa route. The outcome that trips people up isn't ownership — it's the foreign-exchange registration with the central bank that decides whether you can ever take your money home. Why Americans look at Colombia

For a US buyer, Colombia's appeal starts with something rare in Latin America: unrestricted freehold ownership. Unlike Mexico's coastal restricted zone, which forces foreigners into a bank trust, or the leasehold-only reality of parts of Asia, Colombian law treats an American buyer exactly like a local. You can hold an apartment or house in your own name, rent it, resell it and pass it to your heirs, and you can do all of it while in the country on a 90-day tourist stamp — no visa or permit needed simply to own. Layer on prices that undercut comparable US cities, a peso that has stretched the dollar in recent years, and transaction costs a fraction of Europe's, and the value case is easy to see.

The friction is not permission — it is process. Colombia has no title insurance, so your protection is an independent lawyer pulling the Certificado de Tradición y Libertad and verifying the chain of title before you sign. And there is a currency-control step most buyers never hear about until they try to sell: to keep the legal right to send your capital and sale profits back out, purchase funds from abroad must travel through a registered foreign-exchange channel and be logged with the Banco de la República. Get that discipline right and Colombia is a genuinely secure place to own.

Where to buy — the regions

Colombia's markets behave like distinct cities, each with its own buyer:

  • Medellín (El Poblado & Laureles) — the expat and remote-worker capital, with the deepest short-term-rental demand, a spring-like climate and the country's most liquid foreign-buyer market.
  • Bogotá (Chicó, Chapinero) — the business capital, best for year-round local rental demand, corporate tenants and buyers who want a real working city over a resort.
  • Cartagena — the Caribbean walled city and the prestige coastal play; strong holiday-let appeal but pricier entry and more seasonal demand.
  • Santa Marta & the Caribbean coast — the value beach option, lower entry prices than Cartagena for buyers focused on lifestyle and appreciation.
  • Cali — a lower-cost major city with a warm climate, suited to buyers chasing yield and affordability over prestige.

Two legal boundaries matter for every foreign buyer: vacant land within roughly 100 km of an international border and certain protected environmental zones are off-limits. Ordinary city apartments and houses — what nearly every American is after — are entirely unaffected.

The single most important line here is the last one paired with the money channel: the M-10 investor visa requires the property investment to be registered with the Banco de la República (the same Form 4 that protects repatriation), so residency-minded buyers must set up the FX registration before the first wire — not after closing. And because there is no US–Colombia tax treaty, your only shield against double taxation is the foreign tax credit, so US tax planning belongs at the start of the process, not the end.

Who Colombia suits — and who should look elsewhere

Colombia fits value-focused buyers who want genuine freehold title without European-level transaction costs; remote workers and entrepreneurs drawn to Medellín's expat infrastructure; and investors who prize a clean public registry and a property-based path to residency over maximum yield. Read the full guide to buying property in Colombia before you make an offer, and see the best residency options for Americans if a visa is your goal.

Colombia is a weaker fit if you want a fast, treaty-backed tax setup — there is no US–Colombia income tax treaty, so double-tax relief runs entirely through the foreign tax credit. It is also less suited to buyers who won't run a real title study (there is no title insurance to fall back on), or who can't commit to the registered FX channel that protects repatriation. Skip those disciplines and you can own the apartment but lose the documented right to move your money out.

Colombia FAQ

Is Colombia a good place for Americans to buy property in 2026?

For value, openness and legal security of ownership, it is one of the strongest options in the Americas — genuine freehold title, low transaction costs and a public registry that gives clear title. The risks are about process discipline rather than permission: declare the true price, run a real title study because there's no title insurance, and register your money through the central bank so you can take it back out.

Do Americans need residency or a visa to buy in Colombia?

No. Foreigners own property on the same footing as citizens, with no residency, visa or permit required — you can buy on a 90-day tourist stamp or by power of attorney. Buying can, however, support an M-10 investor visa if the registered investment is at least about US$155,000.

Which city in Colombia is best for a first purchase?

It depends on the goal: Medellín for the largest expat community and short-term rentals, Bogotá for a year-round working city, Cartagena for Caribbean prestige, Santa Marta or Cali for value. Avoid vacant land near international borders and protected zones, which are restricted for all foreigners.

How long does it take to buy?

A straightforward purchase typically completes in about one to two months — engaging a lawyer, running the title study, signing the Promesa de Compraventa, bringing funds in through a registered channel, then signing and registering the deed.

Will I owe US tax on a Colombian property?

Possibly. The US taxes worldwide income, so Colombian rent goes on Schedule E and a future sale is a US capital gain. Because there is no US–Colombia tax treaty, your only relief is the Form 1116 foreign tax credit. The property itself isn't FBAR-reportable, but a Colombian bank account is once your foreign accounts cross US$10,000.
