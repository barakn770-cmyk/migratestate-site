Why Americans look at France

France is the second-home dream that actually lets you buy it. Unlike many countries, it places no restrictions on foreign ownership: as a US citizen you can buy an apartment, a house or land in your own name — a Paris pied-à-terre, a Provence farmhouse, a ski apartment in the Alps, a stone house in the Dordogne — whether or not you live in France. The title system is unusually secure. A state-appointed notaire is legally required to draft the deed, verify title, collect the taxes, and register the transfer, so there is no title-insurance racket and the public land registry does the work. Add a mortgage market built around long fixed rates with no early-repayment trap, and France is one of the safest places an American can own a home.

The friction isn't permission — it's the details the listing portals leave out. A transfer tax quietly rose in 2025; non-EU buyers face a stingy mortgage cap; second homes carry a taxe d'habitation that primary residences shed; and a wealth tax most US buyers underestimate sits on top of the whole thing. One point catches people out constantly: buying property does not grant the right to live in France. As a non-resident American you remain bound by the 90-days-in-180 Schengen tourist limit, and staying longer requires a separate long-stay visa. The purchase and the right to reside are two different processes.

Where to buy — the regions

France is really several property markets, each with its own buyer:

  • Paris — the liquid, blue-chip market: apartments and pieds-à-terre that hold value and let easily, but with the highest entry prices and a second-home taxe d'habitation surcharge. Best for buyers who prioritise capital security and rentability over space.
  • Provence — the classic farmhouse-and-village dream, from the Luberon to the vineyards. Suits lifestyle buyers and second-home owners who want light, land, and rural charm within reach of Marseille and the TGV.
  • French Riviera (Côte d'Azur) — Nice, Cannes, Antibes and the Cap: prestige coastal property, strong seasonal rental demand, and some of the values most likely to cross the €1.3M IFI threshold. For buyers chasing glamour and rental income who can absorb the wealth-tax exposure.
  • Dordogne — the long-standing favourite for Anglophone buyers seeking a stone house with acreage at gentle prices. The most established British and American rural expat presence, and the best value per square metre on this list.
  • The Alps — Chamonix, the Trois Vallées and the ski hubs: apartments and chalets that combine personal use with reliable winter-let income. Best for buyers who want a dual-season asset and don't mind a specialised, seasonal market.

One cost varies sharply by location: the taxe foncière and the second-home taxe d'habitation are set locally, and high-demand communes (Paris, the Côte d'Azur, Atlantic resorts) add surcharges of 5%–60%. Price the annual bill for your specific commune, not a national average.

The trap most buyers underestimate is the IFI. Because it is a tax on net worth rather than income, it generally does not qualify for the US foreign tax credit — so for a high-value French property it is a real, unrelievable annual cost stacked on top of your US bill. The good news runs the other way: since 2019 the IRS accepts France's 17.2% CSG/CRDS social charges as creditable income taxes on Form 1116. Model both, not just one, before you buy.

Who France suits — and who should look elsewhere

France fits buyers who value lifestyle, legal security and liquidity above all — open ownership, a notaire-policed title system, no title-insurance racket, and long fixed-rate mortgages. It suits cash buyers, second-home owners, and anyone who wants a genuinely rewarding place to own where the costs, though real, are predictable. See our full guide to buying property in France for the step-by-step.

France is a weaker fit if you need a fast route to residency or an EU passport from the purchase itself — property buys you nothing on the immigration side, and you'd need a separate visiteur or other long-stay visa to live there. It also disappoints buyers borrowing heavily (the ~50% non-EU cap means a 50% deposit) and high-net-worth buyers who haven't priced the €1.3M IFI wealth tax the US won't credit. France FAQ

Is France a good place for Americans to buy property in 2026?

For lifestyle, liquidity and legal security it is one of the safest places an American can own a home — open ownership, a notaire-policed title system, no title insurance needed, and a mortgage market built on long fixed rates. The costs are real but predictable: a 5% transfer tax in most departments, 7%–8% all-in frais de notaire, a 50% deposit if you borrow, and — above €1.3 million — an IFI wealth tax the US won't credit.

Does buying property in France give me the right to live there?

No. Ownership grants no immigration status. As a non-resident American you remain bound by the 90-days-in-180 Schengen tourist limit, and to stay longer you need a separate long-stay visa such as the visiteur visa. The purchase and the right to reside are two independent processes.

Which region of France is best for a first purchase?

It depends on the goal: Paris for liquidity and easy letting, Provence for the farmhouse lifestyle, the Riviera for prestige and seasonal rental, the Dordogne for value and an established Anglophone community, and the Alps for a dual-season ski asset. Local taxes vary widely, so price the taxe foncière and second-home taxe d'habitation for the specific commune.

Why is the mortgage harder for Americans?

As non-EU nationals, US buyers are typically capped near 50% loan-to-value — a 50% deposit — versus 80%+ for residents, must keep total debt under about 35% of gross income, and can only be processed through a FATCA-compliant bank. Non-resident fixed rates ran roughly 3.5%–4.25% in early 2026. Arrange your agreement in principle early, because non-resident underwriting is slower.

Will the French wealth tax and social charges hit my US return?

Two different answers. The IFI wealth tax (above €1.3M) is a tax on net worth, so it generally cannot be claimed as a US foreign tax credit — a real added cost for high-value buyers. But France's 17.2% CSG/CRDS social charges on rental income and gains have, since 2019, been accepted by the IRS as creditable income taxes. Have a cross-border CPA map both before you sign.
