Buying Property in Costa Rica as an American (2026)

MS By The MigrateState Editorial Team · Reviewed by a licensed international real-estate attorney  ·  Updated June 2026  ·  11 min read
Foreigners own Costa Rican property on the same terms as citizens — full fee-simple title, no residency required. Plan on about 4–6% in closing costs, a 1.5% transfer tax, and 0.25% annual property tax. The two real traps are the coastal Maritime Zone, where foreign ownership is restricted, and the Costa Rican S.A. corporation, which can wreck your US taxes.

Costa Rica has been the soft landing for North American buyers for thirty years, and the appeal is easy to understand: a stable democracy with no standing army, a legal system that treats foreign buyers exactly like locals, and a climate that runs from cloud forest to Pacific surf town. What surprises most first-time buyers is not how hard it is to buy — it's how easy. You do not need residency, a local partner, or even to set foot in the country to take title to a home in San José or a lot in the hills above Tamarindo.

That ease is also where the danger lives. Because the front door is so open, buyers skip the diligence they would never skip at home — and walk straight into the two places Costa Rica genuinely punishes the unprepared: beachfront land inside the Maritime Zone, and the well-meaning advice to "just put it in a Costa Rican corporation." This guide walks the whole transaction, with the 2026 numbers, and flags both traps before they cost you.

Can Americans own property in Costa Rica?

Yes — and with remarkably few caveats. Costa Rica grants foreign nationals the same property rights as its own citizens. You can hold full fee-simple title (called propiedad and recorded in the National Registry, the Registro Nacional), and with it the unrestricted right to sell, lease, mortgage, gift, or pass the property to your heirs. There is no foreign-buyer surcharge on the transfer tax, no government approval process for a residential purchase, and no minimum stay requirement attached to ownership.

The single most important diligence step is confirming the property is titled land and not held by mere possession (derecho de posesión). A meaningful amount of rural Costa Rican land is "possession" land with no clean registered title — it can be cheap and tempting, but it is far harder to defend, finance, or resell, and it is not something a foreign buyer should take on without specialist counsel. Your attorney should pull a current estudio registral (registry study) to verify the title, the owner of record, the exact boundaries on the recorded survey (plano catastrado), and that there are no mortgages, liens, easements, or squatter annotations attached.

The Maritime Zone: where the rules change

The one true ownership restriction is coastal. The Maritime Terrestrial Zone Law (Ley 6043) governs the first 200 meters inland from the high-tide line along most of the coastline. The first 50 meters is public — no one can own or build on it. The next 150 meters is concession land (zona restringida): you do not own it outright, you lease a concession from the local municipality, typically for 5 to 20 years and renewable.

Crucially for Americans, a foreigner who has not been a legal resident of Costa Rica for at least five years cannot hold more than 49% of a maritime-zone concession. Buyers get around this with structures — but those structures are exactly the corporate ownership arrangements that create US tax problems (more on that below). The practical takeaway: a property advertised as "beachfront" may be a concession, not titled land, and the photo of the house on the sand may sit on ground you can never actually own. Always ask, in writing, whether a coastal property is titled or concession, and have your lawyer confirm it in the registry. Most of Costa Rica's safest foreign-buyer purchases are titled lots set back from the 200-meter line.

Closing costs and the buying process

Costa Rica's closing costs are modest by international standards — roughly 4–6% of the purchase price all-in, a fraction of what you'd pay in much of Europe. The components are predictable:

Cost Typical 2026 rate Notes
Transfer tax (Impuesto de Traspaso) 1.5% On the higher of declared price or registered fiscal value
Documentary stamps + registry fees ~0.5–0.8% National Registry, municipal and fiscal stamps
Notary / legal fees ~1–2% Notary fees follow a statutory scale; one notary drafts the deed
Escrow fee ~$500–$1,500 Strongly recommended — use a SUGEF-registered escrow agent
Total ~4–6% Often split 50/50 buyer/seller by custom — negotiable

In Costa Rica a single notary (who is also a licensed attorney) drafts and registers the transfer deed (escritura). Unlike the US, the notary's role is substantive and quasi-public — but the notary is usually chosen by, and serves, the party paying. As a buyer you should have your own attorney running diligence and ideally drafting or reviewing the deed, separate from any notary the seller proposes. Funds should always move through a SUGEF-registered escrow account (SUGEF is the financial regulator); Costa Rica's anti-money-laundering rules require source-of-funds documentation, and a compliant escrow agent protects both your money and your future ability to repatriate sale proceeds cleanly.

Property, luxury, capital-gains and rental taxes

Holding costs in Costa Rica are famously low. The annual property tax (Impuesto sobre Bienes Inmuebles) is just 0.25% of the property's registered value, collected by the local municipality — one of the lowest rates in the Americas. The catch many buyers miss: that registered value is self-declared and must be updated every five years (declaración de bienes inmuebles); under-declaring to save a trivial amount of annual tax can inflate your capital-gains bill later, because gain is measured from the declared basis.

Higher-value homes owe an additional annual luxury tax (Impuesto Solidario, the "solidarity tax" that funds social housing). It kicks in when the construction value of the home exceeds roughly ₡137 million (about US$270,000 in 2026; the threshold is indexed annually) and runs on a progressive scale from 0.25% to 0.55% of total value. Note it is assessed on the building value, not the land — a modest house on expensive land may escape it entirely.

When you sell, capital-gains tax is 15% of the profit, introduced by the 2018 tax-reform law (Ley 9635) and in force since July 2019. Your primary residence is exempt if it is genuinely your habitual home. One useful transitional rule: for property acquired before July 1, 2019, the seller may make a one-time election to pay a flat 2.25% of the sale price instead of 15% of the gain — valuable for long-held, highly appreciated property. Rental income is taxed as capital income on immovable property at 15%, applied after a standard 15% deemed-expense deduction (an effective rate near 12.75%); operating a property as an active short-term-rental business can instead pull it into the profits-tax regime, so confirm your structure with a local accountant.

Does buying get you residency?

It can — through the Inversionista (investor) category. As of 2026 the qualifying investment is US$150,000, lowered from $200,000 under Law 9996. For real estate the full $150,000 must be registered in your personal name in the National Registry — corporate ownership is not accepted for this purpose, which directly contradicts the "put it in an S.A." advice. Critically, the reduced $150,000 threshold is scheduled to expire on July 14, 2026 and revert to $200,000 unless the National Assembly renews it, so timing matters if residency is your goal.

Two other routes don't require a property purchase at all: Rentista (proof of US$2,500/month of stable foreign income for two years, or a $60,000 deposit in a Costa Rican bank) and Pensionado (US$1,000/month of lifetime pension income). All three lead to the same endpoint: temporary residency renewable every two years, permanent residency after three years, and eligibility to apply for citizenship after seven years of legal residence (five for nationals of Spain and Ibero-American countries). Buying a $300,000 home and applying as an investor is a clean, well-trodden path — just remember that residency requires the property in your own name.

The US tax angle — and the S.A. trap

For a US citizen or green-card holder, owning Costa Rican real estate directly is refreshingly simple on the US side. Directly-held foreign real estate is not reportable on the FBAR (FinCEN 114) or on Form 8938 — those apply to foreign financial accounts and assets, not to a house. You will report and pay US tax on any rental income (on Schedule E, with foreign rental property depreciated over 30 years under the ADS method), and on any gain when you sell — but you can generally claim a foreign tax credit for the Costa Rican capital-gains and rental tax you've already paid, and the Section 121 exclusion can shelter up to $250,000/$500,000 of gain on a true primary residence. Watch the currency angle: Section 988 can create a separate, taxable US dollar gain on a colón-denominated mortgage even if the property's dollar value didn't move.

Here is the nuance most blogs skip. Costa Rican real-estate agents and even some local lawyers reflexively recommend holding property in a sociedad anónima (S.A.) — partly out of habit, partly to handle the Maritime-Zone 49% rule. For a US person this is often a costly mistake. A Costa Rican S.A. is a "per se" foreign corporation in the eyes of the IRS. That means:

  • An annual Form 5471 is required — the penalty for not filing starts at $10,000 per year.
  • The structure can trigger CFC and GILTI taxation, or be treated as a PFIC if it holds passive assets — both are expensive and complex.
  • You lose the Section 121 home-sale exclusion (a corporation can't have a "primary residence") and the step-up in basis at death, so your heirs may inherit a large built-in gain.
  • It does not count for the $150,000 investor-residency route, which requires personal-name ownership.

You can elect pass-through treatment by filing a Form 8832 "check-the-box" election, but it must generally be made within 75 days of forming the entity and has its own consequences. The point isn't that corporate ownership is never right — for genuine concession land or multi-owner deals it sometimes is — it's that the decision is a US tax decision that should be made with a cross-border advisor before closing, not a default a broker selects for you.

Step-by-step: how to buy safely

  1. Engage your own Costa Rican attorney — independent of the seller, the broker, and the notary they propose.
  2. Sign an option/purchase agreement (opción de compra-venta) with a refundable deposit held in a SUGEF-registered escrow account.
  3. Order the registry study (estudio registral) and confirm titled vs. possession land, boundaries on the plano catastrado, and that no liens or maritime-zone annotations exist.
  4. Decide the ownership structure — personal name vs. entity — with a US cross-border tax advisor, before you commit.
  5. Fund through escrow with full source-of-funds documentation to satisfy AML rules.
  6. Execute the deed (escritura) before the notary and register the transfer in the National Registry.
  7. Update the municipal value declaration, set up annual property-tax and (if applicable) luxury-tax payments, and keep every receipt for your US basis.

If you're not American: what changes

The Costa Rican rules in this guide — fee-simple ownership for foreigners, the Maritime Zone restriction, the 1.5% transfer tax, ~4–6% closing costs, 0.25% property tax, the luxury tax, 15% capital gains, and the residency thresholds — are set by Costa Rica and apply to every foreign buyer equally, regardless of passport. A Canadian, British, German, or Israeli buyer pays the same transfer tax and faces the same coastal limits as an American. What differs is only the tax wrapper back home.

The entire "US tax angle" section above — FBAR, Form 8938, Form 5471, Section 121, the 30-year ADS depreciation, Section 988 — is US-specific and does not apply to non-Americans. If you're not a US person, swap those for your own country's rules and check three things with a tax adviser in your country of residence: (1) whether your home country taxes foreign rental income and capital gains; (2) whether you must report foreign assets or holding companies at home; and (3) whether your country has a double-taxation treaty with Costa Rica that affects how that income is taxed. The S.A.-ownership warning still matters to everyone, but the reasons it's costly are different outside the US.

Is it worth it in 2026?

For a buyer who wants a low-tax, politically stable place to own a home in the Americas — and who does the title and structuring diligence properly — Costa Rica remains one of the most foreign-friendly markets in the world. Closing costs are low, annual property tax is trivial, capital gains are a flat 15% with a primary-residence exemption, and a $150,000 purchase can double as a residency application (if you act before the July 14, 2026 threshold reversion). Those are genuinely attractive numbers.

The two ways buyers get hurt are entirely avoidable: paying "beachfront" prices for concession land they can never own, and accepting a Costa Rican S.A. structure that quietly creates a five-figure annual US tax-compliance burden and forfeits valuable US exclusions. Get an independent attorney for the title and a US cross-border advisor for the structure, and the deal that looks easy at the front door stays easy all the way through.

Frequently asked questions

Can Americans own property in Costa Rica?

Yes. Foreigners can buy and fully own titled, fee-simple property in Costa Rica with the same rights as citizens — to sell, lease, mortgage, and inherit — with no residency or visa required. The major exception is the Maritime Terrestrial Zone, the 200 meters inland from the high-tide line, where the first 50 meters are public and the next 150 meters are concession land with strict limits on foreign ownership.

How much are closing costs when buying property in Costa Rica?

Budget roughly 4–6% of the purchase price: a 1.5% transfer tax, about 0.5–0.8% in documentary stamps and Public Registry fees, and roughly 1–2% in notary and legal fees, plus a modest escrow fee. By custom these are often split 50/50 between buyer and seller, but the split is negotiable and should be written into the purchase agreement.

What taxes do you pay on Costa Rica property?

An annual property tax of 0.25% of the registered value, paid to the municipality. Homes whose construction value exceeds roughly ₡137 million (about US$270,000) also owe a progressive annual luxury/solidarity tax of 0.25%–0.55%. On sale, capital-gains tax is 15% of the profit (primary residence exempt; pre-July-2019 owners may elect a flat 2.25% of sale price). Rental income is taxed at 15% of net rent.

Can buying property in Costa Rica get you residency?

Yes, via the Inversionista category — US$150,000 of real estate in your personal name as of 2026, under Law 9996. That reduced threshold is set to expire July 14, 2026 and revert to US$200,000 unless renewed. Investor residency renews every two years, becomes permanent after three years, and opens a citizenship path after seven years of legal residence.

Should I hold Costa Rica property in a corporation?

Usually not, if you're American. A Costa Rican sociedad anónima (S.A.) is a "per se" foreign corporation to the IRS: it triggers an annual Form 5471 (minimum $10,000 penalty for non-filing), can create CFC, GILTI, or PFIC exposure, and forfeits the Section 121 exclusion and step-up in basis at death. It also doesn't qualify for investor residency, which requires personal-name ownership. Get US tax advice before choosing a structure.
