Best Golden Visa Programs for US Citizens in 2026

"Golden visa" has become a catch-all for buying your way to a second life abroad. For Americans the category is genuinely useful — a legal Plan B, EU access, and a mobility hedge — but it's also wrapped in marketing that quietly misleads US buyers about taxes. This guide cuts through it: which programs are actually open in 2026, what they really cost, and the one tax fact that reframes the whole decision for a US citizen.

What a golden visa is — for an American

A golden visa is residency by investment: you invest a qualifying amount and receive the right to live in (and usually travel freely across) a country, for you and your family. It is not citizenship and not a passport — that's a separate, later step called naturalization, or a different product entirely (citizenship by investment). For an American, the appeal is rarely about moving tomorrow; it's optionality: an EU residency card, Schengen mobility, a place to land if circumstances change, and a slow road toward a second passport for your family.

Greece — best value

Greece is the last major real-estate golden visa standing, and the cheapest serious EU option. Thresholds are tiered: €800,000 in Attica, Thessaloniki, Mykonos, Santorini and larger islands; €400,000 elsewhere; and €250,000 for restoring a listed building or converting commercial space to residential. There's no minimum stay, the whole family is covered, and you own a tangible asset that can earn rent. The trade-off: your capital is locked in property, and citizenship is a distant ~7 years away. Portugal — best path to a passport

Portugal lost its real-estate route, but it kept the prize Americans actually want: a credible road to an EU passport. With roughly €500,000 in a qualifying investment fund and a minimal stay (~7 days/year), you can become eligible to apply for citizenship after about 10 years under Portugal's May 2026 nationality law (Lei Orgânica 1/2026) — a longer clock than before, but still one of the most credible EU passport paths without relocating. Portugal also has a deep US expat community and English-friendly services. The catch is that the fund route carries market risk and no longer hands you a home. Italy, Malta, Cyprus & the Caribbean

Italy's investor visa suits entrepreneurs: €250K into an innovative startup, €500K into a company, €1M philanthropic donation, or €2M in government bonds — zero physical-presence requirement, but a long ~10-year citizenship road. Important tax note for Americans: Italy's lump-sum flat tax for new residents has been raised in two steps: to €200,000/year for entrants from 10 August 2024 (Decree-Law 113/2024) and to €300,000/year for those who transfer Italian tax residency from 1 January 2026 (2025 Budget Law). Those already in the regime are grandfathered at the rate in force when they joined — model the current €300,000 cost carefully before choosing Italy for tax reasons. The 7% flat tax for foreign pensioners still applies, now in municipalities up to 30,000 residents (expanded April 2026). Malta offers permanent residency through a mix of property rental/purchase and government contributions, with no stay requirement and English as an official language. Cyprus offers a €300K permanent residency program (note: Cyprus's citizenship-by-investment program was suspended in 2020 following EU scrutiny — this is a residency route, not citizenship) with a light visit requirement. The Caribbean programs are a different tool: real citizenship and a passport in months for ~$230K+, ideal for visa-free travel and a fast second nationality, but irrelevant if your goal is living in Europe.

The US tax truth no broker leads with

This is the section that should reframe the whole decision. Golden-visa marketing leans heavily on "tax benefits" — and for most nationalities that's fair. For an American, it is not. The United States taxes its citizens on worldwide income regardless of where they live or hold residency. Getting a Greek, Portuguese or Maltese golden visa does nothing to reduce your US tax bill. You'll keep filing a US return, reporting worldwide income, and disclosing foreign accounts via FBAR and FATCA. The only way to escape US worldwide taxation is to renounce US citizenship — which itself can trigger a one-time exit tax on your unrealized gains (with an exclusion around $910,000 for 2026, indexed annually) if you're a "covered expatriate." So the honest framing for a US buyer: a golden visa is a mobility and lifestyle asset and a possible step toward a future passport — never, by itself, a tax play. Anyone selling it to you as a US tax cut is selling you fiction.

How to choose and apply

  1. Name your real goal — a holiday home, EU mobility/Plan B, or a future passport for the family. Each points to a different program.
  2. Set the budget honestly — investment plus 7%–15% in taxes and fees, plus government and legal costs.
  3. Decide your stay tolerance — Greece, Italy and Malta ask for essentially none; Portugal asks ~7 days/year but counts toward citizenship.
  4. Engage an independent immigration lawyer in the target country — separate from any developer or agency selling you the asset.
  5. Line up a US cross-border CPA to confirm the US filing impact before you invest.
  6. Make the qualifying investment, then file the residency application with full source-of-funds documentation.
  7. Maintain the investment and renew on schedule; track the clock if citizenship is the goal.

If you're not American: what changes

The programs themselves are identical for any nationality — the thresholds, stay rules and citizenship timelines above don't change with your passport. What changes is the tax calculus: unlike Americans, most non-US citizens are taxed on residency, not citizenship, so for them a golden visa can genuinely be a tax-planning tool.

If you're not American, the US worldwide-taxation warning that dominates this guide simply doesn't apply to you. For most countries, where you are tax-resident — not what passport you hold — drives your tax bill, so a golden visa that lets you relocate can legitimately change your tax position. Before you assume a benefit, check three things in your country of residence: (1) whether moving your tax residency actually exits you from home-country tax (some countries have exit taxes or long "tax tail" rules); (2) whether your home country taxes foreign income and requires foreign-asset reporting; and (3) whether the target country offers a special regime (e.g. non-dom or flat-tax) you'd qualify for. Confirm with a tax adviser in your country of residence — the immigration steps are the same, but the payoff is different.

Which golden visa is worth it in 2026?

If you want a home and the cheapest entry, Greece. If you want a realistic EU passport for your family, Portugal. If you want zero-stay flexibility and an entrepreneurial route, Italy or Malta. If you want a fast second passport rather than European residency, look to the Caribbean. Whatever you pick, decide on the goal first, treat the tax-benefit pitch with deep skepticism as an American, and run both the immigration and US tax math before you commit. Used that way, a golden visa is one of the strongest optionality buys a US family can make.

Frequently asked questions

What is the best golden visa for Americans in 2026?

It depends on your goal. Greece offers the cheapest real-estate route (from €250,000 for restorations, €400,000 in most regions) with no minimum stay; Portugal offers the clearest path to an EU passport (~10 years for US citizens under May 2026 law, ~7 days/year). Match the program to whether you want a home, mobility, or a future passport.

Does a golden visa lower my US taxes?

No. US citizens are taxed on worldwide income regardless of residency. A golden visa gives mobility and a Plan B, not a US tax cut. Only renouncing citizenship changes that — and it can trigger an exit tax.

Which golden visa is cheapest for US citizens?

Among real-estate routes, Greece starts at €250,000 (restoration/conversion) or €400,000 in most regions. Malta can start lower via rental, and Caribbean citizenship programs begin around $230,000 — but those grant a passport, not EU residency.

Can a golden visa lead to an EU passport?

Yes, but slowly. Residency comes first; citizenship is later. Portugal allows non-EU nationals (including Americans) to apply after about 10 years (per Lei Orgânica 1/2026, effective May 2026); Greece typically requires seven years of genuine residence, with language and maintenance conditions.

Did Spain and Portugal end their golden visas?

Spain closed its investor route to new applicants in 2025. Portugal kept its golden visa but removed the real-estate option — current routes center on funds (~€500,000). Greece, Italy, Malta, Cyprus and Hungary remain open in 2026.
