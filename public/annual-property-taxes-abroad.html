Annual Property Taxes Abroad: What You'll Really Pay (2026)

MS By The MigrateState Editorial Team · Reviewed by a licensed international real-estate attorney  ·  Updated July 2026  ·  11 min read
Expect to pay roughly 0.3–1.1% of the property's official taxable value per year in most of Europe — often far less than the US average because taxable values sit well below market prices. Dubai charges no annual property tax at all; Spain and France are the priciest once their second layers kick in.

Closing costs get all the attention, but the tax that actually shapes your ownership experience is the one that arrives every year, forever. Annual property taxes abroad are structured differently from the American model in one crucial way: almost every country in this guide taxes an official administrative value — Portugal's VPT, Spain's valor catastral, Italy's rendita catastale — that sits well below what you paid for the property. The headline rates therefore overstate the pain. A €400,000 apartment in Lisbon might carry a taxable value of €180,000 and an annual IMI bill under €700.

The trap is the second layer. Several countries add a supplementary charge aimed squarely at non-resident or high-value owners — Spain's imputed income tax on empty homes, France's second-home taxe d'habitation with surcharges up to 60% in tight housing zones, Portugal's AIMI wealth surcharge above €600,000 of aggregate taxable value. Buyers who budget only for the headline property tax routinely discover the second layer in their first year of ownership, usually via a letter in a language they don't read.

How annual property taxes work abroad

Three design features repeat across nearly every country here. First, the tax base is an administrative value, updated irregularly, that typically runs 30–70% of market value. Second, the rate is set locally — by the municipality in Portugal, Spain, Italy, and Mexico, and by a stack of local authorities in France — so two identical houses in neighboring towns can carry meaningfully different bills. Third, payment is the owner's responsibility whether or not a bill reaches you. Foreign owners lose on this point constantly: the demand goes to the property address or an old fiscal representative, nobody forwards it, and surcharges accrue silently. Setting up direct debit from a local bank account in your first month of ownership is the single cheapest piece of insurance in this entire guide.

Portugal: IMI and AIMI

IMI (Imposto Municipal sobre Imóveis) is charged on the property's tax value (VPT) at a municipal rate of 0.3–0.45% for urban property (rural land pays 0.8%). Because VPT commonly runs well below market price, effective bills are modest: a coastal apartment bought for €350,000 might carry a VPT near €160,000 and an IMI bill of €480–€720. Payment falls in May, with bills over €100 split into two or three installments through November.

AIMI is the wealth-style surcharge: individuals whose aggregate Portuguese residential VPT exceeds €600,000 (€1.2 million for couples filing jointly) pay 0.7% on the excess, rising to 1% above €1 million and 1.5% above €2 million. Companies holding residential property pay 0.4% from the first euro — one of several reasons corporate holding structures in Portugal need careful thought before, not after, purchase.

Spain: IBI plus the tax on an empty home

IBI (Impuesto sobre Bienes Inmuebles) is the municipal bill: 0.4–1.1% of cadastral value for urban property, set by each town hall. So far, so ordinary. The layer Americans consistently miss is the non-resident imputed income tax: Spain deems you to earn phantom rental income from any Spanish property that is not your main home, equal to 1.1% of cadastral value (if revised in the last ten years) or 2% (if not) — and non-EU residents, including Americans, pay 24% tax on that deemed income through the Modelo 210 filing. A €120,000 cadastral value on the 2% track produces €576 of tax on income that never existed. If you actually rent the property, the imputed tax is replaced by real tax on the rent — for non-EU landlords, 24% on gross rent with no deductions, which changes the arithmetic of casual holiday letting.

Greece: ENFIA

ENFIA is assessed on whoever owns Greek property each January 1, computed from a per-square-meter scale that depends on the state's zone value for the location, plus the building's age, floor, and features. For a typical 80–100 m² apartment outside the priciest zones, bills commonly land in the €250–€800 range, with high-value property paying more through the upper bands. Two current wrinkles: insured primary residences under €500,000 of taxable value get a 20% reduction, and from 2026 Greek tax residents whose main home sits in a village of under 1,500 inhabitants get a 50% cut — neither of which typically helps a non-resident owner, but both signal the direction of policy: squeezing empty and foreign-held homes gently, favoring occupied ones.

Italy: IMU

IMU applies to second homes — and for a non-resident, every Italian property is a second home. The taxable base is the cadastral income (rendita catastale) revalued by 5% and multiplied by 160 for residential property; the base rate is 0.86%, which municipalities can push to roughly 1.06% or trim downward. Because rendite were set decades ago, the resulting base is usually far below market value: a countryside house bought for €250,000 often generates an IMU bill of €800–€1,500. Italian tax residents who occupy a non-luxury home as their registered primary residence pay no IMU at all — one of the quiet financial arguments for actually moving in rather than owning from afar.

France: taxe foncière and the second-home surcharge

France abolished the taxe d'habitation for primary residences — but kept it for second homes, and let over a thousand municipalities in designated tight housing markets (zones tendues) add a surcharge of 5% to 60% on top. The owner's tax, taxe foncière, is charged separately and has been climbing steeply as municipalities replace lost revenue; for a typical house, combined annual bills of €1,500–€4,000 are now unremarkable in sought-after areas. France is the clearest case in this guide where the annual layer, not the purchase tax, is what erodes a marginal deal — model it over ten years before you fall in love with the stone farmhouse.

Mexico: predial

Predial is famously light: municipal rates commonly work out to 0.05–0.3% of an assessed value that itself lags far behind market prices, so bills of $150–$600 USD a year on a substantial home are normal, and most municipalities give 10–25% discounts for paying the full year in January or February. The recurring cost that actually matters for Americans in coastal areas is the fideicomiso bank-trust fee — roughly $700–$1,200 per year — which is a banking charge, not a tax, and never appears on any government bill. Budget both.

Dubai / UAE: the no-property-tax model

The UAE levies no annual property tax. Recurring ownership costs come from two other directions: service charges set per square foot by the building or community (substantial in amenity-heavy towers), and Dubai Municipality's housing fee of 5% of annual rental value, billed monthly through the DEWA utility account of whoever occupies the unit — the tenant if rented, you if you live there. An empty, unmetered unit generates service charges but no housing fee. For investors comparing net yields against Europe, the absence of a property tax and of any tax on rental income is a structural advantage that partially offsets Dubai's higher volatility.

The US-tax treatment most owners get wrong

Here is the nuance that surprises even sophisticated buyers: since the 2017 Tax Cuts and Jobs Act, foreign real property taxes are not deductible on Schedule A at all. They are not merely squeezed by the SALT cap — they are excluded from the itemized deduction entirely, and the 2025 tax law that lifted the SALT cap to $40,400 for 2026 did not bring them back. Nor do they qualify for the foreign tax credit, which applies only to income taxes.

The treatment flips completely the moment the property produces rental income. On Schedule E, foreign property taxes — IMI, IBI, ENFIA, IMU, taxe foncière, predial, and even condo service charges — are ordinary deductible expenses against rent, with no SALT cap. And Spain's imputed income tax occupies a strange middle seat: it is legally an income tax, so it can generate a foreign tax credit — but for a personal-use home there is usually no corresponding US income for the credit to offset, so in practice it is simply a cost. The planning implication is real: for owners on the margin between leaving a property empty and renting it part of the year, the US deductibility of every annual charge is a thumb on the scale in favor of renting.

How to estimate your annual bill before you buy

  1. Get the official taxable value — ask the seller or your lawyer for the latest IMI/IBI/ENFIA/IMU/taxe foncière bill; it states the administrative value and last year's tax.
  2. Check the local rate — municipal rates vary within each country; your lawyer can confirm the current year's rate and any announced increases.
  3. Add the second layer — Spain's imputed tax, France's second-home taxe d'habitation and surcharge, Portugal's AIMI if your aggregate VPT will exceed €600,000, Mexico's fideicomiso fee.
  4. Add non-tax recurring costs — community/service charges, mandatory insurance, and (in Greece and Spain) the cost of a fiscal representative or annual filing help.
  5. Model ten years, not one — apply each country's recent trajectory: France's taxe foncière has risen fastest; Portugal's IMI is the most stable.
  6. Set up direct debit from a local bank account for every recurring bill in your first month of ownership.

If you're not American: what changes

Everything local in this guide is passport-blind: IMI, IBI and the imputed-income tax, ENFIA, IMU, taxe foncière, predial, and Dubai's fee structure apply identically to any foreign owner. Only the home-country wrapper differs — the Schedule A/Schedule E analysis above is purely American.

If you live outside the US, swap the American layer for your own: check (1) whether your country of residence taxes foreign rental income and capital gains, and whether it credits the annual taxes you pay abroad against anything; (2) whether you have a foreign-asset or foreign-account reporting obligation at home that owning (or the bank account that comes with owning) triggers; and (3) whether a double-taxation treaty between your country and the property country changes the outcome — many treaties let the property country tax rental income first, with your home country giving a credit. One EU-specific note: EU/EEA residents pay 19% instead of 24% on Spain's imputed and rental income and can deduct expenses from rent — a meaningfully better deal than Americans get. Confirm the details with a tax adviser where you live.

Is buying abroad still worth it with these taxes in 2026?

Almost everywhere in this guide, the annual property-tax burden is lighter than what the same owner would pay on comparable US property — often dramatically so. The genuine budget threats are concentrated and nameable: Spain's phantom-income tax on empty homes, France's escalating second-home levies, and Portugal's AIMI for portfolios above €600,000. A buyer who knows those three numbers before signing, and who sets up direct debit in month one, will find the annual tax layer abroad a pleasant surprise rather than a trap. The ones who get hurt are those who assumed "no bill received" meant "no bill exists."

Frequently asked questions

Which country has the lowest annual property taxes?

Dubai has no annual property tax at all — recurring costs are service charges and, for occupied units, a 5% housing fee on annual rental value billed through DEWA. Mexico's predial is the lowest true property tax here, often a few hundred dollars a year, because assessed values lag far behind market value. Among European markets, Portugal and Italy are typically cheapest for a mid-range property; Spain (with the imputed income tax) and France (with the second-home layer) sit at the expensive end.

Can I deduct foreign property taxes on my US tax return?

Not on Schedule A — since the 2017 Tax Cuts and Jobs Act, foreign real property taxes are excluded from the itemized SALT deduction entirely, and the 2025 law's higher cap didn't restore them. If the property is rented, the same taxes are fully deductible on Schedule E against rental income, with no SALT cap. They also don't qualify for the foreign tax credit, which covers income taxes only.

Why does Spain tax me if my property sits empty all year?

Spain deems non-resident owners to earn phantom income from any property that isn't their main home: 1.1% of cadastral value (if revised in the last ten years) or 2% (if not), taxed at 24% for Americans via Modelo 210, on top of IBI. On a €120,000 cadastral value at the 2% rate, that's €576 of annual tax on income that never existed.

What happens if I don't pay annual property taxes abroad?

Surcharges and interest accrue, and in most countries the debt attaches to the property. Spain and Portugal can register charges and collect through enforcement; Greece withholds the tax-clearance certificate you need to sell; Mexican municipalities add fines and can eventually auction chronically delinquent property. You usually feel it when you try to sell — the notary will demand proof that annual taxes are current.

Do annual property taxes drop if I get residency or a golden visa?

Usually not directly — these taxes apply to owners regardless of immigration status. What changes the bill is tax residency plus actual use: becoming an Italian tax resident in a non-luxury primary home eliminates IMU; moving to Spain ends the non-resident imputed tax; Greece's new village ENFIA cut applies only to residents' main homes. A visa alone, without moving in, changes little.
