Why Americans look at Mexico

Proximity is the headline: a coastal home a short flight from most US cities, in the same time zones, with mature expat communities and dollar-friendly pricing. But the reason Mexico works so well for Americans is legal, not just logistical. There is no prohibition on US citizens owning real estate here, and you don't need to be a resident or citizen to buy. The only meaningful restriction is geographic — and it comes with a decades-old, well-tested workaround that hundreds of thousands of foreign owners already use.

Under the Mexican Constitution, foreigners can't hold direct title to residential land inside the restricted zone — within 50 km of the coastline or 100 km of a land border. Since most destinations Americans want are coastal, this affects the majority of buyers. The answer is the fideicomiso: a Mexican bank holds legal title as trustee while you, the beneficiary, keep every meaningful right — to live in it, renovate, rent, sell and pass it on. It's a secure instrument, not a loophole, and outside the zone you simply take direct title with no trust at all.

Where to buy — the regions

Mexico's markets split sharply between coastal resort demand and inland city life:

  • Riviera Maya (Cancún, Playa del Carmen, Tulum) — the strongest short-term-rental and vacation market; high tourist demand, and firmly inside the restricted zone (fideicomiso required).
  • Puerto Vallarta & Riviera Nayarit — a long-established Pacific expat and retiree hub with deep rental infrastructure; also restricted-zone.
  • Los Cabos (Baja California Sur) — the prestige coastal market, strong luxury and second-home demand; restricted-zone.
  • Mexico City — the year-round urban play (Roma, Condesa, Polanco), popular with remote workers; inland, so direct title.
  • San Miguel de Allende & the colonial highlands — the classic retiree and lifestyle inland market, direct title, cooler climate.
  • Mérida & the Yucatán interior — a value inland option with a large and growing expat community and direct title.

The single question that decides your process is simply: is the property in the restricted zone? Coastal or near a border means a fideicomiso; inland means direct title.

The line that saves Americans the most grief is the US-tax one that isn't in this table. A standard residential fideicomiso that merely holds title is not a foreign trust for US purposes under IRS Revenue Ruling 2013-14, so the onerous Forms 3520/3520-A generally don't apply — a point most "move to Mexico" blogs get wrong. Rental income and a future sale are still US-reportable, with the foreign tax credit relieving double tax, and a Mexican bank account can trigger FBAR/FATCA.

Who Mexico suits — and who should look elsewhere

Mexico fits lifestyle buyers and retirees who want low carrying costs, a short trip home and a mature expat ecosystem; dollar holders who value the favorable pricing; and investors chasing strong tourist-hub rental yields — provided they model both countries' taxes and the higher financing costs honestly. Start with the full guide to buying property in Mexico, then the US-tax guide before you sign.

Mexico is a weaker fit if you're buying primarily to gain residency — a purchase does not grant it, though ownership plus sufficient income can support a Temporary or Permanent Resident visa. It's also less compelling if you need cheap local financing (peso mortgages run 11%–14%), or if you'd spend more than 183 days a year here without planning for it — cross that line and you may become a Mexican tax resident on worldwide income.

Mexico FAQ

Is Mexico a good place for Americans to buy property in 2026?

For lifestyle buyers, retirees and dollar holders it's one of the strongest options anywhere — low carrying costs, proximity to the US and mature expat infrastructure. The keys to a clean purchase are confirming restricted-zone status early, using an independent attorney separate from the notario, and planning the US-tax side before year-end.

Do I need a fideicomiso, or can I own directly?

It depends on location. Inside the restricted zone — within 50 km of the coast or 100 km of a land border — residential property is held through a fideicomiso bank trust that conveys full ownership rights. Inland, you take direct title with no trust needed.

Which region is best for a first purchase?

For rental income and vacation use, the Riviera Maya, Puerto Vallarta and Los Cabos lead (all restricted-zone). For a year-round base with direct title, look at Mexico City, San Miguel de Allende or Mérida. The choice usually comes down to resort income versus inland lifestyle.

How long does it take to buy?

A straightforward purchase generally closes in a couple of months — offer and promissory agreement, due diligence, setting up the fideicomiso and SRE permit if in the zone, then closing before the notario público and registering the deed.

Will I owe US tax on a Mexican property?

Owning a personal-use home alone usually triggers no US filing, and a standard residential fideicomiso is not a reportable foreign trust under IRS Revenue Ruling 2013-14. Rental income and a sale are US-reportable, with a foreign tax credit to avoid double taxation, and a Mexican bank account may trigger FBAR/FATCA.
