Buying Property in Greece as an American (2026 Guide)

MS By The MigrateState Editorial Team · Reviewed by a licensed international real-estate attorney  ·  Updated June 2026  ·  9 min read
Yes — Americans can buy property in Greece outright, with the same rights as a local. You don't need residency, only a Greek tax number (AFM), which a lawyer can get for you remotely. The headline cost is low: the transfer tax is just 3.09%, and all-in closing costs land around 7%–10%. Buy at the right threshold — €400K in most regions, €800K in prime zones, or €250K for a qualifying restoration/conversion — and the purchase can also earn a Golden Visa.

Greece has quietly become one of the most attractive places in Europe for an American to own a home. Prices in Athens and on many islands still sit below comparable Mediterranean spots in Italy, France or Spain; the transfer tax is among the lowest on the continent; and — unlike Spain and Portugal — Greece still runs a genuine real-estate Golden Visa. The buying process is open to foreigners, but it is paperwork-heavy and built around a notary and the cadastre. Here's the grounded 2026 version, with the legal and tax nuances most listing sites skip.

Can Americans buy property in Greece?

Yes — fully and with the same property rights as a Greek citizen. Greece imposes no general restriction on foreign ownership. As a US citizen you can buy an apartment, a villa or land in your own name and complete the whole transaction remotely through a lawyer holding power of attorney. The one true prerequisite is a Greek tax number, the AFM (Arithmós Forologikoú Mētrṓou). The only meaningful exception is a handful of designated border and certain island military zones, where non-EU buyers need a simple permit from a local committee — your lawyer checks this at the due-diligence stage, and it is routinely granted.

What you need before you buy

Four essentials turn an American into a "ready" buyer in Greece:

  • An AFM (Greek tax number) — required for the purchase, taxes and a bank account. A lawyer can obtain it remotely under power of attorney, often in days.
  • A Greek bank account — full payment for the property must flow through a Greek account so the source of funds is documented. Expect a FATCA check and a request for an IRS W-9.
  • An independent lawyer (dikigóros) — effectively mandatory in practice. Greek title can be tangled (inheritance shares, old contracts, cadastre mismatches), so independent due diligence is non-negotiable.
  • A civil engineer / surveyor — commonly engaged to confirm the building is legal, matches its permits, and has no planning violations, which are common on older and island properties.

The transfer tax — and the new-build VAT break

Greece's purchase tax is refreshingly simple compared with Spain or Portugal. On a resale property you pay a single property transfer tax (FMA) of 3%, plus a small municipal surcharge that brings the effective rate to 3.09%. Crucially, the tax is charged on the higher of the purchase price or the property's "objective value" (antikeimenikí axía) — a government-assessed figure. On undervalued or rural property the objective value can exceed the price you agreed, so always have your lawyer confirm the objective value before you budget.

New-build property is normally subject to 24% VAT instead of transfer tax — a brutal difference. But Greece has suspended that VAT: under Law 5246/2025 the suspension runs through 31 December 2026, so qualifying new homes pay only the 3.09% transfer tax instead of 24% VAT. That single break can save a buyer tens of thousands of euros — but it has conditions and an expiry date, so confirm in writing that your specific property qualifies.

There is no foreigner surcharge. A non-resident American pays the same 3.09% as a Greek buyer on the same property.

The Golden Visa: €250K / €400K / €800K

This is where Greece pulls ahead of its neighbours. While Spain scrapped its Golden Visa in 2025 and Portugal killed its real-estate route, Greece still grants residency for a qualifying property purchase. The 2026 thresholds are tiered by location and property type:

Minimum investmentWhere / what
€800,000Attica (Athens), Thessaloniki, Mykonos, Santorini, and islands with over 3,100 residents
€400,000All other (less in-demand) regions of Greece
€250,000Restoring a listed/heritage building, or converting a commercial property to residential

A key size rule that catches many buyers off guard: the €400,000 and €800,000 tiers require a single property of at least 120 square metres of habitable floor area (interior living space, excluding parking, storage and auxiliary areas). You cannot combine several smaller units to reach the threshold. The €250,000 restoration/conversion route carries no minimum size requirement.

The Golden Visa grants residency, not an immediate passport. It lets you and your family live in Greece and travel the Schengen area, with no minimum-stay requirement to keep it. Citizenship is a separate, far longer road (typically seven years of genuine residence plus a language requirement). For most Americans the Golden Visa is a lifestyle-and-mobility play, not a fast-track passport — and our dedicated Greece Golden Visa guide covers the rules in full.

Step-by-step: buying property in Greece as an American

  1. Get your AFM — your Greek tax number, obtained at the tax office or remotely by your lawyer under power of attorney.
  2. Open a Greek bank account (expect a W-9 / FATCA check) so the full purchase price can flow through it with documented source of funds.
  3. Engage an independent lawyer and a surveyor to run title, cadastre, encumbrance, permit and planning-violation checks.
  4. Sign a preliminary contract with a deposit, typically around 10%, locking the price while due diligence finishes.
  5. Pay the transfer tax (FMA) of 3.09% before the final deed is signed.
  6. Sign the final contract before a notary — in person or via your lawyer under power of attorney — with full payment made through your Greek account.
  7. Register the deed at the Land Registry and the Cadastre (Ktimatologio) in your name to secure ownership.
  8. Apply for the Golden Visa (if your spend qualifies) and set up US tax compliance — FBAR for the bank account, plus rental/resale reporting.

Start to finish, the typical timeline is two to four months; the Golden Visa permit itself usually follows within a few months of the purchase.

The US tax rules that still apply

Your Greek lawyer handles Greece; the IRS is your problem. The United States taxes citizens on worldwide income, so rental income from your Greek home goes on Schedule E, and a future sale is a US capital gain. The US–Greece tax treaty and the foreign tax credit normally stop you being taxed twice on the same income. A Greek bank account can trigger FBAR (FinCEN Form 114) and FATCA (Form 8938) once balances cross the thresholds.

Here's the nuance almost every Greece guide gets backwards. Greece has suspended its capital-gains tax on individual property sales every year since 2013, and the current suspension runs through 31 December 2026. Sellers cheer this — but for an American it can be a trap. The foreign tax credit only offsets US tax to the extent you actually paid foreign tax. If Greece charges zero capital-gains tax on your sale, there is nothing to credit — so the IRS taxes your entire gain (up to 20% federal, plus the 3.8% net investment income tax) with no offset at all. The Greek "tax holiday" that looks like a gift to locals delivers an American buyer no US relief whatsoever. Add the usual currency-gain wrinkle — a stronger euro at sale can create a taxable dollar gain even if the euro price didn't move — and the message is clear: model the US exit tax before you buy, not when you sell.

If you're not American: what changes

The Greek side is identical for any foreign buyer. The 3.09% transfer tax, the AFM, the notary-and-cadastre process, the 7%–10% costs and the Golden Visa tiers apply the same whether you hold a US, UK, Canadian, Australian or Israeli passport. Only the home-country tax wrapper on top is different.

If you're not American, keep everything above on the Greek process exactly as written — that part doesn't change with your nationality. What you swap out is the US-specific tax layer (worldwide-income filing, FBAR, FATCA). In its place, check three things in your own country of residence: (1) whether your home country taxes foreign rental income and capital gains earned in Greece; (2) whether you must report foreign property, bank accounts or assets at home (many countries have their own disclosure regimes); and (3) whether your country has a double-taxation treaty with Greece that lets you credit Greek tax against your home bill. EU and EEA citizens also skip the border-zone permit and may face lower Golden Visa relevance since they already hold EU residency rights. Confirm the specifics with a tax adviser in your country of residence before committing.

Is buying property in Greece worth it in 2026?

For value, lifestyle and mobility, Greece is one of the strongest cases in Europe right now: a 3.09% transfer tax, a temporary VAT break on new builds, prices that still undercut the western Mediterranean, and a real Golden Visa that its neighbours have abandoned. The catches are procedural and personal, not political — Greek title and the cadastre demand a careful independent lawyer, and the celebrated capital-gains suspension quietly offers Americans no US tax relief at sale. Buy with an independent lawyer and surveyor, budget the real 7%–10%, line up your US tax plan first, and Greece remains one of the most rewarding — and affordable — places an American can own a home in Europe.

Frequently asked questions

Can a US citizen buy property in Greece?

Yes. Greece places no general restriction on foreign ownership; US citizens can buy outright with the same rights as a local. You need a Greek tax number (AFM), which a lawyer can obtain remotely. Only a few border and island military zones require a simple, routinely granted permit.

How much does it cost to buy property in Greece as an American?

Plan for 7%–10% on top of the price. The transfer tax (FMA) is just 3.09%; lawyer and notary fees run about 1%–2% each, land registry/cadastre about 0.5%–1%, and a buyer's agent about 2% plus VAT.

Does buying property in Greece give you a Golden Visa?

It can. Greece still runs a real-estate Golden Visa in 2026: €800,000 in Attica, Thessaloniki, Mykonos, Santorini and larger islands; €400,000 elsewhere; or €250,000 for restoring a listed building or converting commercial to residential. Properties at the €400,000 and €800,000 tiers must be a single unit of at least 120 square metres of habitable area. It grants residency, not an immediate passport.

Do you pay VAT on a new-build property in Greece in 2026?

Usually not. The 24% VAT on new builds is suspended through 31 December 2026 under Law 5246/2025, so qualifying new homes pay only the 3.09% transfer tax. Confirm the specific property qualifies, because the suspension has conditions and an end date.

Do I pay US tax on a property I buy in Greece?

Potentially. The US taxes worldwide income, so Greek rental income and a future sale are IRS-reportable. The US–Greece treaty helps prevent double taxation, but Greece's suspended capital-gains tax can mean no Greek tax to credit against your US gain, and a Greek bank account can trigger FBAR/FATCA.
