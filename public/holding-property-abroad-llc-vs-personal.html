LLC vs Personal Name: How to Hold Property Abroad (2026)

Ask five advisers how to hold a property abroad and you will hear five confident answers: your own name, a US LLC, a local company, a foreign holding company, a trust. Each answer is right for someone — and expensively wrong for someone else. The structure you choose determines which taxes you pay every year, which US information returns you must file, what happens when you die, and whether the local land registry will even complete your purchase. Unlike almost every other decision in a cross-border purchase, this one is hard to undo: moving a property out of the wrong structure later usually means paying the country's transfer tax a second time.

This guide walks through the four realistic options for an American buyer in 2026, what each one actually delivers, and the entity-level taxes several popular countries quietly impose on company-held homes.

The default answer: your own name

For a single home or vacation property that you will use yourself, personal ownership — alone or jointly with a spouse — is the right answer far more often than the internet suggests. It is the cheapest to set up (nothing to set up), the cheapest to run (no annual company filings, registered agents, or corporate tax returns in two countries), and the cleanest at death in most jurisdictions, particularly where a will or the EU Succession Regulation lets you elect the law that governs your estate.

Personal ownership also carries a US tax advantage that structures can destroy: directly held foreign real estate is not reportable on the FBAR or Form 8938, and a personally held home keeps you eligible for clean capital-gains treatment — including the Section 121 primary-residence exclusion if you live in it and qualify — plus the basis step-up machinery your heirs expect. Interpose the wrong entity and some of those advantages degrade or vanish.

The honest case against personal ownership is narrower than the sales pitch for structures: local liability exposure on an actively rented property, probate friction in countries that require a local succession process, forced-heirship regimes that override your will, and privacy. Each is real; each also has narrower fixes than wrapping the home in a company — landlord insurance, a local will, a usufruct or joint-titling arrangement, or simply accepting that land registries are public.

What a US LLC actually does abroad

The US LLC is the reflex of American buyers — it works at home, so why not abroad? Three reasons to slow down.

First, recognition is not protection. Most civil-law land registries (Portugal, Spain, France, Italy) will register a properly documented foreign company as owner, but the registration process is slower and costlier: apostilled and translated corporate documents, proof of authority of the signer, and in several countries a local tax number for the entity itself. The liability shield you are buying is also thinner than it feels: for a passively held vacation home, your realistic liabilities (a guest injury, a contractor dispute) are exactly the risks a €300-per-year insurance policy covers, and courts in some countries will look through a single-asset shell in tort cases anyway.

Second, some countries tax entity-held homes harder. France imposes an annual tax of 3% of the market value of French real estate held by any legal entity — French or foreign — unless the entity claims an exemption, typically by disclosing its ownership chain each year (declaration no. 2746, due by mid-May). For a US LLC that files the disclosure, the tax is usually avoided; for one that doesn't know the rule exists, the bill compounds annually. Portugal punishes entities from blacklisted jurisdictions with IMT at a flat 10% and IMI at 7.5% per year (a US LLC is not blacklisted, but the regime shows how deliberately Portugal discourages opaque structures — and Portugal trimmed that blacklist again on January 1, 2026, removing Uruguay, Hong Kong, and Liechtenstein).

Third, the US tax treatment is neutral at best. A single-member US LLC is disregarded for US income tax, so it saves you nothing; the rental income and eventual gain land on your Schedule E and Form 1040 exactly as if you owned the property directly. What you gain is a layer of foreign paperwork; what you risk is a foreign tax authority treating the LLC as a non-transparent company and taxing it as one.

Local companies: when they're required, when they're a trap

In some markets a local vehicle is either mandatory or overwhelmingly customary — and in others it is a trap dressed up as local wisdom.

Mexico is the clearest example of both. In the restricted zone (within 50 km of the coast or 100 km of a border), a foreigner buys residential property through a fideicomiso bank trust — full stop. A 100% foreign-owned Mexican corporation may hold restricted-zone property only for non-residential, commercial use (a genuine rental business, a hotel), must include the Calvo clause in its bylaws, and must notify the SRE. Promoters who pitch a Mexican corporation for your personal beach house are selling you annual Mexican corporate tax returns, accountant fees, and exposure as a circumvention structure. The fideicomiso, by contrast, is settled ground for US tax too: the IRS confirmed in Rev. Rul. 2013-14 that a standard residential fideicomiso is not a foreign trust, so no Forms 3520/3520-A.

Elsewhere, local companies solve local problems. In Croatia, buyers from countries without reciprocity historically bought through Croatian companies. In Thailand, foreigners cannot own land personally, and the widely marketed "Thai company with nominee shareholders" workaround is illegal — a condo owned in your own name under the foreign quota is the clean route. In Dubai, freehold property in designated areas can be held personally or through certain approved vehicles; most individual buyers hold personally. The pattern: use a local company when local law makes it the honest route, not as a bolt-on for imagined protection.

The foreign-corporation tax trap

The most expensive mistake in this entire subject is holding a personal-use home inside a foreign corporation (or a foreign entity the IRS classifies as a corporation by default). Three problems stack on top of each other.

First, reporting: you file Form 5471, one of the longest and most penalty-laden information returns in the code — again with penalties starting at $10,000 per year for non-filing. Second, income: a controlled foreign corporation's rental income can be swept into US anti-deferral regimes and taxed to you personally at unfavorable rates, even if the company distributes nothing. Third — the nuance most blogs miss entirely — living rent-free in a home owned by your own company is not a freebie. Several countries treat personal use of a company asset as a taxable benefit-in-kind or deemed rental income to the shareholder, and the IRS can characterize it as a constructive dividend. In other words, the structure can manufacture taxable income on both sides of the Atlantic from a house that earns you nothing.

A foreign corporation also complicates the endgame: on sale, gains may be trapped at the corporate level and taxed again on distribution, and at death your heirs may lose the practical benefit of the basis step-up that personally held property delivers almost automatically.

US reporting by structure

The structure you pick decides your IRS paperwork. Directly held foreign real estate is famously invisible to the FBAR and Form 8938 — but the moment an entity or foreign account enters the picture, the reporting switches on:

How to decide, step by step

  1. Start from use, not fear. Personal/vacation use points to personal ownership; an actively managed rental portfolio or multi-investor project points toward a structure.
  2. Check the local law first. Confirm whether your target country restricts foreign personal ownership (Mexico's restricted zone, Thailand's land ban) or penalizes entity ownership (France's 3% tax, Portugal's blacklist rates).
  3. Price the structure honestly. Add formation costs, annual filings in two countries, registered-agent and accounting fees, and the US information returns — typically $2,000–$5,000+ per year all-in for a foreign entity.
  4. Solve liability with insurance before entities. Get a local landlord/homeowner policy quote; it usually neutralizes the realistic risks at a fraction of the cost.
  5. Plan succession in both systems. Ask a local lawyer how the property passes at death under personal vs. entity ownership — and make a local will either way.
  6. Get the US side signed off. Have a US cross-border CPA confirm the entity classification (disregarded vs. corporation) before closing; a check-the-box election is often due early and is painful to miss.
  7. Only then structure. If the numbers still favor an entity, form it before you sign the purchase contract — retitling later means paying transfer tax again.

If you're not American: what changes

The local rules above — who can own, how entities are taxed, France's 3% tax, Portugal's blacklist rates, Mexico's restricted zone — are the same for every foreign buyer regardless of passport. Only the home-country tax wrapper changes: swap the US forms for your own country's regime.

If you are reading this from outside the US, replace the American layer with your own: check (1) whether your home country taxes foreign rental income and capital gains, and how it credits the tax you pay where the property sits; (2) whether it imposes foreign-asset or foreign-company reporting of its own — many countries now have CFC rules and asset-declaration regimes that mirror the US forms; and (3) whether a double-taxation treaty exists between your country of residence and the country where you are buying, since treaties often decide whether an entity is looked through or taxed as opaque. The structural logic of this guide — personal ownership as the default, entities only for a proven reason — holds for any nationality, but confirm the details with a tax adviser in your country of residence before you commit.

Is a structure worth it in 2026?

For the typical American buying one home or vacation property abroad: no. Personal ownership plus good insurance, a local will, and clean US reporting beats an entity on cost, tax, and simplicity in almost every mainstream market. For a genuine rental business, a multi-investor purchase, a development project, or a country whose law channels you into a specific vehicle, a structure is not just worth it — it is the compliant route, and the right one is usually the local vehicle the country itself expects, not a US LLC bolted on from home. The decision is cheap to get right before you sign and brutally expensive to fix afterward. Spend one hour with a cross-border adviser; it is the best-value line item in the entire purchase.

Frequently asked questions

Should I buy property abroad through an LLC?

For most Americans buying a single home or vacation property abroad, no — personal ownership is simpler, cheaper, and avoids entity-level taxes several countries impose on company-held homes. Structures earn their keep for rental portfolios, multi-investor deals, development projects, or countries whose local law forces a specific vehicle. Decide with a cross-border tax adviser before you sign, because restructuring after closing usually means paying transfer tax twice.

Does a US LLC protect me if I buy property in Europe?

Less than most buyers assume. Civil-law land registries will generally register a foreign company as owner, but the LLC does not shield you from local property taxes, and some countries tax entity-held homes harder — France's 3% annual tax on the market value of entity-held real estate is the classic example, avoidable only via the required annual ownership disclosures. Liability protection for a passively held home is also weaker than in the US context.

What US forms do I file if I own foreign property through a foreign company?

A foreign entity disregarded for US tax purposes is reported on Form 8858, with penalties starting at $10,000 per form per year. A foreign corporation is reported on Form 5471, one of the most complex forms in the code. The company's shares can also trigger Form 8938 and FBAR filings even though directly held foreign real estate alone is not reportable on either.

Can a corporation own residential property in Mexico's restricted zone?

A foreign-owned Mexican corporation can hold restricted-zone property only for non-residential, commercial use — a genuine rental business, for example — with the Calvo clause in its bylaws and SRE notification. For a home you will live in or use personally, the fideicomiso bank trust is the correct route; a corporation holding a purely personal residence invites scrutiny as a circumvention structure.

Does holding property in a company avoid inheritance tax abroad?

Sometimes it changes which tax applies rather than eliminating tax. Company shares are movable property and may pass under different succession rules than land — which is why entities are sometimes used around forced-heirship regimes. But several jurisdictions look through companies whose main asset is local real estate and tax the shares as the property itself. This is a two-country estate-planning question, not a DIY decision.
