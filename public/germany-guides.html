Why Americans look at Germany

Germany is the EU's largest economy and one of its most open real-estate markets: a foreigner — resident or not — can buy land and buildings freehold, in their own name, with no permit, no nationality restriction, no minimum investment and no foreign-buyer surcharge. Combine that with an incorruptible land registry (Grundbuch), a notary system that all but eliminates title fraud, and cities like Berlin, Munich, Leipzig and Frankfurt with genuine rental demand, and it is easy to see why American buyers keep looking.

The catch is that openness is not the same as cheapness. Buying grants no residency of any kind — there is no German golden visa. Closing costs run a heavy 9%–12% and are not financeable. The transaction is run by a neutral notary who does not represent you, so a foreign buyer still needs their own attorney. And for a US buyer, Germany's celebrated 10-year tax-free-resale rule is worthless at the IRS — a trap covered in full in the guide below.

Who Germany suits — and who should look elsewhere

Germany fits the buyer who wants a legally bulletproof asset in a stable economy — a Berlin or Leipzig rental, a Munich family base, a foothold in industrial Europe — and who can fund the high closing costs and deposit in cash. Read the full guide to buying property in Germany for the state-by-state transfer tax, the 2025 Grundsteuer reform, and the traps.

Germany is a weaker fit if you want residency or a passport from the purchase (it grants neither — see our best golden visas for Americans instead), if you need high leverage as a non-resident, or if you're counting on the 10-year tax-free resale without realising the IRS ignores it.

Germany FAQ

Can an American buy property in Germany?

Yes — freely. Germany places no nationality restriction on real estate. A foreigner, resident or not, can buy a house, apartment, or land freehold in their own name, with no visa, no residency requirement, no minimum spend and no foreign-buyer surcharge. Ownership grants no residency in return.

What does it cost to buy in Germany?

Budget roughly 9%–12% of the price in one-time costs: the transfer tax (Grunderwerbsteuer) of 3.5% in Bavaria up to 6.5% in states like North Rhine-Westphalia, plus statutory notary and land-registry fees of about 1.5%–2% combined and any agent commission. These are paid in cash and cannot be financed.

What changed with German property tax in 2025?

The annual property tax (Grundsteuer) was reformed on 1 January 2025, revaluing about 36 million properties; Germany's Federal Fiscal Court upheld the new rules in December 2025. Owners now receive a recalculated bill based on the assessed value and the municipal multiplier (Hebesatz), and municipalities can levy a higher Grundsteuer C on undeveloped buildable land.

Is a German resale really tax-free after 10 years?

For a privately held property, yes — Germany's speculation tax applies only within 10 years of purchase, and after that the gain is tax-free (owner-occupiers can qualify sooner). But a US citizen still owes US capital-gains tax on the gain, with no German tax to credit against it, so the exemption does not free an American from the IRS.

Does buying property in Germany give me a visa?

No. Ownership grants no immigration status and no path to residency or citizenship. Living in Germany requires a separate route — the freelance/self-employment permit, the EU Blue Card, employment, or family reunification.
