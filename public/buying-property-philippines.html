Buying Property in the Philippines as an American (2026)

MS By The MigrateState Editorial Team · Reviewed by a licensed international real-estate attorney  ·  Updated July 2026  ·  13 min read
Foreigners cannot own land in the Philippines — full stop — but they can own condominium units outright and lease land for the long term. The hard limit is the 40% foreign cap on any condo project; the newest opening is the 99-year investor land lease under RA 12252, live since its implementing rules took effect on 4 January 2026. Plan on 3–5.5% in buyer closing costs and a 6% capital gains tax at sale. The real trap for Americans is that 6% tax — it's charged on the gross price, not your profit, so it can create a US foreign-tax credit you can't use.

The Philippines is one of the most emotionally magnetic destinations on this site. For millions of Filipino-Americans it is home, or a parent's home; for retirees drawn by warm weather, English fluency, and a low cost of living, it is a genuine landing spot. And yet it is also one of the most legally restrictive property markets an American can walk into — restrictive in a way that catches people who assume that "I'm married to a Filipina" or "I have money to invest" is enough to buy a beachfront lot. It usually isn't, and the mistake can be expensive.

The rules are not impossible; they are just specific. Get them right and Americans buy condominiums in Makati or Cebu with clean, registered title. Get them wrong — by parking land in a relative's name, or by trusting a developer who can't actually register a foreign buyer — and you can lose the property outright. This guide walks the whole transaction with current 2026 numbers, then does the part the sales offices skip: the constitutional wall around land, the new 99-year lease law, and why the country's headline 6% property tax is a quiet trap for a US filer.

Can Americans own property in the Philippines?

The honest answer is: the building yes, the land no. Article XII of the 1987 Constitution reserves ownership of Philippine land to Filipino citizens and to corporations that are at least 60% Filipino-owned. That prohibition is absolute for foreigners — it does not bend for a resident visa, a long marriage, decades of living in the country, or the size of your bank account. There is no "invest ₱X and own land" exception of the kind Portugal or Greece offer.

What a foreigner can own outright is a condominium unit (subject to the 40% cap below) and a building or improvement considered separately from the land beneath it. Everything else is about structuring around the land ban: leasing the land, holding it through a majority-Filipino corporation, or — if you were once a Filipino citizen — using the special allowances the law grants former nationals. We'll take each in turn.

Condos and the 40% rule

The condominium is the foreigner's front door. Under the Condominium Act (Republic Act 4726), a foreign national can own a condo unit in fee simple, evidenced by a Condominium Certificate of Title (CCT) in their own name, with full rights to sell, lease, mortgage, and bequeath it. The reason this works is a legal fiction that serves buyers well: in a registered condominium, the land is owned by the condominium corporation, and unit owners hold their units plus a proportionate interest in that corporation. Because the corporation must remain majority-Filipino, foreign ownership across all units is capped.

That cap is the 40% rule, and it is the single most important number on this page. Foreign nationals combined may own no more than 40% of a condominium project (measured by floor area). The remaining 60% must be held by Filipinos or majority-Filipino entities. The quota is enforced at the project level, not per unit — which produces the classic trap: a building can be at its foreign limit even though the specific unit you want is unsold. If the project is at 40%, the Registry of Deeds will not register your CCT, no matter how much you've already paid or how firm your reservation looks.

Two practical cautions. First, always get the developer or condo corporation to certify — on paper — that foreign capacity remains available for your unit before you sign anything. Second, be wary of pre-selling towers: you may be buying into a project years from completion, and the foreign quota can fill before your title is ever issued. A local lawyer verifying the developer's license to sell and the current foreign-ownership percentage is not optional here; it is the whole ballgame.

The land problem — and the 99-year lease

If you want a house and lot, a beach parcel, or a farm, you cannot own the land — but you have three legitimate routes to control it.

1. Lease the land. A foreigner can lease private land and then own the house built on it. This just got materially better: Republic Act 12252, the law extending long-term land leases for foreign investors, took effect with its implementing rules on January 4, 2026, raising the maximum lease term to 99 years (up from the previous 50-plus-25-year structure). It is aimed at qualifying investment projects rather than a casual holiday cottage, and the lease must be annotated on the land title to bind third parties — but for a serious buyer it turns a shaky handshake into a registered, near-lifetime interest. For ordinary residential situations, the older framework allowing a 25-year lease renewable for another 25 still applies.

2. A Filipino spouse holds the land. If you are married to a Filipino citizen, the land can be bought in your spouse's name (it is generally treated as their exclusive property, not conjugal, precisely because you can't co-own land). You can be named in the deed as the spouse and, in practice, protect your position with a long-term lease from your spouse and a will — but understand that legally the land is theirs, with all that implies if the marriage ends.

3. A 60/40 corporation. Land can be held by a Philippine corporation that is at least 60% Filipino-owned, with the foreigner holding up to 40% of the equity. This is common for commercial and larger residential projects, but it is a real company with real compliance costs and real Filipino shareholders — not a shell you secretly control.

The Anti-Dummy trap most buyers walk into

Here is the mistake that turns a dream purchase into a lawsuit. To get around the land ban, foreigners are routinely advised — by well-meaning relatives and by agents who should know better — to buy land in the name of a Filipino "dummy": a girlfriend, a cousin, a friend who holds title on paper while the foreigner puts up the money and controls the property. This is illegal. The Anti-Dummy Law (Commonwealth Act 108) criminalizes exactly this arrangement, and the Supreme Court has repeatedly held that a foreigner who funds a land purchase through a Filipino nominee cannot even recover the money, because the underlying contract is void for violating the Constitution. You can lose the land and the funds, with no court willing to help you.

The same logic quietly undermines the "anti-dummy" corporation — a 60/40 company whose Filipino shareholders are strawmen holding shares they never paid for and don't control. Regulators and courts look at economic reality, not the cap table on file. The rule of thumb for an American here is blunt: if a structure only works because everyone agrees to pretend a Filipino owns something they don't, it is not a structure — it is a time bomb. Lease the land properly, or buy a condo you can actually title.

Closing costs and the buying process

Compared with the ownership rules, the transaction mechanics are refreshingly cheap. Total buyer-side closing costs typically run 3–5.5% of the price. The split between buyer and seller is customary, not fixed, and is negotiated in the deed — but the market default looks like this:

Capital gains tax6% of gross price or zonal value (higher)Seller (by law)

Two things surprise first-time buyers. First, the headline 6% "capital gains tax" is the seller's cost and is charged on the gross selling price or the government zonal value, whichever is higher — not on the seller's actual profit. It behaves like a transfer tax, and it must be paid within 30 days of notarization. Second, if you buy a brand-new unit from a developer above the VAT-exemption threshold (currently around ₱3.6 million for a house-and-lot), 12% VAT is layered on — usually already inside the quoted price, but confirm it.

The process itself: you sign a reservation and then a Contract to Sell, your lawyer verifies the title at the Registry of Deeds (confirming it is clean, matches the seller, and — for a condo — that foreign capacity remains), taxes are paid and the BIR issues a Certificate Authorizing Registration (CAR), and only then is a new title (a CCT for a condo, a TCT for land held through a permitted structure) issued in the buyer's name. The CAR step is the bottleneck; nothing transfers cleanly until the BIR signs off.

Purchase taxes, capital gains, and annual RPT

At purchase. The buyer's tax load is the 1.5% DST plus the local transfer tax and registration fees above — modest by global standards. The 6% capital gains tax and the broker's commission are the seller's economics, though everything is negotiable in a soft market.

Capital gains at sale. When you sell a capital-asset property, the Philippines takes a flat 6% of the gross selling price or zonal/fair-market value, whichever is higher. Read that twice: it is not 6% of your gain, it is 6% of the whole price. Sell for what you paid, or at a loss, and you still owe the 6%. This is central to the US tax trap discussed below.

Annual real property tax (RPT, locally "amilyar"). You pay a yearly tax to the local government — capped at 1% of assessed value in provinces and 2% in Metro Manila cities, plus an additional 1% Special Education Fund levy. The saving grace is that "assessed value" is a low, government-set fraction of market value, so the real annual bill on a mid-range condo is usually small. Pay it on time — accumulated unpaid RPT can eventually lead to a tax sale of the unit.

Residency: the SRRV and former Filipinos

Buying property does not grant residency in the Philippines, and residency is not required to own a condo. But two paths matter to buyers.

The SRRV (Special Resident Retiree's Visa). Run by the Philippine Retirement Authority, the SRRV gives indefinite residence in exchange for a deposit in a PRA-accredited bank. After a September 2025 restructuring, the program was streamlined: the "Smile" and "Human Touch" categories were abolished, the minimum age was raised to 40, the application fee is now US$1,500, and only SRRV Classic and SRRV Courtesy remain. The required time deposit under the Classic option scales with age and pension status (broadly in the US$10,000–US$50,000 range), and part of it can, under program rules, be converted into an eligible condominium or long-term lease — one of the few places where the retirement visa and a property purchase actually connect. Confirm the current deposit tiers directly with the PRA, as they changed in the restructuring.

Former natural-born Filipinos. If you were born a Filipino citizen and later naturalized elsewhere (the situation of countless Filipino-Americans), you are treated far more generously than an ordinary foreigner. Under Batas Pambansa 185 you may own up to 1,000 m² of urban land or 1 hectare of rural land for residential use, and under Republic Act 8179 up to 5,000 m² of urban or 3 hectares of rural land for business use. Better still, if you reacquire Philippine citizenship under the Dual Citizenship Act (RA 9225), the land ban lifts entirely — you become a Filipino again for ownership purposes. For many readers with Philippine roots, reacquiring citizenship is the cleanest answer of all.

The US tax angle

Owning a Philippine condo doesn't create a US filing event by itself, but earning from it, banking for it, or selling it does.

Bank accounts and reporting. If you open a Philippine peso account to pay for the unit, its dues, and its RPT, and the aggregate balance of your foreign accounts tops US$10,000 at any point in the year, you must file an FBAR (FinCEN Form 114). The condo itself is not an FBAR or Form 8938 asset — directly held real estate never is — but the bank account behind it usually is, and larger holders can cross the Form 8938 (FATCA) thresholds.

Rental income. Rent from a Manila or Cebu condo is fully US-taxable. You report it on Schedule E and depreciate the building over the 30-year ADS period the US applies to foreign residential rental property (versus 27.5 years for a US property). Philippine income tax paid on that rent generally supports a foreign tax credit against the US tax on the same income.

The nuance nobody advertises. When you sell, the US taxes your actual capital gain — sale price minus your cost basis. The Philippines, by contrast, takes 6% of the gross sale price regardless of whether you made a profit. These two systems don't line up, and the mismatch bites in a specific way: if you sell for roughly what you paid (or at a loss), you have little or no US gain to be taxed — but you've still handed the Philippines 6% of the whole price. The US foreign tax credit can only offset US tax on the same category of income, so a foreign tax paid against a US gain that doesn't exist can be largely wasted: you can't credit it against your salary or your dividends, and a passive foreign tax credit you can't use this year may simply expire. The practical lesson is that the 6% Philippine tax is best thought of as a real, non-recoverable exit cost for an American — plan your sale price and timing around it, not on the assumption that the US credit makes it free.

Two more for the file. The Section 121 home-sale exclusion ($250,000 single / $500,000 married) can still apply to a foreign home if it was genuinely your primary residence and you meet the use tests. And because a foreign purchase financed in pesos can generate a Section 988 currency gain on repaying the loan that Section 121 does not shelter, most Americans are better off buying a Philippine condo in cash or with US-dollar funds than with a local peso mortgage.

Step-by-step: how to buy safely

  1. Decide what you can actually own. A condo you can title in your name, or land you can only lease or hold through a permitted structure. Match the property type to your legal options before you fall in love with a listing.
  2. Engage your own Philippine lawyer. Independent of the developer or seller — to verify title at the Registry of Deeds and, for a condo, confirm remaining foreign capacity in writing.
  3. Check the 40% cap and the developer's license. Get the condo corporation's certification that foreign capacity exists for your unit; verify the developer's License to Sell for pre-selling projects.
  4. Never use a nominee. No "put it in her name," no strawman 60/40 company. If you need land, lease it properly under RA 12252 or the 25+25 framework.
  5. Sign the Contract to Sell and pay through traceable channels. Keep every receipt; funds should move by bank transfer, not cash.
  6. Clear the BIR and get the CAR. Taxes (DST, transfer tax) paid, Certificate Authorizing Registration issued, new CCT/TCT registered in your name.
  7. Line up US compliance. FBAR, Form 8938 if applicable, Schedule E for rentals, and a cross-border advisor before any sale — especially given the 6% gross-price tax.

If you're not American: what changes

The Philippine rules in this guide — the constitutional ban on foreign land ownership, the 40% condominium cap, the 99-year investor lease under RA 12252, the ~3–5.5% buyer closing costs, the 6% capital gains tax, the SRRV, and the Anti-Dummy Law — are set by the Philippines and apply to every foreign buyer equally, whatever passport you hold. A Canadian, British, Australian, or Emirati buyer faces the same land ban and the same 40% cap as an American. What differs is only the tax wrapper back home.

The entire "US tax angle" section above — FBAR, Form 8938, Schedule E, the 30-year ADS depreciation, Section 121, Section 988, and the foreign-tax-credit mismatch on the 6% tax — is US-specific and does not apply to non-Americans. If you're not a US person, swap those rules for your own and check three things with a tax adviser in your country of residence: (1) whether your home country taxes foreign rental income and capital gains; (2) whether you must report foreign assets or accounts at home; and (3) whether your country has a double-taxation treaty with the Philippines — many do, and it can change how that 6% is credited (or not) at home. One point applies to everyone: if you have Filipino ancestry, the former-natural-born-citizen allowances and citizenship reacquisition can dwarf every other structuring option, regardless of where you now live.

Is it worth it in 2026?

For the right buyer, yes — but "the right buyer" is narrower than the brochures suggest. If you want a titled condominium in a major city, or you have Filipino roots that unlock land ownership or citizenship, the Philippines is a warm, English-speaking, low-cost place to own with a clean CCT and cheap annual taxes. The 2026 arrival of the 99-year lease also makes larger, land-based investment projects far more bankable than they were a year ago.

If, on the other hand, your dream is to own a beachfront lot outright as a foreigner, the honest answer is that the Constitution simply won't let you, and every workaround that promises otherwise is either a lease dressed up as ownership or an Anti-Dummy liability waiting to detonate. The buyers who get hurt here are almost never the ones who bought a condo by the book — they're the ones who trusted a nominee, a pre-selling tower's paperwork, or the idea that marriage or money overrides the land ban. Buy what you can title, lease what you can't, keep a US cross-border advisor in the loop for that 6% exit tax, and the Philippines rewards you. Improvise around the land rules, and it won't.

Frequently asked questions

Can Americans buy property in the Philippines?

Partly. The 1987 Constitution bars foreigners from owning land regardless of visa, marriage, or investment. But Americans can own condominium units in their own name (subject to the 40% foreign cap), own buildings separately from the land, and lease land long-term — up to 99 years for qualifying investors under the 2026 rules. Former natural-born Filipinos can own limited land outright.

What is the 40% rule for condos?

Foreign nationals combined may own no more than 40% of the units (by floor area) in any single condominium project. If a project is at that cap, the Registry of Deeds will not register your title even with a signed contract and paid deposit. Always get the developer or condo corporation to confirm remaining foreign capacity in writing before you sign.

How much are closing costs in the Philippines?

Roughly 3–5.5% on the buyer's side: the 1.5% documentary stamp tax, a local transfer tax of about 0.5–0.75%, registration fees around 0.25%, and notarial fees of 1–2%. The 6% capital gains tax is the seller's cost by law. Developer sales above the ~₱3.6 million VAT-exemption threshold add 12% VAT.

Can a foreigner own a house and lot?

Not the land. You can own the house but not the lot. The legitimate routes are to lease the land (up to 99 years for qualifying investors under RA 12252, or 25+25 under the older law), have a Filipino spouse hold it, or use a 60/40 Filipino-majority corporation. Using a Filipino nominee to hold land for you is illegal under the Anti-Dummy Law and can void the title.

Do Americans pay US tax on Philippine property?

Yes. The US taxes worldwide income: rental income goes on Schedule E (30-year depreciation), a Philippine bank account over $10,000 triggers an FBAR, and a sale is US-taxable with a foreign tax credit for the Philippine 6% tax. Because that 6% is charged on the gross price rather than your gain, a low-profit sale can leave you with foreign tax you can't fully credit.
