US Taxes on Buying Property in Mexico (2026)

MS By The MigrateState Editorial Team · Reviewed by a licensed international real-estate attorney  ·  Updated June 2026  ·  10 min read
Buying property in Mexico triggers no US tax at purchase — but it creates US reporting and taxes the property once it earns or sells. Expect an FBAR if your Mexican accounts top $10,000, possible Form 8938, US tax on rental income (offset by a foreign tax credit), and capital-gains tax on sale. The one thing most blogs get wrong: a standard fideicomiso is not a foreign trust, so Forms 3520/3520-A usually aren't required.

Buying a condo in Tulum or a casa in San Miguel doesn't put you on the IRS's radar the day you sign — there is no US tax on the act of buying. What changes is everything afterward. As a US citizen you are taxed on your worldwide income and saddled with worldwide reporting, so a Mexican property quietly attaches a small stack of US obligations to your life: a couple of information forms, US tax on any rent it earns, and US tax on the gain when you sell. Get them right and the cost is paperwork. Get them wrong and the penalties dwarf the tax. Here's the 2026 map — including the single rule that saves most coastal buyers from a form they were told they had to file.

Does buying trigger US tax?

No. Purchasing real estate abroad is a movement of capital, not a taxable event for US purposes, so the IRS collects nothing at closing. (Mexico does charge an acquisition tax, the ISAI, of roughly 2%–4% of value, plus notary and registration fees — a Mexican cost, not a US one.) What the purchase does do is start the clock on two things: US information reporting for the year you acquire, and a future US tax exposure on income and gain. The mistake isn't paying tax at purchase — it's forgetting that ownership itself is now a reportable fact in some circumstances.

The fideicomiso & the foreign-trust myth

If your property lies in Mexico's restricted zone — within 50 km of the coast or 100 km of a border, which covers nearly every beach market Americans want — a foreigner cannot hold direct title. You hold it through a fideicomiso, a bank trust where a Mexican bank is the trustee and you are the beneficiary with full rights to use, rent, improve and sell. It runs ~50 years, is renewable, and costs a setup fee plus ~$500–$700 a year. So far, ordinary.

Here's the genuine nuance, and the reason this section exists: for years, advisors insisted a fideicomiso was a foreign trust, dragging owners into the dreaded Forms 3520 and 3520-A — onerous filings with $10,000-plus penalties for lateness. Then the IRS issued Revenue Ruling 2013-14, holding that a standard fideicomiso that merely holds title to residential real estate is not a trust for US tax purposes. The bank holds bare legal title; you keep all the economic rights. Result: a properly structured fideicomiso does not require Form 3520 or 3520-A. Plenty of blog posts — and even some preparers — still tell buyers to file them. For a clean, real-estate-only fideicomiso, that advice is out of date. The caveat: if the trust is modified, holds cash or other assets, or strays from the plain title-holding arrangement, it can flip back into foreign-trust territory, so have a cross-border CPA confirm your specific deed.

FBAR, 8938 & the reporting map

Even with the 3520 problem off the table, two reporting regimes can still apply — and they hinge on accounts and financial assets, not on the bricks. The real estate itself is never a "foreign financial account," so the house alone triggers neither form. The cash around it can.

The practical trap: you open a Mexican bank account to pay the predial (property tax) and HOA, park a few thousand dollars in it, and never think about it again — and that account, not the condo, is what creates an FBAR duty once your foreign accounts collectively cross $10,000. Many fideicomiso owners also file an FBAR for the trust itself out of caution, even when it holds no cash, because the cost of over-reporting is zero and the cost of a missed FBAR is severe.

US tax on rental income

Rent the place out and the income is taxable in both countries — Mexico because the property is there, the US because you're a citizen. You report the gross rent and expenses on Schedule E, and you avoid double tax by claiming a foreign tax credit on Form 1116 for the Mexican income tax (ISR) you paid, which typically wipes out most or all of the US tax on that rent. Two details most owners miss: first, foreign residential rental property must be depreciated over 30 years under the Alternative Depreciation System — not the 27.5 years used for a US rental — which slightly lowers your annual depreciation deduction. Second, you can only depreciate the building, not the land, so you'll need a defensible land-vs-structure split. Keep Mexican receipts and your ISR payment records; the foreign tax credit is only as good as your proof.

Capital gains when you sell

When you sell, Mexico taxes the gain first because the property is Mexican — under the US-Mexico treaty (Article 13), real-property gains are taxed where the property sits. A non-resident seller pays 25% of the gross sale price by default — withheld by the Mexican notary at closing. If the seller appoints a Mexican fiscal (legal) representative and provides verifiable cost-basis documentation, the optional alternative rate of 35% of the net gain may be applied instead. The notary compares both when documentation is available and applies the lower; however, the 35%-of-net method requires prior planning and is not an automatic free choice — without a fiscal representative and proper records, only 25% of gross applies. The US then taxes the same gain on your return at long-term capital-gains rates (0/15/20%) plus the possible 3.8% net investment income tax — but a foreign tax credit for the Mexican tax offsets it, so you rarely pay twice.

Two US-side levers matter. If the home was your main residence for at least two of the last five years, the Section 121 exclusion ($250k single / $500k married) applies to a foreign home exactly as to a US one — though any depreciation you took as a rental after May 1997 is recaptured and can't be excluded. And remember the gain is computed in US dollars: because you bought in pesos and sell in pesos, exchange-rate movement between the two dates can create — or erase — a dollar gain that doesn't match what you feel in pesos. A peso that strengthened against the dollar can manufacture a US gain even on a flat peso sale.

What Mexico taxes (so you can credit it)

Because the foreign tax credit is your main shield against double taxation, it helps to know what Mexico itself collects. Annual property tax (predial) is famously low — often a few hundred dollars a year. Rental income is taxed under Mexico's ISR; residents face progressive brackets up to 35% (small landlords often use the simplified RESICO regime), while non-residents are generally taxed at flat withholding rates on Mexican-source rent. Capital gains follow the 25%-of-gross default or the conditional 35%-of-net option described above. Every peso of Mexican income tax you legitimately pay is potential foreign-tax-credit fuel on the US side — so file in Mexico properly, and keep the documentation, precisely so your US bill shrinks. Step-by-step compliance

  1. Confirm your structure — restricted-zone purchase via fideicomiso, or direct title outside the zone — and have a CPA confirm the trust is "clean" for US tax.
  2. Skip 3520/3520-A for a standard fideicomiso (Rev. Rul. 2013-14), unless your advisor flags a non-standard arrangement.
  3. Track foreign accounts — file an FBAR if they exceed $10,000, and Form 8938 if you pass the FATCA threshold.
  4. Report rental income on Schedule E; claim the foreign tax credit (Form 1116) for Mexican ISR; depreciate the building over 30 years.
  5. Keep every Mexican receipt — ISR payments, notary statements, the ISAI at purchase — to support credits and basis.
  6. Plan the sale early — model the Mexican 25%/35% election, the US gain in dollars, Section 121 eligibility, and depreciation recapture.
  7. Coordinate both filings with a cross-border CPA so the Mexican tax year and US credit line up.

If you're not American: what changes

The Mexican side is identical for any foreign buyer. The fideicomiso, the ISAI at purchase, the predial, Mexican ISR on rent, and the 25%/35% capital-gains election apply the same whatever passport you hold. What's purely American is the home-country wrapper — FBAR, Form 8938, Schedule E, the foreign tax credit and the foreign-trust analysis.

If you're not a US person, drop the IRS forms above and swap in your own country's rules. Check three things where you're tax-resident: (1) whether your country taxes foreign rental income and capital gains on a Mexican property (most do, on a worldwide basis); (2) whether you must report foreign property, accounts or assets at home — Canada's T1135, the UK's worldwide self-assessment, and EU wealth/asset disclosures all differ; and (3) whether your country has a double-taxation treaty with Mexico so the Mexican tax you pay is credited rather than stacked on top. A Canadian, Brit or German will run a very different return than an American on the same condo. Confirm the specifics with a tax adviser in your country of residence.

Is the US tax worth the hassle in 2026?

For most buyers, yes — the US tax cost of owning in Mexico is usually compliance, not dollars. The foreign tax credit means you rarely pay the same income twice, the fideicomiso myth that frightened a generation of buyers is settled in your favor, and the forms are routine once a cross-border CPA sets them up. The real risk is silence: an unfiled FBAR or a missed Schedule E carries penalties far larger than the tax itself. Treat the Mexican purchase as a US reporting project from day one — line up the right advisor, keep your peso receipts, and a beach condo stays a pleasure rather than an audit. Price the reporting before the property, and Mexico remains one of the most US-friendly places to own abroad.

Frequently asked questions

Do you pay US tax when you buy property in Mexico?

No US tax on the purchase itself. Ownership can create reporting (FBAR if Mexican accounts exceed $10,000, possibly Form 8938) and US tax on rental income or a future gain. The IRS taxes the property only when it earns or sells.

Does a Mexican fideicomiso require Form 3520?

Generally no. Under Rev. Rul. 2013-14 a standard real-estate fideicomiso is not a foreign trust, so Forms 3520/3520-A aren't required. Older articles saying otherwise are outdated. A modified trust holding cash can change the answer — confirm with a CPA.

Is rental income from Mexican property taxable in the US?

Yes — you report worldwide income, so Mexican rent goes on Schedule E. Claim a foreign tax credit (Form 1116) for the Mexican ISR you paid. Foreign residential rentals depreciate over 30 years, not 27.5.

How is capital gains taxed when an American sells Mexican property?

Mexico taxes the gain — 25% of the gross sale price by default; 35% of the net gain is an optional alternative requiring a Mexican fiscal representative and documented cost basis. The US also taxes the worldwide gain at 0/15/20% plus possible 3.8% NIIT, offset by a foreign tax credit. Section 121 ($250k/$500k) can apply to a former main home.

Do I have to file an FBAR for my Mexican property?

The real estate isn't a financial account, so the property alone doesn't trigger it. But a Mexican bank account for HOA/utilities/taxes does, once all your foreign accounts exceed $10,000. Many fideicomiso owners file an FBAR for the trust conservatively.
