Portugal NIF Number: How Foreigners Get One in 2026

MS By The MigrateState Editorial Team · Reviewed by a licensed international real-estate attorney  ·  Updated June 2026  ·  8 min read
A NIF (Número de Identificação Fiscal) is a nine-digit Portuguese tax number you must have before you can buy property, open a Portuguese bank account, or sign virtually any legal contract in Portugal. Getting one is free and — if you're physically in Lisbon or Porto — takes about 30 minutes at a Finanças office. If you're abroad, a fiscal representative can apply on your behalf via the official e-Balcão platform, typically within 3–7 business days. Non-EU nationals (Americans, Brits, Canadians) also need to appoint that representative formally — though a 2022 law change created a limited exemption that many property buyers misunderstand.

Portugal's property market draws more American buyers every year — for the lifestyle, the comparatively affordable prices by Western European standards, and the country's established Golden Visa and residency pathways. But before any of that machinery moves, you need a NIF. It is step zero in every Portuguese transaction, and no notary will proceed without one. This guide covers everything a foreign buyer needs to know about the NIF, with particular attention to the fiscal representative rules that trip up most non-European applicants.

What is a NIF and why do you need it?

NIF stands for Número de Identificação Fiscal — Portugal's equivalent of a US Social Security number for tax purposes, or the Spanish NIE. It is a permanent nine-digit number issued by the Portuguese Tax and Customs Authority (Autoridade Tributária e Aduaneira, known as AT). Once issued, your NIF does not change, and it follows you for life regardless of whether you become a resident or not.

In practical terms, a NIF is required for any of the following in Portugal: purchasing real estate (deed and transfer tax payment), opening a Portuguese bank account, signing a lease or rental agreement, invoicing as a freelancer or company, applying for the Golden Visa, Non-Habitual Resident (NHR) status, or its post-2024 replacement the IFICI (Incentivo Fiscal à Captação de Investimento) regime, and paying annual property taxes (IMI and AIMI). Without a NIF, none of these transactions can proceed. Even pre-purchase steps — signing the CPCV promissory contract that locks in your purchase and deposits your reservation payment — require one.

When do you need the NIF?

In a typical Portuguese property purchase, the sequence is: NIF first → bank account second → CPCV reservation contract third → final deed (escritura) last. The NIF needs to be in hand before the CPCV stage, because the contract itself must reference both parties' NIFs. Most property lawyers advise getting your NIF at least two to four weeks before you intend to sign the CPCV — enough time to apply remotely and also open a local bank account, which requires the NIF. If you are applying for a Golden Visa, the NIF is required on the investment application as well.

The fiscal representative requirement explained

This is the part of the NIF process that most non-European applicants find confusing, and it matters more than the NIF application itself. Portuguese law requires that any individual whose official tax address is outside the EU or European Economic Area (EEA) appoint a representante fiscal — a fiscal representative — when registering for a NIF or acquiring a Portuguese asset.

In plain terms: if you are an American, British (post-Brexit), Canadian, Australian, or any other non-EU/EEA national, you cannot simply apply for a NIF as an individual. You must designate a person or company resident in Portugal to act as your representative with the Portuguese tax authorities. This representative's Portuguese address becomes your official AT mailing address. They receive your tax correspondence, submit your Portuguese returns if any are due, and are jointly liable for ensuring you comply with Portuguese tax obligations. In practice, most buyers appoint a Portuguese law firm, accountant, or one of several specialist services that offer fiscal representation to foreigners for a fixed annual fee.

The fiscal representative must themselves hold a Portuguese NIF and be resident in Portugal. They are appointed by a power of attorney (procuração), which can be signed remotely and apostilled, or signed before a Portuguese consulate in your home country.

The Decree-Law 44/2022 exemption — and its practical limits

Decree-Law 44/2022 (in force since 2022) made a meaningful change: EU and EEA citizens no longer need a fiscal representative if they register an EU/EEA address and opt into electronic notifications (notificações eletrónicas) via the Portal das Finanças. A secondary provision in the same decree extended a partial exemption to non-EU/EEA nationals who have no ongoing tax obligations in Portugal and who activate electronic notifications on the AT portal.

This is the nuance that circulates on property forums and causes confusion. Technically, a US buyer who has no Portuguese-source income and activates e-notifications may be able to apply for a NIF without a fiscal representative. In practice, however, this exemption has two serious limitations that experienced property lawyers consistently flag.

First, the exemption disappears the moment you have Portuguese tax obligations — and owning property creates them immediately. Annual IMI (Imposto Municipal sobre Imóveis, Portugal's property tax) is billed by the municipality to the AT address on file. AIMI (Additional IMI) applies to properties above €600,000. Any rental income from a Portuguese property is taxable in Portugal at a flat 28% non-resident rate (or progressive rates by election). All of these generate tax correspondence that AT will send to your registered address — which, if you have no fiscal rep, must be the e-Balcão portal. Missing a filing deadline or a tax bill results in immediate surcharges and interest. The fine for non-compliance can range from €200 to over €2,500 depending on the situation.

Second, many Finanças offices and third-party e-Balcão processors still require a fiscal representative for non-EU/EEA applicants regardless of the 2022 exemption, particularly for property-related NIF applications. The law allows the exemption; local enforcement is inconsistent. Most Portugal-focused lawyers advise non-EU property buyers to appoint a fiscal rep for the initial NIF application and to keep one ongoing — the €200–500/year cost is trivial relative to the cost of a missed IMI deadline or a Portuguese return that needs an in-country professional to untangle.

Getting a NIF in person in Portugal

If you are physically in Portugal, visiting a local Finanças (tax office) is the fastest method. You do not need an appointment at all Finanças branches, though booking one online via the AT website is recommended to avoid queues. Bring your passport (or EU ID card), a document establishing your Portuguese address or the address of the property you are buying (a purchase agreement letter from the agent, or even a hotel booking, may be accepted for a temporary address), and if you are a non-EU/EEA national, either the details of your fiscal representative or the power of attorney document. The clerk enters your details and issues a printed NIF confirmation on the same day. The full taxpayer card (cartão de contribuinte) can be requested for approximately €7 — it is optional, as the number itself is all that matters for transactions.

Getting a NIF remotely from abroad

You do not need to travel to Portugal to get a NIF. The official route for non-residents is through the e-Balcão portal of the AT website, submitted by a fiscal representative on your behalf. You supply the representative with certified copies of your passport and proof of address; they submit the e-Balcão request electronically and the NIF is issued digitally, typically within two to five business days. Your representative then forwards the confirmation to you.

There is also an informal market of specialist NIF services — firms that exist specifically to obtain NIFs for foreigners, offer fiscal representation as an ongoing service, and charge a range of fees depending on what is included. These are legitimate, widely used, and often the most efficient route for buyers who have not yet selected their property lawyer. Prices for NIF-only services start around €50–70; full fiscal representation for a year runs €200–700 depending on the firm and the scope of services. Your property lawyer will typically offer NIF application and fiscal representation as part of their standard engagement, often at a bundled rate — it is worth confirming this upfront.

What comes after the NIF: bank account and IMI

The NIF is the key that opens the next two doors: a Portuguese bank account and property tax registration. Once you have your NIF, you can approach a Portuguese bank to open a non-resident account, which you will need to pay the IMT property transfer tax and stamp duty at closing, and to manage ongoing costs. Major Portuguese banks that routinely serve foreign buyers include Millennium BCP, Caixa Geral de Depósitos, Santander Portugal, and Novo Banco. Bring your NIF confirmation, passport, proof of address, and source-of-funds documentation; many branches have international client desks with English-speaking staff.

Once you own Portuguese property, your NIF is the identifier AT uses to send your IMI bill each year, typically in April/May for the prior year. IMI rates for urban properties are set by each municipality within a national band of 0.3% to 0.45% of the taxable property value. Lisbon and Porto have historically set their rates at 0.3%. AIMI (Additional IMI) applies at 0.7% on the portion of property value above €600,000 (single taxpayer) and 1.5% on the portion above €1 million, with an exemption for properties held through companies. Your fiscal representative's job includes receiving and forwarding these bills — one more reason to keep the representation active for as long as you own the property.

US tax implications: your NIF and Portuguese income

Having a Portuguese NIF does not by itself trigger any US tax obligation. The NIF is Portugal's identifier; the IRS does not care that you have one. What creates US obligations is Portuguese-source income — rental payments, capital gains on sale, or interest in a Portuguese bank account. Portuguese rental income from a property you own must be declared both to AT (on a Portuguese non-resident return) and to the IRS on Schedule E. Portugal applies a 28% flat withholding rate to non-resident rental income; the US taxes the same income at ordinary income rates. The Portugal–US double-taxation treaty, in force since 1995, allows you to claim a foreign tax credit on your US return for Portuguese tax paid, generally eliminating the double-taxation but not eliminating the Portuguese filing obligation. A cross-border tax advisor who knows both systems is valuable here — the treaty mechanics are not complex, but they do require coordinated filings on both sides.

One specific US point worth noting: directly-held Portuguese real estate is not reportable on your FBAR or Form 8938. The property itself is not a "financial account" or a "specified foreign financial asset" for either form. Your Portuguese bank account is an FBAR account once it exceeds $10,000 at any point in the year. Keep those two categories clear — they are different reporting obligations with different thresholds.

Step-by-step checklist: NIF to purchase-ready

  1. Identify a fiscal representative (non-EU/EEA buyers) — your property lawyer, an accountant, or a specialist NIF service. Confirm they will also handle ongoing fiscal representation after the NIF is issued.
  2. Prepare your documents — certified copy of passport, certified proof of address, power of attorney (apostilled) if applying remotely. Your lawyer can advise on the exact apostille requirement for your state.
  3. Submit the NIF application — in person at Finanças (same day) or via e-Balcão through your fiscal rep (2–7 business days).
  4. Receive and store your NIF — a nine-digit number on an AT-issued confirmation sheet. Keep digital and physical copies.
  5. Open a Portuguese bank account — using your NIF as the primary identifier. Bring NIF confirmation, passport, and source-of-funds documentation.
  6. Sign the CPCV — the preliminary purchase contract. Both parties' NIFs must appear on the contract; your lawyer will insert them.
  7. Activate e-notifications on AT portal — optional but useful: log in to portaldasfinancas.gov.pt using your NIF and create an AT login to monitor your tax position and verify your fiscal rep is properly linked.
  8. Maintain fiscal representation — keep your fiscal rep appointed for as long as you own Portuguese property. Budget €200–500/year and treat it as a fixed ownership cost alongside IMI.

If you're not American: what changes

The NIF process itself is identical regardless of your passport. Every foreigner — American, British, Canadian, German, Australian — follows the same AT procedure. What changes is the home-country reporting wrapper around Portuguese income you earn as an owner.

Non-US readers should substitute the following for the US-specific analysis above: (1) check whether your home country taxes foreign rental income — most do, including the UK, Canada, Australia, and all EU member states, though rates and allowable deductions differ; (2) check whether your country requires reporting of foreign assets, bank accounts, or property holdings — Canada's T1135, the UK's self-assessment foreign income pages, and Germany's Finanzamt foreign-income rules all impose analogous duties; (3) confirm whether a double-taxation treaty between your home country and Portugal covers rental income and capital gains — Portugal has treaties with most developed nations, and the treaty typically provides a credit in your home country for Portuguese tax paid. The NIF and fiscal representative obligations are driven by Portuguese law, not your passport, and apply equally to all non-residents who own property in Portugal.

Is getting a NIF worth it in 2026?

The question barely applies — the NIF is not optional. If you want to buy property, open a bank account, sign a lease, or establish any legal or financial footprint in Portugal, you need one. The real question is how to set it up cleanly from the start.

The common mistake is treating the NIF as a simple bureaucratic box to tick and using the cheapest online service to get the number — then discovering six months after purchase that the fiscal representative was never properly appointed, that AT has been sending IMI bills to a stale address, and that there are now penalty surcharges on unpaid property tax. The NIF application itself costs nothing; the ongoing fiscal representation costs €200–500/year. Skipping that annual cost to save money is false economy when an overlooked IMI bill or a missed Portuguese return can generate penalties in the hundreds or thousands of euros. Structure it correctly at the start, appoint a competent fiscal rep — ideally the same lawyer handling your purchase — and the NIF becomes the invisible foundation that the rest of your Portuguese property ownership runs smoothly on top of.

Frequently asked questions

Do Americans need a NIF to buy property in Portugal?

Yes — without exception. The NIF must appear on the CPCV (promissory contract) and the final escritura deed. It is also required to pay IMT transfer tax and open the Portuguese bank account you will use to fund the purchase. Apply for it before any money changes hands.

Do non-EU nationals need a fiscal representative for a Portuguese NIF?

In practice, yes — especially for property owners. Decree-Law 44/2022 created a limited exemption for non-EU nationals who activate electronic notifications and have no Portuguese tax obligations, but owning property creates ongoing IMI and potentially rental-income obligations that make a fiscal representative essential regardless. Most property lawyers include fiscal representation in their standard service package.

How much does a Portuguese NIF cost?

The NIF number itself is free from AT. A physical taxpayer card costs ~€7. Remote third-party NIF services charge €50–150 for the application. Ongoing fiscal representation — which property owners need — runs €200–700/year depending on the provider and service level.

How long does it take to get a NIF in Portugal?

Same day in person at a Finanças office. Two to five business days via e-Balcão through a fiscal representative. Three to seven business days through a third-party remote service, once documents are submitted. Plan for at least two weeks before your intended CPCV date to allow for the NIF, bank account opening, and any document certification delays.

Can I get a NIF without visiting Portugal?

Yes. A Portuguese-resident fiscal representative can apply on your behalf via the official e-Balcão portal. You need only provide a certified copy of your passport and proof of address, plus a power of attorney. You do not need to set foot in Portugal before or during the NIF application.
