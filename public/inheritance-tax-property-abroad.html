Inheritance Tax on Property Abroad (2026)

MS By The MigrateState Editorial Team · Reviewed by a licensed international real-estate attorney  ·  Updated June 2026  ·  11 min read
Three different things decide what happens to your foreign property when you die — don't confuse them. US estate tax reaches your worldwide assets, but the 2026 exemption is $15 million per person (40% above it), so most estates owe nothing. A separate local inheritance tax usually applies where the property sits. And forced heirship in civil-law countries can dictate who inherits, regardless of your will. A US citizen can often override that with a Brussels IV will election — but it changes succession, not the tax.

Buying a place abroad is a hopeful act; planning how it passes on is the part almost everyone postpones. Yet foreign property is exactly where estates go wrong, because three separate systems collide at once — the US estate tax on your worldwide wealth, a local inheritance tax in the country where the bricks sit, and, in much of Europe and Latin America, forced-heirship rules that decide who inherits no matter what your will says. They are routinely lumped together as "inheritance tax," and that confusion is what produces frozen estates, surprise bills, and children inheriting shares their parent never intended. Here is the 2026 map: what each system actually does, how they interact for a US owner, and the one piece of paperwork that quietly protects your heirs' tax bill years later.

Three taxes people confuse

Separate these three and most of the fog clears. Estate tax is levied on the deceased's estate before assets pass — the US uses this model. Inheritance tax is levied on the heir who receives, and the rate often depends on how closely related they are — most of Europe uses this model. Forced heirship is not a tax at all; it is a succession rule that reserves part of the estate for certain heirs and overrides the will. You can owe local inheritance tax and be bound by forced heirship and sit inside the US estate-tax system simultaneously — they are independent layers, and each is handled with a different tool.

US estate tax on worldwide property

As a US citizen or domiciliary, your worldwide estate is within reach of the federal estate tax — your apartment in Lisbon counts exactly like your house in Ohio. The reassuring part is the size of the shield: for deaths in 2026 the federal exemption is $15 million per person (made permanent by the 2025 tax law and indexed thereafter), with a top rate of 40% on the excess. A married couple can combine exemptions and, with portability, effectively shield $30 million. For the overwhelming majority of cross-border owners, that means no federal estate tax is due — the foreign property simply uses up a sliver of a very large exemption.

Two cautions. First, the generous figure is for US persons; a non-domiciled non-citizen who dies owning US-situated assets gets only a $60,000 exemption — relevant if your heirs or spouse are non-citizens. Second, the US maintains estate- and gift-tax treaties with about 16 countries, which can reallocate taxing rights and prevent the same asset being taxed twice. If your estate is large enough to approach the exemption, those treaties — and a non-citizen spouse's lack of the unlimited marital deduction (a QDOT trust is the usual fix) — are where the real planning happens.

Local inheritance tax abroad

This is the bill US owners actually meet. Most countries where Americans buy tax the heir, and the rate typically turns on the relationship: a spouse or child is taxed lightly or exempt; a distant relative or unrelated beneficiary is taxed heavily. The spread is enormous and it is local, not federal. A child inheriting a Spanish flat may pay little after regional allowances; the same flat passing to a friend can be taxed at a punishing rate. Some countries (Portugal, for instance) have no inheritance tax between close family, charging only a small stamp duty to others. Others — France, Spain, Italy in part — apply real inheritance tax with bands and reliefs that differ by region.

The practical point: the place to model the tax is the country where the property sits, using that country's relationship-based rates, not the US estate tax. And because most of these countries do not have an estate-tax treaty with the US, the foreign inheritance tax your heirs pay may not be creditable against any US tax — though with a $15M US exemption, there is usually no US estate tax to credit it against anyway.

Forced heirship: who actually inherits

Here is the rule that surprises Americans most, because there is no US equivalent. In civil-law countries, forced heirship reserves a guaranteed slice of the estate for protected heirs — usually children, sometimes the spouse — and that slice cannot be written out of a will. It attaches to real estate located in the country regardless of the owner's nationality. You cannot simply leave your French villa to a partner or a charity if you have children; the law reserves their share first.

It is why a US-style will that "leaves everything to my spouse" can be partly void over an EU property, and why probate stalls when heirs and reserved-share rules conflict. The Brussels IV will election

The EU Succession Regulation (Brussels IV, EU 650/2012) changed cross-border estates across the EU. Its default rule is that the law of your habitual residence at death governs your succession. But it grants a powerful option: anyone — including a non-EU national such as a US citizen — can elect in their will to have the law of their nationality govern instead. For an American that means electing the law of your US home state, which has no forced heirship, thereby displacing the reserved-share rules over your EU property and letting you leave it as you wish.

The election is made by an express clause in your will (an implied election is riskier and best avoided). Get it right and a US citizen can leave a Spanish or Italian property entirely to a spouse or a charity. Two limits matter. First, Brussels IV governs succession, not tax — electing New York law over your Tuscan house does nothing to the Italian inheritance tax the heirs will owe. Second, France is the exception: since a 2021 reform, a French clawback (droit de prélèvement) can restore reserved shares for children who are EU residents or nationals even where foreign law was validly chosen, so the election is less bulletproof for French property. Ireland and the UK never opted into Brussels IV, which has its own knock-on effects. This is precisely the kind of clause that needs a lawyer in the property's country, not a US online will.

Step-up basis & Form 3520 — the part heirs forget

This is the genuine nuance most articles skip, and it works in the heir's favour. When a US person inherits foreign real estate, the property generally takes a step-up in basis to its fair market value on the date of death — the same rule that applies to US assets, confirmed by IRS guidance even when the property never entered the US estate-tax net. The effect is large: decades of local-currency appreciation can be wiped out for US capital-gains purposes, so an heir who sells soon after inheriting may owe little or no US tax on the sale.

The trap is proof. Years later, when the property is sold and the gain is computed in US dollars, the IRS will expect support for that date-of-death value. Heirs who never obtained a contemporaneous appraisal can lose the benefit and overpay capital gains. So the move is to get a written valuation as of the date of death and keep it.

Separately, receiving the inheritance triggers a reporting obligation, not a tax one. A foreign inheritance is not US income. But if you receive more than $100,000 in aggregate in a year from a nonresident alien or a foreign estate, you must file Form 3520 to report it. The form is informational, yet the penalties for missing it are severe — and, helpfully, it is one more place to memorialize the inherited value that supports your future basis. If the property's rental income or proceeds later sit in a foreign account, remember the ordinary FBAR and Form 8938 reporting follows too.

Step-by-step to protect your heirs

  1. Map the three layers — confirm whether your estate is anywhere near the $15M US exemption, what local inheritance tax the heir would owe, and whether the country has forced heirship.
  2. Make a will in the property's country — a local will that dovetails with your US will avoids contradictory probate and delay.
  3. Add the Brussels IV clause if it's an EU property — expressly elect your US state's law to control succession (and get specialist advice for French property given the 2021 clawback).
  4. Handle a non-citizen spouse — the unlimited marital deduction doesn't apply; a QDOT or other structure may be needed if the estate is large.
  5. Check the treaty — if one of the ~16 US estate-tax treaties applies, use it to avoid double taxation.
  6. On inheriting, get a date-of-death appraisal — lock in the stepped-up basis with a contemporaneous valuation.
  7. File Form 3520 if the inheritance from a foreign person or estate tops $100,000 — and keep FBAR/Form 8938 in view for the accounts involved.

If you're not American: what changes

The property side is identical for any foreign owner. Forced heirship, local inheritance tax, and the Brussels IV election all attach to the property and its location, not to your passport — a Canadian, Brit or Australian faces the same French réserve, the same Spanish legítima, and can make the same nationality election under Brussels IV. What's purely American is the US estate tax wrapper: the $15M worldwide exemption, the $100,000 Form 3520 trigger, and the US step-up in basis.

If you're not a US person, drop the IRS layer above and substitute your own country of tax residence. Check three things: (1) whether your home country levies any estate or inheritance tax on worldwide assets — some (the UK) tax a domiciliary's worldwide estate, while others (Canada, Australia) have no inheritance tax but instead deem a capital-gains disposal at death; (2) whether you have a home-country reporting duty on the foreign asset (Canada's T1135, for example); and (3) whether a double-taxation or estate-tax treaty exists between your country and the country where the property sits, so a tax paid in one is relieved in the other. The destination country's inheritance tax and succession rules don't change — only the home-country layer does. Confirm the specifics with a tax adviser where you're resident.

Is it worth planning in 2026?

Inheriting or leaving foreign property in 2026 is entirely manageable — but only with a plan made in advance, because the failures here are almost always failures to prepare. The federal estate tax rarely bites thanks to the $15M exemption, so the real work is local: model the heir's inheritance tax where the property sits, defeat forced heirship with a properly drafted Brussels IV election (France aside), and put a country-specific will in place so probate doesn't freeze the asset for your family. Then, on the receiving end, lock in the date-of-death valuation and file Form 3520 so the step-up actually sticks when the property is one day sold. None of this is exotic; it is a short checklist that, done early, turns a cross-border estate from a years-long headache into a clean handover. The owner who plans the succession as carefully as the purchase is the one whose heirs keep the property — and the value in it.

Frequently asked questions

Do Americans pay US estate tax on property they own abroad?

Potentially, but rarely in practice. A US citizen or domiciliary is taxed on worldwide assets, including foreign real estate, yet the 2026 federal exemption is $15 million per person (40% above it), so most estates owe nothing federally. The property may still face a separate local inheritance tax where it sits — and that is the tax US owners usually encounter.

What is forced heirship and does it affect my foreign property?

Forced heirship (réserve in France, legítima in Spain, legittima in Italy) reserves a fixed share of an estate for children and sometimes a spouse, overriding the will. It applies to real estate in those civil-law countries regardless of your nationality — in France from 50% for one child to 75% for three or more. It governs who inherits, which is separate from how much tax is due.

Can a US citizen avoid EU forced heirship with a will?

Often, yes. Under the EU Succession Regulation (Brussels IV), a US citizen can include an express clause electing the law of their nationality — their US state's law — to govern succession of EU property, displacing forced heirship. France is the exception: a 2021 reform lets a clawback restore reserved shares for children who are EU residents or nationals.

Does inherited foreign property get a step-up in basis for US tax?

Yes. Even when foreign real estate never entered the US estate-tax net, a US heir generally takes it at fair market value on the date of death, which can erase decades of appreciation for US capital-gains purposes. The catch is proof — obtain a contemporaneous date-of-death appraisal so the IRS accepts the stepped-up basis when the property is later sold.

Do I have to report a foreign inheritance to the IRS?

A foreign inheritance is not income and is not taxed as income. But if you receive more than $100,000 in a year from a nonresident alien or a foreign estate, you must file Form 3520 to report it. The form is informational, not a tax, but penalties for missing it are steep — and it helps document the inherited property's basis.
