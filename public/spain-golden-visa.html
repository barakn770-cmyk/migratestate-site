Spain Golden Visa: Abolished April 2025 — Alternatives & US Tax Guide (2026)

MS By The MigrateState Editorial Team · Reviewed by a licensed international real-estate attorney  ·  Updated June 2026  ·  9 min read
Spain's Golden Visa is gone. On 3 April 2025, Spain formally abolished its residency-by-investment program. Buying property in Spain — at any price — no longer earns a residence permit. Applicants who filed before that date are grandfathered and can renew. Everyone else must use a different route: the Non-Lucrative Visa (€2,400/month passive income) or the Digital Nomad Visa (€2,850/month, remote work). You can still buy property freely — the purchase rules haven't changed.

What Spain's Golden Visa was

Launched in 2013, Spain's Visa de Residencia para Inversores — the Golden Visa — gave non-EU nationals a two-year residence permit in exchange for a qualifying investment. The most popular route by far was real estate: a minimum purchase of €500,000 (free of mortgages and liens) in any Spanish property. Additional routes covered government bonds, bank deposits, and business investment, but property accounted for the vast majority of applications.

The program's attraction for Americans was the absence of a minimum-stay requirement. You could own the permit, travel freely within Schengen, and only visit Spain for a few weeks a year. After five years of legal residency the holder could apply for permanent residency; after ten years (provided certain language and integration criteria were met), Spanish citizenship was theoretically possible — though few property-only holders accumulated enough qualifying time to reach citizenship.

At its peak the program attracted roughly €4 billion a year in qualifying investment, with Chinese, Russian, American, and British nationals among the top applicants. It was also politically controversial: critics argued it inflated urban property prices and provided residency to wealthy individuals who never actually lived in Spain.

Why and when it ended

The Spanish government under Prime Minister Pedro Sánchez moved to abolish the program in 2024, citing housing affordability. The argument — that €500,000 property purchases by wealthy foreigners pushed up prices in already-tight urban markets like Barcelona and Madrid — was politically potent even if economists debated the magnitude. The formal abolition came through Organic Law 1/2025, with an effective date of 3 April 2025. From that date, Spain stopped accepting new Golden Visa applications. The investment routes (bonds, deposits, business) were also abolished simultaneously; the entire scheme was shut down, not merely the real-estate route.

It is worth noting what the abolition did not do: it did not restrict the right of foreigners to buy property in Spain, it did not impose new purchase taxes on non-EU buyers (that remains a separate proposal — not yet law as of 2026), and it did not affect the rights of existing Golden Visa holders.

Who is grandfathered

The key rule: applicants who submitted a complete Golden Visa application — either at a Spanish consulate abroad or directly to the UGE-CE (Large Enterprises and Strategic Sectors Central Unit) — before 3 April 2025 are not affected by the abolition. Their permits can be issued and, once in hand, renewed under the original terms. The grandfathering also extends to family members included in a pre-cutoff application.

What this means practically: if you or a family member holds a valid Spain Golden Visa today, you can renew it indefinitely, accumulate years toward permanent residency, and the abolition does not interrupt your pathway. If you were in mid-application process and submitted all required documents before the deadline, your application should proceed — though processing delays at Spanish consulates have created some uncertainty and individual outcomes may depend on which documents were received when. If you are in this situation, an immigration lawyer in Spain is essential.

If you did not apply before 3 April 2025, you cannot obtain a Golden Visa regardless of willingness to invest €500,000 or more in Spanish real estate. There are no exceptions and no announced plans to reinstate the program.

Residency alternatives in 2026

Spain did not close off legal immigration when it ended the Golden Visa — it closed off one specific and expensive route. Several other permits remain open to Americans and other non-EU nationals:

Non-Lucrative Visa (NLV)

The NLV (Visado de Residencia No Lucrativa) is designed for financially self-sufficient individuals who will live in Spain without working there. It requires proof of sufficient income or savings — the 2026 minimum is approximately €2,400 per month for a single applicant (roughly 400% of Spain's monthly Public Indicator of Multiple Effects, the IPREM), with additional amounts for dependents. The permit is initially valid for one year, then renewable for two years at a time. A key restriction: you cannot work (employed or self-employed) for any entity while on an NLV — including remote work for a US company. Passive income — dividends, rental income, a pension, capital distributions — is fine; wages are not.

Digital Nomad Visa (DNV)

Introduced in January 2023 under Spain's Startup Act (Ley de Startups), the Digital Nomad Visa is for remote workers and freelancers employed by or providing services to companies based outside Spain. The income minimum in 2026 is approximately €2,850 per month (200% of average Spanish salary) for an individual, with 75% of the monthly SMI (minimum wage) added for the first dependent. Initial permits last one year if applied from outside Spain, or three years if applied from within on a tourist visa conversion. Up to 20% of income may come from Spanish clients. Unlike the NLV, the DNV specifically authorises you to work — just not for a Spanish employer or local clients beyond the 20% threshold.

Entrepreneur and Highly Qualified Professional Visas

For those who will launch a business in Spain or have been offered a job by a Spanish company. The entrepreneur visa requires a business plan vetted by Spain's National Innovation Enterprise office (ENISA). These routes are outside the scope of most property-focused buyers but are worth noting if your goal is to build a business in Spain.

How to apply for the NLV or DNV

  1. Gather income documentation — 3–6 months of bank statements, Social Security award letters, brokerage statements, or employment contracts (DNV). All must be translated into Spanish by a certified translator.
  2. Get an FBI background check with apostille — required for both visas. US applicants need a federal-level check, not a state-level one. Processing takes 8–12 weeks via the FBI's standard channel (or 3–4 weeks via a channeler service).
  3. Obtain a medical certificate — issued by a doctor within 3 months of application, translated and apostilled.
  4. Private health insurance — comprehensive coverage for Spain is required. Note: US health plans typically do not satisfy this requirement; you'll need a Spain-specific policy.
  5. Apply at the Spanish consulate in your jurisdiction. In the US, consulate appointments fill up months in advance — build this into your timeline.
  6. Enter Spain on the visa and within 30 days register your NIE and apply for the residency card (Tarjeta de Identidad de Extranjero, TIE).
  7. Set up Spanish bank account and register for taxes — once you become a tax resident (183+ days), you must file Spanish income tax (IRPF) and, if assets exceed €2 million, a solidarity wealth tax.

From application to approval, the NLV and DNV typically take three to five months when consulate availability is included. Plan accordingly if you're targeting a specific move date.

US tax rules: what changes if you move to Spain

This is the section most immigration guides skip — and for Americans, it's where the real complexity lives.

You remain a US taxpayer regardless

The United States taxes its citizens on worldwide income, full stop. Moving to Spain doesn't relieve you of IRS obligations. You will file both a US return and a Spanish return once you've been in Spain for more than 183 days in a calendar year, triggering Spanish tax residency.

Spain's income tax (IRPF) and how the treaty helps

As a Spanish tax resident, your worldwide income is subject to Spanish IRPF at progressive rates up to 47% on income above €300,000 (and some regions add surcharges). You'll also pay into Spain's social security system if you're employed or self-employed there. The US-Spain tax treaty and the US foreign tax credit generally let you credit Spanish taxes paid against your US bill, avoiding true double taxation — but you must file both returns and the credit calculations require a cross-border CPA.

The Beckham Law trap for Americans

Here is the nuance most guides miss entirely. Spain's Digital Nomad Visa comes with the option to elect a special tax regime, colloquially called the "Beckham Law" (formally Article 93 of Spain's Income Tax Act, Ley 35/2006), which was expanded in 2023 to cover digital nomads. Under this regime, you pay a flat 24% on Spanish-source income up to €600,000 for your first six years of Spanish residence — and your foreign-source income is largely excluded from the Spanish tax base.

That sounds attractive, but for US citizens it creates a problem. Electing the Beckham regime means your foreign income is taxed only in the US. You can no longer claim Spanish taxes on that foreign income as a foreign tax credit on your US return (since Spain didn't tax it). This structure also conflicts with claiming the Foreign Earned Income Exclusion (FEIE), since the FEIE requires you to be a bona fide foreign resident — but the Beckham regime technically puts you in a kind of hybrid tax status. Most cross-border CPAs advise US citizens to skip the Beckham election and instead use the regular IRPF scale plus the US-Spain treaty foreign tax credit, which provides cleaner double-taxation relief.

FBAR, Form 8938, and the wealth tax

A Spanish bank account for day-to-day living will almost certainly trigger US FBAR (FinCEN 114) once the aggregate of your foreign accounts exceeds $10,000 at any point in the year. Form 8938 may also apply if financial assets exceed $200,000 single (resident abroad) or $400,000 married filing jointly (resident abroad) at year-end. Note: your Spanish home itself — held directly in your name — does not need to be reported on FBAR or Form 8938; only financial accounts do.

Spain also has a solidarity wealth tax on residents with net global assets above €3 million (rates 1.7%–3.5% on the excess). For Americans with significant US investment portfolios who become Spanish tax residents, this can represent a material annual cost that has nothing to do with the property purchase itself.

If you're not American: what changes

The property purchase rules — no restrictions on foreign ownership, ITP/IVA transfer taxes, notary and registry costs, and the step-by-step process — are identical for all foreign buyers regardless of passport. Only the home-country tax wrapper differs.

Non-US readers should substitute the US-specific sections above with their own country's rules. In particular, check: (1) whether your home country taxes foreign rental income or capital gains — many do, including the UK, Canada, and Australia; (2) whether you have any foreign-asset reporting obligation at home (UK residents with foreign accounts, Australian residents with offshore interests, and Canadians with foreign property above CAD $100,000 all face their own disclosure regimes); (3) the double-taxation treaty between your country and Spain — the treaty determines whether you can credit Spanish taxes against your home bill and vice versa; and (4) any exit or departure tax your country may levy when you move abroad (Canada's deemed-disposition rule, for instance, can trigger a capital-gains bill on your worldwide assets the day you cease Canadian tax residency). Confirm all of this with a tax adviser qualified in your country of residence before committing to a Spanish move.

Is Spain residency still worth it in 2026?

The short answer is yes — for the right profile. The Golden Visa's abolition closes the "buy a property, pocket a permit, never show up" model. What remains requires genuine engagement: living in Spain more than half the year, filing Spanish taxes, navigating a Spanish bureaucracy that rewards patience over speed. For Americans who actually want to live in Spain — for the quality of life, Mediterranean climate, cost of living lower than most US coastal cities, and the EU's cultural and travel access — the NLV and DNV routes are entirely viable paths. The income thresholds are achievable for many remote professionals and retirees.

Where Spain has become genuinely less attractive is for investors seeking an EU base with minimal physical presence. For that use case, Greece, Malta, or Italy now have a cleaner offer. Investors who want the Spain lifestyle and a residence permit should now budget the time and compliance cost of actually living there — and start the lawyer and CPA conversations long before they board a flight.

Frequently asked questions

Is Spain's Golden Visa still available in 2026?

No. Spain abolished the program on 3 April 2025 under Organic Law 1/2025. New applications are not accepted. Holders who applied before that date can still renew under the original rules.

Who was grandfathered when Spain abolished the Golden Visa?

Applicants who submitted a complete application before 3 April 2025 retain their rights — their permits can be issued and subsequently renewed. Family members included in a pre-cutoff application are also covered. Anyone who had not yet applied by that date cannot obtain a Golden Visa, regardless of investment size.

What residency options remain for Americans in Spain after the Golden Visa ended?

Two main routes: the Non-Lucrative Visa (NLV) for financially independent individuals with at least ~€2,400/month in passive income who won't work locally; and the Digital Nomad Visa (DNV) for remote workers earning at least ~€2,850/month from foreign clients. Property purchase remains unrestricted — it just no longer grants residency.

Does the end of Spain's Golden Visa affect my right to buy property there?

No. Spain places no restrictions on foreign property ownership. You can still buy as an American with the same rights as a local. The change is that a purchase no longer earns a residence permit.

Should a US citizen elect Spain's Beckham Law (special tax regime) as a digital nomad?

Usually not. The Beckham regime caps Spanish tax at 24% on income up to €600,000 but excludes foreign income from the Spanish base — which means you can't use the foreign tax credit to offset it on your US return. It also conflicts with the Foreign Earned Income Exclusion. Most US cross-border CPAs recommend using the regular IRPF scale plus the US-Spain treaty foreign tax credit instead.
