Why Americans look at Malaysia

Malaysia is one of Southeast Asia's most open markets for foreign buyers. Unlike Thailand, where foreigners can't own land, or the Philippines, which caps foreign ownership of a condo building, Malaysia lets an American take clean freehold title to a Kuala Lumpur condo or, in most states, a landed house — in their own name, resident or not. Prices are low by Western standards, the legal system is common-law and English-language, and KL rental yields are solid.

The catch is that openness comes with conditions. Every foreign purchase must clear a state-set minimum price and win discretionary state consent (typically 3–6 months). Foreigners cannot touch Malay Reserved or Bumiputera-quota land, and some states bar them from landed homes. And from 1 January 2026 foreign buyers pay a flat 8% stamp duty — a material jump that reshapes the entry math.

Who Malaysia suits — and who should look elsewhere

Malaysia fits the buyer who wants freehold ownership in a stable, English-speaking, common-law market at a fraction of Western prices, who values a liquid KL income asset or a Penang/Johor lifestyle base, and who may want real time in-country via MM2H. Read the full guide to buying property in Malaysia for the thresholds, the consent process, the 8% stamp duty, RPGT and the US tax side.

Malaysia is a weaker fit if you want a quick flip (the 8% entry and 30% RPGT for five years make short holds irrational), if you're set on landed property in a state that bans it for foreigners, or if you want residency from the purchase alone — it grants none (see our best golden visas for Americans instead).

Malaysia FAQ

Can an American buy property in Malaysia?

Yes — freehold, in their own name. Foreigners can own strata units freely and landed homes in most states. But every purchase must exceed the state's minimum price (commonly RM1,000,000, ranging RM500,000–RM3,000,000) and win written state-authority consent. Malay Reserved land, Bumiputera-quota units, and generally agricultural land are off-limits, and some states bar foreigners from landed property.

What are the costs of buying in Malaysia in 2026?

From 1 January 2026, foreign buyers pay a flat 8% stamp duty on residential transfers — the largest single cost. Add conveyancing legal fees on the Solicitors' Remuneration Order scale (about 1.25% on the first RM500,000, then 1%), plus 8% service tax, state consent fees, and disbursements. Budget roughly 10%–12% of the price all-in.

What is RPGT and what will I pay when I sell?

Real Property Gains Tax. A foreign individual pays 30% on the gain for a disposal within the first five years, then 10% from year six onward — foreigners never reach 0%. On sale, the buyer's solicitor retains 7% of the disposal price and remits it to LHDN under Section 21B as an advance against the bill. Treat Malaysian property as a medium-to-long hold.

Does buying property in Malaysia give me a visa?

No. Ownership grants no residency. The route is MM2H, restructured into SEZ, Silver, Gold and Platinum tiers — each mainland tier now requires a qualifying property purchase (RM600,000 to RM2,000,000) plus a fixed deposit (USD 150,000 to USD 1,000,000). Property can be part of an MM2H application, but a purchase alone confers no right to reside.

What US taxes apply to a Malaysian property?

The US taxes worldwide income. Rental income goes on Schedule E, with the building depreciated over 30 years under the ADS method for foreign property. You can usually claim a foreign tax credit for Malaysian RPGT. Directly-held real estate isn't itself FBAR- or Form 8938-reportable, but the MM2H fixed deposit is — it's a foreign bank account that triggers FBAR once your foreign accounts exceed USD 10,000, and likely Form 8938 too.
