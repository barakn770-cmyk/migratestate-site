Closing Costs When Buying Property Abroad (2026)

Buying property abroad works like buying at home in one uncomfortable way: the price tag on the listing is never the price you pay. On top of the agreed sale price sits a layer of government taxes, notarial or registry fees, legal costs, and agent commissions that varies dramatically by country — and, in a federal system like Spain's, by region within a country. In most of Europe you should budget between seven and fifteen percent of the purchase price in closing costs alone. In Mexico and Dubai that range shrinks to four to eight percent, but Mexico adds a recurring annual trust fee that most buyers discover long after they've congratulated themselves on a great deal.

The single most dangerous mistake buyers make is to let their real-estate agent prepare the cost estimate. Agents quote the fees they know — their own commission — and sometimes omit or understate the taxes. The correct approach is to hand the preliminary contract to a local attorney and ask for a written closing-cost schedule before you pay the deposit. What follows is the 2026 map of what that schedule typically looks like in the seven markets where Americans most commonly buy.

Why closing costs matter so much abroad

In the United States, buyer closing costs on a standard purchase typically run 2–5% of the purchase price. In Spain, the same figure can reach 14%. The gap is almost entirely explained by transfer taxes — a category that barely registers in most US purchases but that is the single largest line item in Europe. In France the notaire's office collects roughly 7–8% on a resale property; of that, around five to six percentage points flow straight to the government as droits de mutation (transfer duties), and only the remainder is the notaire's actual professional fee. Understanding this distinction — government tax versus professional fee — helps you know which costs are fixed by law and which might be discussed.

For an American buyer, closing costs abroad also have one US-tax consequence worth noting: costs you pay at closing generally become part of your adjusted cost basis in the property for US capital-gains purposes. Legal fees, transfer taxes, and registration costs paid by the buyer all increase basis, which reduces the gain you eventually report. Keep every receipt.

Spain: ITP is the headline, but the region decides the number

Spain's property purchase tax for resale homes is the Imposto de Transmisiones Patrimoniales (ITP), and it is levied by the autonomous community — meaning the rate depends on where the property is, not a national rule. In Madrid the rate is 6%; in Andalucía it is 7%; in Valencia and Catalonia it rises to 10%. For new builds, buyers instead pay 10% VAT (IVA) plus Stamp Duty (Actos Jurídicos Documentados, AJD) of 0.5–2% depending on the region — making new builds in some regions more expensive in tax terms than resales.

On top of transfer tax, expect a notary fee of roughly €1,000–€2,000, a Land Registry fee of approximately €500–€1,500, and a lawyer's fee of 1–1.5% of the purchase price. Estate agents in Spain are typically paid by the seller, so buyer's agent commission may not apply — but confirm this in writing. Total costs for a resale property in Spain realistically run 9–12% in low-tax regions and 12–15% in high-tax ones.

The nuance most buyers miss: Spain's Golden Visa program was abolished for new applications as of April 3, 2025. Buyers chasing residency through real estate must now look at the long-stay visa route instead. Closing costs are unchanged by this abolition, but the investment thesis is different.

Portugal: IMT flat rate for non-residents and stamp duty

Portugal applies two separate taxes at purchase. Since 1 January 2026, non-resident buyers (including Americans) pay a flat 7.5% IMT (Imposto Municipal sobre Transmissões) on residential property, regardless of price — a change introduced under the Construir Portugal reform. Portuguese tax residents still use the old progressive scale (0%–7.5% based on price brackets). A separate Imposto do Selo (stamp duty) of 0.8% applies to the full purchase price on top of IMT — a line item frequently omitted in agent quotes. There is a refund mechanism: if you become a Portuguese tax resident within two years, or rent the property at an affordable rate within six months and hold that tenancy for 36 months, you can reclaim the difference between 7.5% and the lower progressive resident rate.

Notary, land registry, and legal fees add another 1–2%. A buyer's attorney specialising in property is strongly recommended and typically charges 1–1.5% of the purchase price. Total buyer costs at closing in Portugal usually fall in the 6–10% range. For a Golden Visa purchase (residential fund units), the route no longer requires buying direct real estate — so the property purchase-cost structure is increasingly moot for that program, but direct buying for lifestyle or rental remains common.

Greece: the lowest transfer tax in Europe, but objective value can surprise you

Greece charges a flat 3.09% transfer tax on residential property purchases — the lowest headline rate among major European buyer markets. The critical detail: the tax is calculated on the objective (assessed) value set by the tax authority, not necessarily on the price you agreed with the seller. In some areas the objective value is lower than the market price, which works in your favour. In gentrifying urban neighbourhoods, it can run close to market, neutralising the advantage.

New residential buildings in Greece have had their new-build VAT suspended since 2020; as of mid-2026 that suspension remains in force. New builds therefore pay only the 3.09% transfer tax — the same as resale. Notary fees run approximately 1–1.5% of the objective value, lawyer fees another 1–2%, plus Land Registry fees. A buyer's agent commission of 2–3% (negotiable) may apply. Total buyer costs in Greece: 7–10%, lower if you negotiate agent commission.

Italy: the highest all-in costs in southern Europe

Italy has some of the highest closing costs in Europe for foreign buyers of resale residential property. A non-resident foreigner buying a second home (not primary residence) pays:

  • Registration tax: 9% of the cadastral value (not the sale price)
  • Cadastral tax: 1% of cadastral value
  • Mortgage tax: 1% of cadastral value (if not mortgaged, a fixed nominal fee applies)
  • Notaio fee: 1–3% of the purchase price (statutory scale, sliding down for high-value properties)
  • Agent commission: 3–5% (buyer typically pays their own agent in Italy)

The important nuance: Italy's taxes are calculated on the cadastral value, which is an official assessed figure that is typically well below the market price — often 20–50% lower in rural areas. This significantly reduces the actual tax bill relative to the headline 9% rate. On a €200,000 farmhouse whose cadastral value is €80,000, the 9% registration tax is €7,200, not €18,000. Understanding this distinction is critical to budgeting accurately.

New builds from a developer incur 10% VAT (reduced to 4% for primary residence buyers) instead of registration tax. Total costs for a foreign buyer of a resale Italian property: 10–16% — the wide range reflects the gap between market price and cadastral value, and whether you use a buyer's agent.

France: frais de notaire includes the government, not just the notaire

France has a unique closing-cost structure: the buyer pays a single package called frais de notaire that bundles both the government's transfer duties and the notaire's professional fee together. For an existing (resale) property, frais de notaire typically runs 7–8% of the purchase price. Of that, approximately 5–6% flows to the government as droits de mutation (transfer duties and surcharges), and only 1–2% is the notaire's actual remuneration. For a new-build, the government duties are much smaller and frais de notaire drops to 2–3% — a significant difference that sometimes makes new builds more attractive on an all-in basis even if the headline price is higher.

In France the notaire represents both buyer and seller, which is legally required but means you have no independent advocate unless you separately hire your own avocat (lawyer). For a foreigner unfamiliar with French property law, hiring an independent lawyer at €150–€300/hour is money well spent, particularly for rural properties with complex inheritance or easement issues.

Mexico: ISABI, notario, and the recurring trust fee

Mexico's closing costs are lower on a percentage basis than Europe, but the structure has a wrinkle unique to foreign buyers: the fideicomiso. Foreigners buying in the "restricted zone" (within 50 km of a coast or 100 km of a border — which covers most desirable beach towns) must hold title through a bank trust. The trust costs money twice: once at setup and every year you own the property.

At closing, expect:

  • ISABI (acquisition tax): 2–4.5% of the higher of sale price or appraised value, rate set by the municipality
  • Notario fees: 1–2% (the notario in Mexico is a government-appointed public official, not just a private lawyer)
  • Fideicomiso setup fee: roughly $1,000–$2,500 USD paid to the bank
  • Appraisal and registration: combined ~0.5–1%
  • Agent commission: 3–5% (typically paid by seller in Mexico but varies)

Then every year you own the property: an annual trust (fideicomiso) fee of roughly $700–$1,200 USD — payable to the bank. Most buyers focus on the closing percentage and forget this annual charge entirely. Over a ten-year holding period it adds $7,000–$12,000 to your cost of ownership. Budget for it from day one.

Important US tax note: the fideicomiso is a nominee arrangement, not a foreign trust, per IRS Revenue Ruling 2013-14. That ruling means the standard foreign trust reporting forms (Forms 3520/3520-A) do not apply to a standard fideicomiso — a widely-misunderstood point that can lead to unnecessary and expensive tax filings.

Dubai: the 4% DLD fee is fixed, the split is negotiable

Dubai's property market is notable for its transparency: the Dubai Land Department (DLD) charges a flat 4% transfer fee on all transactions. By market convention this is split evenly — 2% paid by the buyer and 2% by the seller — but nothing in law requires that split, and in a buyer's market sellers may absorb the full 4% as a negotiating concession.

Beyond the DLD fee, buyers pay:

  • Registration fee: AED 2,040 for properties under AED 500K; AED 4,080 above AED 500K
  • Trustee office fee: AED 4,200 + VAT (for properties under AED 500K) or AED 2,100 + VAT (properties above)
  • Agent commission: 2% buyer-side, 2% seller-side (total 4%, split by convention)
  • Mortgage registration fee: 0.25% of loan value if financing (rare for foreigners)

For off-plan purchases the equivalent registration is called Oqood (4%). For the 10-year Golden Visa, the property must be completed and fully owned (not mortgaged above AED 2M threshold). As of April 2026, sole owners of any completed freehold unit qualify for a two-year investor visa — the AED 750,000 floor for the two-year route was removed. Total buyer costs in Dubai (buyer's share of DLD + agent + fees): typically 5–8%, lower than Europe by a significant margin.

The hidden costs every buyer misses

Beyond the standard closing-cost table, a reliable budget adds line items that agent quotes routinely omit. Across all markets the most commonly overlooked costs are:

Currency conversion costs. If you're funding a purchase with dollars, you will convert to euros, pesos, or dirhams at some point. A bank's standard exchange rate can cost 1–3% versus the mid-market rate. Using a specialist FX service for the transfer typically saves $3,000–$10,000 on a $300,000 purchase. Budget this explicitly — it is a real cost, even if it appears nowhere in the lawyer's cost schedule.

Pre-purchase survey. Unlike the US, most European countries do not have a standard home-inspection contingency culture. Buyers in the UK use surveyors routinely; in France, Spain, Greece, and Italy, buyers often skip a structural survey and later discover problems. A local architect or engineer hired to inspect the property for €500–€1,500 is almost always worth it.

Apostille and translation fees. Foreign buyers must usually have their ID documents notarised, apostilled, and translated. In Spain and Portugal this can add €500–€1,500. In Greece, a certified translator for all your documents plus the apostille process is mandatory.

Power of attorney (POA). Most buyers cannot be present for every signature. A POA to your local attorney, properly notarised and apostilled, costs €200–€500 but is essential if you plan to close remotely.

Bank account setup time and cost. You'll almost certainly need a local bank account to pay the seller, utility deposits, and ongoing bills. Opening an account as a non-resident can take 4–8 weeks in Portugal, Greece, and Italy. Factor this into your timeline, not just your budget.

How to build your own closing-cost estimate

  1. Hire a local property lawyer before signing anything. Pay for a one-hour consultation to get a written closing-cost estimate for your specific property. In most markets €200–€500 buys you this clarity before you commit to a deposit.
  2. Identify the property type (new build vs. resale) — this single fact changes the applicable tax regime in Spain, Italy, and France by several percentage points.
  3. Calculate on the purchase price, not the list price. You and the seller may agree a lower price after negotiation. The taxes apply to the agreed price (or assessed value, whichever is higher in some countries).
  4. Add agent commission explicitly. Clarify in writing who pays the agent and how much. Do not assume it is included in the seller's price.
  5. Add a 10–15% buffer above your line-by-line estimate for incidentals — documents, translations, bank wires, POA, survey, unexpected municipal fees.
  6. Budget for currency conversion as a line item, separate from the property costs.
  7. In Mexico only: add the annual trust fee as a recurring line item in your ownership cost model, not just the closing total.

If you're not American: what changes

The closing costs in the table above — transfer taxes, notary fees, registration, legal — are imposed by the country where the property sits, and they apply to all foreign buyers equally, regardless of passport. Whether you hold a US, British, Canadian, Israeli, or Australian passport, you pay the same transfer tax on closing day. The country-of-origin distinctions above are purely informational, not legal differences. What does vary by nationality: (1) whether you qualify for any treaty-based exemptions or reduced rates (rare, but worth checking); (2) your home country's tax treatment of the purchase costs — some countries allow partial deductions; and (3) whether any bilateral double-taxation treaty affects how your home country taxes future rental income or capital gains from the property.

Non-American buyers should substitute their home country's rules wherever this guide references US tax consequences. Specifically: the US capital-gains basis treatment of closing costs applies only to US taxpayers — check your own country's cost-of-acquisition rules with a tax adviser in your country of residence. The FBAR and Form 8938 obligations discussed in related articles are US-specific and do not apply to non-Americans.

Is it worth it in 2026?

The cost of entry is real, but in most markets it is a one-time event, not an annual drag. A buyer who pays 10% in closing costs on a €300,000 Italian property has spent €30,000 to buy something that costs far less per year to maintain than the US equivalent, potentially generates rental income taxed at Italy's flat 21% cedolare secca rate, and — in the right location — appreciates alongside a low-inventory market. The mistake is to treat closing costs as a sunk loss rather than as the full cost of acquisition, to be recovered over the holding period.

The two genuinely disadvantageous structures are: (1) a short holding period — if you plan to sell within three to five years, closing costs erode a large fraction of any appreciation; and (2) Mexico's cumulative fideicomiso fee over a long holding period. In both cases, knowing the full cost structure before you commit is the difference between a transaction that works and one that quietly disappoints.

Frequently asked questions

How much should I budget for closing costs when buying property abroad?

Add 7–15% of the purchase price on top of the headline price in most of Europe, 4–8% in Mexico and Dubai, and 8–12% in France. The wide range exists because transfer taxes alone vary by country, by whether the property is new build or resale, and — especially in Spain — by which region it's in. Never budget by adding a round number; run a line-by-line estimate with a local lawyer before you sign the preliminary contract.

Who pays closing costs — buyer or seller?

In most countries covered here, the buyer pays the transfer tax, notary fee, and registration costs. In Mexico, the seller pays capital-gains tax on their end, but the buyer bears the ISABI acquisition tax, notario fees, and fideicomiso setup. In Dubai the DLD fee is split 2/2 by convention but that split is negotiable. In France the buyer pays the frais de notaire in full, even though the notaire represents both parties.

Are closing costs negotiable abroad?

Government taxes are fixed by law and not negotiable. Professional fees — notary, lawyer, agent — are sometimes regulated by law at a maximum rate and sometimes market-rate. Notary fees in Italy, Germany, and France are set by statutory scales. Agent commissions in Spain, Portugal, and Mexico are market-rate and negotiable. The biggest lever is choosing new-build versus resale, since tax regimes differ substantially.

Do closing costs differ for new-build versus resale properties?

Yes, significantly. In Spain a resale pays ITP (6–11%) while a new build pays 10% VAT plus stamp duty (0.5–2%). In France notaire fees drop from 7–8% on existing property to 2–3% on new builds. In Greece new residential builds have had VAT suspended since 2020, paying only the 3.09% transfer tax. In Italy new builds incur 10% VAT (4% primary residence) rather than registration tax. Always confirm the regime for the specific property.

What is the one closing cost most buyers forget to budget for?

In Mexico: the annual fideicomiso fee (~$700–$1,200 USD/year) — it repeats every year you own the property. In Portugal: the 0.8% stamp duty on top of IMT. In Spain: legal translation and apostille costs (€500–€1,500). In Greece: the notary retains a fee based on transaction value. In general, add a 10–15% buffer above your line-by-line estimate for incidentals.
