Buying Property in Bali as an American (2026)

MS By The MigrateState Editorial Team · Reviewed by a licensed international real-estate attorney  ·  Updated July 2026  ·  11 min read
You cannot own freehold land in Bali. No foreigner can — Indonesian law reserves Hak Milik title for Indonesian citizens. Americans buy through one of three legal routes: a leasehold (25–30 years, extendable), Hak Pakai right-to-use title (up to 80 years with renewals, but it requires an Indonesian residence permit), or a PT PMA company holding HGB. Budget 8–12% in transaction costs. The "nominee" structure your agent may suggest is illegal and void.

Bali sells a fantasy better than almost anywhere on earth: a villa in Canggu or Uluwatu, an 8–12% rental yield on paper, a price that looks like a down payment in California. What the glossy listing rarely says in plain English is that you are not buying the land. You are buying a lease, a right of use, or shares in an Indonesian company — three very different legal animals, each with its own expiry date, tax profile and failure mode.

This is the guide the Bali sales office won't give you: what you can actually hold, what it costs, the structure that has left an estimated 10,500 properties in Bali legally exposed, and the US tax consequence that turns the most popular "ownership" structure into an annual IRS filing obligation with a $10,000 minimum penalty attached.

Why you can't own land in Bali

Indonesia's Basic Agrarian Law (Law No. 5 of 1960) is built on a nationality principle: the strongest form of title, Hak Milik (freehold), can be held only by Indonesian citizens. It is not a tax, a permit or a quota you can buy your way past — it is a constitutional-level rule, and a transfer of Hak Milik to a foreigner is void by operation of law, with the land liable to fall to the state.

So every legitimate foreign purchase in Bali is a lesser right than freehold. That is not a scandal; it is simply the market. Long leases of 25–30 years are a normal, functioning asset class in Bali, and Hak Pakai is a real registered title with real protection. The danger is never the structure itself — it's buyers who assume, or are allowed to assume, that what they bought behaves like a US deed. It doesn't.

One point of 2026 housekeeping: Government Regulation PP 28/2025 reorganised land and spatial-planning licensing through Indonesia's OSS system. It changed the process, not the entitlement. The underlying framework (PP 18/2021) and the 30 + 20 + 30-year Hak Pakai structure are unchanged. Anyone marketing PP 28/2025 to you as "Indonesia has opened freehold to foreigners" is misinformed or selling.

Leasehold (Hak Sewa) is how most foreigners buy their first Bali villa. You pay the whole term up front, the deed is signed before a notary, and it is open to any foreigner with no visa requirement. It is simple and it works — provided the lease deed is drafted properly (see the extension trap below).

Hak Pakai is the closest thing to ownership available to a foreigner: an actual registered title in your name at the land office, an initial 30-year term, renewable for 20 and extendable for a further 30 — up to 80 years of tenure under PP 18/2021. The price of that security is eligibility: you need a valid Indonesian residence permit (KITAS or KITAP), it is intended for one residence, and the property must meet a regional minimum value. Foreigners can also hold apartment strata title, provided the building stands on Hak Pakai or HGB land.

PT PMA — a foreign-owned Indonesian limited company — is the route for anyone running a genuine rental operation. The company can hold HGB (Hak Guna Bangunan, "right to build"), build, operate and legally invoice guests. It is the closest practical substitute for freehold, and it is also the structure with the ugliest US tax profile. Hold that thought.

The nominee trap: a $10bn problem

Somewhere in the sales process, someone will explain that the "easy" way is to register the land as Hak Milik in the name of a trusted Indonesian — a friend, a spouse's relative, a staff member of the agency — backed by a stack of side documents: a loan agreement, a mortgage deed, an irrevocable power of attorney, a statement letter. This is the nominee structure, and it is the single most expensive mistake made in Bali.

It is illegal and void. Indonesian courts treat nominee arrangements as circumventing the nationality principle of the Basic Agrarian Law and have repeatedly annulled them. When a nominee deal is struck down, the outcome is not "the foreigner gets their money back" — the registered Indonesian owner remains the legal owner of the land, and the side agreements that were supposed to protect you are unenforceable precisely because their purpose was unlawful. Industry estimates put roughly 10,500 Bali properties in nominee structures, some $10.4 billion of at-risk value. Indonesian authorities have been increasingly vocal about enforcement.

There is no clever version of this. Not a "strong" nominee agreement, not a notarised one, not one your agent has "done 200 times." Use a lease, a Hak Pakai, or a PT PMA — all three are legal, and all three actually survive a dispute.

Two practical notes. First, the government has repeatedly extended property-sector stimulus packages that have in the past reduced or waived BPHTB and PPh Final on qualifying transactions — worth asking about explicitly, as the relief is transaction-specific and time-limited. Second, taxes here are charged on assessed values, not always the price you pay, so ask the notary for the actual NPOP figure rather than assuming 5% of your purchase price.

The leasehold maths nobody shows you

Here is the nuance that separates a good Bali investment from a slow-motion loss, and it is almost never in the brochure.

A leasehold extension is a contract right, not a property right. If your original deed says "extendable for 25 years" without fixing the extension price, then when year 30 arrives the landowner can quote you whatever the market — or your desperation — will bear. Your villa is standing on their land; you have no leverage. A properly drafted lease fixes the extension term and the extension price (or a formula for it) in the original notarial deed, and registers it. Insist on it.

And a lease is a depreciating asset. Buyers price the remaining years. The same villa with 28 years left and 12 years left are not the same asset — the second one is worth a fraction of the first, and it gets harder to sell every year, because your buyer pool shrinks to people who will accept a short tail. The rental yield you're quoted (often 8–12%) has to cover not just your costs but the annual burn-down of the lease itself. Run the numbers as an amortising asset, not as a freehold that also pays rent. If the yield only works when you ignore the lease decay, it doesn't work.

The US tax angle — and the Form 5471 bomb

The US taxes citizens and green-card holders on worldwide income, regardless of where you live or what an Indonesian agent tells you.

  • Rental income goes on Schedule E. A building you own is depreciated over 30 years under the ADS method required for foreign residential rental property. A prepaid lease premium is not depreciated — it is generally amortised over the lease term, which is a different (and often better-matched) result.
  • Foreign tax credit: Indonesian tax paid can generally be credited against US tax on the same income, so you're rarely taxed twice on the same dollar — but you file in both places.
  • FBAR / Form 8938: directly-held foreign real estate is not itself reportable. The Indonesian bank account you open to run the villa usually is, once your foreign accounts exceed $10,000 in aggregate at any point in the year. Shares in a PT PMA can also be a specified foreign financial asset on Form 8938.

Now the part that catches people. A PT PMA is a foreign corporation. If you own 10% or more of it, you generally must file Form 5471 every year — an information return whose penalty starts at $10,000 per form, per year, and which does not begin the statute of limitations running on your return until it's filed. Worse, if US persons own more than 50% of the PT PMA, it is a controlled foreign corporation (CFC). Villa rental profit sitting inside a CFC is generally passive income that can be taxed to you currently — under Subpart F or GILTI — even if the company never distributes a rupiah. You can end up owing US tax on money you have not received, on a villa you cannot easily sell, in a company whose Indonesian accounts were never prepared with US tax rules in mind.

None of this makes a PT PMA wrong — it is the correct vehicle for a real rental business. It makes it a structure you should enter with a US cross-border tax advisor in the room before incorporation, not after your first tax season. Modelling the same villa as (a) a personal leasehold and (b) a PT PMA asset frequently produces a five-figure annual difference in US tax and compliance cost.

Step-by-step: how to buy in Bali

  1. Decide the structure first, not the villa. Personal home + you hold a KITAS → Hak Pakai. No residence permit, first purchase → leasehold. Rental business → PT PMA. The structure dictates what you're allowed to buy.
  2. Engage an independent Indonesian lawyer and notary/PPAT — not the one the seller or agent provides. Their loyalty is the entire product.
  3. Run due diligence on the certificate. Verify the title type and the certificate at the land office (BPN), check the land isn't in dispute, mortgaged, subject to customary (adat) claims, or in a protected/green zone, and confirm the zoning actually permits a villa or a rental operation.
  4. Confirm the zoning and building permits (PBG/SLF). Bali has been actively enforcing against villas built in non-permitted zones. An unpermitted villa is not a bargain.
  5. Negotiate the deal terms that matter: for a lease — term, a fixed-price extension, sublet and resale rights, what happens to the building at expiry. For a purchase — who bears BPHTB and PPh Final.
  6. Get the US tax opinion before you sign, especially if a company is involved.
  7. Sign before the PPAT and pay through traceable channels — bank transfer, ideally escrow or staged payments. Never cash, never to a personal account with no deed.
  8. Register the deed and take a certified copy. Then keep the Indonesian and US filings running annually.

If you're not American: what changes

The Indonesian half of this guide is identical for every foreign buyer. Hak Milik is closed to all non-Indonesians; leasehold, Hak Pakai (with a residence permit) and PT PMA are the same three routes; nominee structures are just as void; and BPHTB, PPh Final, PBB and the 8–12% cost range don't care what passport you hold. Only the home-country tax wrapper changes.

If you're British, Australian, Canadian, Singaporean, Israeli or anything else, swap the US-specific layer — Schedule E, ADS depreciation, FBAR, Form 8938, Form 5471, Subpart F/GILTI — for your own country's equivalents, and check three things with an adviser where you are tax resident:

  1. Does your home country tax foreign rental income and capital gains? Most residence-based systems do, with a credit for Indonesian tax paid. Some territorial systems don't tax foreign-source income at all — which can make Bali materially more attractive for you than for an American.
  2. What are your foreign-asset and foreign-company reporting rules? Many countries have controlled-foreign-company regimes that will look through a PT PMA much as the US does. Others barely notice it. This single difference can flip which structure is optimal.
  3. Is there a double-taxation treaty with Indonesia, and does it reduce the withholding rate on rental or lease income? Indonesia has a wide treaty network, and the 20% non-resident rate is often reduced under one.

The land law is the same for everyone. The tax bill is not — confirm yours with a tax adviser in your country of residence.

Is it worth it in 2026?

Bali is worth it if you go in with clear eyes about what you're buying. A well-drafted 30-year leasehold on a properly zoned, properly permitted villa, bought at a sane price with a fixed extension option, is a legitimate lifestyle-plus-yield asset in one of the strongest tourism markets on earth. A Hak Pakai home, if you hold a residence permit, is genuinely secure title for up to 80 years. A PT PMA, structured with the US tax layer priced in from day one, is a real business.

Bali is not worth it if you are buying a nominee "freehold," if your yield model quietly assumes the lease doesn't decay, if the villa's permits don't exist, or if you're going to discover Form 5471 in April. It is also not a residency route — ownership grants no visa; that's a separate immigration process. Compare it against markets where you actually get title in your own name — see our guides to Japan (unrestricted freehold), Portugal, and the best countries to buy property abroad — before you commit. If Bali still wins after that comparison, it probably wins for the right reasons.

Bali property FAQ

Can Americans buy property in Bali?

Not freehold. Indonesian law reserves Hak Milik title for Indonesian citizens, so no foreigner can hold it. Americans buy through a leasehold (Hak Sewa, typically 25–30 years plus extensions), Hak Pakai (a registered right-to-use title running 30 + 20 + 30 years, up to 80 years, which requires an Indonesian residence permit), or a PT PMA foreign-owned company holding HGB — the licensed route if you plan to rent the property out.

Are nominee structures legal in Bali?

No. Registering land in an Indonesian nominee's name with side agreements circumvents the Basic Agrarian Law, and Indonesian courts have repeatedly declared these arrangements null and void. The registered Indonesian owner stays the legal owner and your side documents are unenforceable. Roughly 10,500 Bali properties — about $10.4 billion of value — are estimated to sit in these structures. Don't join them.

What does it cost to buy property in Bali?

About 8–12% of the price in transaction costs. The buyer pays BPHTB acquisition duty of 5% of the assessed acquisition value (after the non-taxable allowance), notary/PPAT fees of roughly 1%, plus legal and due-diligence costs. The seller pays 2.5% PPh Final, usually priced into the deal. Annual PBB land and building tax is low, commonly 0.1%–0.3% of assessed value. Mortgages are effectively unavailable to non-residents — Bali is a cash market.

How long is a Bali leasehold and what happens at the end?

Usually 25 or 30 years with contractual extension options. The extension is a contract right, not a property right: if the original deed doesn't fix the extension price, the landowner can reprice it at expiry while your villa sits on their land. A lease is also a depreciating asset — buyers price the remaining years, so a villa with 12 years left is worth far less than the same villa with 28. Fix the extension price in the notarial deed, and model the lease as an amortising asset.

Do Americans pay US tax on a Bali property?

Yes — the US taxes worldwide income. Rental income goes on Schedule E; a building is depreciated over 30 years under ADS, while a prepaid lease premium is amortised over the lease term. The big one: a PT PMA is a foreign corporation, so a 10% US shareholder generally files Form 5471 (penalties start at $10,000 a year), and if US persons control it, its rental profits can be taxed to you currently under Subpart F or GILTI — even with no distribution. Get US tax advice before incorporating, not after.
