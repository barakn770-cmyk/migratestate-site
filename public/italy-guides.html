Italy Real Estate & Residency Guides

Lawyer-reviewed guides for Americans buying property or gaining residency in Italy — the legal and tax reality, not the dream.

Italy lets Americans buy property outright with the same rights as a local — but its famous "golden visa" cannot be earned by buying property at all. On a resale home the headline 9% purchase tax is charged on the much lower cadastral value, so the real bite is smaller than it looks; budget 10%–15% all-in. Residency instead runs through the separate Investor Visa (from €250K into a startup, company, donation or bonds — never real estate). The two things that decide your outcome: using the cadastral-value mechanism on the buy, and understanding that Italy's flat tax likely isn't creditable for Americans. Why Americans look at Italy

Italy sells the dream better than almost anywhere — a €1 village house, a Tuscan farmhouse, a Lake Como apartment. Behind the romance sits a buying system that is genuinely open to Americans: the US and Italy have a reciprocity agreement, so a US citizen can buy an apartment, a villa or land in their own name, with no residency or visa required to own. Two things are non-negotiable — a codice fiscale (Italian tax code) and a notaio, the public notary who by law verifies title and registers the transfer.

What most buyers misunderstand is the tax base. Get the structure right and Italy is far more affordable to buy in than its 9% headline tax suggests, because on a resale home the tax is charged on cadastral value, not the price you pay. But Italy also draws a hard line the brochures gloss over: property ownership and residency are two entirely separate transactions here. You can buy a €5 million villa and it earns you an asset, not a visa — residency comes only through a qualifying financial investment. Our guides untangle both sides.

Where to buy — the regions

Italy is really a collection of regional markets, each with a distinct buyer:

  • Tuscany & Umbria — the classic countryside dream: farmhouses, vineyards and hill towns. Best for lifestyle buyers and second-home owners who want the postcard Italy.
  • Lake Como & the northern lakes — prestige waterfront property at the top of the market, strong for trophy assets and premium rentals.
  • Rome, Milan & Florence — the major cities, with the deepest year-round rental demand and the most liquid resale markets for urban-focused buyers.
  • Puglia & the southern regions — the value play, home to trulli and coastal towns, and to the €1 and low-cost house schemes; also where the 7% pensioner flat tax now reaches more municipalities.
  • The Amalfi Coast & the islands (Sicily, Sardinia) — resort and holiday-let territory, strong seasonal income, higher entry prices on the marquee stretches.

Wherever you buy, older Italian property is a minefield of building amnesties and abusi edilizi (unpermitted works), so an independent avvocato and a geometra (surveyor) to confirm the building matches its cadastral records are essential, not optional.

The single biggest cost lever is the first line. Because Italy lets an individual buying a resale home from a private seller use the prezzo-valore system, the 9% is calculated on the cadastral value — typically 30%–60% below market — so a €300,000 home with a €120,000 cadastral value is taxed on €120,000, roughly €10,800 rather than €27,000. You must ask the notary to apply it in the deed. On the residency side, note the trap for Americans: Italy's €300,000 flat tax is a fixed fee, not a tax on net income, so the IRS is very likely to deny it as a foreign tax credit.

Who Italy suits — and who should look elsewhere

Italy fits lifestyle buyers who want a genuinely affordable purchase once the prezzo-valore math is applied, and who value world-class healthcare, culture and quality of life over rental yield. On the residency side it suits investors comfortable with equity or bond risk who want a fast, clean process — the Investor Visa's four-to-six-month timeline beats the 12-month-plus backlogs in Greece and Portugal, with no minimum-stay requirement on the permit. See our guide to buying property in Italy and the Italy Investor Visa guide for the full mechanics.

Italy is a weaker fit if your plan is to buy a property for residency — there is no property route, so you'd be committing capital twice, once to a home and once to a qualifying investment. It's also a harder sell if you were counting on the flat tax to cut your US bill (it likely won't) or if you want the fastest passport — Italian citizenship takes ten years and demands genuine presence of roughly 270 days a year. Italy FAQ

Is Italy a good place for Americans to buy property in 2026?

For lifestyle and, increasingly, value, Italy is hard to beat — the prezzo-valore mechanism quietly makes the purchase tax far gentler than the 9% headline implies. The catches are practical: title and building records are a minefield of old amnesties and unpermitted works, so an independent lawyer and a geometra are essential, and non-residents should budget IMU and the second-home rate honestly.

Can I get residency in Italy by buying property?

No. Italy deliberately excluded real estate from its Investor Visa, so no property purchase — at any price — earns residency. Residency comes only through a qualifying financial investment (from €250,000 into a startup, up to €2,000,000 in government bonds). Buying a home and getting the visa are two separate expenditures.

Which region is best for a first purchase?

It depends on the goal: Tuscany and Umbria for the countryside dream, the northern lakes for prestige waterfront, Rome, Milan or Florence for year-round rental demand, and Puglia and the south for value — including the €1 and low-cost house schemes and the expanded 7% pensioner flat tax.

Should I move my residency to claim the lower purchase tax?

Only if you genuinely can. The reduced 2% prima casa rate requires moving your residency to the property's comune within 18 months, and IMU is waived only on a true main residence. Most Americans keeping a US home cannot meet that, so they correctly budget the 9% second-home rate and ongoing IMU.

Will I owe US tax on an Italian property — and does the flat tax help?

Potentially yes on the property (worldwide income means Italian rent and a future sale are reportable, with a bank account triggering FBAR/FATCA). The flat tax rarely helps Americans: because it's a fixed €300,000 fee rather than a tax on net income, the IRS is very likely to deny it as a foreign tax credit.
