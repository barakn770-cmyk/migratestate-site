How to Buy a House in Portugal as a US Citizen (2026)

MS By The MigrateState Editorial Team · Reviewed by a licensed international real-estate attorney  ·  Updated June 2026  ·  9 min read

The big 2026 change: non-residents now pay a flat 7.5% IMT transfer tax regardless of price, so budget roughly 8%–12% on top of the purchase price for taxes and fees. The catch for US citizens is on the IRS side, not the Portuguese one.

Portugal remains one of the most popular destinations for Americans buying a home abroad — mild climate, low crime, EU access, and a property market that, while no longer the bargain it was a decade ago, is still far cheaper than coastal California or the Northeast. The good news is that the purchase itself is open and straightforward. The part that trips Americans up in 2026 is a recent tax change that quietly doubled the entry cost for non-residents, plus a separate set of US filing obligations that no Portuguese lawyer will warn you about. Here's the full picture.

Can Americans buy property in Portugal?

Yes — fully and without restriction. Portugal imposes no limits on foreign ownership of real estate. As a US citizen you can buy an apartment, a house, or land with exactly the same legal rights as a Portuguese national, and you can hold it in your own name. You do not need to be a resident, hold a visa, or even set foot in Portugal — the entire purchase can be completed remotely through a lawyer holding power of attorney. The only non-negotiable prerequisite is a Portuguese tax number.

What you need before you buy

Four things turn an American buyer into a "ready" buyer in Portugal:

  • A NIF (Número de Identificação Fiscal) — your Portuguese tax number, required for the purchase, utilities, and a bank account.
  • A fiscal representative — mandatory for non-EU residents, including Americans (more below).
  • A Portuguese bank account — strongly recommended to pay the deposit, taxes, and ongoing bills.
  • An independent lawyer (advogado) — separate from the seller's agent, to run due diligence and represent you at signing.

The detail that surprises most Americans: because the United States sits outside the EU and EEA, a US citizen who is not yet a Portuguese tax resident is legally required to appoint a fiscal representative — a Portuguese-based person or firm that receives tax correspondence on your behalf. Crucially, you must appoint the representative before applying for your NIF, not after. Most Portugal-focused law firms bundle this into their service.

The 2026 IMT change every non-resident must know

This is the single most important update for American buyers, and the one generic blogs still get wrong. IMT (Imposto Municipal sobre Transmissões) is Portugal's one-off property transfer tax, paid before you sign the final deed. Historically it followed a progressive scale from 0% up to about 7.5%, so buyers of modest homes paid very little.

From 2026, that changed for non-residents. If you are not a Portuguese tax resident, you now pay a flat 7.5% IMT on residential property — regardless of the price. On a €400,000 home that is €30,000 in transfer tax alone, before stamp duty and fees. For a non-resident buying a lower-priced property, the effective cost can be roughly double what a resident pays on the same home.

Here is the nuance worth real money, and the genuine legal point most guides miss: the flat 7.5% is refundable. Portuguese law allows a non-resident to reclaim the difference between 7.5% and the lower progressive resident rate through one of two routes. The first: become a Portuguese tax resident within two years of purchase. The second: place the property on the rental market within 6 months of purchase at a monthly rent not exceeding the affordable-rent ceiling (currently €2,300/month), and keep it rented for at least 36 months within the first 5 years of ownership. You pay 7.5% up front and claim the refund once the applicable condition is met. The timeframes are different for each route, so discuss with your lawyer which path fits your plans before signing — reconstructing eligibility after the fact is far harder.

Annual taxes after you own

Owning the property carries a modest yearly cost called IMI (Imposto Municipal sobre Imóveis), the equivalent of US property tax. It is calculated on the property's official tax value (the VPT, usually below market price), not what you paid:

Property typeAnnual IMI rate
Urban property0.3%–0.45% (set by municipality)
Rural property0.8% (standard nationwide)

IMI bills are issued each April. Higher-value portfolios (combined VPT above €600,000) can also attract AIMI, a wealth-style surcharge — relevant mainly to buyers of trophy properties. For most single-home buyers, the annual tax is comfortably in the few-hundred-to-low-thousands-of-euros range.

Mortgages for US buyers

Portuguese banks do lend to non-resident Americans, though on more conservative terms than to residents. Expect a loan-to-value of roughly 60%–70%, meaning a 30%–40% down payment, with rates that move with the Euribor benchmark. Banks will want proof of income, US tax returns, and bank statements, and the process adds a few weeks. Many American buyers pay cash for speed, then consider financing later. Note that mortgage borrowing carries its own stamp duty (0.6% of the loan for terms of five years or more).

Buying ≠ residency: the Golden Visa myth

One persistent misconception: that buying a home in Portugal earns you residency. It does not. Portugal removed real estate from its Golden Visa program in 2023; the visa now requires a qualifying fund investment (typically €500,000) or other non-property routes, not a house purchase. If your goal is to actually live in Portugal, the practical paths in 2026 are the D7 visa (for those with stable passive income such as pensions, rental income or dividends) or the D8 digital-nomad visa (for remote workers meeting an income threshold). Owning a Portuguese home strengthens those applications but is not, by itself, a residency permit.

Step-by-step: buying a house in Portugal as an American

  1. Appoint a fiscal representative and get your NIF — done remotely through a lawyer, usually in days.
  2. Open a Portuguese bank account for the deposit, taxes, and utilities.
  3. Engage an independent lawyer to run due diligence on title, debts, licenses and habitation certificate.
  4. Sign the CPCV (promissory contract) and pay a deposit, typically 10%. This contract is binding — if the seller backs out they owe double; if you do, you forfeit the deposit.
  5. Pay IMT and stamp duty before the deed (structure the 7.5% refund here if eligible).
  6. Sign the escritura (final deed) before a notary — in person or via your lawyer under power of attorney.
  7. Register the property at the Land Registry in your name.
  8. Set up US tax compliance — FBAR for the bank account, and plan for rental/resale reporting.

Start to finish, the typical timeline is two to four months.

Portugal's preferential tax regime for new residents: IFICI (formerly NHR)

Portugal previously offered the Non-Habitual Resident (NHR) regime — a 10-year flat tax of 20% on Portuguese-source income with exemptions on most foreign income. The NHR was abolished on 1 January 2025. Its replacement — the IFICI (Incentivo Fiscal à Captação de Investimento) — is narrower: it applies to workers in qualifying high-value activities (technology, science, innovation, highly qualified professions) who hold at least an L6 degree and three years of experience. Unlike NHR, IFICI is not available to retirees or passive-income earners. If you were counting on NHR-style tax benefits for a Portugal move, confirm with a Portuguese tax adviser whether you qualify for IFICI — the two regimes are structurally different. Buying property in Portugal does not in itself trigger IFICI eligibility; you must apply separately upon establishing tax residency.

The US tax rules that still apply

Here's what your Portuguese lawyer won't tell you, because it isn't their job: the IRS still wants to hear about your Portuguese house. The United States taxes citizens on worldwide income, so any rental income is reportable on Schedule E, and a future sale is a US capital gain. Unlike Dubai, Portugal does levy its own tax, so you can generally use the foreign tax credit to offset most of the US bill — but the returns still have to be filed in both countries. Your Portuguese bank account can also trigger FBAR (FinCEN Form 114) and possibly FATCA (Form 8938) reporting once balances cross the thresholds. One more wrinkle most Americans never see coming: currency gains. If the euro strengthens against the dollar between purchase and sale, the IRS can tax a "phantom" gain on the mortgage payoff measured in dollars, even if the property's euro value didn't move. A cross-border CPA should be on your team before, not after, you buy.

Is buying a house in Portugal worth it in 2026?

For many Americans, yes — but go in with eyes open. The market is open, the legal process is clean, and the lifestyle case is strong. What's changed is the math: the flat 7.5% non-resident IMT makes the all-in entry cost meaningfully higher than the figures floating around older guides, and the refund mechanism only helps if you'll genuinely become a tax resident. Pair that with disciplined US tax planning and a good independent lawyer, and Portugal is still one of the most rewarding places an American can own a home abroad. Just budget the real 8%–12%, not the old 7%, and structure the purchase for the refund if Portugal is in your future.

Frequently asked questions

Can a US citizen buy a house in Portugal?

Yes. Portugal places no restrictions on foreign ownership. US citizens can buy any residential property outright with the same rights as a local, and you don't need residency or a visa — only a Portuguese tax number (NIF).

How much does it cost to buy a house in Portugal as an American?

Budget roughly 8%–12% on top of the price. The biggest item in 2026 is IMT transfer tax, now a flat 7.5% for non-residents, plus 0.8% stamp duty, about 1%–2% for a lawyer, and ~€1,000–€1,500 in notary and registry fees.

Do Americans need a fiscal representative to buy property in Portugal?

Yes. Because the US is outside the EU/EEA, a US citizen who isn't a Portuguese tax resident must appoint a fiscal representative before applying for a NIF. Your lawyer or buyer's agent usually arranges it.

Does buying a house in Portugal give you residency?

No. Portugal removed real estate from its Golden Visa in 2023. Buying gives you ownership, not residency. To live in Portugal, Americans typically apply through the D7 passive-income or D8 digital-nomad visa.

Do I pay US tax on a house I buy in Portugal?

Potentially. The US taxes worldwide income, so Portuguese rental income and a future sale are IRS-reportable. You can usually credit Portuguese tax against the US bill, and a Portuguese bank account can trigger FBAR/FATCA reporting.
