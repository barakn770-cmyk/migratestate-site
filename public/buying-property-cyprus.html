Buying Property in Cyprus as an American (2026 Guide)

MS By The MigrateState Editorial Team · Reviewed by a licensed international real-estate attorney  ·  Updated June 2026  ·  11 min read
Yes — Americans can buy property in Cyprus, with one formality: a Council of Ministers acquisition permit. Your acquisition cost hinges on one fork: a new build carries VAT (19%, or 5% on the first 130 m² of a qualifying home) but no transfer fees; a resale carries no VAT but 3%–8% transfer fees, cut 50% in practice. Cyprus is an English-speaking, common-law EU island where contracts read the way an American expects, the courts work in English, and a single new-build purchase can hand you and your family a residency permit that never expires. It is also one of the few European markets with no annual property tax and, as of 2026, no stamp duty on a purchase. But the headline simplicity hides a fork that decides your whole cost base — whether the property is taxed under VAT or under transfer fees, never both — plus a non-EU acquisition permit, a tightened 5% VAT rule, and a US tax layer that follows you home. Here is the grounded 2026 version, with the legal and tax nuance the listing portals skip.

Can Americans buy property in Cyprus?

Yes — and the process is unusually buyer-friendly because Cyprus inherited English common law. You buy and register in your own name at the Department of Lands and Surveys (the Land Registry), which issues a government title deed that is the definitive proof of ownership. There is one extra step for non-EU nationals, and Americans are non-EU: you need an acquisition permit from the Council of Ministers, processed in practice by the local District Office. It is largely a formality — a clean criminal record and proof of funds are the substance of it — but it is mandatory, and it generally limits a non-EU buyer to one dwelling, or one plot of up to about 4,014 m² (roughly three donums) for personal use.

Crucially, the permit does not hold up the deal. You can sign, pay, take possession and even move in while the permit is pending; what waits on it is the final transfer of the title deed into your name. Until then, your lawyer protects you by lodging the contract of sale at the Land Registry (specific-performance protection) — a step we explain below that is the single most important safeguard in a Cyprus purchase.

What you need before you buy

Four things turn an American into a "ready" buyer in Cyprus:

  • An independent lawyer — not the developer's or the agent's. Cyprus has a history of buyers stung by hidden developer mortgages on the land, so independent title due diligence is non-negotiable.
  • A Cyprus tax identification number and bank account — to bring funds in cleanly, pay the seller, and later run rental income or the residency file. Expect bank compliance (source-of-funds) checks; budget time.
  • The acquisition-permit paperwork — passport copy, a clean criminal-record certificate, and proof of funds, filed with the District Office after signing.
  • Contract lodged at the Land Registry — within the statutory window after signing, your lawyer deposits the contract of sale to secure specific performance, which blocks the seller from re-selling or further encumbering the property and preserves your right to the title deed.

Transfer fees vs VAT — and the abolished stamp duty

This is the part buyers most often get wrong, because Cyprus runs two mutually exclusive purchase-tax regimes and you only ever pay one of them:

New builds (first sale from a developer) carry VAT. The standard rate is 19%. A reduced 5% rate applies to the first 130 m² of a qualifying primary residence, provided the total transaction value is under €475,000 and the buildable area does not exceed 190 m². The 5% rule was tightened in recent reforms — stricter "first occupation" and declaration requirements mean many sales that once slipped in at 5% now attract the full 19%, so confirm eligibility in writing before you bank on it. When VAT applies, property transfer fees do not.

Resale properties carry no VAT but do carry transfer fees. These are charged on a sliding scale: 3% on the first €85,000, 5% from €85,001 to €170,000, and 8% above €170,000. In practice a long-standing 50% reduction applies to transactions where no VAT was paid, roughly halving the effective rate.

And the genuinely new 2026 item: stamp duty on purchase contracts was abolished with effect from 1 January 2026. Until the end of 2025, buyers paid a small stamp duty (up to 0.2%) to make the contract admissible; that line item is now gone. Most "Cyprus closing costs" articles still list it — if yours does, it is out of date.

⚠️ Model the total acquisition cost both ways before you choose new vs resale. On a €300,000 home, a resale costs roughly €6,000–€12,000 in (reduced) transfer fees and zero VAT; an identical new build at full 19% VAT costs about €57,000 in tax, or far less if you qualify for the 5% band. The "cheaper" property on the listing is often the more expensive one after tax. Run both numbers first.

The €300,000 permanent-residency route

This is Cyprus's headline draw for Americans, and it is a residency program, not citizenship (the old citizenship-by-investment scheme was scrapped years ago). Invest at least €300,000 (plus VAT) in new residential property bought from a development company — one home or two units combined — and you, your spouse and dependent children under 18 qualify for fast-track permanent residency under Regulation 6(2), usually granted in about two to six months.

Two conditions trip people up. First, you must show a secured annual income of at least €50,000, rising by €15,000 for a spouse and €10,000 per dependent child — and that income must come from sources outside Cyprus (salary, pensions, dividends, rents abroad). Second, you need a clean criminal record and health insurance. The payoff is unusually generous: the permit is valid for life, never needs renewal, and only requires you to visit Cyprus once every two years to keep it.

The €300k must be NEW — which puts you in the VAT regime, not transfer fees. The residency rule only counts first-sale property from a developer, so your qualifying home will carry VAT (potentially the 5% band on the first 130 m²) and no transfer fees. A bargain resale, however cheap, does not qualify — and the €50,000 income must be foreign-sourced, so Cyprus rental income from the property itself does not count toward it.

Annual taxes and capital gains

Holding property in Cyprus is cheap — cheaper than almost anywhere else in the EU:

  • No annual immovable property tax. Cyprus abolished the national property tax in 2017; there is no yearly state levy on what you own. You pay only modest municipal/local rates (refuse, sewerage, communal services), typically a few hundred euros a year.
  • Rental income tax. Cyprus-source rent is taxable in Cyprus. Long-term residential rent is exempt from VAT, and special rules (including the Special Defence Contribution, which applies to Cyprus tax residents) can affect the net — get local advice on your specific status.
  • Capital gains tax on exit. A flat 20% applies to the gain from selling Cyprus immovable property. From 2026, the lifetime exemptions rose: €30,000 general and €150,000 for a qualifying primary residence, deductible once over your lifetime, on top of allowable acquisition and improvement costs.

The combination — no annual tax, no stamp duty, a generous primary-residence CGT exemption — is what makes Cyprus a genuinely low-friction place to hold real estate long term, provided you bought on the right side of the VAT/transfer-fee fork.

The US tax rules that still apply

Your Cyprus lawyer handles the title; the IRS is still your job. The United States taxes citizens on worldwide income, so rental income from your Cypriot property goes on Schedule E and a future sale is a US capital gain — even in years Cyprus charges you little or nothing. The foreign tax credit (Form 1116) relieves most double taxation, and the US–Cyprus income-tax treaty (in force since 1985) backs that up. One US-specific quirk: foreign residential rental property must be depreciated over 30 years under the Alternative Depreciation System (ADS), slower than the 27.5 years used for US property.

Here is the reporting nuance most American buyers — and most blogs — get wrong. Directly held foreign real estate is not a reportable asset: a house or apartment you own in your own name does not go on the FBAR (FinCEN Form 114) or on FATCA Form 8938. But the Cyprus bank account you open to buy and run it absolutely can — the FBAR is triggered when your foreign accounts together exceed $10,000 at any point in the year, and Form 8938 by its own (higher) thresholds. So you can own a €300,000 apartment that never appears on a US disclosure form, yet owe an FBAR purely because of the bank account that funded it. Two further traps: if you ever hold the property through a Cyprus company (a structure some agents push), the shares become an 8938-reportable asset and can drag in complex anti-deferral rules; and because Cyprus prices and any mortgage are in euros, repaying a euro loan with cheaper future euros can create a taxable Section 988 currency gain the IRS expects you to report — and which the Section 121 home-sale exclusion does not shelter, even on your own residence. Map all three before you buy, not after.

Step-by-step: buying property in Cyprus as an American

  1. Engage an independent lawyer — not the developer's or agent's — and agree a fixed scope for title due diligence and the permit filing.
  2. Run due diligence — your lawyer pulls the title search to confirm clean ownership and, critically, that there is no developer mortgage or other charge on the land.
  3. Sign the contract and pay the deposit — terms, completion date and (for off-plan) staged payments are fixed in the contract of sale.
  4. Lodge the contract at the Land Registry within the statutory window to secure specific performance — this is your core legal protection until the title transfers.
  5. File the acquisition permit with the District Office (passport, clean criminal record, proof of funds). You can complete and take possession while it is pending.
  6. Settle the purchase tax — VAT on a new build (check 5% vs 19% eligibility) or reduced transfer fees on a resale. No stamp duty from 2026.
  7. Transfer the title deed at the Land Registry once the permit issues, registering the property in your name.
  8. Set up compliance — file your FBAR if the bank account crossed $10,000, plan rental/resale reporting with a cross-border CPA, and (if applicable) lodge the Regulation 6(2) residency application.

Start to finish, a clean resale typically completes in four to eight weeks; the acquisition permit and any residency application then run in parallel over the following months.

If you're not American: what changes

The Cyprus side is identical for any foreign buyer. The title deed, the VAT-vs-transfer-fee fork, the abolished stamp duty, the 20% capital-gains tax, the no-annual-tax rule and the €300,000 permanent-residency route all apply the same whether you hold a US, UK, Canadian, Gulf or Israeli passport. The one variable on the local side is the acquisition permit: EU/EEA nationals don't need it, while non-EU nationals (Americans included) do. Only the home-country tax wrapper on top is otherwise different.

If you're not American, keep everything above on the Cyprus process exactly as written — only EU buyers skip the Council of Ministers permit. What you swap out is the US-specific tax layer (worldwide-income filing, FBAR, FATCA, 30-year depreciation, Section 988). In its place, check three things in your own country of residence: (1) whether your home country taxes foreign rental income and capital gains earned in Cyprus; (2) whether you must report foreign property, bank accounts or assets at home; and (3) whether your country has a double-taxation treaty with Cyprus that lets you credit Cypriot tax against your home bill. Confirm the specifics with a tax adviser in your country of residence before committing.

Is buying property in Cyprus worth it in 2026?

For an American who wants an English-speaking, common-law EU base with a clean residency path and rock-bottom holding costs, Cyprus is one of the strongest options in the Mediterranean. The 2026 abolition of stamp duty, the absence of any annual property tax, a 20% capital-gains regime with a generous primary-residence exemption, and a lifetime residency permit for a €300,000 purchase add up to a rare combination. The risks are specific rather than systemic: getting the VAT-vs-transfer-fee math wrong, skipping the specific-performance lodgement, or buying a unit with a hidden developer mortgage. Use your own lawyer, model both tax routes before you choose new vs resale, and line up your US tax plan first. Do that, and Cyprus delivers one of the cleanest legal and tax setups an American can find in Europe.

Frequently asked questions

Can a US citizen buy property in Cyprus?

Yes. Americans buy in their own name and register a government title deed at the Land Registry. As non-EU buyers they need a Council of Ministers acquisition permit (filed at the District Office) — largely a formality, generally limited to one home or one plot up to about 4,014 m². You can pay and take possession before the permit issues; it is needed to register the final title.

How much does it cost to buy property in Cyprus in 2026?

Roughly 1.5%–8% over the price, depending on the tax route. New builds carry VAT (19%, or 5% on the first 130 m² of a qualifying primary residence under €475,000) and no transfer fees. Resales carry no VAT but transfer fees of 3%–8% on a sliding scale, reduced about 50% in practice. Stamp duty was abolished from 1 January 2026. Add legal fees of about 1% plus VAT.

Can I get residency in Cyprus by buying property?

Yes. A €300,000-plus (plus VAT) purchase of new residential property from a developer qualifies you, your spouse and children under 18 for fast-track permanent residency, usually in about two to six months. You must show €50,000 of secured annual income from abroad (plus €15,000 for a spouse and €10,000 per child), a clean record and health insurance. The permit is for life and needs only a visit once every two years.

Does Cyprus tax capital gains when I sell?

Yes — a flat 20% on the gain from Cyprus immovable property. From 2026 the lifetime exemptions rose to €30,000 general and €150,000 for a qualifying primary residence, deductible once on top of allowable costs. Cyprus has no annual immovable property tax (abolished in 2017), so the 20% CGT is the main exit cost.

Do I pay US tax on a property I buy in Cyprus?

Potentially. The US taxes worldwide income, so Cypriot rental income goes on Schedule E and a sale is a US capital gain, with the foreign tax credit and the US–Cyprus treaty relieving most double tax. The real estate itself isn't FBAR/Form 8938 reportable, but the Cyprus bank account you open to buy it can be, and a euro mortgage can create a Section 988 currency gain.
