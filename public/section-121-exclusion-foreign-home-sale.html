Section 121 on a Foreign Home Sale, 2026

MS By The MigrateState Editorial Team · Reviewed by a licensed international real-estate attorney  ·  Updated July 2026  ·  10 min read
Yes — the home-sale exclusion works on a house abroad. IRC Section 121 lets a US citizen exclude up to $250,000 of gain ($500,000 filing jointly) on a principal residence anywhere in the world, provided you owned and lived in it for 24 of the last 60 months. The catch is that the gain is measured in dollars, and claiming the exclusion can strand the foreign tax you already paid.

What Section 121 does — and where it applies

Most Americans who own a home abroad assume the US tax breaks they know from home stop at the water's edge. On the biggest one, they are wrong. Section 121 of the Internal Revenue Code excludes gain "from the sale or exchange of property if, during the 5-year period ending on the date of the sale, such property has been owned and used by the taxpayer as the taxpayer's principal residence" for at least two years. Read that again and notice what is absent: any mention of the United States.

There is no geographic limitation in the statute. A house in Lisbon, an apartment in Mexico City, a machiya in Kyoto — if it is genuinely your principal residence and you meet the tests, it qualifies. This surprises people because so much of the surrounding US framework is territorial in feel, but Section 121 simply is not. What matters is your status as a US taxpayer and the character of the property in your life, not the flag flying over it.

The mechanics are unchanged for 2026. Single filers exclude up to $250,000 of gain; married couples filing jointly exclude up to $500,000. Those figures were set in 1997 and — unusually for the tax code — have never been indexed for inflation, which is why an exclusion that once covered almost any middle-class house now falls well short of the gain on a long-held property in Lisbon or Barcelona.

The ownership and use tests

Three conditions have to hold, and they are tested independently.

Ownership. You must have owned the property for at least 24 months during the 60 months ending on the sale date. Use. You must have used it as your principal residence for at least 24 months in that same window. Frequency. You must not have excluded gain on another home sale in the two years before this one.

The 24 months of use do not have to be continuous, and they do not have to overlap with the 24 months of ownership. This matters more abroad than it does domestically, because expatriate lives are rarely tidy: you rent the place for a year while you wait for a residence permit, buy it, live in it, get posted elsewhere for eighteen months, come back. Add the qualifying months up across the five-year window rather than looking for a single unbroken block.

On a joint return the tests are asymmetric in a way that is easy to miss. Only one spouse needs to satisfy the ownership test, but both must satisfy the use test to reach the full $500,000. If one spouse arrived late — a common pattern where one partner relocates first — the couple may be limited to $250,000 plus that spouse's own allowance rather than the full joint figure.

There is also a reduced-exclusion safety valve. If you fall short of 24 months because of a change in place of employment, health, or certain unforeseen circumstances, you get a pro-rated exclusion rather than nothing. Someone who lived in a Madrid flat for 12 of the required 24 months before a job moved them keeps roughly half the allowance.

The dollar-basis trap

Here is where foreign sales genuinely diverge from domestic ones. The IRS does not care what happened to your house in euros. Your gain is computed in US dollars: basis translated at the exchange rate on the day you bought, proceeds translated at the rate on the day you sold. The currency movement between those two dates is not a separate item. It is baked into the gain itself.

That cuts both ways, and the direction is not intuitive.

The third row is the one that catches people. A property that sold for exactly what it cost, in the only currency the seller ever thought in, produced a $72,000 US taxable gain out of nothing but the exchange rate. Section 121 will usually absorb it — which is precisely why the exclusion is more valuable to an American abroad than to one at home. It is doing work no domestic seller ever needs it to do.

The reverse case is a genuine trap. A dollar loss on a foreign personal residence is not deductible. Losses on personal-use property never are, and the fact that the loss was manufactured by currency movement rather than a falling market does not change that. You are taxed on phantom gains and denied phantom losses, symmetrically unfairly.

The foreign tax credit Section 121 strands

This is the nuance almost no guide mentions, and it is the one that costs real money.

Most Americans selling abroad have already paid tax locally. Portugal taxes a non-resident's property gain; Spain withholds; France, Greece and Mexico all take their cut at or before closing. The natural assumption is that you claim Section 121 to kill the US tax, then claim the foreign tax credit on Form 1116 for the local tax, and come out clean.

It does not work that way. The foreign tax credit exists to prevent double taxation. If Section 121 has already removed the gain from your US taxable income, there is no second layer of tax to relieve. Under the Section 904 limitation, your credit is capped by the US tax attributable to foreign-source taxable income in the relevant basket — and excluded income is not taxable income. The foreign tax you paid on the sheltered portion of the gain therefore generates excess credit rather than a refund.

That excess is not immediately lost. Passive-basket credits carry back one year and forward ten. But a carryforward only has value if you generate future foreign-source passive income to absorb it — foreign dividends, interest, rental profit. An American who sells their only foreign asset and repatriates typically has no such income and watches the credit expire.

The practical consequence: in a high-tax country, Section 121 can shelter a gain the US was going to tax lightly anyway while the local tax — often 19–28% on the same gain — becomes a stranded credit. The exclusion is still worth having. But it is not free, and the total tax you pay across both countries is often set by the foreign rate, not the American one.

Where the gain exceeds the exclusion, the picture improves markedly. The excess gain stays in US taxable income, generates US tax, and the foreign credit then has something real to offset. This is why sellers with very large gains sometimes end up with a cleaner outcome than sellers with modest ones — a genuinely counterintuitive result worth modelling before you commit to a closing date.

Four things Section 121 never shelters

The exclusion is generous but narrow in scope. Four categories sit outside it entirely.

ItemSheltered by Section 121?How it is taxed instead
Gain on the residence itselfYes, up to $250K / $500KExcess at 0/15/20% long-term capital rates
Depreciation after 6 May 1997NoUnrecaptured Section 1250 gain, up to 25%
Section 988 currency gain on a foreign mortgageNoOrdinary income, up to 37%
Gain allocated to nonqualified useNoCapital gain, pro-rated by time
Net investment income tax on the taxable excessNoAdditional 3.8% above the NIIT thresholds

The depreciation carve-out bites hardest on properties that were rented before becoming a home. Foreign residential rental property is depreciated over 30 years under the alternative depreciation system rather than the 27.5 years applied domestically, and recapture applies to depreciation "allowed or allowable" — meaning the IRS recaptures it whether or not you actually took the deduction. Americans who rented out a foreign flat for years without filing Schedule E are recaptured on depreciation they never claimed a benefit for.

The Section 988 carve-out is the other expensive one. If you financed the purchase with a euro or peso mortgage and the dollar strengthened before you repaid, the debt generates its own ordinary-income gain, computed separately from the house. Section 121 shelters the residence; it does not touch the loan. We cover the mechanics in detail in our guide to the foreign mortgage phantom gain.

Partial exclusions and nonqualified use

If the property was not your principal residence for the whole time you owned it, the gain is split. Periods after 31 December 2008 during which the property was not your main home — rented out, left to a family member, used as a pure holiday let — are "nonqualified use", and the fraction of gain attributable to them cannot be excluded.

The formula is mechanical: multiply the gain by the ratio of nonqualified-use months to total ownership months. Own a Greek apartment for ten years, rent it for the first four and live in it for the last six, and roughly 40% of the gain falls outside the exclusion regardless of how comfortably the rest fits.

One important exception rescues the more common expatriate pattern. Time after your last day of use as a principal residence does not count as nonqualified use. If you live in the house and then rent it out while trying to sell — a very ordinary sequence when you move country — that trailing rental period is ignored for the allocation. It is only rental use before you moved in that dilutes the exclusion.

How to claim it, step by step

  1. Fix your dollar basis. Take the purchase price plus capital improvements and transfer taxes, each translated at the spot rate on the date it was actually paid — not a blended annual average.
  2. Translate the sale. Convert gross proceeds and selling costs at the spot rate on the closing date. The difference is your dollar gain or loss.
  3. Strip out depreciation. Identify all depreciation allowed or allowable after 6 May 1997 and set it aside as unrecaptured Section 1250 gain. It is taxed at up to 25% and cannot be excluded.
  4. Apply the nonqualified-use fraction. Allocate the remaining gain between qualified and nonqualified periods, remembering that trailing rental use after you moved out is disregarded.
  5. Apply Section 121. Exclude up to $250,000 or $500,000 of the qualifying gain. Confirm the ownership, use and two-year frequency tests are all met.
  6. Report it properly. Report the sale on Form 8949 and Schedule D, then enter the exclusion as a negative adjustment with code H. A sale that is fully excluded still generally needs reporting if you received a settlement statement or the gain exceeds the limit.
  7. Compute the mortgage separately. If foreign-currency debt was repaid, run the Section 988 calculation on its own and report the gain as ordinary income.
  8. Run Form 1116 last. Claim the foreign tax credit only against gain that actually remained in US taxable income, and check whether the excess credit has anywhere useful to go before you assume it is recovered.

If you're not American: what changes

The sale itself is identical for any foreign seller. The local capital gains tax, the notary process, withholding at closing, agent fees and the country's own reliefs for a main residence do not change with your passport. What is purely American is the wrapper described above: Section 121, the dollar-basis measurement, Form 8949 and the Form 1116 credit mechanics.

If you are not a US person, discard the IRS layer and substitute the rules of your own country of tax residence. Check three things. First, whether your home country taxes gains on foreign property at all and whether it offers its own main-residence relief — the UK's private residence relief, Canada's principal residence exemption and Israel's exemption for a single dwelling all have their own tests, and they are usually stricter about the property actually being your home than Section 121 is. Second, whether you have foreign asset or account reporting obligations at home covering the property and the proceeds. Third, whether a double-taxation treaty exists between your country and the country where the property sits, since immovable property is almost always taxable first in the country where it is located and relieved second at home. A Canadian, Australian or British seller computes everything in their own currency under their own reliefs. Confirm the specifics with a tax adviser where you are resident.

Is it worth planning around in 2026?

Emphatically yes — and more so abroad than at home. For an American selling a foreign home, Section 121 is doing double duty: it shelters the real appreciation and it absorbs the phantom dollar gain created by exchange-rate movement, which a domestic seller never faces. On a property held through a full currency cycle, that second job is frequently worth more than the first.

But treat it as one component of an exit, not the whole answer. The exclusion is unindexed and shrinking in real terms, it stops dead at depreciation and at the mortgage, and — the part that surprises even well-advised sellers — claiming it can quietly waste the foreign tax you already paid. The sellers who do well are the ones who model the whole exit in dollars before they list: basis translated properly, recapture identified, the Section 988 position on any local mortgage computed, and an honest view of whether the Form 1116 credit will ever be usable. Do that six months before closing rather than the following April, and Section 121 does exactly what it was designed to do. Discover it late and you will still get the exclusion — you will just have paid more elsewhere than you needed to.

Frequently asked questions

Does the Section 121 exclusion apply to a home outside the United States?

Yes. IRC Section 121 refers to the taxpayer's principal residence and contains no geographic restriction. A US citizen or green card holder who owned and used a house in Portugal, Mexico or Japan as their main home for at least 24 months out of the five years ending on the sale date can exclude up to $250,000 of gain, or $500,000 on a joint return, exactly as they could with a house in Ohio.

How much gain can I exclude on a foreign home sale in 2026?

Up to $250,000 of gain for a single filer and up to $500,000 for a married couple filing jointly. These figures are fixed in the statute and are not indexed for inflation, so they are the same for 2026 as they have been since 1997. The exclusion can generally only be claimed once in any two-year period.

Is the gain calculated in dollars or in the local currency?

In US dollars, always. Your basis is translated at the exchange rate on the purchase date and your sale proceeds at the rate on the sale date. That means a property which barely moved in euros or pesos can still produce a large dollar gain if the dollar weakened in between, and a property that rose in local terms can produce a dollar loss if the dollar strengthened. The currency movement is part of the taxable gain, not separate from it.

Does Section 121 shelter depreciation I claimed while renting the property out?

No. Depreciation allowed or allowable for periods after 6 May 1997 can never be excluded under Section 121. It is recaptured as unrecaptured Section 1250 gain and taxed at up to 25% federal, even if the rest of your gain falls comfortably inside the exclusion. Foreign residential rental property is depreciated over 30 years under ADS, and the recapture applies whether or not you actually claimed the deduction.

Can I claim Section 121 and the foreign tax credit on the same sale?

Only in a limited way, and this is the trap. Section 121 removes the gain from your US taxable income, so that gain no longer generates any US tax for a credit to offset. Under the Section 904 limitation, foreign tax paid on income the US does not tax produces excess credit in the passive basket. It can be carried back one year and forward ten, but if you have no other passive foreign-source income it frequently expires unused.
