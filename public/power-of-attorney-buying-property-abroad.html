Power of Attorney to Buy Property Abroad (2026)

Almost every American who buys abroad eventually faces the same logistical wall. The deed has to be signed in front of a notary in Lisbon, Athens or Playa del Carmen, on a date the seller's bank chooses, often at ten days' notice. Flying over for a two-hour appointment costs more than the entire legal fee. The standard solution — used by essentially every serious cross-border buyer — is a power of attorney: a document in which you authorize a named person, usually your own lawyer at the destination, to sign the purchase deed on your behalf.

Done properly, it is unremarkable. Done the American way, it fails. The single most common cause of a delayed closing abroad is a power of attorney that was drafted using a US template, notarized at a bank branch, apostilled, couriered over — and then rejected by the foreign notary as the wrong kind of document.

Why a POA is the normal way foreigners close

In civil-law countries — which is most of the world outside the US, UK and Commonwealth — a property transfer is not a private contract that you sign and file. It is a public deed, drafted and authenticated by a notary who is a public official, then lodged in a land registry. The notary reads the deed aloud, verifies identity, confirms the money, and takes responsibility for the legality of the transfer. Your physical presence is one way to satisfy that; a valid representative holding a proper power is the other.

Because the notary is on the hook, they are unforgiving about the paperwork. A power of attorney is not accepted because it looks official — it is accepted because it satisfies the specific formal requirements of that country's law.

The rule that trips Americans up: form follows the deed

Here is the principle that almost no English-language blog states plainly: in civil-law systems, a power of attorney must generally be granted in the same form the law requires for the act it authorizes. Buying real estate requires a public (notarial) deed. Therefore the power to buy real estate must itself, in most of these countries, be a public or notarial instrument — not a private document that happens to carry a notary's stamp.

This matters because the American notary does something entirely different from a Latin notary. A US notary public witnesses a signature. They do not draft the document, do not vouch for its content, and have no public-official status. A US "durable power of attorney" form is, in civil-law terms, a private document with a certified signature — a different species from a notarial deed. Some countries accept it once apostilled and translated (Spain and Portugal generally do, in practice, when the content is right); others firmly do not (Greece and Turkey are the classic refusals). Greece even distinguishes between a notarial plirexousio and a simple written authorization — only the former can be used for a property transfer.

The practical takeaway: never buy a POA template online. Have the notary or lawyer at the destination draft the text, then bring that text to your US notary or to the consulate. It is their document; they set the wording.

Two routes: US notary + apostille, or the consulate

Route A — sign in the US, then authenticate. Your lawyer abroad sends the draft text (usually bilingual). You sign before a US notary public. You then send the notarized original to the Secretary of State of the state where that notary is commissioned — not to Washington — for an apostille, a one-page certificate created by the 1961 Hague Apostille Convention. As of the end of 2025 the Convention had roughly 129 contracting parties, which covers essentially every country Americans buy in except the Gulf states and parts of Southeast Asia. Then a sworn or officially certified translator translates the whole package. Typical all-in: $250–$600 and one to four weeks.

Route B — sign at the destination country's consulate in the US. Consuls of Mexico, Portugal, Spain, Greece, Italy and Turkey exercise notarial functions on behalf of their own state. The consular officer drafts (or receives) the document in the local language, identifies you, and issues it as a document of that country. No apostille. No translation. It arrives in the local system already valid. The cost is usually modest — often well under $150 in consular fees — and the only real friction is the appointment queue, which in busy consulates runs several weeks.

Where you can wait for a consular slot, Route B is cleaner, cheaper and far less likely to bounce. Where the closing date is tight, Route A is the one that moves at your speed.

The sequence most people get wrong

If your POA needs both an apostille and a translation, apostille first, translate second. The sworn translator must translate the document and the apostille attached to it, producing one certified package. Buyers who translate first — often because the translation quote came back faster — end up with a certified translation of the body only, plus an untranslated apostille floating outside the sworn text. Foreign notaries reject this constantly, and the fix costs another round of fees and another week.

Two smaller traps in the same family. First, the apostille must come from the Secretary of State of the state where the notary is commissioned: a POA notarized in New Jersey cannot be apostilled in New York. Second, many receiving countries only treat a POA as "fresh" for a limited window — Spanish and Mexican notaries in particular are wary of powers more than six months old — so do not have it executed months before you have a property under contract.

Mexico: the apostille is not the last step

Mexico deserves its own paragraph because the process has one more stage than buyers expect. An apostilled US power of attorney is not simply handed to the closing notary. It must be translated by a court-registered perito traductor in Mexico, and then protocolized — formally incorporated by a Mexican notario público into their protocol, the official public record. Only then does it exist as a Mexican instrument. Skipping protocolization is the single most common Mexican paperwork failure among American buyers.

The alternative is far simpler: book an appointment at a Mexican consulate in the US, where the consul drafts a poder especial in Spanish and issues it as a Mexican notarial document on the spot — no apostille, no translation, no protocolization. If you are buying in the restricted zone through a fideicomiso, make sure the power expressly covers instructing the trustee bank, not just signing a purchase deed.

Dubai and other non-Hague countries

The United Arab Emirates is not a party to the Hague Apostille Convention, so an apostilled US document has no standing there. Documents must run the full consular-legalization chain: authentication in the US (state and, where required, US Department of State), attestation by the UAE embassy or consulate in the US, then final attestation by the UAE Ministry of Foreign Affairs (MOFAIC) once the document arrives — a step MOFAIC generally completes within zero to three business days. Add an attested Arabic translation. Plan on three to six weeks and several hundred dollars more than the European routes. Thailand sits in the same non-Hague camp, with a comparable legalization chain.

Note that the UAE also offers its own route once you are in-country: a POA notarized before a Dubai Courts notary, which is why some buyers grant the power during a short visit rather than fight the legalization chain from abroad.

Keeping the POA safe: scope, ceiling, expiry

You are handing a person the legal ability to buy real estate in your name and move your money. Constrain it deliberately:

  • Special, not general. Name the property by registry, cadastral or title reference. A general power to "manage my affairs" is both a fraud exposure and, in several registries, grounds for rejection.
  • Itemize the acts. Sign the purchase deed, pay transfer taxes, obtain the tax number, open utilities, register the title — and nothing else. Notably, do not include the power to sell or mortgage unless you actually need it.
  • Cap the price. State a maximum purchase price and the currency. It costs nothing and closes the worst scenario.
  • Set an expiry. Six to twelve months. An open-ended power that outlives the transaction is a liability you will forget you created.
  • Address self-dealing. The attorney-in-fact should not be permitted to act on both sides of the transaction, and should generally not be an employee of the seller or the developer — a genuinely common arrangement in new-build sales abroad, and one to decline.
  • Know how to revoke. Revocation usually requires the same formality as the grant, plus notice to the notary who holds it. Ask, in writing, how revocation would work before you sign.

Step-by-step: from your kitchen table to a registered deed

  1. Engage the local lawyer first. The POA is their instrument; they draft the wording their notary will accept.
  2. Decide the route — consulate (cleaner, slower to book) or US notary + apostille (faster to start, more steps).
  3. Confirm the scope: property identifier, itemized powers, price ceiling, expiry, no power to sell or mortgage.
  4. Execute. Sign before the US notary with government photo ID — or attend the consular appointment in person.
  5. Apostille at the Secretary of State of the notary's commissioning state (skip if consular; replace with the full legalization chain for the UAE or Thailand).
  6. Translate — sworn or officially certified translator, translating the apostille together with the body.
  7. Courier the original by trackable service. Most notaries abroad require the physical original, not a scan.
  8. Local formalities: protocolization in Mexico; registration or filing where required elsewhere.
  9. Close and verify. Ask for the signed deed and the registry entry showing your name — and put the expiry date of the POA in your calendar.

If you're not American: what changes

Nothing in the local mechanics changes with your passport. The notarial form a country demands, the apostille-versus-legalization split, the sworn translation, Mexican protocolization and Turkey's consular-only rule apply to every foreign buyer alike. Only the starting point of the chain — where you sign and who authenticates it — is country-of-residence specific.

If you live outside the US, replace the American half of the chain with your own: sign before a notary (or, in the UK, a notary public rather than a solicitor — many destination notaries will not accept a solicitor's certification), then obtain the apostille from your own country's competent authority, which is the Foreign Office, ministry of justice or equivalent rather than a state Secretary of State. Two checks are worth making before you spend anything. First, confirm that both your country and the destination are Hague parties — if either is not, you are on the legalization track, not the apostille track. Second, check whether the destination country has a consulate near you that offers notarial services, since the consular route removes the apostille and translation steps entirely regardless of your nationality. Separately, on the tax side, ask an adviser where you live whether your home country taxes foreign rental income and gains, whether it requires foreign-asset reporting, and whether a double-taxation treaty with the property country applies — the US rules discussed across this site are not yours.

Is a POA worth it in 2026?

For a remote closing, it is not really optional — it is the mechanism. The honest question is which route to take, and the arithmetic is simple. A consular power of attorney typically costs under $150 and requires one appointment; an apostille-route POA runs $250–$600 with courier and translation; a round-trip flight to Europe for a closing that may well slip by two weeks runs into four figures and cannot be rescheduled cheaply. The only real cost of a power of attorney is the discipline required to keep it narrow. Buyers who get burned are not those who used a POA — they are those who signed a broad, open-ended one drafted by someone on the seller's side of the table.

Frequently asked questions

Can I buy property abroad without traveling there?

Yes. In every country in this guide a properly granted power of attorney lets your local lawyer sign the deed, pay the transfer tax and register the title while you stay home. What varies is the form the power must take — in civil-law countries a property transfer is a public deed, so the power generally has to be a notarial instrument too. A plain US durable-power-of-attorney form, even apostilled, is frequently rejected.

What is the difference between an apostille and consular legalization?

An apostille is a single certificate from the Secretary of State of the state where your notary is commissioned, and it works only between parties to the 1961 Hague Apostille Convention — roughly 129 countries as of the end of 2025. Countries outside the treaty, including the UAE and Thailand, require full consular legalization: state authentication, then attestation at that country's US embassy, then a final attestation by its foreign ministry after the document arrives. Legalization is slower and more expensive.

Do I need my power of attorney translated?

Almost always — unless you sign at the destination country's consulate in the US, where it is drafted in the local language from the start. Where translation is required, sequence matters: apostille first, then have a sworn translator translate the document and the apostille as one certified package. Translating first leaves the apostille outside the sworn text and is a common reason files are refused. A sworn translation usually costs €50–€150.

Is it safer to sign a power of attorney at the country's consulate?

Usually yes, and it is the cleanest route. A consul of Mexico, Portugal, Spain, Greece, Italy or Turkey acts as a notary of that country: the document is drafted in the local language and needs no apostille and no translation. The trade-off is the appointment queue, which can run several weeks, and you must appear in person at the consulate covering your address.

How narrow should the power of attorney be?

As narrow as the deal allows. Ask for a special power covering one identified property, with an itemized list of acts, a maximum price, and an expiry of six to twelve months — and no power to sell or mortgage. Civil-law notaries read powers strictly, so vague grants get rejected anyway, and a broad open-ended power is a standing fraud exposure long after the closing.
