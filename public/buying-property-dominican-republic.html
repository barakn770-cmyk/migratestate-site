Buying Property in the Dominican Republic as an American (2026)

MS By The MigrateState Editorial Team · Reviewed by a licensed international real-estate attorney  ·  Updated July 2026  ·  11 min read
Foreigners buy Dominican property on the same terms as citizens — full freehold title, no residency required. Plan on about 4–8% in closing costs, a 3% transfer tax, and a 1% annual IPI tax that only bites above roughly US$182,000 of value. The real risk here isn't the tax — it's the title. Untitled "possession" land and falsified certificates are common, and only a direct check at the Registro de Títulos protects you.

The Dominican Republic has quietly become one of the most open real-estate markets in the Western Hemisphere for foreign buyers. There are no nationality quotas, no approval boards, and no coastal ownership ban of the kind you meet in Mexico or Costa Rica. An American can fly into Punta Cana, fall for a condo, and take full titled ownership of it without ever becoming a resident. Prices are still a fraction of comparable Caribbean coastlines, rental yields on tourism-zone units are strong, and a generous tax incentive called CONFOTUR can wipe out property tax for fifteen years.

That openness is exactly why diligence matters more here than almost anywhere else. Because the front door is wide open, buyers skip the checks they'd never skip at home — and the DR's weak spot is not its tax code, it's its land registry. Falsified title certificates and land that was never properly surveyed ("deslindado") are the two things that turn a dream purchase into a lawsuit. This guide walks the entire transaction with the 2026 numbers, and puts the title problem where it belongs: right up front.

Can Americans own property in the Dominican Republic?

Yes — and with remarkably few strings attached. The Dominican Republic grants foreign nationals essentially the same property rights as its own citizens. You can hold full freehold title (recorded in the national real-estate registry, the Registro Inmobiliario), with the unrestricted right to sell, lease, mortgage, gift, or leave the property to your heirs. There is no foreign-buyer surcharge on the transfer tax, no minimum-investment floor for a plain residential purchase, and no requirement to hold residency or a visa. Even beachfront condominiums — the exact category that is restricted in Mexico's coastal zone — are freely and directly ownable by Americans here.

The practical machinery of a purchase runs through a Dominican notary (a licensed attorney) who authenticates the sale contract (contrato de venta), and the transfer is then registered at the Registro de Títulos (Title Registry). What you are buying, in the cleanest cases, is a Certificado de Título — a state-guaranteed certificate of title. The single most important thing to understand is that not all Dominican land carries one, and even when it does, the copy the seller hands you is not proof of anything until you verify it at the registry yourself.

The title trap: deslinde and possession land

This is the section most glossy listing sites skip, and it is the one that actually costs Americans money. Two distinct problems live here.

First, falsified or stale title certificates. A physical Certificado de Título can be forged, or it can be genuine but out of date — the person named on it may have already sold, mortgaged, or lost the property. The only reliable verification is a direct query to the Registro de Títulos for a current Certificación del Estado Jurídico del Inmueble (Certificate of Legal Status), which shows the true registered owner and any liens, mortgages, or annotations as of today. Never accept the seller's or developer's copy as sufficient. Industry estimates put the share of overseas Dominican purchases touched by some form of fraud in the mid-single-digit percentages — small, but entirely avoidable with one registry check.

Second, the deslinde — the legal survey. A large amount of Dominican land, especially rural and semi-rural parcels, has never been individually surveyed and demarcated. Where a property is not properly deslindado, the boundaries on the ground may not match what is registered, and in the worst case you are buying an undivided share of a larger parcel (terreno en copropiedad) rather than a defined plot. A related trap is "possession" land (derecho de posesión) — occupied land with no registered title at all. It is cheaper for a reason: it is far harder to defend, finance, or resell, and it is not something a foreign buyer should take on without specialist counsel. Your attorney should confirm the parcel is titled, that a valid deslinde exists, and that the survey matches the fence line before any money moves.

Closing costs and the buying process

Dominican closing costs are moderate and, importantly, predictable. All-in you should budget roughly 4–8% of the purchase price, with most standard residential deals landing around 5.5–7.5%. The components:

Cost Typical 2026 rate Notes
Transfer tax (Impuesto de Transferencia) 3% Charged on the DGII-appraised value, not necessarily your price
Legal fees ~1–1.5% Independent buyer's attorney running full title diligence
Notary, registry & certificate fees ~0.5–1.5% Contract authentication and registration of the new title
Total (typical) ~5.5–7.5% CONFOTUR-approved projects can be exempt from the 3% transfer tax

Two mechanics matter. First, the 3% transfer tax is assessed on the DGII (tax authority) appraised value, which the DGII sets during registration — it can differ from your negotiated price, and the tax must be paid before the Registro de Títulos will issue the new title in your name. Second, unlike the US there is no MLS-wide escrow custom, so funds security is on you: use a written promise-of-sale (contrato de promesa de venta) with the deposit held by a neutral escrow agent or the attorney's client account, released only against a clean registry check. Dominican anti-money-laundering rules also require source-of-funds documentation for large transfers, so keep your paper trail clean from the first wire.

Transfer tax, IPI, capital gains and rental tax

Holding costs in the DR are genuinely low, especially for mid-priced homes. The annual property tax, called IPI (Impuesto sobre el Patrimonio Inmobiliario), is 1% — but it applies only to the portion of value above an exemption threshold that indexes each year. For 2026 the threshold is about RD$10,695,494 — roughly US$182,000. A home appraised below that owes no IPI at all; a US$400,000 home owes 1% on the roughly US$218,000 above the line, about US$2,180 a year. Critically, the threshold is measured against an individual's total Dominican real estate, and IPI is a tax on individuals — one reason ownership structure deserves thought.

When you sell, capital-gains tax is 27% of the net gain — sale price minus your cost basis (original price plus transfer tax, legal fees, and documented improvements) minus selling costs. That headline rate looks steep next to Costa Rica's 15%, but the DR softens it in a way buyers routinely miss: DGII inflation-indexes your cost basis using its published CPI series. On a property held several years, that indexing can lift your basis substantially and cut the taxable gain by half or more. The seller is legally responsible for the capital-gains tax, and — like the transfer tax — it must be cleared with the DGII before the registry will record the sale.

Rental income earned in the DR is taxable there; non-resident owners are generally subject to Dominican tax on Dominican-source rent, and a local accountant should set up your filing so you can later claim a US foreign tax credit for it. The one legal way to erase most of this is not a loophole at all — it's a government program, and it deserves its own section.

CONFOTUR: the 15-year tax break

The Dominican Republic's flagship investment incentive is CONFOTUR, created by Law 158-01 (and extended by Law 195-13) to draw capital into tourism development. If you buy a unit in an officially CONFOTUR-approved project, two things happen: you are exempt from the 3% transfer tax at purchase, and you pay no annual IPI property tax for up to 15 years from the date the project was approved. On a US$400,000 tourism-zone condo, that is thousands of dollars saved at closing and potentially US$25,000-plus in property tax avoided over the exemption's life.

The catch — and the thing brokers sometimes blur — is scope. CONFOTUR benefits attach to the approved project, not to any property in a tourist area. A resale home, or a bare lot you buy on your own, does not qualify just because it sits in Punta Cana or Las Terrenas. And because the 15-year clock runs from the project's approval date, a unit in an older approved development may have only a few years of exemption left. Always demand the CONFOTUR resolution number and its approval date in writing, and have your attorney confirm the unit you're buying is actually inside the approved development. Treated correctly, it's one of the most valuable buyer incentives in the Caribbean; taken on faith, it's a promise that may not apply to your specific unit.

Does buying get you residency?

It can. A real-estate investment of at least US$200,000 qualifies you for investor residency under the expedited procedure of Decree 950-01 — a faster track than ordinary residency, with residency cards typically issued within a couple of months of a complete application. The Dominican Republic does not brand this as a "golden visa," but functionally it is one: property is a qualifying asset class.

What makes the DR unusual is the speed of the citizenship path. Naturalization runs under Law 1683, and while the standard requirement is two years of permanent residency, there is a reduced track — as little as six months of residency — for property owners, business owners, and spouses of Dominican nationals, subject to a Spanish-language interview and oath. The DR's constitution expressly permits dual citizenship, so an American need not renounce. Two non-purchase routes also exist under Law 171-07: a pensioner visa (proof of about US$1,500/month in pension income) and a rentier visa (about US$2,000/month of stable income), both of which carry their own tax perks. If a second passport is part of your plan, the DR is one of the quicker legitimate routes in the hemisphere — but treat the six-month naturalization timeline as a best case that depends on paperwork and processing, not a guarantee.

The US tax angle

For a US citizen or green-card holder, owning Dominican real estate directly, in your own name, is refreshingly clean on the US side. Directly-held foreign real estate is not reportable on the FBAR (FinCEN 114) or on Form 8938 — those apply to foreign financial accounts and assets, not to a house or condo. Where reporting does bite is the Dominican bank account you'll likely open to pay IPI, utilities, and HOA fees: once your foreign accounts together exceed US$10,000 at any point in the year, the FBAR is required.

On income, you still owe US tax on your worldwide income, so rental income goes on Schedule E, with the building depreciated over 30 years under the ADS method that applies to foreign residential rental property (not the 27.5 years used domestically). You can generally claim a foreign tax credit for the Dominican income and capital-gains tax you've paid, which usually prevents true double taxation. When you sell a genuine primary residence, the Section 121 exclusion can shelter up to US$250,000 (US$500,000 for a couple) of gain. And watch the currency angle: because the DR peso can move against the dollar, Section 988 can create a separate taxable US dollar gain on a peso-denominated mortgage even if the property's dollar value barely changed.

The under-appreciated nuance for Americans is the mismatch between the two capital-gains systems. The DR taxes your inflation-indexed peso gain at 27%; the US taxes your nominal dollar gain at long-term capital-gains rates. The foreign tax credit only offsets so far, and the two figures are computed on different bases and in different currencies — so a sale that looks tax-neutral on the Dominican side can still generate a US bill, and vice versa. Model both before you sell, ideally with a cross-border advisor, rather than assuming the credit washes it all out.

Step-by-step: how to buy safely

  1. Engage your own Dominican attorney — independent of the seller, the broker, and the developer's in-house lawyer.
  2. Sign a promise-of-sale (contrato de promesa de venta) with a deposit held in escrow or the attorney's client account, released only on a clean title check.
  3. Verify title at the Registro de Títulos — obtain a current Certificación del Estado Jurídico del Inmueble and confirm the deslinde matches the ground.
  4. Check CONFOTUR status if the unit is marketed with tax benefits — get the resolution number and approval date in writing.
  5. Decide ownership structure — personal name vs. entity — with a US cross-border tax advisor before you commit.
  6. Fund through escrow with full source-of-funds documentation to satisfy AML rules.
  7. Pay the transfer tax and register the new title at the Registro de Títulos, then set up IPI and keep every receipt for your US basis.

If you're not American: what changes

The Dominican rules in this guide — full freehold ownership for foreigners, the 3% transfer tax, ~4–8% closing costs, the 1% IPI above the ~US$182,000 threshold, CONFOTUR, 27% capital gains, and the residency thresholds — are set by the Dominican Republic and apply to every foreign buyer equally, regardless of passport. A Canadian, British, German, or Israeli buyer pays the same transfer tax and faces the same title-diligence risk as an American. What differs is only the tax wrapper back home.

The entire "US tax angle" section above — FBAR, Form 8938, Schedule E, the 30-year ADS depreciation, Section 121, Section 988 — is US-specific and does not apply to non-Americans. If you're not a US person, swap those for your own country's rules and check three things with a tax adviser in your country of residence: (1) whether your home country taxes foreign rental income and capital gains; (2) whether you must report foreign assets or accounts at home; and (3) whether your country has a double-taxation treaty with the Dominican Republic that affects how that income is taxed. The title, deslinde, and CONFOTUR points, by contrast, matter to every foreign buyer identically — those are Dominican rules, not tax-residence rules.

Is it worth it in 2026?

For a buyer who wants a low-cost, low-holding-tax place to own in the Caribbean — with strong rental demand, a real path to residency and even a second passport, and one of the most generous tax incentives in the region in CONFOTUR — the Dominican Republic is hard to beat on paper. Closing costs are moderate, IPI is zero on most mid-priced homes, and the citizenship timeline is among the fastest legitimate routes anywhere.

The way buyers get hurt is almost always the same, and it is entirely avoidable: they trust a beautiful listing and a seller's photocopied title, skip the registry check and the deslinde, and discover too late that the land was never properly surveyed or the seller never truly owned it. Hire an independent Dominican attorney to verify title and survey at the Registro de Títulos, get the CONFOTUR paperwork in writing, and talk to a US cross-border advisor about structure and the capital-gains mismatch before you sign. Do those three things and the DR's open front door stays an advantage all the way through the deal.

Frequently asked questions

Can Americans own property in the Dominican Republic?

Yes. The DR imposes no nationality restrictions, quotas, or approval requirements on foreigners buying standard residential real estate — including beachfront condos. Americans hold full freehold title with the same rights as citizens and need no residency or visa to buy. The real requirement is diligence: verify title through the Registro de Títulos, because falsified certificates and untitled "possession" land are genuine risks.

How much are closing costs when buying property in the Dominican Republic?

Budget roughly 4–8% of the price, most commonly 5.5–7.5%. The main components are the 3% transfer tax on the DGII-appraised value, legal fees of about 1–1.5%, and notary, registry and certificate fees of roughly 0.5–1.5%. There is no foreign-buyer surcharge, and CONFOTUR-approved projects can be exempt from the 3% transfer tax entirely.

What is CONFOTUR and how does it cut my taxes?

CONFOTUR (Law 158-01) is a tourism-investment incentive. Buy a unit in an officially CONFOTUR-approved project and you're exempt from the 3% transfer tax and from the annual 1% IPI property tax for up to 15 years from the project's approval date. It applies only to registered tourism projects — resale homes and bare lots don't qualify — so always get the resolution number and approval date in writing.

What taxes do you pay on Dominican Republic property?

A one-time 3% transfer tax at purchase, then a 1% annual IPI tax charged only on value above roughly RD$10.7 million (about US$182,000 for 2026). On sale, capital-gains tax is 27% of the net gain, but DGII inflation-indexes your cost basis, which often cuts the taxable gain sharply. Rental income is taxable in the DR, and CONFOTUR can exempt transfer and IPI taxes for up to 15 years.

Can buying property get you residency in the Dominican Republic?

Yes. A real-estate investment of at least US$200,000 qualifies for investor residency under the expedited Decree 950-01. Investor residents and property owners can naturalize on a reduced track — as little as six months of residency under Law 1683, versus two years for ordinary residents — and the DR permits dual citizenship. Pensioner (US$1,500/month) and rentier (US$2,000/month) routes exist under Law 171-07.
