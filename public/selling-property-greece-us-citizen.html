Selling Property in Greece as a US Citizen, 2026

MS By The MigrateState Editorial Team · Reviewed by a licensed international real-estate attorney  ·  Updated July 2026  ·  10 min read
Greece charges you nothing to sell in 2026 — but the IRS still charges you everything. Greece has suspended its 15% capital gains tax on individual property sales through 31 December 2026. For a US citizen that zero is a mixed blessing: with no Greek tax paid there is no foreign tax credit to claim, so the US taxes your entire gain — up to 23.8% federal — with nothing to offset it.

The 2026 Greek capital-gains suspension

Start with the good news, because it is real. Greece imposes a 15% tax on the gain an individual makes when selling immovable property — but that tax has been suspended, repeatedly, for more than a decade. The current extension runs through 31 December 2026 under Article 90 of Law 5162/2024. During the suspension an individual seller pays no Greek tax on the profit, even where the property sold for far more than it cost.

There is one meaningful carve-out. If the Greek tax office decides you are not an ordinary owner disposing of a home but a person trading in property, the profit is taxed as business income at ordinary rates and can attract VAT. The rough tests are pattern-based: three or more sales within a two-year period, or building or acquiring a property with the specific intention of reselling rather than using it. A single American selling one long-held home or apartment is nowhere near that line. A buyer flipping a string of Athens flats is.

Assume, then, that your Greek capital-gains bill on a normal 2026 sale is zero. That is exactly where the American problem begins.

Why the suspension is a trap for Americans

Every US citizen carries the US tax system with them wherever they own property. The United States taxes its citizens and green-card holders on worldwide income and gains, and nothing Greece does changes that. A Greek seller who is not a US person walks away from a 2026 sale genuinely tax-free. A US person does not.

The instinct is to reach for the foreign tax credit on Form 1116 — the mechanism that normally stops the same income being taxed twice. But the foreign tax credit only credits tax you actually paid. If Greece charged you nothing, there is nothing to credit. The credit that would have cancelled most or all of your US bill on a Greek sale simply does not exist during the suspension.

The counterintuitive result: the Greek capital-gains holiday can raise an American's total tax bill, not lower it. A 15% Greek tax, if it were charged, would usually be creditable against the roughly 15%–23.8% the US wants — leaving little or no net US tax. With the Greek tax at zero, that credit disappears and the American pays the full US rate out of pocket. The seller who benefits most from the suspension is the one who was never American in the first place.

This flips ordinary timing advice on its head. A Greek seller races to close before 31 December 2026 to catch the holiday. For a US person, closing inside the window can be the worse move: you forgo a foreign tax that would have been creditable and hand the IRS a clean, uncredited gain. Whether that matters depends entirely on the size of your gain and whether the Section 121 exclusion covers it — which is why the US exit should be modelled before you pick a closing date, not after.

What it actually costs to sell

Even with capital gains at zero, selling is not free. The transaction taxes fall on the buyer — most notably the 3.09% transfer tax (FMA) — but the seller carries a distinct set of professional costs, and a notary will not complete the deed until you are fully tax-clear on the property.

How the US taxes your Greek sale

The US result turns on three questions: was the property your principal residence, how did the dollar move against the euro, and did you ever rent it out. Here is the whole picture on one page.

The dollar-basis rule is the one that turns a modest Greek profit into a large US gain — or the reverse. The IRS does not care what your apartment did in euros. Your basis is the purchase price translated at the exchange rate on the day you bought; your proceeds are translated at the rate on the day you sold. If the dollar weakened against the euro in between, a flat sale in euros can still throw off a five-figure US gain out of nothing but currency. If the dollar strengthened, you can show a US loss on a euro profit — but a loss on a personal residence is not deductible, so that phantom loss buys you nothing.

The Section 121 exclusion is your main line of defence, and it works on a Greek home exactly as on a house in Ohio: up to $250,000 of gain excluded for a single filer, $500,000 filing jointly, provided you owned and lived in it as your principal residence for at least 24 of the 60 months before the sale. A pure holiday home or a Golden-Visa buy-to-let that was never your main residence does not qualify, and the whole gain there stays taxable. For the detail, see our guide to the Section 121 exclusion on a foreign home sale.

The mortgage twist: Section 988

If you financed the Greek purchase with a euro mortgage, the loan has its own separate US tax life. Under Section 988, repaying foreign-currency debt when the dollar is stronger than it was at origination produces a currency gain on the debt itself, taxed as ordinary income at rates up to 37%. It is computed independently of the house, and — crucially — Section 121 does not touch it. You can fully exclude the gain on the residence and still owe ordinary-income tax on the mortgage payoff.

This catches Americans who assume the home-sale exclusion cleans up the entire transaction. It does not. The residence and the loan are two calculations, and the loan is the one people forget. We walk through the mechanics in our guide to the foreign mortgage phantom gain.

If the property backs your Golden Visa

Many Americans who own in Greece bought at a Golden Visa threshold, and the residence permit is tied to holding that qualifying investment. Sell the property that supports the permit and, unless you replace it with another qualifying investment, you remove the basis for renewal — the permit can be revoked or simply not renewed at the next cycle. If your Greek residency matters, line up the reinvestment before you sell, not after, so there is no gap in the qualifying asset. Selling step by step

  1. Model the US exit first. Translate your basis and expected proceeds into dollars, apply Section 121 if the home qualifies, and see what US tax actually remains before you commit to a closing date.
  2. Get the certificates ready. Commission the engineer's certificate and the Energy Performance Certificate early; missing paperwork is the most common cause of a stalled Greek sale.
  3. Clear your Greek taxes. Settle ENFIA and any municipal dues and obtain the tax-clearance certificate — a notary cannot complete the deed without it.
  4. Appoint an independent lawyer. Use counsel who does not also act for the buyer, and grant a power of attorney if you are handling the sale from the US.
  5. Sign the notarial deed. The notary reads and executes the contract; the buyer pays the 3.09% transfer tax and the balance of the price.
  6. Handle the currency separately. If a euro mortgage is being repaid, run the Section 988 calculation on its own and report any gain as ordinary income.
  7. Report the sale in the US. File Form 8949 and Schedule D, claim the Section 121 exclusion with adjustment code H if it applies, and remember there is no Form 1116 credit to claim because Greece charged nothing.
  8. Protect any residency. If the property backed a Golden Visa, complete the qualifying reinvestment so the permit survives the sale.

If you're not American: what changes

The Greek side of the sale is identical for any foreign seller. The suspended capital-gains tax, the 3.09% transfer tax the buyer pays, the notary and engineer certificates, the agent commission and the tax-clearance requirement do not change with your passport. What is purely American is the wrapper: worldwide taxation, Section 121, the dollar-basis measurement, Section 988 on a euro mortgage, and Form 8949.

If you are not a US person, drop the IRS layer and substitute the rules of your own country of tax residence. Check three things. First, whether your home country taxes gains on foreign property at all, and whether it offers its own main-residence relief — the UK's private residence relief, Canada's principal residence exemption and Israel's single-dwelling exemption each have their own tests. Second, whether you have foreign asset or account reporting obligations at home covering the Greek property and the sale proceeds. Third, whether a double-taxation treaty exists between your country and Greece, remembering that immovable property is almost always taxable first where it sits and relieved second at home. Because Greece charges zero during the suspension, a resident of a country that does tax foreign gains may face the same "nothing to credit" problem an American does. Confirm the specifics with a tax adviser where you are resident.

Is it worth selling in 2026?

For a non-American, the 2026 window is close to ideal: a genuine Greek capital-gains holiday on a market that has run hard since the mid-2010s. For a US citizen, the answer is more nuanced, and it depends almost entirely on Section 121. If the Greek property was your principal residence and the dollar gain fits inside the $250,000 or $500,000 exclusion, you can exit the 2026 window essentially clean on both sides — the best of both systems. If it was a rental or a holiday home, or the gain runs past the exclusion, the suspension is working against you: you lose the Greek tax that would have been creditable and pay the full US rate on the excess.

The sellers who do well are the ones who run the numbers in dollars months before listing — basis translated properly, Section 121 tested honestly, the Section 988 position on any euro mortgage computed, depreciation recapture identified if the place was ever rented, and, where a Golden Visa is involved, the reinvestment lined up so residency survives. Do that, and the Greek holiday plus Section 121 can be a genuinely efficient exit. Discover the credit problem the following April, and you will have paid the IRS in full for a gain a Greek neighbour walked away from tax-free.

Frequently asked questions

Do I pay Greek capital gains tax when I sell property in Greece in 2026?

Almost certainly not. Greece has suspended the 15% capital gains tax on individual sales of immovable property through 31 December 2026 under Article 90 of Law 5162/2024. An individual who sells within the window pays no Greek tax on the profit, even where the sale price exceeds the purchase price. The exception is if the tax office reclassifies you as trading in property — broadly three or more sales in two years, or building or buying specifically to resell — which is taxed as business income instead.

Does a US citizen owe US tax on a Greek property sale even if Greece charges nothing?

Yes, and this is the part most sellers miss. The United States taxes its citizens on worldwide gains regardless of what Greece does. Because Greece collects zero during the suspension, there is no foreign tax to claim on Form 1116, so nothing offsets the US bill. The full gain is taxed in the US at 0/15/20% long-term capital gains rates plus the 3.8% net investment income tax, subject only to the Section 121 exclusion if it applies.

Can I use the Section 121 exclusion on a Greek home?

Yes, if it was genuinely your principal residence. Section 121 has no geographic limit, so a US citizen who owned and used a Greek property as their main home for at least 24 of the 60 months before the sale can exclude up to $250,000 of gain, or $500,000 filing jointly. A pure holiday home or a rental that was never your main residence does not qualify, and the gain there is fully taxable in the US.

What are the seller's costs when selling property in Greece?

For the seller, the main costs are the estate agent commission of roughly 2%–4% plus 24% VAT, a lawyer at about 0.5%–1%, an engineer's certificate of the property of €700–€1,000, and an Energy Performance Certificate of €100–€300. The 3.09% transfer tax is paid by the buyer, not the seller. You must also be tax-clear — ENFIA and any other property taxes settled — before a notary will complete the transfer.

Does selling my Greece Golden Visa property affect my residence permit?

Yes. The Greek Golden Visa is tied to holding the qualifying investment. If you sell the property that supports the permit and do not replace it with another qualifying investment, you lose the basis for renewal and the permit can be revoked or not renewed. If residency matters to you, plan the reinvestment before you sell rather than after.
