Buying Property in Germany as an American (2026)

MS By The MigrateState Editorial Team · Reviewed by a licensed international real-estate attorney  ·  Updated July 2026  ·  11 min read
Germany places no restriction at all on foreign buyers — an American can own a house, apartment, or land freehold, in their own name, exactly like a German citizen. No visa, no residency, no minimum spend, no foreign-buyer surcharge. Budget about 9–12% in one-time costs, driven mostly by a transfer tax that runs 3.5% to 6.5% depending on the federal state. The two things to plan for in 2026: the reformed 2025 Grundsteuer annual bill, and a 10-year rule that makes a resale tax-free in Germany but not in the US.

Germany FAQ

Germany is one of Europe's most open property markets to a foreigner — and one of the most misunderstood by Americans, who tend to assume the largest economy in the EU must wrap real estate in red tape. It doesn't. Unlike Switzerland, which caps and licenses foreign buyers; unlike Denmark, which demands a permit; unlike the surcharges layered on foreign purchasers in Canada or Australia — Germany lets a US citizen buy a Berlin apartment, a Munich townhouse, or a rural plot on exactly the same freehold basis as a German national. Title is title, recorded in the land register (Grundbuch), with the same rights and the same protections.

What Germany does have is a transaction that is notary-driven, precise, and front-loaded with tax — plus a US tax layer that no German agent will mention. This guide walks the whole purchase with 2026 figures: the transfer tax that swings by up to three percentage points depending on which state you buy in, the annual property tax that was rebuilt from scratch in 2025, the 10-year rule that quietly turns into a US tax bill, and why most overseas buyers end up paying cash.

Can Americans own property in Germany?

Yes — with full freehold ownership. Germany imposes no nationality-based restriction on real estate. A US citizen, whether living in Germany or buying entirely from abroad, can take clean title to a house, a condominium (Eigentumswohnung), or bare land in their own name. There is no visa requirement, no residency requirement, no minimum investment, and no foreign-buyer surcharge of the kind seen in Vancouver, Sydney, or Singapore. Your nationality has no effect on the tax you pay; only the property's location does.

The flip side, and the point most "move to Germany" content blurs: buying property gives you nothing on the immigration side. Germany has no "golden visa," and owning a home does not grant, extend, or support a residence permit. If you want to live in Germany you need a separate qualifying status — the self-employment/freelance route, the EU Blue Card, a job, or family reunification. Own as much property as you like; you still enter Germany as any other American, and as a US passport-holder you can visit visa-free for up to 90 days in any 180 (the ETIAS travel authorisation for the Schengen area is being phased in through 2026–2027).

Costs, transfer tax & the buying process

Germany's buying costs are heavy but transparent, and the single biggest variable is which state you buy in. The real-estate transfer tax (Grunderwerbsteuer) is set by each of the 16 federal states and ranges from 3.5% to 6.5% of the purchase price. Bavaria is the cheapest at 3.5%; at the top end, North Rhine-Westphalia, Brandenburg, Saarland, Schleswig-Holstein and others charge 6.5%, with most remaining states sitting at 5.0%, 5.5%, or 6.0%. On a €500,000 purchase, that spread alone is a €15,000 difference between Munich and Cologne. Here are the 2026 headline charges:

All-in, one-time buying costs run about 9–12% of the purchase price — high by US standards, and crucially not financeable: German banks lend against the property value only, so the transfer tax, notary, registry, and agent fees must be paid in cash on top of any deposit. The transaction itself is built around the notary (Notar), a neutral public official (not your advocate) who is legally required to draft and read out the entire purchase contract, register a priority notice (Auflassungsvormerkung) to protect you, confirm the funds and any mortgage, and only then record the transfer. It is orderly and safe, but it means the notary — not a title company or your own lawyer — runs the closing. Because the notary is neutral, a foreign buyer should still engage their own attorney to review the contract, and, if their German is not fluent, arrange a sworn interpreter at the signing (the notary can insist on one).

Annual tax: the 2025 Grundsteuer reform

Germany's annual property tax, the Grundsteuer, was rebuilt from the ground up on 1 January 2025 after the Federal Constitutional Court ruled the old 1960s/1930s valuations unconstitutional. Roughly 36 million properties were revalued on updated assessment bases, and in December 2025 Germany's Federal Fiscal Court confirmed the new rules were lawful, dismissing the main legal challenges. In practice this means every owner now receives a recalculated annual bill: the tax is the property's assessed value × a federal base figure × the municipal multiplier (Hebesatz), which each city and town sets itself, so the final amount varies widely by location — from a few hundred euros a year on a modest flat to several thousand on a large house.

The reform also created Grundsteuer C, an optional higher rate municipalities can levy on buildable but undeveloped land to discourage speculators from land-banking in housing-short cities. For most buyers of an existing home or apartment this is irrelevant, but if you are eyeing a vacant plot to hold, check whether the municipality applies Grundsteuer C before you assume the annual cost. The headline for an American buyer: Germany's annual property tax is still modest by US standards, but the exact number now depends on a post-2025 assessment you should confirm in writing before you buy.

Selling: the 10-year speculation rule

Germany's treatment of resale gains is unusually generous — with a catch that matters enormously to Americans. For a privately held property, the speculation tax (Spekulationssteuer) applies only if you sell within 10 years of the notarised purchase. Sell inside that window and the gain is added to your income and taxed at your personal rate, up to about 45%. But hold for more than 10 years and the entire gain is completely tax-free in Germany — one of the most valuable exemptions in German tax law. There is also a shorter path for a home you live in yourself: if you occupied it in the year of sale and the two preceding calendar years, the sale is exempt immediately, no 10-year wait. As of 2026 there is no parliamentary majority to abolish the 10-year rule, so it remains in force.

The catch: that German exemption does not follow you to the IRS — the single most important point of this whole guide for a US buyer, and the one covered next.

Financing: mortgages for non-residents

German banks will lend to foreigners, but the terms tighten sharply for a non-resident with no German income. Expect a maximum loan-to-value of about 50–60% — so a 40–50% deposit, plus the 9–12% closing costs, all in cash — against roughly 80–90% for a resident with local employment. Banks want solid proof of income, and paperwork in German. Rates in 2026 sit around 3.6%–4.2% for owner-occupied loans, with non-residents typically paying a premium of a few tenths of a percentage point. German mortgages are usually structured as fixed-rate for a set term (often 10 years) with the balance refinanced afterward.

Because of the high deposit, the language barrier, and the documentation load, many overseas buyers simply pay cash — and, as the next section explains, a cash purchase with no euro-denominated mortgage also sidesteps a nasty US currency-tax trap.

The US tax angle

For a US citizen or green-card holder, owning German property directly is clean on the reporting side but carries real traps at rental and sale. The house itself is not reportable on the FBAR (FinCEN 114) or Form 8938 — those cover financial accounts, not real estate. But the German bank account you open to pay the Grundsteuer, utilities, or receive rent is a foreign financial account: once your non-US accounts together top $10,000 at any point in the year, the FBAR is required, and Form 8938 may be too depending on your filing status and where you live.

Rental income goes on Schedule E at ordinary rates (the Foreign Earned Income Exclusion does not shelter rental income), and the building must be depreciated over 30 years under the Alternative Depreciation System — slower than the 27.5 years used for a US rental. You can generally claim a foreign tax credit for German income tax paid on the rent under the US–Germany tax treaty.

The nuance most blogs miss lives at the sale. Germany's 10-year exemption means a long-term owner pays zero German tax on the gain — but the US taxes its citizens on worldwide income and recognises no such exemption. So the American who holds for 11 years and sells tax-free in Germany still owes US capital-gains tax on the full gain — and because there is no German tax to credit, the foreign tax credit that normally cushions a cross-border sale is zero. The very feature that makes German property attractive to a German is worthless to a US person; you get the US bill with nothing to offset it. Worse, the gain the IRS taxes is measured in dollars: under Section 988, if you funded the purchase with a euro mortgage, a swing in the euro/dollar rate between buying and selling — or even at each mortgage repayment — can create a "phantom" US taxable gain on nothing but currency movement, which is a second reason a cash purchase is cleaner. A true primary residence can still claim the Section 121 exclusion (up to $250,000/$500,000 of gain), and note that inheritance is governed by a separate US–Germany estate-and-gift-tax treaty worth checking if the property will pass to heirs. Model all of this with a cross-border advisor before you buy.

Step-by-step: how to buy

  1. Fix the location first — the state sets the transfer tax (3.5% Bavaria vs 6.5% NRW), so the "where" changes your all-in cost before you even choose a property.
  2. Budget 9–12% in cash costs on top of any deposit; closing costs are not financeable in Germany.
  3. Decide cash vs mortgage early — a non-resident should assume a 40–50% deposit and a 50–60% LTV cap, or plan to pay cash.
  4. Engage your own attorney to review the contract; the notary is neutral and does not represent you.
  5. Get a tax ID and a German bank account for the transfer and ongoing bills; the account may trigger a US FBAR.
  6. Sign at the notary — with a sworn interpreter if your German isn't fluent — who reads the contract, registers the priority notice, and controls the funds.
  7. Pay, then register: once funds and the transfer tax clear, the notary records the transfer and you're entered in the Grundbuch as owner.
  8. Decide US treatment with a cross-border advisor before closing, and keep every receipt — in euros and at the exchange rate on each date — for your US cost basis.

If you're not American: what changes

The German rules in this guide — full freehold for foreigners with no nationality restriction, no foreign-buyer surcharge, the 3.5%–6.5% state transfer tax, the ~1.5%–2% notary and registry costs, the 2025 Grundsteuer annual bill, the 10-year speculation rule, and the notary-run closing — are set by Germany and apply to every foreign buyer equally, regardless of passport. A British, Canadian, Australian, or Israeli buyer faces the same freehold rights, the same transfer tax, and the same paperwork as an American. Only the home-country tax wrapper differs.

The entire "US tax angle" section — FBAR, Form 8938, Schedule E, the 30-year ADS depreciation, the loss of the German 10-year exemption at the IRS, and the Section 988 currency trap — is US-specific and does not apply to non-Americans. If you're not a US person, swap those rules for your own and check three things with a tax adviser in your country of residence: (1) whether your home country taxes foreign rental income and capital gains at all — and crucially, whether it respects Germany's 10-year exemption or taxes the gain anyway; (2) whether you must report foreign assets or accounts at home; and (3) whether your country has a double-taxation treaty with Germany that governs how rental income and any German tax are credited. The German transfer tax, notary process, and Grundsteuer apply to all foreign buyers alike.

Is it worth it in 2026?

For an American who wants a genuinely owned asset in a stable, rule-of-law economy — a Berlin or Leipzig apartment with real rental demand, a family base in Munich, or a foothold in Europe's industrial heartland — Germany is one of the most legally secure foreign-ownership markets on earth. You get true freehold, an incorruptible land registry, a notary system that all but eliminates title fraud, moderate annual property tax, and, for long-term holders, a resale that is tax-free on the German side after 10 years.

The ways buyers get hurt are financial rather than legal, and mostly avoidable: underestimating the 9–12% in non-financeable closing costs, buying in a 6.5% transfer-tax state without realising a neighbouring state charges 3.5%, assuming a mortgage a non-resident won't actually be offered, or — the big one — treating Germany's 10-year tax exemption as if it also frees you from the IRS. Buy with clear eyes, use your own attorney alongside the notary, confirm the post-2025 Grundsteuer assessment in writing, and get US cross-border advice before you wire — and Germany stays exactly as open and secure as it looks at the front door.

Frequently asked questions

Can an American buy property in Germany?

Yes, with full freehold rights and no nationality restriction. A US citizen, resident or not, can buy a house, apartment, or land in their own name on the same legal basis as a German national — no visa, no residency, no minimum spend, and no foreign-buyer surcharge. Buying property does not grant any German residency or visa in return.

What are the costs of buying property in Germany?

Budget roughly 9–12% of the price in one-time costs. The biggest item is the transfer tax (Grunderwerbsteuer), 3.5% in Bavaria up to 6.5% in states like North Rhine-Westphalia, Brandenburg, Saarland and Schleswig-Holstein. Add statutory notary and land-registry fees of about 1.5–2% combined, plus any agent commission, which since 2020 is normally split between buyer and seller. These costs are paid in cash and are not financeable.

What changed with the German property tax in 2025?

A nationwide reform of the annual property tax (Grundsteuer) took effect on 1 January 2025, revaluing about 36 million properties on updated bases; Germany's Federal Fiscal Court upheld the new rules in December 2025. Most owners now get a recalculated bill based on the assessed value and the municipal multiplier (Hebesatz). The reform also added Grundsteuer C, a higher rate municipalities can levy on undeveloped, buildable land.

Do I pay capital gains tax when I sell in Germany?

For a privately held property, Germany's speculation tax applies only if you sell within 10 years; after 10 years the gain is entirely tax-free in Germany, and if you lived in the home in the year of sale and the two prior years it is exempt immediately. As of 2026 the 10-year rule is unchanged. Important for Americans: the US does not recognise this exemption, so a US owner can still owe US capital-gains tax even when Germany charges none.

Can a non-resident get a mortgage in Germany?

Yes but on tighter terms. Non-resident foreign buyers typically face a maximum loan-to-value of about 50–60% — a 40–50% deposit plus closing costs in cash — and must show solid income. Rates in 2026 are roughly 3.6–4.2%, with non-residents often paying a small premium. Because of the high deposit and paperwork, many overseas buyers purchase in cash.
