Buying Property in Italy as an American (2026 Guide)

Italy sells the dream better than almost anywhere — a €1 village house, a Tuscan farmhouse, a Lake Como apartment. Behind the romance sits a buying system that is open to Americans but built around a powerful public official, the notaio, and a tax base most foreign buyers misunderstand. Get the structure right and Italy is more affordable to buy in than its 9% headline tax suggests. Here's the grounded 2026 version, with the legal and tax nuances the listing sites skip.

Can Americans buy property in Italy?

Yes — fully and with the same rights as an Italian citizen. The United States and Italy have a reciprocity agreement (Italy lets citizens of countries that let Italians buy there), so as a US citizen you can buy an apartment, a villa or land in your own name. You do not need to be a resident or hold a visa to own. Two things are non-negotiable: a codice fiscale (Italian tax code) and a notaio — a public notary who, by law, verifies title and registers the transfer. The notary is neutral; you should still hire your own lawyer for due diligence.

What you need before you buy

Four essentials turn an American into a "ready" buyer in Italy:

  • A codice fiscale — Italy's tax ID, required for the purchase, a bank account, utilities and the deed. Free from an Italian consulate in the US, or via a representative in Italy who files form AA4/8 with your passport.
  • An Italian bank account — to pay the deposit, taxes and bills. Expect a FATCA check and a request for an IRS W-9.
  • An independent lawyer (avvocato) — separate from the agent and the notary, to check title, planning, building amnesties, and any abusi edilizi (illegal works), which are common on older property.
  • A surveyor (geometra) — to confirm the building matches its cadastral and planning records, a frequent source of trouble in Italy.

The purchase tax — and the cadastral-value trick

Which tax you pay depends on whether the home is resale or new-build, and whether it's a first or second home for you.

Resale homes pay registration tax (imposta di registro). As a non-resident treated as a second-home buyer, the rate is 9%, with a €1,000 minimum, plus fixed cadastral and mortgage taxes of €50 each. New-build homes pay VAT instead — generally 10% (or 4% as a first home, 22% on luxury class A/1, A/8, A/9) — plus fixed registration, cadastral and mortgage taxes.

⚠️ Italy flat-tax update (2026): Italy's lump-sum flat tax for new residents has been raised twice. Under Decree-Law 113/2024, the rate rose from €100,000 to €200,000 for those who transferred Italian tax residency after 10 August 2024. Under the 2025 Italian Budget Law, it rose again to €300,000 per year for those who transfer residency from 1 January 2026 onward. Grandfathering applies at each step: those who joined before August 2024 remain at €100,000; those who joined August 2024–December 2025 remain at €200,000; only new entrants from 2026 face the €300,000 rate. If you are considering relocating to Italy to benefit from this regime, model the current €300,000 annual cost against your actual foreign-source income. Additionally, Italy's 7% flat tax for foreign pensioners now applies in municipalities with up to 30,000 residents (raised from 20,000 in April 2026), unlocking 74 additional towns in qualifying southern regions.

Here is the nuance that changes the math, and that most American buyers never hear. When an individual buys a resale residential home from a private seller, Italy lets you use the "prezzo-valore" system: the 9% is calculated not on the price you pay, but on the property's cadastral value (valore catastale) — a formula-based figure that is typically 30%–60% below market value. So a €300,000 home with a €120,000 cadastral value is taxed 9% on €120,000 — about €10,800, not €27,000. You must be an individual (not a company), buying residential, and ask the notary to apply prezzo-valore in the deed. It is the single biggest cost lever in an Italian purchase.

Annual IMU and the prima casa rule

Italy's annual property tax is IMU (Imposta Municipale Unica), set by each municipality, generally 0.46%–1.06% of the revalued cadastral value. The catch for Americans: IMU is waived on a genuine first home, but only if it is your actual main residence. A non-resident always pays IMU — there is no first-home exemption for a holiday home. The same logic governs the purchase tax: the 2% prima casa rate is available only if you move your residency to the property's comune within 18 months. Most American buyers keeping a US home cannot meet that, so they correctly budget the 9% second-home rate and ongoing IMU.

Step-by-step: buying property in Italy as an American

  1. Get your codice fiscale — at an Italian consulate in the US or via a representative in Italy filing form AA4/8.
  2. Open an Italian bank account (expect a W-9 / FATCA check) for the deposit, taxes and bills.
  3. Engage an independent lawyer and a geometra to run title, cadastral, planning and abusi edilizi checks.
  4. Make a written offer (proposta d'acquisto) with a small deposit; once accepted it becomes binding.
  5. Sign the preliminary contract (compromesso) with a deposit (caparra), usually 10%–20%. Back out and you lose it; if the seller backs out they repay double.
  6. Sign the deed (rogito) before the notaio, who collects the taxes, pays the seller, and registers the transfer. Ask for prezzo-valore in the deed.
  7. Register and set up US compliance — the notary registers your title; you file FBAR for the bank account and plan rental/resale reporting.

Start to finish, the typical timeline is two to four months.

The US tax rules that still apply

Your Italian lawyer handles Italy; the IRS is your problem. The United States taxes citizens on worldwide income, so rental income from your Italian home goes on Schedule E, and a future sale is a US capital gain. The US–Italy tax treaty and the foreign tax credit normally stop you being taxed twice. An Italian bank account can trigger FBAR (FinCEN Form 114) and FATCA (Form 8938) once balances cross the thresholds. The nuance most Americans miss: Italy generally exempts the capital gain on a resale home held more than five years, so an Italian sale after five years often carries no Italian tax — which sounds great, but means there is no foreign tax credit to offset your US capital-gains bill. As in Greece, a local tax holiday can leave the American fully exposed to the IRS, plus the usual currency-gain wrinkle on a euro mortgage payoff. If you're not American: what changes

The Italian side is identical for any foreign buyer. The codice fiscale, the notaio, the 9% registration tax on cadastral value, the prezzo-valore mechanism, the 10%–15% costs and the IMU rules apply the same whether you hold a US, UK, Canadian, Australian or Israeli passport. Only the home-country tax wrapper on top is different.

If you're not American, keep everything above on the Italian process exactly as written — reciprocity means most nationalities can buy, and the mechanics don't change with your passport. What you swap out is the US-specific tax layer (worldwide-income filing, FBAR, FATCA). In its place, check three things in your own country of residence: (1) whether your home country taxes foreign rental income and capital gains earned in Italy; (2) whether you must report foreign property, bank accounts or assets at home; and (3) whether your country has a double-taxation treaty with Italy that lets you credit Italian tax against your home bill. EU/EEA citizens may also find it easier to establish residency to claim the 2% prima casa rate. Confirm the specifics with a tax adviser in your country of residence before committing.

Is buying property in Italy worth it in 2026?

For lifestyle and, increasingly, value, Italy is hard to beat — and the prezzo-valore mechanism quietly makes the purchase tax far gentler than the 9% headline implies. The catches are practical, not legal: Italian title and building records are a minefield of old amnesties and unpermitted works, so an independent lawyer and a geometra are essential, and non-residents should budget IMU and the second-home rate honestly. Buy with your own advisers, ask the notary for prezzo-valore, line up your US tax plan first, and Italy remains one of the most rewarding places an American can own a home.

Frequently asked questions

Can a US citizen buy property in Italy?

Yes. Italy and the US have a reciprocity agreement, so US citizens buy outright with the same rights as an Italian. You don't need residency — only a codice fiscale (tax code), and a notaio (notary) is mandatory to complete the sale.

How much does it cost to buy property in Italy as an American?

Plan for 10%–15% on top of the price. Non-residents pay 9% registration tax, but on a resale home from a private seller it's charged on the lower cadastral value via prezzo-valore. Notary runs ~1%–2.5% and an agent ~3% plus VAT.

Do non-residents pay more tax buying property in Italy?

Yes. The reduced 2% prima casa rate requires moving your residency to the property's municipality within 18 months. Non-residents who don't relocate pay the 9% second-home rate and full annual IMU, with no first-home exemption.

What is the codice fiscale and how do I get one?

It's Italy's tax ID, required to buy property, open a bank account and sign the deed. Get it free at an Italian consulate in the US, or appoint a representative in Italy who applies with form AA4/8 and your passport.

Do I pay US tax on a property I buy in Italy?

Potentially. The US taxes worldwide income, so Italian rental income and a future sale are IRS-reportable. The US–Italy treaty and foreign tax credit help prevent double taxation, and an Italian bank account can trigger FBAR/FATCA.
