Foreign Bank Accounts for Americans: FBAR, FATCA & How to Open One (2026)

MS By The MigrateState Editorial Team · Reviewed by a licensed international real-estate attorney  ·  Updated June 2026  ·  9 min read
Americans can open foreign bank accounts — and for most property purchases abroad, you practically need one. The IRS catch: once any of your foreign accounts collectively cross $10,000 at any point during the year, you must file FinCEN Form 114 (FBAR) by April 15. The good news from a 2023 Supreme Court ruling: the non-willful penalty is now capped at $16,536 per annual report, not per account — a significant limit most expat guides still misstate.

Opening a foreign bank account is one of the first practical tasks any American property buyer faces abroad. You need a local account to pay the purchase deposit, wire the balance, cover transfer taxes and notary fees, and handle ongoing expenses like utilities and local property tax once you own. The mechanics of opening the account have gotten harder since 2010 — but they're still doable, and the reporting rules, while tedious, are manageable if you understand them from day one. Here is the complete picture for 2026.

Why property buyers need a foreign bank account

Most foreign property transactions require at least some funds to flow through a local account. A Portuguese notary typically won't accept a wire from a US bank for the final deed payment; a Spanish seller expects a local cheque. In Mexico, a peso account lets you pay the ISAI transfer tax and HOA fees without conversion costs on every transaction. In Greece, a local account is required to open a utility contract. Beyond the transaction itself, ongoing ownership creates regular local expenses — IMI in Portugal, IBI in Spain, IMU in Italy, predial in Mexico — that local autopay simplifies enormously.

Even if you could technically manage without a local account, doing so exposes you to unfavorable bank-to-bank exchange rates on each outbound transfer, delays that can jeopardize closing timelines, and the inability to receive rental income directly in-country. Most buyers who skip the local account regret it by year two of ownership.

Can Americans open a foreign bank account in 2026?

Yes — but it is harder than it used to be. The Foreign Account Tax Compliance Act (FATCA), enacted in 2010 and phased in through 2014, requires foreign financial institutions to report US account holders to the IRS or face a 30% withholding penalty on US-source income. The compliance burden is so significant that many smaller foreign banks now simply decline US persons rather than build FATCA programs. The European private banking sector has been especially cautious.

That said, several categories of institution remain reliably accessible to Americans in 2026. Large international banks with existing FATCA infrastructure — HSBC, Santander, Deutsche Bank, BNP Paribas, Citibank's international branches — are the most straightforward. Country-specific options include Millennium BCP and Caixa Geral de Depósitos in Portugal, Bankinter and CaixaBank in Spain, Alpha Bank and Piraeus Bank in Greece, BBVA and Banorte in Mexico, and Emirates NBD in the UAE. Your property lawyer often has referral relationships with a specific banker who handles international clients routinely, which can shortcut the process considerably.

Fintech alternatives like Wise (formerly TransferWise) and Revolut issue local IBANs and work for transfers and day-to-day spending. Their practical limitation is that a Wise EUR IBAN is not always accepted as a "real bank account" for mortgage applications, some closing payment requirements, or tax direct-debit setups. Use them as a complement, not a substitute, for a regulated bank account.

Documents you'll need

Requirements vary by country and institution, but Americans opening a foreign bank account for a property purchase should bring: a valid US passport; a second ID (driver's licence or second passport if you hold one); proof of a local address or the address of the property you are buying (a purchase agreement or notary letter often works before you have a utility bill); a bank reference letter or recent statements from your US bank; proof of the source of funds (especially for amounts over €50,000 or the local equivalent — expect anti-money-laundering questions); and a completed W-9 form, which foreign banks require from US persons to satisfy their FATCA reporting obligations. Some countries require a local tax number first — Portugal's NIF and Spain's NIE must be obtained before you can open a bank account.

FBAR (FinCEN 114): the $10,000 rule

Any US person — citizen, green card holder, or certain residents — who holds a financial interest in, or signature authority over, one or more foreign financial accounts must file FinCEN Form 114 (the FBAR) if the aggregate maximum value of those accounts exceeded $10,000 at any point during the calendar year. The form is filed electronically through the Financial Crimes Enforcement Network (FinCEN) BSA E-Filing System — not the IRS — and is due April 15, with an automatic extension to October 15 if you miss the initial deadline. No form is needed to request the extension.

The $10,000 threshold is aggregate: if you have €6,000 in a Portuguese checking account and $6,000 in a Mexican savings account, the combined value in USD terms may exceed the threshold even though neither account alone does. Joint accounts count in full for both account holders, so a couple holding a joint Spanish account must each file their own FBAR.

The FBAR timing trap most buyers hit

The trigger is any single moment during the year, not an average or year-end balance. Property buyers commonly trip this rule the day their purchase wire lands. A buyer who sends $350,000 to a Portuguese bank account in February and then draws it down by March for closing has still triggered the FBAR obligation for that entire calendar year — because the account balance exceeded $10,000 during the year. The account doesn't need to stay above $10,000; it just needs to have crossed the line once. File the FBAR for any year this happened, even if you've since closed the account.

Form 8938 (FATCA): the higher threshold

Form 8938 (Statement of Specified Foreign Financial Assets) is a separate reporting requirement, attached to your annual Form 1040 and filed with the IRS. It covers a broader set of assets than the FBAR — including foreign stocks, securities, interests in foreign partnerships, and certain foreign insurance contracts — but applies only above higher dollar thresholds that depend on your filing status and where you live.

For the 2025 tax year filed in 2026: if you live in the US, you file Form 8938 if total foreign financial assets exceed $50,000 on the last day of the year or $75,000 at any time during the year (single); $100,000 / $150,000 for married filing jointly. If you live abroad, the thresholds are much higher: $200,000 / $300,000 for single filers and $400,000 / $600,000 for married filing jointly. A property buyer who keeps their primary home in the US and opens a €30,000 foreign bank account will almost certainly be below the Form 8938 threshold — but should still file the FBAR because it has a different, lower trigger.

Does foreign real estate trigger FBAR or Form 8938 reporting?

This is the question that generates the most confusion, and the answer is nuanced. Foreign real estate held directly in your own name is not a foreign financial account (so no FBAR) and is not a "specified foreign financial asset" (so no Form 8938). The IRS has been explicit on this: a house or condo you own outright is excluded from both forms. What does trigger reporting is the infrastructure around the property: a foreign bank account opened to manage it (FBAR at $10,000), and any foreign entity — corporation, LLC, or trust — used to hold the property (potentially both forms, depending on the structure). If you buy a Mexican condo through a fideicomiso bank trust, the trust arrangement is generally excluded from Form 3520 under Rev. Rul. 2013-14, but the Mexican bank account associated with the trust is still an FBAR account if its balance crosses $10,000.

Penalties — and the Bittner ruling

FBAR penalties have always been the scariest part of the foreign-account story, and they still are significant. What changed materially in 2023 was the US Supreme Court's decision in Bittner v. United States, which resolved a circuit split in favor of taxpayers on how non-willful penalties are counted. Before Bittner, some IRS field offices were assessing one penalty per foreign account per year — meaning a person with five accounts who missed filing for three years could face 15 separate $10,000+ penalties. The Supreme Court held that the non-willful penalty is assessed per annual report, not per account. In 2026, that maximum non-willful penalty per annual FBAR report is $16,536 (inflation-adjusted).

Willful violations remain severe: up to $165,353 or 50% of the highest account balance, whichever is greater, per violation. "Willful" can be established through circumstantial evidence — the IRS has successfully argued that a sophisticated taxpayer who signed a return with a checkbox for foreign accounts and didn't file was willful, even without proof they knew the FBAR rules. The practical defense is to file, and to file promptly when you realize you've missed a year.

How to catch up on missed FBARs

If you've owned a foreign bank account — perhaps to manage a property you bought years ago — and never filed an FBAR, the IRS provides three main remediation paths. The Delinquent FBAR Submission Procedures (DFSP) apply if you have not been contacted by the IRS and have no unreported income: you file the late FBARs with a statement of reasonable cause, and penalties are typically zero. The Streamlined Filing Compliance Procedures (SFCP) are for taxpayers with unreported income: US-based filers pay a 5% miscellaneous offshore penalty; abroad-based filers pay $0 offshore penalty (but must have lived outside the US for the requisite period). The Voluntary Disclosure Program (VDP) is for potential willful violations and involves a formal negotiation with the IRS's Criminal Investigation division. All three paths are immeasurably better than doing nothing and waiting to be found.

Step-by-step: opening a foreign account and staying compliant

  1. Get your local tax number first — a Portuguese NIF, Spanish NIE, Greek AFM, or Italian codice fiscale is required before most local banks will open an account for a foreigner.
  2. Choose the right institution — for property purchases, a regulated local bank is strongly preferred over fintech-only. Ask your property lawyer for a referral to their bank contact for non-resident clients.
  3. Bring a completed W-9 — foreign banks servicing US persons require it. Refusing or forgetting it is the surest way to get declined.
  4. Document your source of funds — anti-money-laundering rules mean banks will ask where the purchase money came from. A statement from your US bank and, if applicable, evidence of a home sale or investment liquidation should travel with the wire.
  5. Note the date of the first large deposit — that date starts the FBAR clock for the calendar year. Alert your US CPA immediately.
  6. File the FBAR by April 15 of the following year (auto-extension to October 15). File through the BSA E-Filing System at bsaefiling.fincen.treas.gov — no paper filing allowed.
  7. Assess Form 8938 separately — check whether your total foreign financial assets (accounts, not real estate) exceed the relevant threshold and attach the form to your 1040 if so.

If you're not American: what changes

The local bank-account process is the same whatever passport you hold. Foreign banks apply their anti-money-laundering checks and FATCA-equivalent rules to all foreigners. What's uniquely American is the FBAR/FATCA wrapper — the FinCEN filing and Form 8938 are US obligations only.

If you're not a US person, you can ignore the FBAR, Form 8938, and all the IRS-specific analysis above. What you should check with a tax adviser in your country of residence: (1) whether your home country requires reporting of foreign bank accounts or financial assets — Canada's T1135 Foreign Income Verification Statement, the UK's self-assessment foreign income pages, German Finanzamt foreign-account disclosure, and EU Common Reporting Standard (CRS) filings all impose analogous duties; (2) whether interest or rental income earned in the foreign account is taxable at home; and (3) whether a double-taxation treaty between your home country and the property country governs how local withholding tax on interest is credited. The local bank-opening hassle and documentation requirements are broadly similar everywhere — but the home-country reporting framework you plug into after the account is open is country-specific.

Is maintaining a foreign bank account worth it in 2026?

For any American who owns or is buying property abroad, the answer is almost always yes — and the compliance burden, while real, is lower than most people fear once they understand it. The FBAR is a disclosure form, not a tax: you're not paying anything extra on the account itself, you're just telling FinCEN it exists. The annual filing takes minutes with the right software or CPA. The genuine risk is not in filing — it's in not filing. A missed FBAR for a $300,000 property escrow account sits at up to $16,536 for a non-willful violation under Bittner; a willful failure sits at up to $150,000 or more. Neither outcome is worth avoiding a free form.

Open the account, document your source of funds, hand your CPA the account number and country by January, and file the FBAR in the spring. Done correctly, a foreign bank account is one of the simplest parts of owning property abroad — and the compliance cost is a rounding error compared to the transfer taxes and legal fees you're already paying.

Frequently asked questions

Do Americans have to report a foreign bank account?

Yes, if the combined balance of all your foreign financial accounts exceeded $10,000 at any point during the calendar year. File FinCEN Form 114 (FBAR) by April 15, with an automatic extension to October 15. It goes to FinCEN, not the IRS, and is filed electronically only.

Can Americans open a foreign bank account in 2026?

Yes, but FATCA compliance costs mean many smaller banks decline US persons. Large international banks (HSBC, Santander, Citibank, Deutsche Bank) and major local banks in your target country are the most reliable options. Fintech platforms like Wise and Revolut supplement but don't fully replace a regulated bank for property closings.

Does foreign real estate trigger an FBAR?

No — real estate held directly in your own name is not a financial account. What triggers the FBAR is the foreign bank account associated with the property. If you hold the property through a foreign corporation or trust, additional reporting may apply to the entity interest.

What is the FBAR penalty in 2026?

Non-willful: up to $16,536 per annual report (not per account), per the Supreme Court's Bittner decision. Willful: up to $165,353 or 50% of the highest account balance, whichever is greater. Voluntary late filers under the Delinquent FBAR Submission Procedures typically pay no penalty.

What is the Form 8938 threshold for Americans living in the US?

For the 2025 tax year, single filers living in the US must file Form 8938 if total foreign financial assets exceed $50,000 on the last day of the year or $75,000 at any time. Joint filers: $100,000 / $150,000. Americans living abroad face higher thresholds: $200,000 / $300,000 single, $400,000 / $600,000 joint.
