Why Americans look at Panama

Panama has quietly become the default landing pad for Americans who want a foothold abroad without the friction. The reasons are practical rather than romantic: the country runs on the US dollar (the balboa is pegged 1:1 and US notes are legal tender), the residency programs are unusually open to US citizens, the tax system is territorial, and a foreign buyer takes title to an apartment in Panama City or a hill town in Boquete with the same rights a Panamanian has under Article 47 of the Constitution. You do not need to be a resident, hold a visa, or even visit to buy. And because the economy is dollarized, the Section 988 "phantom currency gain" that bites US owners in Mexico, Costa Rica or Europe simply does not exist here — there is no second currency to move against the dollar.

That openness is also where unprepared buyers get hurt. Because the front door is wide, people skip the diligence they would never skip at home and walk into the three places Panama genuinely punishes the careless: untitled "Rights of Possession" land sold as if it were titled, the constitutional ban on foreign ownership near the borders, and the broker's reflexive advice to "just put it in a Panama corporation." The last one matters most on the US side: a Panamanian sociedad anónima is a "per se" foreign corporation to the IRS, triggering an annual Form 5471 (minimum $10,000 penalty for non-filing), potential CFC/GILTI/PFIC exposure, and loss of the Section 121 exclusion and step-up in basis at death. Ownership structure is a US tax decision to make with an advisor before closing — not a default a broker picks for you.

Where to buy — the regions

Panama packs several distinct markets into a small country, each with its own buyer:

  • Panama City — the dollarized, skyline-and-banking hub with the deepest rental market and the best connectivity in Central America. Titled high-rise condos, strong long-term letting demand. Best for investors and professionals who want liquidity and city life.
  • Boquete — the cool-climate mountain town in Chiriquí that made Panama famous with American retirees. An established expat community, gentler prices, and a temperate escape from the coastal heat. Best for retirees and remote workers — but note parts of Chiriquí sit near the Costa Rica border zone.
  • Coronado — the Pacific-coast beach town closest to the capital, with a large, well-serviced expat base and resort-style condos. Suits second-home and lock-and-leave buyers who want the beach within a 90-minute drive of the city.
  • Bocas del Toro — the Caribbean archipelago of beach and island parcels — beautiful, but the epicentre of the Rights of Possession problem, where untitled land is routinely marketed to foreigners, and much of it also falls inside the 10 km border zone near Colombia and Costa Rica. Titled land only, with independent counsel, or not at all.

Two constitutional limits cut across all of these: foreigners (and even companies with any foreign capital) cannot own land within 10 kilometers of a national border, and no one can title land within the public coastal strip (22 metres of the high-tide line on the Pacific, 10 on the Atlantic). Islands themselves are fine unless they fall inside the border zone.

The date to watch is October 15, 2026: the Qualified Investor Visa threshold is scheduled to jump from US$300,000 to US$500,000, so timing matters if residency is your route. The owner-occupier property tax is genuinely low — zero up to $120,000 of registered value — but the exemption is not automatic: you must register the property as a Primary Residence or Family Tax Patrimony with the DGI. A rental condo uses the less generous second-home scale, so it won't get the $120,000 break.

Who Panama suits — and who should look elsewhere

Panama fits Americans who want a dollar-denominated home in a stable, well-connected hub, and who do the title and structuring diligence properly. Buyer closing costs are modest, owner-occupier property tax can be zero, capital gains are a flat 10%, and a purchase can double as a fast track to permanent residency — the Friendly Nations Visa takes a US$200,000 property, the Qualified Investor Visa US$300,000 (for now). Retirees have an even easier path: the celebrated Pensionado visa needs no purchase at all, just US$1,000/month of lifetime pension income.

Panama is a weaker fit if your heart is set on untitled beach or island land in Bocas del Toro (the Rights-of-Possession trap), on border-zone or coastal-strip parcels the Constitution puts off-limits, or on the idea that a Panama corporation is a costless convenience — for a US person it is often a five-figure annual compliance burden. And Panama's famous territorial tax system does nothing for a US citizen, who is taxed by the IRS on worldwide income regardless. Panama FAQ

Is Panama a good place for Americans to buy property in 2026?

For a dollar-denominated home in a stable, well-connected hub — with modest closing costs, zero owner-occupier property tax up to $120,000 of value, a flat 10% capital-gains rate, and a purchase that can fast-track permanent residency — Panama is one of the most foreign-friendly markets anywhere. The risks are specific and avoidable: untitled land, the border zone, and the Panama corporation.

Do I need residency or a visa to buy in Panama?

No. Article 47 of the Constitution gives foreigners the same property rights as citizens, with no residency or visa required to buy titled real estate. Buying can instead open the door to residency — US$200,000 qualifies for the Friendly Nations Visa and US$300,000 for the Qualified Investor Visa, though that investor threshold rises to US$500,000 after October 15, 2026.

What is the difference between titled land and Rights of Possession?

Titled property is registered in the Public Registry with a clean, defensible legal title — this is what you want. Rights of Possession (ROP) is an unregistered, possessory claim to government land: there is no title, the "owner" cannot give you one, and it is far harder to defend, finance or resell. ROP land is common in Bocas del Toro and rural areas; insist on titled land verified in the registry.

Should I hold my Panama property in a corporation?

Usually not, if you're American. A Panamanian sociedad anónima is a "per se" foreign corporation to the IRS: it triggers an annual Form 5471 (minimum $10,000 penalty for non-filing), can create CFC, GILTI or PFIC exposure, and forfeits the Section 121 exclusion and the step-up in basis at death. It also cannot bypass the 10-kilometer border rule. Get US tax advice before choosing a structure.

Does Panama's US-dollar economy really change my US taxes?

Yes, favorably. Because Panama uses the dollar, there is no second currency to move against it, so the Section 988 "phantom" currency gain that can hit a peso- or euro-denominated mortgage elsewhere simply doesn't arise. The flip side: Panama's territorial tax system does nothing for you, since the US taxes its citizens on worldwide income regardless of where they live.
