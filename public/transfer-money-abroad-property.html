How to Transfer Money Abroad to Buy Property (2026)

Why the exchange rate is your biggest hidden cost

Most buyers focus on purchase price, notary fees, and transfer taxes — and completely overlook the exchange rate. But when you're converting $300,000 into euros, pounds, pesos, or dirhams, a 3% difference in the rate you receive is $9,000 lost before you've signed a single document. A 5% swing — well within normal year-to-year currency movement — is $15,000.

The problem is that retail banks almost never offer the interbank (mid-market) rate that appears on Google or XE.com. They embed a margin of 2–4% into the quoted rate and call it nothing. There are no hidden fees listed because the fee is the spread. A specialist foreign-exchange firm, by contrast, typically charges a small flat transaction fee and quotes a rate at or near mid-market — a structural difference that matters enormously on a real-estate purchase.

The other risk is timing. If you sign a purchase agreement in October and close in March, five months of currency movement can add or subtract tens of thousands of dollars from your effective purchase price. Managing that risk with a forward contract is something your bank almost certainly won't offer at competitive terms, but specialist providers routinely do.

The 2026 remittance tax — and why bank wires are exempt

The One Big Beautiful Bill Act, signed into law in July 2025, introduced a 1% federal excise tax on certain remittance transfers effective January 1, 2026. For anyone wiring money abroad to buy property, the critical question is: does it apply to me?

Almost certainly not — if you wire from a US bank account. The law covers a broad set of electronic transfers, including wire transfers, money orders, cashier's checks, prepaid card reloads, and online bill payments. The key exemption is institution-based: transfers sent by or through a US financial institution regulated under the Bank Secrecy Act — US banks, credit unions, and investment companies — are explicitly exempt. An international bank wire sent from your US checking or savings account falls squarely in that exempt category, because your US bank is a BSA-regulated institution. The distinction is not the payment method ("wire" vs "cash") but who processes it.

The 1% tax is collected by the remittance provider at time of transfer and remitted to the IRS quarterly. If you are sending the funds through a specialist FX platform using a US bank-funded transfer, confirm with that provider in writing that your transfer is classified as an account-funded wire (and therefore exempt) rather than a cash-funded remittance. Platforms like Wise and Currencies Direct have confirmed their account-funded transfers are exempt under the statute; cash-loaded transfers on certain money-service apps may not be.

The nuance most blogs miss: even for transfers that are subject to the 1%, the 1% is paid by the sender and collected by the provider — it is not a withholding on the amount received abroad. The $300,000 lands in your destination account; the $3,000 tax (if it applied) would be an additional cost charged on top, on the US side.

IRS reporting requirements when you send large sums

There is no legal ceiling on how much you can wire internationally. The US government's concern is reporting, not prohibition. Under the Bank Secrecy Act, any single transaction of $10,000 or more is automatically reported by your bank to FinCEN via a Currency Transaction Report (CTR). You do not file this yourself — the bank does it on your behalf. You don't sign anything and receive no paperwork, but a record is created.

For very large transfers — a full purchase price in the hundreds of thousands — your bank's compliance department will typically ask you to document the source of funds. A recent bank statement, a letter from a prior real-estate closing, a brokerage account statement, or a loan document are all standard. Having these ready in advance prevents a frustrating delay the week you need to close.

One caution: never structure transfers to avoid the $10,000 threshold — for example, by sending $9,800 repeatedly. "Structuring" is a federal crime regardless of whether the underlying money is legitimate. If your purchase requires moving $400,000, send it in whatever configuration your bank allows, document the source, and let the CTR reports take care of themselves.

FBAR and Form 8938: what triggers once the money lands

Once your wire arrives in a foreign bank account or an escrow account held in your name, US reporting obligations kick in on the other side of the equation.

FBAR (FinCEN Form 114) is required if the aggregate maximum value of all your foreign financial accounts exceeded $10,000 at any point during the calendar year. "At any point" means a single day counts — if your wire clears and sits in a foreign account for 48 hours before being paid to the notary, that balance is reportable. The FBAR is filed electronically with FinCEN (not the IRS) and is due April 15, with an automatic extension to October 15. Penalties for non-willful failure to file run up to $16,536 per violation for 2026; willful violations carry penalties of $165,353 or 50% of the account balance, whichever is greater, plus potential criminal charges.

Form 8938 (FATCA) applies when your foreign financial assets exceed higher thresholds: $50,000 on the last day of the year or $75,000 at any point during the year for US residents (double those amounts for married couples filing jointly). If you maintain a foreign bank account after closing — common if you have rental income or ongoing expenses — and that account's balance crosses these thresholds, Form 8938 is attached to your regular tax return. Note that real property itself is not a "foreign financial asset" for Form 8938 purposes — the account that holds the money is what counts.

Because property purchases often involve the money landing in a foreign account briefly (even for a single day), most Americans who wire substantial sums to buy abroad will have at least an FBAR obligation for that year. It's one page filed electronically and carries no tax — only failure to file creates a problem.

Your bank vs a specialist FX provider

Your existing US bank can wire any amount internationally. The wire itself is safe, fast (typically 1–3 business days), and widely accepted by notaries, escrow accounts, and foreign banks. The problem, as noted above, is the exchange rate.

Specialist foreign-exchange firms — Wise, XE, Currencies Direct, OFX, TorFX and others — are regulated financial institutions licensed to handle international transfers. They aggregate large volumes of currency and pass the savings on via rates far closer to mid-market. The table below shows a simplified comparison. Forward contracts: lock in your rate before closing

In most international property transactions, there is a gap of weeks or months between signing the purchase agreement and completing the purchase. During that window, exchange rates can move significantly. A forward contract eliminates that uncertainty.

Here is how it works: you agree today with an FX specialist on a fixed exchange rate for a transfer you will make on or before a specified future date (typically up to 12 months out). You deposit a margin — usually 2–10% of the contract value — and the provider holds the rate for you. When closing arrives, you send the balance and the transfer goes at the agreed rate regardless of where the market moved.

On a €300,000 purchase in Portugal, a forward contract executed when the EUR/USD rate is 1.08 guarantees your cost at roughly $324,000. If the euro strengthens to 1.15 before closing, you still pay $324,000 — not $345,000. If the euro weakens, you pay the locked rate and forgo the gain, but you had certainty to plan your finances. For most buyers, the certainty is worth the optionality cost.

Providers that offer forward contracts to US buyers for property purchases include Currencies Direct, OFX, Smart Currency Exchange, and TorFX. Wise does not offer forwards but does allow limit orders (transfer automatically when your target rate is hit). Check that your chosen provider is registered as a Money Services Business with FinCEN before committing.

Step-by-step: how to move a down payment or full purchase price abroad

  1. Get a foreign bank account open early. Most countries require foreign buyers to have a local bank account before closing — Portugal's NIF, Spain's NIE, and Greece's AFM are all prerequisites for opening one. Start this process 4–8 weeks before your target closing date.
  2. Document your source of funds. Gather 3–6 months of bank statements, your most recent brokerage statement, a sale settlement statement if the money came from a prior property sale, or your loan documentation. Foreign notaries, escrow companies, and local banks increasingly require this under anti-money-laundering rules — as does your US bank for outgoing wires over $50,000.
  3. Open an account with a specialist FX provider. Identity verification (passport, utility bill) typically takes 24–48 hours. Do this well before you need to transfer, not the same week as closing.
  4. Consider a forward contract if you've signed a purchase agreement and know your amount and target date. Lock the rate, pay the deposit, and eliminate currency risk.
  5. Initiate the transfer as a funded wire from your US bank account. Transfer from your US bank to your FX specialist's US holding account; the specialist converts and sends the local currency. This keeps the transfer in the bank-wire category, which is exempt from the 2026 remittance excise tax.
  6. Confirm receipt at the destination. Ask your notary or local lawyer to confirm the funds have cleared before the closing date. International wires occasionally experience correspondent-bank delays; build in at least 3 business days of buffer.
  7. File your FBAR. If the funds sat in a foreign account at any point during the calendar year at a balance above $10,000, file FinCEN Form 114 by April 15 of the following year. It's electronic, free, and takes under 30 minutes.

If you're not American: what changes

The mechanics of moving money abroad are the same for any foreign buyer. The destination country's bank-account requirements, anti-money-laundering documentation, and currency logistics apply regardless of your passport. What differs is your home-country tax and reporting wrapper.

Non-US buyers should check three things with their own advisers: (1) Whether your home country requires you to report or declare large outbound transfers — the UK, Canada, and Australia all have anti-money-laundering and beneficial-ownership rules that may require notification to a revenue authority or financial regulator for large cross-border movements. (2) Whether your home country has a foreign-account or foreign-asset reporting regime analogous to FBAR — Canada's T1135, for example, requires disclosure of foreign property (including real estate held for investment) with a cost above CAD $100,000. (3) Whether a double-taxation treaty exists between your home country and the destination country, which may affect how rental income and eventual gains are taxed at home. The 2026 US remittance excise tax on certain cash-funded transfers is a US-specific rule and does not apply to senders who are not US tax residents. Confirm all of this with a tax adviser in your country of residence before you wire.

Is wiring money abroad worth the hassle in 2026?

In 2026, an international wire from a US bank account is a routine transaction — not an exotic or complicated one. The mechanics are mature, the specialist FX industry is well-regulated, and the compliance burden (source-of-funds documentation, an annual FBAR if relevant) is modest relative to the asset you're acquiring.

The real risk is not the wire itself but the decisions around it: using your retail bank's exchange rate and silently losing 3% of the purchase price, failing to lock a forward contract and watching the currency move $20,000 against you in the months before closing, or letting the FBAR deadline slip because you didn't realize the brief balance in a foreign escrow account counted. None of these are catastrophic if caught early — all of them are avoidable with the right adviser and a bit of advance planning.

The new 1% remittance tax, which generated significant anxiety when it was announced, turns out to be largely irrelevant for real-estate buyers who wire from a US bank account. It was designed to tax cash remittances sent by individuals through storefront money-service businesses — not to burden the movement of capital for property investment. Bank wires from deposited accounts are exempt, as confirmed in IRS proposed regulations published in 2026. Keep that fact in your back pocket when advisers or competitors try to make the process sound more complicated than it is.

Frequently asked questions

Is there a legal limit on how much money I can wire abroad to buy property?

No. US law does not cap the dollar amount you can wire internationally. What the law requires is reporting: any single wire of $10,000 or more is automatically filed with FinCEN under the Bank Secrecy Act's Currency Transaction Reporting rules. Your bank handles this — you don't fill out a separate form — but be prepared to document that the funds came from a legitimate source (savings, a prior property sale, a brokerage account, etc.).

Does the new 2026 remittance tax apply when I wire money to buy property abroad?

Almost certainly not — if you send from a US bank account. The 1% excise tax under the One Big Beautiful Bill Act covers wire transfers and other electronic transfers broadly, but provides a blanket exemption for transfers sent through BSA-regulated US financial institutions (US banks, credit unions, investment companies). A wire from your US checking or savings account qualifies for that exemption. Confirm with your FX provider that they route the transfer through a BSA-regulated US institution.

Does wiring money abroad trigger an FBAR filing?

Not automatically, but often it does. The FBAR (FinCEN Form 114) is required if your foreign financial accounts collectively exceeded $10,000 at any point during the calendar year. Once the wire lands in a foreign bank or escrow account held in your name, that balance counts toward the threshold. If it exceeds $10,000 even briefly, you must file for that year. The deadline is April 15, with an automatic extension to October 15, and the form is free to file electronically at BSA E-Filing.

Can my regular bank wire the full purchase price, or should I use a specialist?

Your regular bank can wire any amount. The problem is the exchange rate: most retail banks quote a rate 2–4% worse than mid-market, which on a $300,000 purchase translates to $6,000–$12,000 in hidden cost. Specialist foreign-exchange firms — Wise, XE, Currencies Direct and others — offer rates near mid-market for a flat fee of $10–$30 on most transfers. For amounts above roughly $50,000, the savings almost always justify opening an account with a specialist.

What is a forward contract and should I use one when buying property abroad?

A forward contract lets you lock in today's exchange rate for a transfer you'll make at a future closing date. You pay a deposit of 2–10% of the contract value and the specialist holds the rate for you for up to 12 months. Given that EUR/USD, GBP/USD, and MXN/USD can move 5–10% in the months between signing and closing, a forward contract turns currency risk into a fixed, predictable cost — and is one of the most underused tools available to US property buyers abroad.
