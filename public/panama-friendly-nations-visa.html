What the Friendly Nations Visa is — and what changed in 2021

The Friendly Nations Visa (FNV) is Panama's flagship residency route for citizens of a defined list of countries the government considers friendly and economically aligned. For a decade it was famous as the easiest residency in the Americas: open a Panamanian bank account, register a corporation, show some paper "economic activity," and you were a permanent resident almost immediately. That version is gone.

Under Executive Decree No. 197 of 2021, effective from August 2021, Panama rebuilt the program. The old "bank-account-and-a-corporation" shortcut was replaced with a requirement to show a real economic tie — property, a substantial deposit, or genuine employment — and, crucially, permanent residency stopped being immediate. New applicants now receive a two-year provisional residency and must apply separately to convert it to permanent status once those two years are up. The FNV is still one of the most accessible residency programs anywhere for an American; it is simply no longer a same-day formality.

Do Americans qualify?

Yes. The United States sits on Panama's list of roughly 50 friendly nations, alongside Canada, the United Kingdom, Australia, most of the EU, and a scattering of others including Israel, Japan, Singapore, South Korea, Argentina, Brazil, Chile and Mexico. Being a passport-holder of a listed country is the entry ticket; it does not, on its own, get you residency. You still have to satisfy one of the qualifying routes below, pass a clean criminal-background and health check, and appear in Panama in person to register your biometrics with the National Immigration Service (Servicio Nacional de Migración).

The three qualifying routes

You qualify by demonstrating one of three ties to Panama. Two are investment-based and share the same US$200,000 headline number; the third is employment.

Note a subtlety the marketing glosses over: on the property route, the US$200,000 is the registered value of titled real estate — untitled "Rights of Possession" land will not satisfy the requirement, and financing is allowed, so a US$200,000 apartment bought with a Panamanian mortgage can still qualify. The two-step timeline: provisional to permanent

This is the biggest change from the old program, and the point most outdated blog posts still get wrong. The path now has two distinct stages:

  • Stage 1 — provisional residency (2 years). You file the application with your qualifying route documented. The file is typically adjudicated within about three to five months, and you receive a provisional residency card valid for two years. You must keep the qualifying investment or job in place for the whole period.
  • Stage 2 — permanent residency. Once the two years elapse, you file a separate petition to convert to permanent status. Approved, you receive a permanent residency card and a Panamanian ID (cédula E).

End to end, plan on roughly three years from first filing to a permanent card. After five years of legal permanent residency you may become eligible to apply for naturalization as a Panamanian citizen — a separate, discretionary process involving a Spanish-language and civics assessment, not an automatic step. Physical-presence demands during residency are light: there is no minimum number of days you must spend in Panama to keep the FNV alive, though you should avoid leaving the country for two continuous years, which can be treated as abandonment.

On the property route, remember Panama's own purchase costs — a 2% transfer tax (paid by the seller), buyer closing costs of roughly 2–5%, and 10% capital-gains tax on eventual sale — sit on top of everything here.

The US tax angle most guides get wrong

Panama's territorial tax system is the headline attraction in most FNV marketing: Panama taxes only Panama-source income, so foreign dividends, rent, capital gains and salary are exempt. For a US citizen, that exemption is close to worthless. The United States taxes its citizens on worldwide income no matter where they live, so becoming a Panamanian resident does not remove a single dollar from your US return. Residency in a zero-foreign-tax country can even raise your net US bill, because there is no foreign tax to claim as a credit.

Two traps are specific to the FNV's investment routes, and they are the nuance most guides skip:

  • The deposit route creates an FBAR/Form 8938 filing. A US$200,000 fixed deposit in a Panamanian bank is a foreign financial account. That alone blows past the US$10,000 FBAR threshold (FinCEN Form 114) and, depending on your other foreign assets, the Form 8938 thresholds too. These are information returns, not tax — but the penalties for missing them are severe, and the deposit route quietly signs you up for both.
  • The property route often gets structured through a Panama entity — and that is a US tax event. Applicants are routinely told to hold the qualifying property in a Panamanian sociedad anónima or private-interest foundation. To the IRS a Panama corporation is a "per se" foreign corporation, triggering an annual Form 5471 (minimum US$10,000 penalty for non-filing) and possible CFC/GILTI exposure; a private-interest foundation can be treated as a foreign trust, dragging in Forms 3520 and 3520-A. Holding the same property in your personal name usually avoids all of that — so the structure is a US tax decision to make before you file, not a default your Panamanian lawyer picks for immigration convenience.

None of this makes Panama a bad choice; it makes the structuring a two-country decision. The immigration file and the US tax file have to be designed together.

Step by step: how the application runs

  1. Confirm eligibility and choose a route. Verify you hold a friendly-nation passport (Americans do) and decide between property, deposit or employment — ideally after a US tax advisor has weighed in on structure.
  2. Get the qualifying tie in place. Close on a titled US$200,000+ property (verified in the Public Registry), open and fund the US$200,000 three-year deposit, or sign the Panamanian employment contract and have the employer file your work permit.
  3. Assemble documents. Passport, a police-clearance certificate from your home country, a health certificate, proof of the qualifying tie, and photos — most foreign documents must be apostilled and translated into Spanish.
  4. File through a Panamanian lawyer. An immigration attorney submits the application to the National Immigration Service; power of attorney lets them handle most of it.
  5. Appear in Panama for biometrics. You must be physically present in Panama City to register fingerprints and photos and collect your provisional card.
  6. Hold the tie for two years, then convert. Keep the investment or job in place, then file the separate permanent-residency petition after two years to receive your permanent card and cédula.

If you're not American: what changes

The Panama side is identical for any friendly-nation passport-holder: the same US$200,000 property or deposit thresholds, the same two-year provisional-to-permanent path, the same government fees. Only your home-country tax wrapper differs. Panama's territorial exemption is genuinely valuable to residents of countries that tax on residence rather than citizenship — but only if you actually break tax residency at home.

If you are not a US citizen, first confirm your country is on Panama's friendly-nations list (most of the EU, the UK, Canada, Australia and much of Latin America are). Then swap the US-specific parts above for your own rules and check three things. First, whether your home country still taxes your worldwide income while you hold the residency — many countries stop taxing you only once you cease to be tax-resident there, which usually means genuinely leaving, not just holding a Panama card. Second, whether your home country requires reporting of foreign accounts or assets, so the US$200,000 deposit or the Panama company does not create an undisclosed-account problem back home. Third, whether a double-taxation treaty exists between Panama and your country — Panama has relatively few. Confirm the specifics with a tax adviser in your country of residence before you file.

Is the Friendly Nations Visa worth it in 2026?

For the right person, yes. Even after the 2021 reform, the FNV remains one of the most accessible residency programs an American can access, with a modest fee stack, light physical-presence demands, a credible path to permanent residency and eventually citizenship, and — for those who genuinely relocate — a genuinely low-tax, dollarized home base. The US$200,000 requirement is low by golden-visa standards, and the deposit route lets you get residency without buying real estate at all.

It is a weaker fit if you were hoping the old same-day version still existed, if you cannot commit a real US$200,000 tie for the two-year provisional window, or if you are a US citizen expecting Panama's territorial tax to shrink your IRS bill — it won't. Treat the FNV as what it now is: a genuine, well-priced residency built on a real economic footing in Panama, best executed with the immigration and US tax sides planned together. Frequently asked questions

Do Americans qualify for the Panama Friendly Nations Visa?

Yes. The United States is one of the 50-plus countries on Panama's friendly-nations list, so US citizens qualify. Since the 2021 reform you must show a genuine economic tie to Panama — most commonly a titled property worth at least US$200,000, a US$200,000 three-year fixed deposit in a Panamanian bank, or a real job with a Panamanian employer that has secured a work permit.

How much do I need to invest for the Friendly Nations Visa?

The two investment routes both use a US$200,000 threshold: either a titled Panamanian property valued at US$200,000 or more (local mortgage financing is allowed, and the property can be held personally or through a company or foundation you ultimately own), or a US$200,000 fixed-term deposit placed with a Panamanian bank for at least three years. The employment route requires no investment but does require a genuine Panamanian job and work permit.

How long does the Friendly Nations Visa take to become permanent?

Since the 2021 reform it is a two-step process. You first receive a two-year provisional residency (the file itself is usually resolved in roughly three to five months). You must keep the qualifying investment or job in place, and after the two years you file a separate petition to convert to permanent residency — so plan on about three years from start to a permanent card.

Does the Friendly Nations Visa make me a Panama tax resident?

No. Holding the residency card does not by itself make you a Panamanian tax resident. Tax residency requires either 183 days of physical presence in Panama in a year or establishing your centre of vital interests there. Panama taxes only Panama-source income, so foreign income is exempt — but that exemption does nothing for a US citizen, who is taxed by the IRS on worldwide income regardless of where they live.

Can the Friendly Nations Visa lead to a Panamanian passport?

Potentially. After five years of legal permanent residency you may become eligible to apply for naturalization as a Panamanian citizen, subject to a Spanish-language and civics assessment and government discretion. Naturalization is a separate, discretionary process, not an automatic outcome of the visa, and Panama does not formally recognise dual nationality for naturalized citizens even though enforcement is inconsistent.
