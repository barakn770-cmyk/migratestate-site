What Italy's Investor Visa actually is

Italy's residency-by-investment program is officially called the Visto per Investitori — the Investor Visa for Italy. Outside Italy it is commonly marketed as Italy's "golden visa," though the Italian government does not use that term. The program was launched in 2017 and is administered by the Italian Ministry of Economic Development (MIMIT) in coordination with the Ministry of Interior and the Ministry of Foreign Affairs.

The permit grants a two-year residence permit renewable for additional two-year periods. Family members — spouse and children under 18 (or older dependents who are students) — can be included in the same application. There is no minimum-stay requirement attached to the permit itself, which is one of the program's chief attractions for investors who want the optionality of Italian residency without committing to living there full-time.

After five years of uninterrupted legal residency, the investor may apply for permanent residency (permesso di soggiorno UE per soggiornanti di lungo periodo). After ten years, Italian citizenship becomes possible — though the citizenship pathway carries a far heavier physical-presence requirement than simply holding the visa (more on this below).

Why there is no real estate route — and why this matters

This is the fact that surprises most Americans researching European golden visas: you cannot obtain Italy's Investor Visa by purchasing property. Not at any price. Italy's government deliberately excluded real estate from the qualifying investment categories when it designed the program, in contrast to countries like Greece, Portugal (formerly), Spain (formerly), and Malta, all of which included or still include property routes.

The rationale was to channel foreign capital into the productive Italian economy — startups, companies, and government bonds — rather than into property markets. Whether you buy a €300,000 apartment in Rome or a €5 million villa in Tuscany, that purchase does not count toward a Nulla Osta application. It is entirely legal to buy property in Italy as a foreigner (including as an American — see our full guide to buying property in Italy), but the two transactions are entirely separate: the property purchase gives you an asset; only a qualifying financial investment gives you residency.

The practical implication: Americans looking to move to Italy who also want to buy property there will need to qualify for the visa independently of the property purchase, and will be committing both capital to an investment (for the visa) and capital to real estate (for a home) — two separate expenditures.

The 4 qualifying investment routes

The Investor Visa for Italy currently offers four qualifying categories. All investments must be maintained for the duration of the residence permit; withdrawing or liquidating before the permit expires risks non-renewal.

Route 1 — €250,000: Innovative Italian startup

The lowest entry point is a minimum €250,000 equity investment in a company registered as an startup innovativa under Italian law (Legislative Decree 179/2012). These are early-stage, high-technology Italian companies registered in a special section of the Companies Register. The investor receives an equity stake in the startup; the capital is at risk like any venture investment. This route attracts investors who want the lowest threshold and are comfortable with early-stage startup risk. The startup must be active on the Companies Register at the time of application and at each renewal.

Route 2 — €500,000: Established Italian company

An equity or share capital investment of at least €500,000 in an established Italian limited liability company (SRL or SPA) that is already trading. The company must be incorporated and operating in Italy and cannot be a shell or holding company. This route offers more stability than the startup route — the company already has revenue and a track record — though the capital invested remains subject to the company's business risk. The investor must document the source of funds and prove the investment was made through the Italian banking system.

Route 3 — €1,000,000: Philanthropic donation

A qualifying €1,000,000 donation to an Italian public benefit project. Eligible recipients include public institutions in the arts, culture, education, research, and social welfare — universities, museums, theaters, research institutes, and cultural foundations. The donation must be irrevocable, directed to an entity approved by the relevant ministry, and cannot be structured as a loan or recoupable contribution. This route is the only non-investment path — the capital is genuinely given away — but for wealthy individuals with philanthropic goals or estate-planning motivations, the tax treatment in both Italy and the US warrants careful analysis.

Route 4 — €2,000,000: Italian government bonds

Purchase of at least €2,000,000 in Italian government bonds (titoli di Stato) with a minimum maturity of two years. These are direct purchases of BTPs (Buoni del Tesoro Poliennali) or other Italian sovereign debt instruments through an authorized financial intermediary. The bonds must be held continuously for the permit duration. While this is the highest-threshold option, Italian sovereign bonds carry no company risk and currently offer positive real yields. The investor retains the bond principal at maturity (subject to Italy's creditworthiness).

The Nulla Osta process, step by step

Italy's Investor Visa uses a two-stage process. The first stage — obtaining the Nulla Osta (literally "nothing stands in the way") — is done entirely online before you visit a consulate. Only after the Nulla Osta is in hand can you apply for the physical visa.

  1. Create an account on the official Investor Visa portal (investoritalia.mise.gov.it). This is the government's single-entry point for all applications.
  2. Submit your application and supporting documents online: proof of investment (term sheets, subscription agreements, or bond purchase documentation depending on your route), source of funds documentation, clean criminal record certificate, passport copies, and a personal statement. Documents in English require certified Italian translation.
  3. Preliminary review by the Secretariat — an administrative check for completeness, typically completed within 30 days of submission. If documents are missing, the clock pauses until you supply them.
  4. Review by the Evaluating Committee — a multi-ministry committee that assesses the substance of your investment. This stage can add 30–80 additional days. Total Nulla Osta issuance in 2026 typically runs 30 to 110 days from submission, depending on the route (startup investments tend to require more scrutiny than bond purchases).
  5. Apply for the Investor Visa at the Italian consulate in your jurisdiction within 6 months of Nulla Osta issuance. Americans apply at the Italian consulate responsible for their state of residence. Appointments at major US consulates (New York, Los Angeles, Chicago) fill up — build 4–8 weeks of consulate scheduling time into your plan.
  6. Enter Italy on the visa and within 8 days of arrival register your residency (dichiarazione di residenza) at the local municipality and apply for the residence permit (permesso di soggiorno) at the police headquarters (Questura).
  7. Complete the investment within the deadline specified in your Nulla Osta (typically 3 months of visa issuance for financial investments). Submit proof of completed investment to the Secretariat.

Total timeline from initial application to holding a residence permit in Italy: four to six months under current processing times, assuming no document issues or consulate backlogs.

Minimum stay and the citizenship clock

One of the most frequently misunderstood aspects of Italy's Investor Visa is the relationship between the visa's stay requirements and the citizenship pathway.

For the visa and permit renewals: no minimum number of days per year is required. You can hold the permit and live primarily in the United States, visiting Italy occasionally. Technically, the permit can be renewed as long as the investment is maintained, regardless of how many days you actually spent in Italy.

However, for permanent residency after five years, Italian law requires genuine residence — you generally need to spend more than half the year (183+ days) in Italy to qualify. Absences of more than six consecutive months can break the residency continuity needed for the long-term residency permit.

For Italian citizenship after ten years, the requirements are even more demanding. Citizenship requires actual, uninterrupted residency. Italian naturalization law is interpreted strictly — most immigration lawyers advise planning for roughly 270 days per year in Italy during the naturalization period. Extended trips abroad can reset the ten-year clock or result in refusal. The visa itself does not grant any acceleration of the naturalization timeline; time spent legally resident in Italy is what counts, and the Questura has discretion to examine actual presence records.

The practical conclusion: Italy's Investor Visa is excellent for investors who want optionality — an EU residency card, Schengen travel rights, and the ability to move to Italy if desired — without committing to living there. For those whose actual goal is Italian citizenship, it is a valid but demanding pathway requiring genuine relocation.

Italy's €300K flat tax for new residents — the 2026 change explained

Italy offers one of the most publicized tax incentives for wealthy new residents: the regime forfettario per i nuovi residenti (non-domiciled resident regime), codified in Article 24-bis of Italy's Tax Consolidation Act (TUIR). Under this regime, a new Italian tax resident pays a fixed annual lump sum in lieu of ordinary Italian income tax on all foreign-source income — regardless of how much that foreign income actually is.

This was originally set at €100,000 per year when introduced in 2017. For qualifying family members (spouse, children), an additional €25,000 per person applied. The regime attracted significant interest from high-net-worth individuals, particularly those with large investment portfolios or business income outside Italy.

As of 1 January 2026, the annual lump sum increased to €300,000 for individuals who transfer their Italian tax residency after that date — up from the €200,000 rate that had applied to new arrivals since August 2024 (raised from the original €100,000 by Decree-Law 113/2024). The family member surcharge simultaneously rose to €50,000 per person. This €300,000 figure was set by Italy's 2026 budget law (Legge di Bilancio 2026) and applies prospectively — the regime is grandfathered, so those who validly opted in before August 2024 keep the original €100,000 rate, and those who entered between August 2024 and the end of 2025 keep the €200,000 rate, each for the remainder of their 15-year enrollment. There is no retroactive clawback.

The regime requires that the individual was not an Italian tax resident in at least 9 of the 10 tax years immediately preceding the election. Enrollment lasts a maximum of 15 years. Italian-source income (e.g., salary from an Italian employer, Italian rental income, Italian dividends) is taxed normally under ordinary IRPEF rates — the flat tax covers only foreign-source income.

For a high earner with €1 million or more of foreign income per year, paying €300,000 instead of ordinary Italian rates (up to 43% plus regional surcharges) can still represent meaningful savings. For someone with €400,000–€600,000 of foreign income, the math is now significantly less compelling than it was pre-2026. Anyone considering Italy primarily for the flat tax regime should model their specific income structure carefully against the new €300,000 cost.

US tax rules for Americans in Italy

You remain a US taxpayer regardless

The United States taxes its citizens on worldwide income for life. Italian residency — including the Investor Visa — does not reduce, defer, or eliminate your US tax obligations. You will file a US Form 1040 every year while you hold an Italian residence permit, and if you become an Italian tax resident (by spending 183+ days there), you will also file Italian taxes. Two countries, two filings, potentially two tax bills.

The critical problem: Italy's flat tax is almost certainly not US-creditable

Here is the nuance that almost every Italy relocation guide skips entirely — and it is the most important planning consideration for American investors.

The US foreign tax credit (IRC §901) allows Americans to offset their US tax liability with income taxes paid to foreign governments. To qualify, a foreign tax must be an income tax (or "in lieu of" an income tax) computed on net income. Italy's flat tax fails this test: it is a fixed annual fee — €300,000 regardless of whether your foreign income is €500,000 or €50 million — not a levy computed on any measure of actual income. The IRS's longstanding position is that lump-sum substitute taxes that are not computed on net gain do not qualify for the foreign tax credit.

The result: a US citizen who pays €300,000 to Italy as a flat tax cannot credit that payment against their US federal income tax. Their worldwide income remains fully subject to US ordinary income rates and capital gains rates. You are not being taxed twice on the same dollar in a technical sense — the Italian flat tax covers foreign income that Italy wouldn't otherwise tax at all — but the economic outcome is that you are paying both a large Italian lump sum and full US taxes. For most Americans, this means the Italian flat tax regime provides little or no economic benefit over not electing it, and may represent a significant pure cost.

Americans who become ordinary Italian tax residents without electing the flat tax can generally use the US-Italy tax treaty and the foreign tax credit to avoid true double taxation on income Italy does tax under its normal IRPEF scale — but this requires careful structuring.

FBAR and Form 8938

Opening an Italian bank account — which you will need to do to complete the investment and handle daily expenses — will almost certainly trigger FBAR (FinCEN 114) once the aggregate of your foreign accounts exceeds $10,000 at any point in the calendar year. The FBAR is filed annually with FinCEN, not with the IRS, and the deadline is October 15 with an automatic extension. Penalties for willful failure to file can reach the greater of $165,353 (2026, inflation-adjusted) or 50% of the account balance per violation, though the Supreme Court's 2023 Bittner decision confirmed that non-willful penalties are assessed per report (not per account), capping unintentional failures at $16,536 per year (2026, inflation-adjusted).

Form 8938 (FATCA) may also apply if your foreign financial assets exceed $200,000 at year-end (single filer living abroad) or $400,000 married filing jointly. Importantly, a directly-held Italian property — in your own name — is not a foreign financial account and is not reportable on either FBAR or Form 8938. Only financial accounts (bank, brokerage, investment) trigger these filings.

The startup or company investment itself (equity in an Italian company) is a foreign financial asset and may require Form 8938 reporting, though it is generally not a "financial account" subject to FBAR unless held through a custodial arrangement. A cross-border CPA or tax attorney should review the specific structure of your investment before you file.

If you're not American: what changes

The investment requirements, Nulla Osta process, and residence permit rules above apply equally to any non-EU national regardless of passport. Italy's Investor Visa is not limited to Americans — citizens of any country outside the EU and Schengen area can apply under identical rules. The local Italian elements (qualifying investments, permit conditions, citizenship timeline) are the same for everyone.

What differs is your home-country tax wrapper. Non-US readers should check four things with an adviser in their country of residence: (1) whether your home country taxes foreign-source income earned while you are an Italian tax resident — many do, and Italy's flat tax does not eliminate home-country obligations; (2) whether your home country requires reporting of foreign financial accounts or assets (equivalents of FBAR/Form 8938 exist in the UK, Canada, Australia, and elsewhere); (3) whether a double-taxation treaty exists between your home country and Italy, and whether Italy's flat tax is creditable under that treaty's terms (it often is not, for the same structural reasons it fails for US citizens); (4) whether becoming an Italian tax resident triggers an exit tax or deemed-disposal event in your home country. Italy's flat tax is structurally unusual and its interaction with other countries' tax systems requires jurisdiction-specific advice, not just an Italian tax opinion.

Is it worth it in 2026?

Italy's Investor Visa is a well-designed, operationally efficient program that delivers on its core promise: a clean, fast EU residence permit with no minimum stay requirement, in exchange for a genuine investment in the Italian economy. The four-to-six-month processing time is genuinely competitive — faster than most European golden visa programs right now, where Greece and Portugal each carry 12+ month backlogs.

The 2026 calculus changed, however, on two fronts. First, Italy's flat tax increased from €100,000 to €300,000 for new entrants — a triple increase that fundamentally alters the attractiveness of Italy as a tax home for high-earners who were motivated primarily by the tax regime. At €300,000, only individuals with very high foreign income (roughly €1 million+ per year) will find the Italian flat tax meaningfully cheaper than their alternative, and for Americans, the non-creditability issue makes the flat tax an even harder sell. Second, the absence of a real estate route means investors must accept equity risk in Italian companies or startups, or tie up €2 million in sovereign bonds — neither of which generates the tangible asset and lifestyle value that a property purchase does.

Where Italy shines: for investors who genuinely want to live in or frequently visit Italy, who have a compelling startup or company investment to make regardless of visa motivations, or who value a fast, transparent process with no physical-presence obligation on the visa itself, the Investor Visa is excellent. Italy is also a uniquely compelling lifestyle destination — its healthcare system, cultural infrastructure, and quality of life are world-class. Investors who would relocate to Italy for life-quality reasons independent of the tax math will find the visa process the easiest part of the equation.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I get Italy's Golden Visa by buying property?

No. Italy's Investor Visa has no real estate investment route. The four qualifying options are: €250,000 in an innovative Italian startup, €500,000 in an established Italian company, €1,000,000 in a philanthropic donation to a qualifying Italian cultural or scientific institution, or €2,000,000 in Italian government bonds. Purchasing an apartment or villa — at any price — does not qualify for the Investor Visa.

How long does Italy's Investor Visa (Nulla Osta) take in 2026?

The Nulla Osta currently takes 30 to 110 days from online submission, depending on the complexity of your application and the investment route. After receiving the Nulla Osta, you have 6 months to apply for the visa at the Italian consulate in your country of residence. Total process from application to holding a residence permit in Italy typically runs 4 to 6 months.

Do I have to live in Italy to keep the Investor Visa?

There is no minimum stay requirement to maintain the Investor Visa residence permit itself. However, if your goal is Italian permanent residency (5 years) or citizenship (10 years), you will generally need meaningful physical presence — around 270 days per year — to satisfy those thresholds. Extended absences can interrupt the residency clock for the citizenship pathway.

What is Italy's flat tax and how much is it in 2026?

Italy's non-domiciled resident flat tax allows new tax residents to pay a single annual lump sum covering all foreign-source income. For individuals establishing Italian tax residency from 1 January 2026 onward, the lump sum is €300,000 per year — up from €200,000 (the rate for new arrivals since August 2024) and €100,000 originally. Each qualifying family member adds €50,000. The regime is grandfathered: those who opted in before August 2024 keep €100,000 and those who entered between August 2024 and the end of 2025 keep €200,000, with no retroactive increase.

Is Italy's flat tax creditable against US income tax?

Almost certainly not. The US foreign tax credit requires that the foreign tax be computed on net income. Italy's flat tax is a fixed annual fee unrelated to the amount of income earned, meaning the IRS is very likely to deny it as creditable. US citizens paying €300,000 to Italy as a flat tax cannot subtract that amount from their US tax bill, resulting in potential double taxation on foreign income. Obtain a formal US tax opinion before relocating.
