Buying Property in Uruguay as an American (2026)

MS By The MigrateState Editorial Team · Reviewed by a licensed international real-estate attorney  ·  Updated July 2026  ·  11 min read
Foreigners buy Uruguayan property on the same terms as citizens — full freehold title, no residency required. Plan on about 9–11% in closing costs, a 2% ITP transfer tax on the (below-market) cadastral value, a modest annual property tax, and a flat 12% capital-gains tax on the real gain when you sell. The trap for Americans isn't the local tax — it's assuming Uruguay's famous "tax holiday" shelters you. It doesn't: the US still taxes your worldwide income, and there is no US–Uruguay tax treaty.

Uruguay has spent decades cultivating a reputation as the quiet, stable corner of South America — the place Americans and Argentines alike park capital when they want the rule of law, a real land registry, and no drama. For property buyers that reputation is largely earned. There are no nationality restrictions on ownership, transactions are customarily denominated and settled in US dollars, and the whole conveyance runs through a notary system that is genuinely protective rather than a rubber stamp. Punta del Este, Montevideo's Pocitos and Carrasco neighborhoods, and the José Ignacio coast draw the bulk of foreign money.

What trips people up is not the buying process — it's the tax story around it. Uruguay is marketed relentlessly as a "tax haven" with an 11-year holiday on foreign income, and that message has now collided with a 2026 law change that quietly quadrupled the price of entry. This guide walks the whole transaction with current numbers, and then does the thing the glossy sites won't: it explains exactly why the tax holiday is far less useful to an American than to almost anyone else.

Can Americans own property in Uruguay?

Yes — with about as few strings as anywhere in the world. Uruguay grants foreign nationals the same property rights as its own citizens. You hold full freehold title, recorded in the national real-estate registry (Registro de la Propiedad), with the unrestricted right to sell, lease, mortgage, gift, or bequeath the property. There is no foreign-buyer surcharge, no minimum-investment floor for an ordinary purchase, no approval board, and no requirement to be a resident or hold any visa. You do not even need to set foot in the country — a purchase can be completed by power of attorney.

The one genuine restriction sits far from a normal buyer: certain large rural (agricultural) landholdings may not be held anonymously through bearer-share companies under Law 18.092, a rule aimed at farmland transparency. It has no bearing on buying an apartment in Montevideo or a house in Punta del Este. For urban and residential property, the door is simply open.

The escribano: Uruguay's built-in safeguard

This is where Uruguay differs sharply from most of Latin America, and it works in the buyer's favor. Every property transfer must be executed by an escribano público — a state-licensed notary who is also a qualified attorney. The escribano is not a passive witness. They conduct the title search, obtain the certificates that prove the seller's ownership and that the property is free of liens, mortgages, and unpaid taxes, draft the public deed (escritura), oversee the funds, register the transfer, and — critically — act as the government's tax withholding agent on the transaction.

Because the escribano's professional and civil liability is on the line, the Uruguayan title search is thorough by default in a way it is not in, say, the Dominican Republic. That said, the escribano is chosen by — and works for — the party who engages them, and in a typical deal the buyer selects and pays their own. Do not simply accept the seller's escribano. Engaging your own independent escribano (and, for a foreign buyer, one who is comfortable working in English and with cross-border clients) is the single most important decision in the process. Their job is to guarantee you a clean escritura; make sure they answer to you.

Closing costs and the buying process

Uruguayan closing costs are on the higher side for the region but entirely predictable. All-in, budget roughly 9–11% of the purchase price on the buyer's side. The components:

CostTypical rate (2026)Notes
ITP transfer tax2%Charged on the cadastral value, which is well below market price; seller pays a matching 2%
Escribano (notary) fee~3% + VATIncludes title search, deed, registration; VAT (IVA) is 22%
Agent commission~3% + VATBuyer's side; often split with the seller's side
Registry & certificates0.3–1%Registration and legal certificates

A subtle but important point: the 2% ITP is levied on the property's cadastral value (valor catastral), not the price you actually pay. Because cadastral values in Uruguay typically sit 30–40% below true market value, the effective transfer-tax cost is often closer to 1.2–1.4% of what you hand over — modest by international standards. The bulk of your closing costs is really the escribano and agent fees plus VAT.

The mechanics of a purchase are orderly. After agreeing terms you sign a reservation or boleto de reserva and place a deposit (commonly 10%), the escribano runs the title and certificate checks over the following weeks, and you complete at the signing of the escritura, when the balance is paid — almost always in US dollars — and the transfer is registered. Uruguay does not maintain foreign-exchange controls, so moving purchase funds in and out is straightforward.

Transfer tax, property tax, capital gains and rental tax

ITP (transfer tax). A one-time 2% on the cadastral value at purchase, with the seller paying an equal 2%. Withheld and paid over by the escribano.

Annual property tax. Two modest recurring taxes. The Contribución Inmobiliaria is a municipal (departmental) tax on the cadastral value — rates vary by department but are generally in the low fractions of a percent, so a mid-priced home carries a small annual bill. On top sits the Impuesto de Primaria, a national school tax, also based on cadastral value and small in absolute terms. Higher-value properties may also fall within the net-wealth tax (Impuesto al Patrimonio) above the annual exemption threshold, which is worth checking on a luxury purchase.

Capital gains. When you sell, Uruguay charges a flat 12% on the real gain — the difference between the sale price and your acquisition cost, with the cost basis adjusted for inflation/indexation and improvements. For a non-resident seller this runs through the non-resident income tax (IRNR) at the same 12% rate, again withheld by the escribano at closing. Because the gain is computed on an indexed basis and Uruguayan property is priced in dollars, the taxable figure is often smaller than a naïve sale-minus-purchase calculation suggests.

Rental income. Rental income from Uruguayan property is taxable in Uruguay. Non-residents are generally taxed under IRNR at 10.5–12% on the rent (with limited deductions), and there are withholding mechanisms for tenants and agents in some cases. Rates and the availability of deductions depend on how you hold and let the property, so price this in before you assume a headline yield.

The 2026 tax-holiday change most guides miss

Here is the fact that most listing sites are still getting wrong. Uruguay offers new tax residents a "tax holiday" — historically an exemption on foreign-source capital income (dividends, interest, and, from 2026, a broader set of foreign capital returns and gains) for the year of arrival plus the following ten years, or, alternatively, a permanently reduced flat rate. To trigger it through real estate rather than through days of physical presence, you had to invest a set amount in Uruguayan property.

On January 1, 2026, Law 20.446 raised that real-estate threshold dramatically — to roughly US$2 million (12.5 million Indexed Units), combined with at least 60 days a year of physical presence in Uruguay. The prior threshold was about US$590,000 (3.5 million Indexed Units). In one stroke the property route to the tax holiday became roughly three-and-a-half times more expensive. Buyers relying on a pre-2026 blog post are budgeting for a benefit whose price of entry has since quadrupled. (There remains a lower-cost alternative — a roughly US$100,000-per-year contribution to a national innovation fund — but that is not a property purchase.)

Just as important: this tax-residency threshold is a completely separate thing from immigration residency. You do not need to spend US$2 million, or buy anything at all, to get the right to live in Uruguay. Conflating the two is the most common and most expensive mistake foreign buyers make here.

Residency and citizenship

Uruguay's legal (immigration) residency is famously accessible and has no minimum property-investment requirement. The core route — often called the rentista or independent-means path — asks you to demonstrate stable, lawful, verifiable income of roughly US$1,500 per month (adjusted for family size), from pensions, dividends, rentals, or similar sources, plus a clean criminal record. Uruguay grants permanent residency relatively directly compared with countries that make you climb a ladder of temporary permits.

Owning a home strengthens a residency application as evidence of ties and a place to live, but it is not itself the qualifying criterion — the income test is. Citizenship (legal naturalization) becomes available after three years of residence if you are married or five years if single, with a Spanish-language and integration expectation. Uruguay permits dual nationality, so US citizens keep their American passport. The Uruguayan passport is strong, with broad visa-free access including the Schengen Area.

The US tax angle

Owning Uruguayan property does not, by itself, create a US filing event — but living off it, renting it, or selling it does, and this is where Americans need to be careful.

Bank accounts and reporting. If you open a Uruguayan bank account to manage the property and its aggregate balance tops US$10,000 at any point in the year, you must file an FBAR (FinCEN Form 114). Directly held real estate itself is not an FBAR or Form 8938 asset, but the associated bank and financial accounts often are, and higher-net-worth owners can cross the Form 8938 (FATCA) thresholds.

Rental income. Uruguayan rent is US-taxable too. You report it on Schedule E, and the building must be depreciated over the 30-year ADS period that applies to foreign residential rental property (versus 27.5 years domestically). The 10.5–12% Uruguayan tax you pay generally supports a foreign tax credit against the US tax on the same income.

Sale. On a sale, the US taxes your capital gain regardless of the Uruguayan 12% withholding, and you claim a foreign tax credit for the Uruguayan tax. If the home was your primary residence and you meet the use tests, the Section 121 exclusion ($250,000 single / $500,000 married) can still apply abroad. One point in Americans' favor here: because Uruguayan deals are typically priced and settled in US dollars, the Section 988 foreign-currency-gain problem that bites buyers who take out a peso or euro mortgage largely does not arise.

The nuance nobody advertises: the United States taxes its citizens on worldwide income no matter where they live, and there is no US–Uruguay income tax treaty and no totalization agreement. That combination means Uruguay's celebrated tax holiday — which exempts foreign income at the Uruguayan level — does essentially nothing for an American, who still owes full US tax on that same income and has no treaty to resolve double-taxation conflicts, only the ordinary foreign tax credit and FEIE mechanics. For a US citizen, the tax holiday is a benefit measured against a tax bill you don't have, while the tax bill you do have keeps running.

Step-by-step: how to buy safely

  1. Engage your own escribano first. Choose an independent, ideally English-speaking notary who represents you — not the seller's — before you sign anything.
  2. Agree terms and sign the boleto de reserva. Place the reservation deposit (commonly 10%) into a secure arrangement your escribano controls.
  3. Let the escribano run title and certificates. They confirm ownership, and that the property is free of liens, mortgages, and unpaid Contribución Inmobiliaria or Primaria.
  4. Budget 9–11% in closing costs and get the ITP, escribano, agent, and registry figures in writing.
  5. Complete at the escritura. Pay the balance (in USD), the escribano withholds the taxes, and the transfer is registered in your name.
  6. Decide residency vs. tax residency deliberately. If you want to live in Uruguay, use the income-based route; only pursue the US$2M tax-holiday route with tax-lawyer modeling — and, if American, with clear eyes about its limited US benefit.
  7. Line up US compliance. FBAR, Form 8938 if applicable, Schedule E for rentals, and a cross-border advisor for the sale.

If you're not American: what changes

The Uruguayan rules in this guide — full freehold ownership for foreigners, the 2% ITP on cadastral value, ~9–11% closing costs, the modest annual property taxes, the flat 12% capital-gains tax, and the residency and tax-holiday thresholds — are set by Uruguay and apply to every foreign buyer equally, regardless of passport. A Canadian, British, German, or Israeli buyer pays the same ITP and faces the same escribano process as an American. What differs is only the tax wrapper back home.

The entire "US tax angle" section above — FBAR, Form 8938, Schedule E, the 30-year ADS depreciation, Section 121, Section 988 — is US-specific and does not apply to non-Americans. Crucially, the tax-holiday point cuts the other way for you: because most countries tax on residence rather than citizenship, a non-American who genuinely relocates and becomes a Uruguayan tax resident may actually capture the foreign-income exemption that is wasted on an American. If you're not a US person, swap the US rules for your own and check three things with a tax adviser in your country of residence: (1) whether your home country taxes foreign rental income and capital gains; (2) whether you must report foreign assets or accounts at home; and (3) whether your country has a double-taxation treaty with Uruguay — many do, and it can change the answer entirely.

Is it worth it in 2026?

For a buyer who wants a stable, dollar-denominated, rule-of-law base in South America — with a protective notary system, no foreign-buyer penalties, easy income-based residency, and a genuinely strong passport at the end of the road — Uruguay is one of the most reassuring places to own property anywhere in the hemisphere. Closing costs are a touch high, but the process is clean and the title system is trustworthy.

The mistakes are almost always tax mistakes, not property mistakes. Buyers over-pay for the "tax haven" story: they assume the holiday shelters income it can't (if they're American), or they budget for a pre-2026 investment threshold that Law 20.446 has since raised to about US$2 million. Engage your own escribano, separate immigration residency from tax residency in your planning, and — if you hold a US passport — talk to a US cross-border advisor before you let any Uruguayan tax-holiday pitch drive the decision. Do that, and Uruguay's stability is exactly the asset it's advertised to be.

Frequently asked questions

Can Americans own property in Uruguay?

Yes. Uruguay places no nationality restrictions, quotas, or approval requirements on foreigners buying residential or urban property, and no residency or visa is required. Americans take full freehold title with the same rights as citizens. The only meaningful restriction affects large rural farmland held through anonymous company structures — irrelevant to a normal home purchase.

How much are closing costs when buying property in Uruguay?

Roughly 9–11% all-in: the 2% ITP transfer tax (on the below-market cadastral value), escribano fees around 3% plus VAT, agent commission around 3% plus VAT, and small registry and certificate costs. There is no foreign-buyer surcharge.

What taxes do you pay on Uruguayan property?

A one-time 2% ITP at purchase (seller pays a matching 2%), a modest annual Contribución Inmobiliaria plus the small Impuesto de Primaria school tax, and a flat 12% capital-gains tax on the indexed real gain when you sell. Rental income is taxed at roughly 10.5–12% for non-residents.

Does buying property give you residency or a tax holiday?

Legal residency requires no property purchase — just proof of about US$1,500/month in income. The separate tax-holiday route does have a property threshold, and under Law 20.446 it rose to about US$2 million (plus 60 days a year in Uruguay) on January 1, 2026, up from roughly US$590,000.

Does Uruguay's tax holiday help American buyers?

Not much. The US taxes citizens on worldwide income regardless of residence, and there is no US–Uruguay tax treaty or totalization agreement, so an American Uruguayan tax resident still pays US tax on the same foreign income — with only the foreign tax credit or FEIE to soften it. The Uruguayan exemption doesn't erase the US bill.
