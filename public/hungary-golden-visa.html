Hungary Golden Visa (Guest Investor Program) 2026: Requirements, Routes & US Tax Guide

MS By The MigrateState Editorial Team · Reviewed by a licensed international real-estate attorney  ·  Updated July 2026  ·  10 min read
Hungary's Guest Investor Program grants a 10-year EU residence permit for a minimum €250,000 investment in a regulated real-estate fund — not for buying property directly. The €500,000 direct-purchase route was abolished on 15 January 2025 before it ever opened. Only two routes remain: the €250,000 fund (held at least five years) or a €1,000,000 university donation. There is no minimum-stay requirement, and citizenship is possible after eight years. For Americans, the fund is almost certainly a PFIC — a serious US tax complication.

What the Guest Investor Program actually is

Hungary's residency-by-investment program is officially the Guest Investor Residence Permit (in Hungarian, vendégbefektetői tartózkodási engedély), created under Act XC of 2023 and launched in July 2024. Outside Hungary it is marketed as the "Hungary Golden Visa," though the government does not use that term. It replaced the older residency-bond program that Hungary ran until 2017.

The headline attraction is the permit's length. Unlike most European golden visas, which issue one- or two-year cards, Hungary's Guest Investor Residence Permit is granted for up to 10 years and can be renewed once for a further 10 years — a 20-year horizon on a single qualifying investment. The main applicant can include a spouse, minor children, and financially dependent parents in the same application.

Crucially, Hungary imposes no strict minimum-stay requirement to keep the permit. You must observe address-registration formalities, but you are not obliged to relocate to Hungary or spend a set number of days there each year to maintain your status. For US investors who want an EU residence card and Schengen travel rights without uprooting their lives, that optionality is the program's core selling point.

Why there is no direct property route anymore — and why it matters

This is the fact that trips up most Americans researching Hungary: you cannot obtain the Guest Investor Visa by buying a house or apartment. When the program was first legislated, a €500,000 direct residential real-estate purchase was meant to be one of the routes, scheduled to open on 1 January 2025. It was first postponed, and then on 15 January 2025 the government abolished the direct-property option entirely — fewer than six months after the program launched and before a single applicant could use it. The stated reason was pressure on Hungary's housing market, particularly in Budapest.

What remains is indirect exposure to real estate through a regulated fund, plus a donation alternative. So while "Hungary Golden Visa" and "real estate" still appear together in marketing, the only property you get through the program is a slice of a pooled fund — not a deed in your own name. If you want to actually own a Budapest apartment, you buy it as a separate transaction (covered below), and it earns you no immigration benefit.

The two qualifying investment routes

As of 2026 the Guest Investor Program has exactly two qualifying investment categories.

Route 1 — €250,000: Regulated real-estate fund

The main route is a minimum €250,000 investment in units of a real-estate fund that is registered with, and supervised by, the Hungarian National Bank (MNB). To qualify, the fund must hold at least 40% of its assets in Hungarian residential real estate. The units must be held for a minimum of five years, and the funds must come from a demonstrably lawful source transferred through traceable banking channels. Only a small number of accredited funds currently qualify — as of 2026 these include the Gravitas Hungary Real Estate Fund and the SPRINT Hungary Real Estate Development and Investment Fund. This is the route the overwhelming majority of applicants use.

Route 2 — €1,000,000: University donation

The alternative is a €1,000,000 donation to a Hungarian higher-education institution, or a public-interest trust maintaining one, for purposes such as education, scientific research, or artistic activity. The donation is irrevocable and non-refundable — the capital is genuinely given away — which is why it attracts far fewer applicants than the fund route despite the simpler compliance profile. For most investors, tying up €250,000 in a fund you eventually redeem is preferable to donating €1,000,000 outright.

The application process, step by step

Hungary's process runs partly through a consulate abroad and partly inside Hungary. The typical flow:

  1. Apply for a Guest Investor Visa (type "D") at a Hungarian consulate or embassy in your country of residence. This entry visa lets you travel to Hungary specifically to make the investment and collect the residence permit. Americans apply at the Hungarian consulate covering their state.
  2. Enter Hungary and complete the qualifying investment — subscribe for the fund units (€250,000+) or execute the donation — within the window specified when your entry visa is issued (generally within three months).
  3. Submit source-of-funds documentation, proof of investment, a clean criminal-record certificate, valid health insurance, proof of accommodation, and passport copies. Non-Hungarian documents generally require certified translation.
  4. Biometrics and background checks. Hungarian authorities run security and due-diligence screening. Applications with clean documentation are typically decided within a few weeks of the file being complete.
  5. Collect the Guest Investor Residence Permit, valid for up to 10 years. Register your Hungarian address as required. Total elapsed time from first application to permit in hand generally runs around four to six months, depending on consulate scheduling and document readiness.
  6. Maintain the investment for at least five years (fund route). Redeeming the units early can jeopardize renewal.

Minimum stay, permanent residency & citizenship

The relationship between the permit and the longer-term status options is widely misunderstood, so it is worth separating clearly.

For the permit itself: there is no minimum number of days per year you must spend in Hungary. You can hold the Guest Investor Residence Permit while living primarily in the United States, using it for Schengen travel and as an EU foothold. As long as the investment is maintained and address-registration rules are met, the permit can run its 10-year term and be renewed once.

For permanent residency and citizenship, the calculus is entirely different. Hungarian naturalization generally requires eight years of continuous residence, passing a Hungarian-language and basic constitutional-knowledge exam, a clean record, and demonstrated integration. "Continuous residence" here means genuine presence — not merely holding a card. Long absences of the kind the Guest Investor permit permits will not, on their own, build the residency record naturalization demands. In practice, the citizenship pathway requires you to actually live in Hungary, learn a notoriously difficult language, and put down roots. Hungary does not permit dual citizens to be stripped of status for holding another passport, so US citizens who do naturalize can generally keep their US citizenship — but the US exit-tax and worldwide-tax consequences of that decision are a separate matter to plan for.

The honest framing: treat the Hungary Golden Visa as a long-dated residency and mobility instrument, not a fast passport. If a second EU citizenship is your real objective, compare it against programs with shorter, presence-light naturalization tracks before committing.

Buying property in Hungary separately

Because the Guest Investor Program no longer includes a direct-purchase route, buying a Hungarian home is now a standalone decision. It is entirely legal for foreigners to own Hungarian property, but the mechanics differ from Western Europe.

Acquisition permit. Non-EU nationals — including Americans — generally must obtain a property acquisition permit from the competent government office in the district where the property is located before completing a purchase. This is an administrative approval, usually granted within a few weeks, but it must be factored into the timeline.

Transfer tax. Buyers pay a 4% property transfer duty on the purchase price, rising to 2% extra on the portion above 1 billion HUF (roughly €2.6 million). Legal, notary, and administrative fees typically add another 1–5%. New-build properties can carry VAT of up to 27% (a reduced 5% rate has applied to certain new residential sales), so confirm the VAT treatment of any specific new unit before signing.

None of this earns immigration benefit — but if you want a Budapest base to actually live in while your fund investment satisfies the visa, the two are complementary purchases. See our broader guidance on closing costs when buying property abroad and transferring large sums for a foreign purchase.

US tax rules for Americans — the PFIC trap

You remain a US taxpayer regardless

The United States taxes its citizens on worldwide income for life. A Hungarian residence permit does not reduce, defer, or eliminate your US filing obligations. You will file a US Form 1040 every year you hold the permit, and if you become a Hungarian tax resident, you may file there too. Hungary's flat 15% personal income tax is relatively low, and the US–Hungary picture is unusual because the bilateral income-tax treaty was terminated by the United States effective 1 January 2024 — so the old treaty protections (including reduced withholding and certain double-tax relief) no longer apply. Americans must now rely on the domestic foreign tax credit alone, which makes careful planning more important, not less.

The critical problem: the fund is almost certainly a PFIC

Here is the nuance nearly every Hungary golden-visa guide omits, and it is the most important consideration for American investors. The €250,000 route requires you to buy units in a pooled Hungarian real-estate fund. A foreign pooled investment fund is the textbook example of a Passive Foreign Investment Company (PFIC) under IRC §1297: it is a foreign corporation the bulk of whose income or assets are passive. US ownership of PFIC shares triggers Form 8621 filing every year and, unless you make a timely election, the punitive default "excess distribution" regime — which taxes gains and certain distributions at the highest ordinary income rate in effect for the year, spread back across your holding period, plus a compounding interest charge. The preferential long-term capital-gains rate does not apply.

There are two escape hatches, both with catches. A Qualified Electing Fund (QEF) election gives near-normal US treatment, but only works if the Hungarian fund will provide the annual PFIC information statement US law requires — many foreign funds simply do not. A mark-to-market election is available only if the units are "marketable," which closed-end real-estate funds often are not. The practical result: an American who subscribes to one of these funds without advance planning can face a US tax bill on redemption that is dramatically worse than the headline 15% Hungarian rate suggests. Get a PFIC analysis before you wire the €250,000, and confirm in writing whether the fund will issue QEF statements.

FBAR and Form 8938

You will need a Hungarian bank account to fund the investment and daily life. Once your foreign accounts exceed $10,000 in aggregate at any point in the year, you must file an FBAR (FinCEN Form 114). The fund units themselves are a foreign financial asset and are generally reportable on Form 8938 (FATCA) once you exceed the applicable threshold — $200,000 at year-end for a single filer living abroad, $400,000 married filing jointly. Note the contrast with directly-held real estate: a Hungarian apartment held in your own name is not an FBAR or Form 8938 asset, whereas fund units are. So the very structure that qualifies you for the visa is also the one that maximizes your US reporting footprint. The Supreme Court's 2023 Bittner decision confirmed non-willful FBAR penalties are assessed per report (capped around $10,000 per year, inflation-adjusted), not per account — but willful penalties remain severe.

Hungary's fund route plus US PFIC rules is a genuinely complex mix.

If you're not American: what changes

The Hungarian rules above — the €250,000 fund route, the removed direct-property option, the acquisition permit, the 4% transfer duty, the 10-year permit, and the eight-year citizenship timeline — are the same for any non-EU applicant regardless of passport. What differs is your home-country tax wrapper: only that part changes with your nationality.

Non-US readers should swap the US-specific sections (PFIC, FBAR, Form 8938, the terminated US–Hungary treaty) for their own country's equivalents and check three things with a tax adviser in their country of residence: (1) whether your home country taxes foreign investment income and capital gains earned while you hold the Hungarian fund — many do, and some have their own anti-deferral rules for foreign funds that mirror the US PFIC regime; (2) whether your home country requires reporting of foreign financial accounts and assets (equivalents of FBAR/Form 8938 exist in the UK, Canada, Australia and across the EU); and (3) whether a double-taxation treaty is in force between your home country and Hungary, and how it treats fund distributions and gains. The Hungarian side is uniform; the tax outcome is not, so confirm with an adviser where you are tax-resident.

Is it worth it in 2026?

Hungary's Guest Investor Program has quietly become one of the more attractive EU residency-by-investment options in 2026 — largely by default. Spain abolished its golden visa in April 2025, Portugal closed its real-estate route back in 2023, and Greece raised its property thresholds. Against that backdrop, Hungary offers a comparatively low €250,000 entry point, an unusually long 10-year (renewable to 20) permit, no minimum-stay obligation, and one of the lowest flat income-tax rates in Europe at 15%.

The caveats are real, though. The convenient €250,000 route runs through a fund, not a deed, so you take fund performance risk and — if you are American — a serious PFIC problem that can erode or exceed the tax advantages Hungary otherwise offers. The termination of the US–Hungary tax treaty removed a layer of protection US investors used to rely on. And the citizenship pathway is long and genuinely demanding, requiring real residence and a hard language exam, so this is a residency-and-mobility play, not a shortcut to a second passport.

Where Hungary shines: for an investor who wants a long, low-maintenance EU residence card with Schengen access, values Budapest's cost of living and central-European location, and will do the PFIC planning properly before subscribing to a fund, the Guest Investor Program is one of the better-value options left on the continent. Frequently Asked Questions

Can I get Hungary's Golden Visa by buying a house or apartment?

No. The €500,000 direct real-estate purchase option was abolished on 15 January 2025 — before it ever opened to applicants. As of 2026 there are only two qualifying routes: a minimum €250,000 investment in units of a real-estate fund registered with and supervised by the Hungarian National Bank, or a €1,000,000 donation to a Hungarian higher-education institution. You can buy a personal home in Hungary separately, but that purchase does not count toward the Guest Investor Residence Permit.

How much does the Hungary Guest Investor Program cost in 2026?

The minimum qualifying investment is €250,000 in an approved real-estate fund, which must be held for at least five years. On top of that, budget for government and administrative fees, fund subscription and management fees, and legal representation — typically a further €15,000 to €30,000 depending on family size and the fund chosen. The donation alternative is €1,000,000 and is non-refundable.

How long is the Hungary Golden Visa valid and does it lead to citizenship?

The Guest Investor Residence Permit is issued for up to 10 years and can be renewed once for a further 10 years. There is no strict minimum-stay requirement to keep the permit. Hungarian citizenship by naturalization generally requires eight years of continuous residence, a Hungarian-language exam, and demonstrated ties to the country — and, unlike the permit itself, that pathway requires genuine physical presence.

Is the Hungarian real-estate fund a PFIC for US tax purposes?

Almost certainly yes, and this is the single most important planning point for Americans. A pooled foreign investment fund like the Hungarian real-estate funds used for the Guest Investor Program typically meets the definition of a Passive Foreign Investment Company (PFIC). That triggers annual Form 8621 filing and, absent a timely QEF or mark-to-market election, the punitive excess-distribution regime that taxes gains at the highest ordinary rate plus an interest charge. US applicants should obtain a PFIC analysis before subscribing to any fund.

Do I have to live in Hungary to keep the Guest Investor Visa?

No. Hungary does not impose a strict minimum-stay obligation on the Guest Investor Residence Permit; you must comply with address-registration rules but full relocation is not required to maintain the permit. However, if your goal is Hungarian permanent residency or citizenship, you will need to demonstrate genuine, continuous residence — meaningful physical presence in Hungary — which the permit alone does not provide.
