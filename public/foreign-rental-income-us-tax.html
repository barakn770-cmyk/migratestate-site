Foreign Rental Income: US Tax Guide (2026)

MS By The MigrateState Editorial Team · Reviewed by a licensed international real-estate attorney  ·  Updated June 2026  ·  11 min read
Rent from a property abroad is fully taxable by the US, even when the host country taxes it too. You report it on Schedule E in US dollars, deduct your costs — including a mandatory 30-year depreciation — and claim a foreign tax credit for the income tax you paid abroad. The big mistake: assuming the FEIE covers it. It doesn't — rent is passive income, so the exclusion that shelters up to $132,900 of wages does nothing here, and the credit is the tool that actually prevents double tax.

Renting out the apartment in Lisbon or the casita in Tulum when you're not using it feels like found money — and it can be. But the day the first month's rent lands in your foreign account, you've created a US tax event, because as a US citizen or green-card holder you are taxed on your worldwide income, wherever the tenant or the property happens to be. The rent goes on your Form 1040 just like income from a duplex in Denver. The good news is the same deductions apply, and a foreign tax credit usually stops you being taxed twice. The bad news is a cluster of foreign-specific mechanics — a longer mandatory depreciation period, a credit that sits in the wrong basket, and a famous exclusion that does not apply — that quietly trip up Americans who assume foreign rent works like everything else. Here is the 2026 map.

How the US taxes foreign rent

There is no special "foreign rental" regime: your overseas rent is taxed as ordinary income at your normal marginal rate, exactly as US rental income would be. You report the gross rent, subtract allowable expenses, and pay tax on the net. Two extra layers can apply on top. First, the 3.8% Net Investment Income Tax (NIIT) hits net rental income once your modified AGI passes $200,000 (single) or $250,000 (married filing jointly) — and, importantly, the foreign tax credit does not offset the NIIT, so that 3.8% can survive even when your regular income tax on the rent is fully credited away. Second, everything is computed in US dollars: you translate rent and expenses using a consistent method, typically the yearly average exchange rate for a steady stream of rent, or the transaction-date rate for one-off items.

None of this depends on bringing the money to the US. The income is taxable when earned, whether it stays in a euro account or is wired home — a point that surprises owners who assume "I never repatriated it" means "I never owed tax on it."

What you report on Schedule E

Foreign rental income and expenses go on Schedule E (Supplemental Income and Loss), the same form a domestic landlord uses, with depreciation calculated on Form 4562. You list the property, the gross rents received, and each category of deductible expense, arriving at a net profit or loss that flows to your Form 1040. There is no separate "foreign Schedule E" — the form simply doesn't care which country the building is in.

What does change is the reporting that surrounds the rent. The rent itself isn't reported on an FBAR or Form 8938 — but the foreign bank account the rent flows into very likely is. If your foreign accounts together exceed $10,000 at any point in the year, you file an FBAR (FinCEN Form 114); cross the higher Form 8938 thresholds and that gets added too. Directly held foreign real estate is not itself an FBAR or 8938 asset, but the cash it generates lands in accounts that usually are.

Which expenses you can deduct

The deductions are generous and they are what turn a scary gross-rent figure into a modest — or zero — taxable net. Against the rent you can deduct the ordinary, necessary costs of producing it, all converted to dollars:

Deductible against foreign rentNotes
Mortgage interestIncluding interest on a foreign mortgage on the rental
Foreign property taxesLocal council, municipal or IMI-type taxes on the property
Management & agency feesLetting agents, property managers, booking-platform commissions
Repairs & maintenanceRoutine fixes; improvements are capitalized, not expensed
Insurance, utilities, HOA/condo feesThe portion you pay as landlord
DepreciationMandatory 30-year ADS straight line (see below)

Two cautions. Foreign property taxes are deductible on Schedule E against the rent without limit — they are not caught by the $10,000 SALT cap, which only restricts itemized personal deductions on Schedule A. And the old standby of deducting foreign real-estate taxes on a non-rented second home as an itemized deduction was suspended through 2025 and remains so under current law, so for a purely personal foreign home you generally get nothing — another reason renting changes the math entirely.

The mandatory 30-year depreciation

This is the foreign-specific rule that most blogs skim and most owners get wrong. A US residential rental is depreciated over 27.5 years. A foreign residential rental must use the Alternative Depreciation System (ADS): straight-line over 30 years. The Tax Cuts and Jobs Act shortened this from 40 years to 30 for property placed in service after 31 December 2017, so the 30-year figure is current for 2026. You depreciate only the building, never the land, so you must split your cost basis between the two.

Here is the genuine nuance worth the price of admission: depreciation is not optional, and the IRS recaptures it whether or not you ever claimed it. At sale, the depreciation you took (or were entitled to take — "allowed or allowable") is taxed as unrecaptured Section 1250 gain at up to 25%. So an American who skips depreciation to keep things simple gets the worst of both worlds: no annual deduction now, yet a recapture bill later on depreciation they never actually used. The discipline is to claim it every year — and if you've been renting for years without depreciating, a Form 3115 change of accounting method can often recover the missed deductions in one catch-up adjustment. Don't leave it on the table; the recapture is coming regardless.

The foreign tax credit (and the FEIE trap)

If the country where the property sits also taxes the rent — and most do, often through a non-resident landlord return — you avoid double taxation with the foreign tax credit (FTC) on Form 1116. You credit the foreign income tax paid on the rent against the US tax on that same rent, dollar for dollar, up to a limit. For most Americans whose host country taxes rent at a normal rate, the FTC wipes out the regular US income tax on the net rent, leaving only the 3.8% NIIT (which the credit can't touch) for higher earners.

Now the trap that costs people real money: the Foreign Earned Income Exclusion does not apply to rent. The FEIE (up to $132,900 per taxpayer in 2026) excludes only earned income — wages, salary, self-employment. Rental income is passive, unearned income, so it sits entirely outside the FEIE. Expats who live abroad and breezily assume "my foreign income is excluded" routinely leave their rent off the return, which is simply wrong. Rent must be reported and is defended from double tax by the credit, not the exclusion.

One subtlety inside Form 1116: rental income falls in the passive category basket. If you also have other passive foreign income (foreign dividends, interest), they pool together, and any foreign tax you can't use this year carries back one year and forward ten — but only against passive-basket income. And because US depreciation often makes your US taxable rent smaller than the rent the foreign country taxed (foreign systems may not allow the same depreciation), you can end up with foreign tax that exceeds the US tax on the same income, producing excess credits you may struggle to use. It's a good problem, but plan for it rather than assume a perfect wash.

If you also use the place yourself

Most people who buy abroad don't rent year-round — they use the property part of the year and let it the rest. The moment you mix personal and rental use, the IRS applies the vacation-home rules, and they hinge on two bright lines.

First, the 14-day "minimal rental" rule: if you rent the property for fewer than 15 days in the year, you report no rental income at all and deduct no rental expenses — the rent is simply tax-free. Second, the personal-use test: if your personal use exceeds the greater of 14 days or 10% of the days it was rented at fair value, the property is treated as a residence. You can still deduct expenses, but only up to the rental income — the property cannot throw off a deductible loss, and disallowed expenses carry forward. Stay under that personal-use line and the property is a true rental, with full expense deductions and a potential loss. Tracking your own nights in the place is therefore not bookkeeping trivia; it directly decides whether you can deduct a loss.

Rental losses and the $25,000 rule

When deductions (especially depreciation) push the property to a paper loss, the passive activity loss rules decide whether you can use it now. Rental real estate is passive by default, so a loss generally only offsets other passive income. The main escape hatch is the $25,000 special allowance: if you actively participate (approve tenants, set terms, authorize repairs) and own at least 10%, you can deduct up to $25,000 of rental loss against ordinary income — but it phases out between $100,000 and $150,000 of modified AGI, vanishing entirely at $150,000. Above that, the loss isn't lost forever; it's suspended and carries forward, freeing up against future passive income or in full when you sell. For higher-income owners, in practice, the loss usually parks itself until the eventual sale.

Step-by-step at tax time

  1. Convert everything to dollars — translate rent (yearly average rate works for a steady stream) and each expense into US dollars.
  2. Check the personal-use lines — count your own nights; confirm whether the 14-day rule or the residence test applies before you deduct anything.
  3. Total your deductions — mortgage interest, foreign property tax, management fees, insurance, repairs, utilities.
  4. Split basis and depreciate — carve out land, depreciate the building straight-line over 30 years on Form 4562; claim it every year.
  5. Report on Schedule E — net the rent against expenses and carry the result to Form 1040.
  6. Claim the foreign tax credit — file Form 1116 in the passive basket for the foreign income tax you paid on the rent.
  7. Handle losses and NIIT — apply the $25,000 allowance if eligible, suspend the rest; add 3.8% NIIT on net rent if your income clears the threshold.
  8. Do the surrounding reporting — FBAR and, if applicable, Form 8938 on the accounts the rent flows into.

If you're not American: what changes

The property and the local tax are identical for any foreign owner. The country where the property sits taxes the rent under its own non-resident landlord rules — withholding, allowable expenses, filing deadlines — regardless of your passport, and the letting process doesn't change. What's purely American is the home-country wrapper: Schedule E, the 30-year ADS depreciation, the FEIE/FTC mechanics, and US dollar conversion.

If you're not a US person, keep the local tax exactly as described above and swap the US layer for your own country of tax residence. Check three things: (1) whether your home country taxes foreign rental income on a worldwide basis — most do, though the deductions and any depreciation follow their rules, not the US 30-year ADS; (2) whether you must make a foreign-asset or income disclosure at home, such as Canada's T1135 or UK self-assessment of overseas property; and (3) whether a double-taxation treaty between your country and the property's country lets you credit the local tax rather than stack it. A Canadian, Brit or Australian computes the net rent in their own currency and reliefs. Confirm the specifics with a tax adviser where you are resident.

Is renting it out worth it in 2026?

For most owners, yes — renting a foreign property in 2026 is very manageable once you treat the tax as part of the plan rather than a surprise. The deductions are real, depreciation often shelters much of the rent, and the foreign tax credit usually erases the regular US tax on what's left. The losses happen at the edges: assuming the FEIE covers rent (it doesn't), skipping depreciation only to be hit with recapture anyway, blowing the personal-use line and forfeiting a loss, or forgetting the 3.8% NIIT that the credit can't reach. Set the structure up correctly in year one, claim depreciation every year, keep clean dollar-converted records and a count of your own nights in the place, and foreign rent becomes a genuinely tax-efficient stream rather than an April headache. The owners who get burned are almost always the ones who assumed it worked like a US rental — it mostly does, except exactly where it doesn't.

Frequently asked questions

Do US citizens pay US tax on rental income from property abroad?

Yes. You're taxed on worldwide income, so foreign rent goes on Schedule E in US dollars, even when the host country taxes it too. You deduct expenses, including a mandatory depreciation, against the rent, and a foreign tax credit on Form 1116 usually offsets the foreign income tax so you aren't taxed twice on the same income.

Can I use the Foreign Earned Income Exclusion to avoid tax on foreign rent?

No. The FEIE (up to $132,900 per taxpayer for 2026) only shelters earned income like wages and self-employment. Rent is passive, unearned income, so it's outside the FEIE entirely. The correct tool is the foreign tax credit, which credits the foreign income tax on the rent against your US tax. Assuming the FEIE covers rent is a common and costly mistake.

How is foreign rental property depreciated for US tax?

Foreign residential rental property must be depreciated under ADS, straight-line over 30 years (versus 27.5 for a US rental), on Form 4562 and Schedule E. It's mandatory and reduces taxable rent now, but it lowers your basis, so it's recaptured at sale at up to 25% — and recapture applies whether or not you actually claimed it, so always claim it.

What expenses can I deduct against foreign rental income?

Ordinary, necessary costs of earning the rent: mortgage interest, foreign property taxes, insurance, management and agency fees, repairs and maintenance, utilities you pay, HOA/condo fees, and depreciation — all converted to US dollars. If you also use the property personally, allocate expenses between rental and personal days and deduct only the rental share.

What happens if I use the foreign property myself as well as renting it?

Personal use changes the rules. Rent it fewer than 15 days a year and you report no income and deduct no expenses. If your personal use exceeds the greater of 14 days or 10% of rented days, the home is a residence: you can deduct expenses only up to the rental income, so it can't produce a deductible loss. Track your personal nights carefully.
