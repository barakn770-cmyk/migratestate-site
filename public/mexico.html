Buying Property in Mexico as an American: The 2026 Legal & Tax Guide

MS By The MigrateState Editorial Team · Reviewed by a licensed international real-estate attorney  ·  Updated June 2026  ·  9 min read
Yes — US citizens can legally buy property anywhere in Mexico in 2026. Outside the "restricted zone" (within 50 km of the coast or 100 km of a land border) Americans can hold title directly. Inside it, residential property is held through a fideicomiso, a renewable 50-year bank trust. Expect closing costs of 5–10% and annual property tax (predial) of just 0.05–0.3% of value.

Mexico is the number-one destination for Americans buying a second home abroad — and for good reason: proximity, affordability, established expat communities, and ownership costs a fraction of the US. But the questions that actually keep buyers up at night aren't about the view. They're legal and financial: Can I even own it? Will the IRS come after me? What's a fideicomiso?

This guide answers each one, with 2026 figures, written in the order a careful buyer should actually think.

Can Americans legally buy property in Mexico?

Yes. There is no prohibition on US citizens owning real estate in Mexico, and you do not need to be a resident or a citizen to buy. The only meaningful restriction is geographic, and it has a simple, well-established workaround.

Under the Mexican Constitution, foreigners cannot hold direct title to residential land inside the restricted zone — any land within 50 kilometers of the coastline or 100 kilometers of an international border. Because most destinations Americans want (Cancún, Playa del Carmen, Tulum, Puerto Vallarta, Los Cabos) sit on the coast, this affects the majority of buyers. The workaround is the fideicomiso.

What is a fideicomiso, and do you need one?

A fideicomiso is a bank trust. A Mexican bank holds legal title as trustee, while you — the beneficiary — keep every meaningful right of ownership: you can live in it, renovate it, rent it, sell it, and pass it to your heirs. The bank cannot use, encumber, or sell the property; it only acts on your instructions.

  • The trust is granted for a 50-year term and is indefinitely renewable.
  • It costs roughly $500–$700 per year in bank trustee fees, plus a setup fee at closing.
  • It applies only inside the restricted zone. Outside the zone, you take direct title — no trust needed.
  • For commercial use inside the zone, a Mexican corporation is often used instead.

The fideicomiso is the single most misunderstood part of buying in Mexico. It is not a lease and not a loophole — it is a secure, decades-old instrument used by hundreds of thousands of foreign owners.

Can Americans get a mortgage in Mexico?

Sometimes — but most Americans pay cash or finance from the US side, and the numbers explain why.

Because peso mortgages are expensive and approval is stricter for non-residents, many buyers use a US home-equity line (HELOC), a cross-border lender, or cash. If you finance, model the rate difference carefully — a 12% peso mortgage changes the entire return on a rental.

What are closing costs and property taxes?

Budget 5%–10% of the purchase price in closing costs, paid by the buyer:

ScenarioTypical closing costs
Outside the restricted zone (direct title)5%–8%
Inside the zone (with fideicomiso)7%–10%

These cover the acquisition tax (ISAI), notary fees, the public registry, appraisal, and — inside the zone — the fideicomiso setup and foreign-ministry permit. In Mexico, the notario público is a senior government-appointed lawyer who validates the transaction; using a strong one is non-negotiable.

The good news on holding costs: annual property tax — predial — runs just 0.05%–0.3% of value per year, far below US levels. A $400,000 home might cost only a few hundred dollars a year.

The part Americans get wrong: US taxes

Owning property in Mexico does not free you from the US tax system. Here's the framework — worth reviewing with a cross-border CPA.

1. Simply owning the property

Buying and holding a personal-use home does not, by itself, trigger an IRS filing requirement.

2. The fideicomiso and Form 3520

For years, advisors feared the fideicomiso would be treated as a foreign trust, triggering onerous Forms 3520/3520-A. IRS Revenue Ruling 2013-14 holds that a standard real-estate fideicomiso that merely holds residential title is not a trust for US tax purposes — meaning Forms 3520/3520-A are generally not required for a clean residential fideicomiso. This is the ruling most generic "move to Mexico" blogs miss. Confirm your specific structure with a CPA, as a modified trust or one holding cash can change the analysis.

3. Rental income

If you rent the property, that income is taxable in the US and reported on Schedule E — even though you also pay tax in Mexico. The Foreign Tax Credit generally prevents true double taxation.

4. Selling at a gain

A sale is a capital gain reportable in the US. Mexico also taxes the gain (ISR), and a Foreign Tax Credit usually applies.

5. Foreign bank accounts

If you open a Mexican bank account (common, often required), you may cross the FBAR (FinCEN 114) threshold of $10,000 aggregate, and possibly FATCA Form 8938. These are about accounts, not the property itself.

Will buying property get you residency in Mexico?

Not automatically. Unlike a Golden Visa, buying real estate in Mexico does not grant residency. However, owning property and demonstrating sufficient income or savings can support a Temporary or Permanent Resident visa application through a Mexican consulate. Separately, note the 183-day rule: spend more than 183 days in Mexico in a calendar year and you may become a Mexican tax resident on worldwide income.

Step-by-step: buying property in Mexico as an American

  1. Define the zone. Coastal/border = restricted zone = fideicomiso. Inland = direct title.
  2. Engage an independent attorney — not the seller's, and separate from the notario.
  3. Get your RFC and, if needed, a Mexican bank account.
  4. Make an offer and sign a promissory agreement (contrato de promesa), usually with a 5–10% deposit.
  5. Due diligence: title search, no-liens certificate, property-tax status, ejido (communal land) check.
  6. Set up the fideicomiso (if in the restricted zone) and obtain the SRE permit.
  7. Close before the notario público, pay closing costs, and register the deed.
  8. Plan your US tax compliance before year-end.

Is buying property in Mexico worth it in 2026?

For lifestyle buyers and retirees, the value equation remains compelling: low carrying costs, a favorable environment for dollar holders, and mature expat infrastructure. For investors, rental yields in tourist hubs can be strong — but model the higher financing costs and both countries' taxes honestly before assuming the headline yield. The buyers who do well treat it as a legal and financial transaction first and a dream second.

Frequently asked questions

Can a US citizen own property in Mexico outright?

Yes. Outside the restricted zone (50 km from coast / 100 km from border) Americans hold direct title. Inside it, residential property is held via a fideicomiso bank trust, which conveys full ownership rights.

Is a fideicomiso safe?

Yes. It is a regulated 50-year renewable bank trust used by hundreds of thousands of foreign owners. The bank holds title as trustee but cannot use, sell, or encumber the property.

How much are closing costs in Mexico?

Typically 5–10% of the purchase price: 5–8% outside the restricted zone, 7–10% inside it with a fideicomiso.

Do I pay US taxes on a Mexican property?

Owning a personal-use home alone usually triggers no US filing. Rental income (Schedule E) and capital gains on sale are US-reportable, with a Foreign Tax Credit to avoid double taxation. A Mexican bank account may trigger FBAR/FATCA reporting.

Does buying property give me Mexican residency?

No, not automatically — but ownership plus sufficient income can support a residency visa application. Staying 183+ days a year can make you a Mexican tax resident.
