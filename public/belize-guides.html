Belize Real Estate & Residency Guides

Lawyer-reviewed guides for Americans buying property in Belize — the legal and tax reality, not the "tax-free" dream.

Foreigners buy Belizean property freehold, on the same terms as citizens — English-language title, US-dollar-pegged prices, no ownership license. Budget about 8% stamp duty plus 1–2% legal fees, expect zero capital-gains tax and only a few hundred dollars a year in property tax. The catch isn't Belizean — it's that Belize's famous "tax-free" status does nothing for your US tax bill. The guide below goes deep; this page is the map.

Why Americans look at Belize

Belize removes almost every source of friction Americans dread abroad. It is the only officially English-speaking country in Central America, so the title deed, the sale contract, and your lawyer's advice all arrive in plain English. The Belize dollar is pegged 2 to 1 to the US dollar and US notes circulate freely, so prices convert with grade-school arithmetic and no exchange-rate anxiety. And since Belize repealed the Alien Landholding Act in 2001, a foreigner takes the same freehold title a Belizean gets — no license, no residency requirement, no foreign-buyer approval. Add no capital-gains tax and a near-zero annual property tax, and it is easy to see why Belize punches far above its size in American search traffic.

That openness is also where unprepared buyers get hurt. Because the front door is wide — and because everyone speaks English — people skip the diligence they would never skip at home. The two places Belize genuinely punishes the careless are title (leasehold, untitled national land, or shoreline-reserve parcels marketed as clean freehold) and US taxes — the "tax-free retirement" pitch that does nothing against the IRS. Both are fully avoidable with an independent Belizean attorney for the title and a US cross-border advisor for the tax.

Where to buy — the regions

Belize packs several distinct markets into a small country, each with its own buyer:

  • Ambergris Caye (San Pedro) — the island that drives most foreign demand: beach condos, canal-front homes, the deepest resale and rental market, and the highest prices. Best for investors and second-home buyers who want liquidity and turnkey title.
  • Placencia — the mainland peninsula of beach and lagoon property, a laid-back expat favorite with strong short-let demand. Suits lifestyle buyers and lock-and-leave owners.
  • Cayo (San Ignacio) — inland jungle, river, and farmland at far gentler prices, popular with self-sufficiency and off-grid buyers. Best for acreage and permanent-residency seekers, but watch for untitled or leasehold parcels.
  • Corozal & Belize City — Corozal in the north (near Mexico, affordable, retiree-friendly) and the commercial hub of Belize City. Municipal property-tax rules differ inside city limits, so confirm the specific figure for your parcel.

Across all of these, one line cuts through: insist on registered freehold title verified at the Lands Registry, and check the survey against the public shoreline reserve the government keeps along the coast. Cheap "beachfront" that sits partly on the reserve, or land that turns out to be leasehold or untitled national land, is the single most common way foreign buyers lose money here.

Who Belize suits — and who should look elsewhere

Belize fits Americans who want an English-speaking, dollar-priced Caribbean home and who do the title diligence properly. Ownership is open and freehold, prices track the US dollar 2:1, there is no capital-gains tax, annual property tax is trivial, and the QRP residency program is within reach on US$2,000 a month of pension or investment income for anyone 40 or over. For a beach condo on Ambergris Caye or a lock-and-leave place in Placencia, the front door really is as easy as it looks.

Belize is a weaker fit if you need mortgage financing (local lending to foreigners is rare and expensive, so most buy cash), if you're drawn to cheap untitled or leasehold parcels without independent counsel, or if you believe the "tax-free" story shields you from the IRS — it doesn't. Belize FAQ

Is Belize a good place for Americans to buy property in 2026?

For an English-speaking, dollar-priced Caribbean home — with open freehold ownership, no capital-gains tax, near-zero annual property tax, and an easy QRP residency on US$2,000 a month — Belize is one of the most frictionless markets anywhere. The risks are specific and avoidable: untitled or leasehold land, the coastal shoreline reserve, and the belief that "tax-free" applies to the IRS.

Do I need residency or a visa to buy in Belize?

No. Since the Alien Landholding Act was repealed in 2001, foreigners buy freehold title with the same rights as citizens — no residency, visa, or approval required. Buying can instead pair with residency: the Qualified Retired Persons (QRP) program grants residency to applicants 40 and over with US$2,000/month of foreign income, though it is not tied to a property purchase.

What is the stamp duty for foreign buyers in Belize?

Foreign buyers pay 8% stamp duty (transfer tax) on the property value above the first BZ$20,000 (about US$10,000), calculated on the higher of the purchase price or the government-assessed market value. Belizean and CARICOM nationals pay 5%. With ~1–2% legal fees and registration, budget roughly 9–11% all-in.

Does Belize tax capital gains or foreign income?

Belize has no capital-gains tax at all, and QRP status exempts your foreign income from Belize tax. But this is only Belize's side: the US taxes citizens on worldwide income, there is no US–Belize tax treaty, and because Belize collects so little there is almost no foreign tax credit to offset your US bill. The low taxes are a lifestyle benefit, not a US-tax one.

Is my Belize property or bank account reportable to the IRS?

Directly-held Belize real estate is not reported on the FBAR or Form 8938 — those cover foreign financial accounts, not a house. But a Belize bank account (for example, the deposit QRP requires) is a foreign financial account: once your aggregate foreign balances cross US$10,000 at any point in the year, the FBAR is mandatory. Rental income also goes on Schedule E, with foreign residential property depreciated over 30 years.
