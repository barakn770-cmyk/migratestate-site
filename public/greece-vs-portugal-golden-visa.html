Greece vs Portugal Golden Visa: Which Is Better for Americans?

MS By The MigrateState Editorial Team · Reviewed by a licensed international real-estate attorney  ·  Updated June 2026  ·  10 min read

For most Americans in 2026, Greece wins on cost and flexibility — real estate from €400,000 in most regions (€250,000 in special categories) and zero minimum stay — while Portugal wins if an EU passport is the goal, despite its new 10-year citizenship timeline and €500,000 fund-only entry. The right answer depends on whether you want a parked asset or a path to a passport.

The 2026 snapshot

Both Greece and Portugal sell the same core promise: a renewable EU residence permit, visa-free travel across the Schengen Area, and the right to bring your family — in exchange for an investment. But after several years of reform, the two programs now look very different, and the gap matters enormously for a US citizen deciding where to put six or seven figures.

Portugal closed its famous real estate route in 2023. The headline option today is a €500,000 investment into a regulated fund. Greece kept real estate at the center of its program but raised prices sharply, introducing a tiered system that runs from €250,000 to €800,000 depending on the property and location. Investment cost compared

This is where the two programs diverge most. In Greece, the Golden Visa is built around buying property, and the minimum depends on where and what you buy. The €800,000 tier covers the most sought-after locations: the entire region of Attica (Athens and Piraeus), greater Thessaloniki, Mykonos, Santorini, and every Greek island with a population over roughly 3,100 residents. Everywhere else — much of the mainland and the smaller islands — sits at the €400,000 tier. A narrow €250,000 tier remains available for two special cases: converting a commercial building into residential use, or restoring a listed heritage property. All three tiers require a single property — you cannot stack several cheaper units to reach the threshold. For the €400,000 and €800,000 tiers, the property must have a minimum habitable area of 120 square meters. The €250,000 restoration/conversion tier is not subject to the 120 sqm minimum.

Portugal no longer offers a property route at all. The mainstream path is a €500,000 investment into a fund accredited by the Portuguese securities regulator (CMVM), and since the reforms those funds may no longer hold Portuguese real estate — they target sectors like technology, industry, and energy. A smaller €250,000 route exists for donations to arts, heritage, or culture (€200,000 in low-density areas), and a €500,000 research-contribution option is also on the books. The practical takeaway: Greece lets you own a tangible, rentable asset; Portugal asks you to accept market risk in a managed fund.

Stay requirements & flexibility

If you simply want an EU residence permit you rarely use, Greece is the clear winner. It imposes no minimum physical-presence requirement: you can hold and renew the permit indefinitely without ever spending a night in the country, which is exactly why it appeals to passive investors and frequent travelers. Portugal asks for an average of seven days per year — modest, but not zero — and renews every two years rather than every five, which means more paperwork over time.

That flexibility cuts both ways. Greece's "you never have to live here" model is excellent for a backup plan or a Schengen travel document, but it quietly undermines the citizenship dream, as we explain below. Portugal's lighter-touch stay rule still keeps you genuinely connected to the country, which historically supported a real naturalization path.

The path to an EU passport

For investors whose real goal is a second passport rather than just residency, 2026 reshuffled the deck. Portugal's nationality law was revised and promulgated in May 2026: non-EU nationals — which includes Americans — now face a 10-year residence requirement before they can apply for citizenship, up from the famous five years. The reform also added an A2-level Portuguese language test, a civic and historical knowledge exam, and a formal declaration of adherence to democratic principles. Importantly, the change affects the path to citizenship, not the residency itself — Golden Visa holders keep their residence rights, renewals, and travel freedom exactly as before.

Greece, on paper, allows naturalization after seven years — faster than Portugal's new ten. But there is a catch that the brochures gloss over, and it is the single most important point in this comparison for anyone chasing a passport. We unpack it in the next section.

The detail most blogs miss

Here is the nuance a generic comparison won't tell you: Greece's seven-year citizenship timeline is largely theoretical for a Golden Visa investor. Greek naturalization requires genuine, continuous physical residence — you have to actually live in Greece, file as a tax resident, and demonstrate integration, including language. The very feature that makes the Greek Golden Visa so attractive (zero minimum stay) is incompatible with the residence that naturalization demands. So an American who buys a Greek apartment and visits twice a year accrues residence-permit years, but not the kind of lived residence that converts into a passport.

Portugal works the opposite way. Even at ten years, the clock is built around a program designed for non-resident investors, and the days you must spend are minimal. If a passport is genuinely the objective, Portugal — slower on paper — is the more realistic route of the two. If you only want residency, Schengen access, and a tangible asset, Greece's "weakness" on citizenship is irrelevant and its flexibility becomes a strength.

A second, related subtlety concerns the counting of Portugal's clock after the 2026 reform. For applications where fees were paid before the law was gazetted, the citizenship clock generally continues to run from the fee-payment date; for those paying after, the clock starts only when the first residence card is physically issued. A few months of timing can therefore shift your eligibility date by more than a year — a reason to get the sequencing reviewed by counsel before you wire funds.

Tax — what Americans must know

A residence permit is not the same as tax residency, and this distinction trips up many first-time investors. In both countries you generally become a tax resident only if you spend more than 183 days there in a year or make the country your habitual home. Simply holding a Golden Visa while living in the US does not, by itself, hand either government the right to tax your worldwide income.

What never changes is your US position. The United States taxes its citizens on worldwide income no matter where they live, so any rental income from a Greek apartment or gains from a Portuguese fund remain reportable to the IRS. You may also trigger foreign-account reporting (FBAR and Form 8938) once you open European bank accounts to fund the investment. Both countries offer attractive regimes for those who do relocate — Greece's 7% flat tax for foreign retirees who transfer their tax residency (for 15 years), and Portugal's reduced-rate successor to its Non-Habitual Resident scheme — but these only matter if you actually move and become tax resident.

How to choose

Strip away the marketing and the decision usually comes down to three questions. First, do you want to own something tangible? If holding a real, rentable European apartment matters to you, Greece is the only option of the two. Second, how important is a future EU passport? If it is the whole point, Portugal is the more viable path despite the longer timeline. Third, how much do you value never having to show up? If you want a residence permit you can ignore for years, Greece's zero-stay rule is unmatched.

Budget then narrows it further. If €250,000–€400,000 is your ceiling, Greece's regional and special-category tiers are within reach while Portugal's main route is not. If you are comfortable at €500,000 and prefer a managed, hands-off financial investment to being a foreign landlord, Portugal's fund route may suit you better.

Step-by-step: how to apply

  1. Define the goal. Decide whether you want residency only, a tangible asset, or a path to citizenship — this determines the country before anything else.
  2. Engage local counsel. Retain a licensed immigration and real-estate attorney in your target country, and a US cross-border CPA, before sending any money.
  3. Get your tax ID and bank account. Obtain the local tax number (Greek AFM or Portuguese NIF) and open a local bank account to route the investment.
  4. Select and verify the investment. For Greece, identify a qualifying property and confirm its tier; for Portugal, confirm the fund is CMVM-accredited and compliant.
  5. Transfer funds compliantly. Move money through traceable channels, keeping records that prove the source and path of funds.
  6. Complete the investment. Close on the property or subscribe to the fund, securing the documentation the immigration authority will require.
  7. File the residence application. Submit biometrics and supporting documents; collect your residence card and diarize the renewal date.
  8. Maintain compliance. Renew on schedule, hold the qualifying investment, and meet any stay requirement (Portugal) for the life of the permit.

Is it worth it in 2026?

For the right American, yes — but the calculus is sharper than it was a few years ago. Greece remains one of Europe's most accessible Golden Visas: a genuine asset, real Schengen mobility, and no obligation to spend a single day there. As a flexible Plan B or a way to own a slice of the Mediterranean, it holds up well. Portugal is now a slower, fund-based bet whose appeal rests almost entirely on the citizenship endgame; if you can accept ten years and minimal annual visits, it is still one of the few credible routes from a US passport to an EU one without relocating outright.

What is no longer true is the old pitch of a cheap, fast, hands-off EU passport. Both governments have deliberately closed that door. Treat a 2026 Golden Visa as a serious, professionally-advised investment with a clear personal objective — residency, an asset, or eventual citizenship — and it can be worth every euro. Treat it as a shortcut, and you will likely be disappointed.

Frequently asked questions

Is the Greece or Portugal Golden Visa cheaper for Americans?

Greece is cheaper at entry. Its Golden Visa starts at €250,000 for commercial-to-residential conversions or restored listed buildings, while most areas require €400,000 or €800,000 in real estate. Portugal no longer offers a real estate route at all; its main path is a €500,000 qualifying investment fund.

Which Golden Visa has no minimum stay requirement?

Greece has no minimum physical-presence requirement to keep the residence permit valid, making it the better choice for purely passive investors. Portugal requires an average of seven days per year (about 14 days per two-year renewal cycle).

Which program leads to an EU passport faster?

Neither is fast for a non-resident American. Under Portugal's May 2026 nationality law, non-EU investors now naturalize after 10 years. Greece allows naturalization after 7 years, but only with genuine physical residence — a zero-stay investor cannot realistically qualify, so Portugal is the more viable citizenship route.

Does a Golden Visa make me liable for European taxes?

Holding a residence permit is not the same as becoming a tax resident. You generally become tax resident only if you spend more than 183 days in the country or make it your habitual home. Either way, US citizens remain taxed on worldwide income by the IRS regardless of where they live.

Can my spouse and children be included?

Yes. Both programs allow a single qualifying investment to cover a spouse, dependent children, and in many cases dependent parents, with each program setting its own age and dependency conditions for adult children and parents.
