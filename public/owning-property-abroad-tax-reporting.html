Owning Property Abroad: US Tax Reporting (2026)

MS By The MigrateState Editorial Team · Reviewed by a licensed international real-estate attorney  ·  Updated June 2026  ·  11 min read
Owning foreign property is mostly a reporting job, not a tax bill. The house in your own name has no IRS form and is not taxed until it earns or sells. What you report is the money around it: an FBAR once your foreign accounts top $10,000, possibly Form 8938, and Schedule E for rent (offset by a foreign tax credit). The trap most owners miss: a local-currency mortgage can create a separate, ordinary-income Section 988 currency gain on payoff.

Here is the thing almost nobody tells you before you buy abroad: the painful part of owning foreign property isn't the tax — it's the reporting. As a US citizen or green-card holder you are taxed on your worldwide income and burdened with worldwide disclosure, so a villa in Portugal or a condo in Mexico quietly bolts a small stack of US information forms onto your life. The forms usually generate little or no actual tax, because a foreign tax credit tends to cancel it out. But miss one and the penalty can dwarf any tax you ever owed on the place. This is the 2026 map of what you must report, what you can ignore, and the one currency rule that ambushes owners who financed locally.

What actually has to be reported

Start with a liberating fact: foreign real estate held directly in your own name is not, by itself, a reportable asset. There is no IRS form for "I own a house in Italy." It is not on the FBAR, it is not on Form 8938, and it creates no US tax simply by existing. The IRS becomes interested only in three things that tend to travel with the property — foreign financial accounts, income the property earns, and the gain when you sell — plus the special case of property held through a company or trust. Get those right and direct ownership is remarkably light-touch. The reporting duties attach to the cash and the income, never to the bricks.

FBAR: the $10,000 account rule

The FBAR — FinCEN Form 114, filed electronically and separately from your tax return — is the one that catches honest people off guard. It is required when the combined value of all your foreign financial accounts exceeds $10,000 at any single moment during the year. Not the average, not the year-end balance — the highest point. The threshold is fixed at $10,000 and does not rise if you live abroad. The property itself is not an account, so the house never triggers an FBAR. But the local bank account you open to pay the property tax, the utilities and the HOA absolutely does, and it counts toward the $10,000 alongside every other foreign account you hold. The classic miss: you park a few thousand dollars in a local account, forget about it, and a second account elsewhere tips your aggregate over $10,000 — now both are reportable. Filing is free and the deadline auto-extends to October; there is no reason to skip it.

Form 8938 & the FATCA thresholds

Form 8938 is the FATCA cousin of the FBAR: it rides on your 1040 and covers specified foreign financial assets — accounts, but also foreign stocks, partnership interests and certain instruments held outside an account. It overlaps the FBAR heavily, so most people who file one file both, but its thresholds are higher and they swing dramatically based on where you live and how you file. Like the FBAR, it does not capture real estate held directly in your name; it captures the financial assets around it.

The takeaway: if you simply own a paid-off house in your own name and keep little cash abroad, you may owe no Form 8938 at all — but the moment you hold significant foreign investments, or buy through a foreign company whose shares are a "specified asset," you can cross the line fast.

Rental income on Schedule E

Rent the place out and the income is taxable in both countries — the host country because the property sits there, the US because you are a citizen. You report gross rent and expenses on Schedule E, the same form used for a US rental, translating every figure into US dollars (the IRS publishes an average annual rate you can use). You deduct the usual costs — management fees, repairs, insurance, interest — and depreciation. Two details trip people up. First, foreign residential rental property must be depreciated over 30 years under the Alternative Depreciation System, not the 27.5 years for a US home, which slightly lowers your annual deduction. Second, you depreciate the building only, not the land, so keep a defensible split. File even at a loss: a paper loss from depreciation can carry forward, and a non-filed Schedule E is a gap the IRS notices.

The foreign tax credit (your shield)

The reason owning abroad rarely means paying tax twice is the foreign tax credit, claimed on Form 1116. Every dollar of foreign income tax you legitimately pay on rent or a gain becomes a dollar-for-dollar credit against the US tax on that same income. In most countries the local rate on rental income meets or exceeds the US rate, so the credit wipes out the US tax entirely and you are left filing forms rather than writing checks. The practical discipline this demands is documentation: the credit is only as good as your proof, so keep the foreign tax receipts, the assessment notices and the payment confirmations. A credit you can't substantiate is a credit you can lose on audit. Note the credit offsets income tax — it does not erase the FBAR or 8938 filing duty, which exist regardless of whether any tax is due.

The Section 988 mortgage trap

This is the nuance most blogs skip, and the one that creates phantom tax bills. If you financed your foreign property with a mortgage denominated in the local currency — euros, pesos, pounds — the IRS treats that debt as a separate foreign-currency transaction under Section 988. When you pay the loan off, refinance it, or even make certain principal repayments, it compares the US-dollar value of the debt at origination with its dollar value at repayment. If the local currency weakened against the dollar in the meantime, you repaid a loan that is "cheaper" in dollars than what you borrowed — and that difference is a currency gain taxed as ordinary income, at your regular rate, entirely apart from the property.

Three things make this genuinely nasty. First, it can fire even if you never sell the house — a refinance alone is enough. Second, the Section 121 home-sale exclusion ($250k/$500k) shelters gain on the residence, but it does nothing for the Section 988 gain on the mortgage, because the IRS treats the home and its loan as two independent personal transactions. Third, the rules are a whipsaw: a currency gain on a personal-residence mortgage is taxable, but a currency loss on it is generally not deductible. So you can owe tax on a paper gain you never felt in your bank account, with no offsetting break when the currency moves the other way. The defense is to model the dollar value of your loan at payoff before you refinance or sell, ideally with a cross-border CPA, so the bill is planned rather than discovered.

Entities & trusts: extra forms

Everything above assumes you hold the property directly in your own name, which is the lightest reporting path. The moment a structure sits between you and the deed, the form count jumps. Buy through a foreign corporation or LLC and you may owe Form 5471 or Form 8865, with their own steep late-filing penalties. Hold property through certain foreign trusts and Forms 3520 and 3520-A can apply — though a standard Mexican fideicomiso that merely holds residential title is not treated as a foreign trust under Revenue Ruling 2013-14, a point we cover in our US taxes on Mexican property guide. The lesson: a structure that looks clever for local liability or inheritance reasons can quietly multiply your US filings, so price the US reporting cost of any holding entity before you set it up, not after.

Step-by-step compliance

  1. Map your accounts — list every foreign account tied to the property and check whether they aggregate over $10,000 for the FBAR.
  2. Test the FATCA threshold — total your specified foreign financial assets and file Form 8938 if you cross the line for your filing status and residence.
  3. Report rental income on Schedule E in US dollars; depreciate the building over 30 years; keep the land split.
  4. Claim the credit — file Form 1116 for foreign income tax paid, and retain every foreign tax receipt.
  5. Watch the mortgage — if your loan is in local currency, model the Section 988 dollar gain before any payoff or refinance.
  6. Check your structure — a foreign company or trust may add Forms 5471, 8865 or 3520; confirm before you rely on it.
  7. Fix the past cleanly — if you missed prior FBARs or 8938s and weren't willful, ask a CPA about the IRS streamlined procedures.

If you're not American: what changes

The property side is identical for any foreign owner. The local purchase taxes, the annual property tax, the host country's tax on your rent and gain, and the buying process apply the same whatever passport you hold. What's purely American is the home-country reporting wrapper — FBAR, Form 8938, Schedule E, Form 1116 and Section 988.

If you're not a US person, drop the IRS forms above and swap in your own country's rules. Check three things where you're tax-resident: (1) whether your country taxes foreign rental income and capital gains on the property (most do, on a worldwide basis); (2) whether you must report foreign assets, property or accounts at home — Canada's T1135 foreign-income verification, the UK's worldwide self-assessment, and various EU asset-disclosure regimes all differ in scope and threshold; and (3) whether your country has a double-taxation treaty with the country where the property sits, so the local tax you pay is credited rather than stacked on top. A Canadian, Brit or Australian runs a very different return than an American on the same apartment. Confirm the specifics with a tax adviser in your country of residence.

Is the reporting worth it in 2026?

For almost everyone, yes — because the cost of owning abroad is compliance, not cash. The foreign tax credit means you rarely pay the same income twice; direct ownership in your own name keeps the form count low; and once a cross-border CPA sets up your FBAR, 8938 and Schedule E, each year is routine. The real risk is silence. An unfiled FBAR or a missed 8938 carries penalties measured in five figures, and a Section 988 gain you never modeled can turn a clean refinance into a surprise tax bill. Treat the purchase as a US reporting project from day one — list the accounts, keep the receipts, model the mortgage in dollars — and a property abroad stays a pleasure rather than an audit. Price the paperwork before the property, and the rest is just maintenance.

Frequently asked questions

Do I have to report property I own abroad on my US taxes?

Foreign real estate held in your own name has no IRS form and isn't taxed until it earns or sells. What's reportable is the money around it: an FBAR if foreign accounts exceed $10,000, Form 8938 if you pass the FATCA threshold, and Schedule E for rent. Property held through an entity or trust can add more forms.

What is the FBAR threshold for 2026?

The FBAR (FinCEN 114) is required when all your foreign financial accounts combined exceed $10,000 at any point in the year. The figure is fixed and doesn't change with residence. The house isn't an account, but a local account for taxes, utilities or HOA counts toward the $10,000.

Is foreign rental income taxable in the United States?

Yes — you're taxed on worldwide income, so rent goes on Schedule E in US dollars even if the host country taxes it too. Claim a foreign tax credit on Form 1116 for the foreign tax paid. Foreign residential rentals depreciate over 30 years, not 27.5, and only the building, not the land.

What is the Section 988 trap on a foreign mortgage?

A local-currency loan is a separate currency transaction. At payoff or refinance, the dollar value of the debt then versus at origination creates an ordinary-income gain or loss. Section 121 won't shelter it, and the rules are asymmetric: a currency gain is taxable but a personal-residence currency loss usually isn't deductible.

What happens if I do not file FBAR or Form 8938?

Penalties attach to the forms, not the tax. A non-willful FBAR miss can cost up to $16,536 per annual report (post-Bittner, not per account); willful failures far more. Form 8938 starts at $10,000, rising to $50,000. Since the foreign tax credit usually erases the actual tax, the missed forms are the real exposure. Streamlined procedures exist for non-willful past non-filers.
