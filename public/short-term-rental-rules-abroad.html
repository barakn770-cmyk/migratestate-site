Short-Term Rental Rules Abroad (2026)

MS By The MigrateState Editorial Team · Reviewed by a licensed international real-estate attorney  ·  Updated July 2026  ·  11 min read
The Airbnb math you assumed at purchase is the first thing to check in 2026. From 20 May 2026, EU Regulation 2024/1028 forces platforms to verify a host registration number and report your nights and guests to the state every month — unregistered listings get delisted. And a property bought for the Greek golden visa from 1 Sep 2024 cannot be short-let at all: the fine reaches €50,000 and the residence permit is revoked.

Almost every overseas purchase gets underwritten with a rental number attached to it. "It pays for itself in high season." "We'll Airbnb it when we're not there." In 2026 that assumption is the single most dangerous part of the deal, because the right to short-let a property abroad is no longer something you own along with the walls — it is a separate permission, granted by a city, revocable, and in some cases already gone before you sign. The last two years have brought a coordinated tightening: an EU-wide registration and data-sharing regime, a Greek ban aimed squarely at golden-visa buyers, a Spanish Supreme Court ruling that reshuffled who regulates what, and Portuguese and Italian city rules that simply close the door in the neighbourhoods buyers most want. Here is the 2026 map.

The EU rule that lands on 20 May 2026

Regulation (EU) 2024/1028 — the first Europe-wide framework for short-term rentals — applies from 20 May 2026. It does not, by itself, ban anything. What it does is make the existing bans enforceable, which for an owner is the more consequential change. Where a member state operates a registration system, the regulation standardises it: you obtain a unique registration number, that number must be displayed on every listing, and platforms such as Airbnb and Booking must verify it and, monthly, transmit your activity data — nights let, guests hosted, per unit — to a single digital entry point in the country where the property sits. Listings without a valid number are removed.

The regulation is technically opt-in: a member state is not obliged to run a registration scheme. But if it wants registration or platform data — and they all do — it must now do it through this channel. The practical effect for a foreign owner is that the informal era is over. The old failure mode was a quiet listing in a city that theoretically required a licence and never checked. From May 2026, the platform itself is the checkpoint, and the tax authority receives a monthly, per-property record of exactly how many nights you let and to how many guests. Compliance and tax exposure now arrive as one package.

Greece: the golden-visa short-let ban

This is the rule that catches the most American buyers, because it collides with exactly the plan they came with: buy for the residency, let it on Airbnb between visits. Law 5100/2024 forbids that outright. A property acquired for the purpose of the golden visa from 1 September 2024 onward cannot be short-let anywhere in Greece for as long as it underpins the permit. The ban is defined narrowly but bites broadly: it covers lettings of fewer than 60 days where no services beyond accommodation and bed linen are provided — which is the definition of a holiday let. Enforcement is not theoretical: the penalty runs to €50,000, and the Ministry moves to revoke the residence permit — the entire reason you bought.

What remains open is worth knowing. Long leases of 60 days or more are permitted, so a conventional residential tenancy is fine. So is leasing the property to a tourism business that runs it hotel-style with additional services. And properties bought before the new regime or under the transitional arrangements are not caught by the ban at all — which is why a 2023 purchase and a 2025 purchase in the same building can have completely different rights attached. Any yield model for a new Greek golden-visa purchase should be built on a long-let assumption, at long-let rates. If a broker's projection shows nightly rates, the projection is describing an illegal use.

Spain: after the Supreme Court

Spain spent 2025 building a national single registry for short-term rentals, and on 21 May 2026 the Supreme Court annulled it, holding that the State had exceeded its competence by imposing a state-wide registry that effectively displaced the registries the autonomous communities already ran. The Ventanilla Única Digital — the digital one-stop shop through which platforms share activity data with the authorities — was upheld.

Read carefully, this is not deregulation, and treating it as a green light is the error to avoid. The layer that was struck down was the national one; the layers that actually decide whether you can let your flat — the regional tourist-licence regimes (Andalusia, Catalonia, the Valencian Community, the Balearics) and the municipal caps and moratoria — were untouched and remain fully binding. Barcelona's phase-out of tourist-flat licences continues. The data pipe to the tax authority still exists. The net position for a foreign owner is unchanged: you need a regional licence, you need the city to allow it at that address, and your nights are visible. What the ruling really changed is who you have to satisfy — and the survivors are the stricter ones.

Portugal & Italy: licences and containment zones

Portugal reversed course twice. The 2023 Mais Habitação nationwide freeze on new Alojamento Local registrations was revoked by Decree-Law 76/2024, which rebuilt the framework — and handed the real power to municipalities, which may declare containment zones and suspend new registrations. Lisbon used it hard: from 6 December 2025, the city applies absolute containment in any parish where AL units already represent 10% or more of permanent housing — meaning no new AL registration is possible in exactly the central parishes foreign buyers target. Porto and Algarve municipalities apply their own, generally lighter, restrictions. The consequence for a buyer: an existing, transferable AL licence attached to a unit is now a real and priced asset, and a flat without one in a contained parish may never be lettable short-term, whatever the agent implies.

Italy took the registration route. Every short-let must carry a CIN (Codice Identificativo Nazionale), obtained through the national database and displayed on every listing and outside the building. On top sit the safety requirements and a growing set of municipal rules — restrictions on keyboxes and remote self check-in in historic centres among them. Italy's short-let income can be taxed under the cedolare secca flat regime, but note the point Americans routinely miss: a favourable Italian flat rate reduces the Italian tax you pay, which reduces the foreign tax credit available against your US bill. A local tax break on foreign rental income is frequently a transfer from the Italian treasury to the IRS, not a saving to you.

The US tax rule most owners miss

Assume you clear the local hurdles and the property lets legally. The US layer applies regardless: as a US citizen or green-card holder you are taxed on worldwide income, so foreign rent is reported on your US return whether or not a single dollar comes home, and whether or not the host country taxed it first. Most owners report on Schedule E, depreciate the building over 30 years under the ADS schedule that applies to foreign residential rental property (not the 27.5 years used domestically), and claim a foreign tax credit on Form 1116 for local income tax paid.

Here is the nuance almost no overseas-property blog mentions, and it is worth real money. Under Treasury Regulation §1.469-1T(e)(3)(ii)(A), an activity is not a "rental activity" for the passive-loss rules if the average period of customer use is seven days or less. A holiday let, by definition, usually is. The consequence: the automatic passive classification that traps losses on a long-term rental does not apply. Whether your short-let is passive then turns on ordinary material participation — and if you materially participate in running it, a loss in the early years (with 30-year depreciation and start-up costs) can be non-passive and offset ordinary income, rather than sitting suspended until you sell. This applies to a foreign property exactly as it does to one in Florida.

The mirror-image risk sits next to it. If you go further and provide substantial services — hotel-style cleaning during the stay, meals, tours, concierge — the activity can be recharacterised as a trade or business reported on Schedule C, which drags the profit into self-employment tax that a Schedule E rental never faces. The dividing line is services, not nightly rates. Owners chasing yield by bundling in "experiences" can cross it without noticing. And separately from income tax: the property itself is not FBAR-reportable, but the foreign bank account that receives the rent almost certainly is, once all your foreign accounts together top $10,000 at any point in the year.

Step-by-step before you buy

  1. Ask the licence question first, not last — before price, before the offer: can this address be short-let today, and is a licence transferable with the sale?
  2. Get the answer in writing from a local lawyer, not from the seller's agent, whose projection is a sales document.
  3. Check the containment or cap layer — Lisbon parish status, Spanish regional licence plus city moratorium, Greek golden-visa restriction, Japanese municipal permit suspension.
  4. Read the condo bylaws — an owners' association can forbid short-lets even where the city allows them, and in Dubai the building's consent is part of the permit.
  5. Register properly — obtain the number (AMA, AL, CIN, regional licence) and display it; from 20 May 2026 an EU listing without a valid number gets removed.
  6. Rebuild the yield model on the legal use — if only a 60-day-plus let is permitted, model long-let rates, not nightly ones.
  7. Line up the US tax position — Schedule E vs Schedule C, 30-year depreciation, the seven-day rule, the Form 1116 credit, and FBAR on the rental account.

If you're not American: what changes

Everything above about the property is identical for any foreign buyer. The Greek ban, the Spanish licence, the Lisbon containment zone, the Italian CIN and the EU registration number apply to the property and the owner, not to the passport — a Canadian, a Brit and an American face exactly the same short-let rules at the same address. What is purely American is the home-country tax wrapper: Schedule E, the 30-year ADS depreciation, the seven-day passive-activity rule, Schedule C self-employment exposure, and FBAR.

If you're not a US person, keep the local rules and swap the US layer for your own country of tax residence. Check three things. (1) Does your home country tax foreign rental income — most residence-based systems do, on a worldwide basis, even where the rent never leaves the country it was earned in. (2) Do you have a foreign asset or account reporting obligation at home, such as Canada's T1135 or the equivalent disclosure in your jurisdiction, once the property or the rental account crosses a threshold. (3) Is there a double-taxation treaty between your country and the country where the property sits, so that local tax on the rent is credited rather than stacked on top. The deduction and depreciation rules will be your country's, not the IRS's. Confirm with a tax adviser where you are resident.

Is a rental-led purchase worth it in 2026?

It still can be — but only if the rental right is verified rather than assumed, and priced into what you pay. The direction of travel across Europe and Japan is unambiguous: registration is becoming universal, enforcement is moving from the town hall to the platform, and the most desirable central neighbourhoods are the ones closing first. That is bad news for the buyer who underwrites nightly rates from a spreadsheet, and quietly good news for the buyer who acquires a property that already carries a valid, transferable licence in a market where new ones are no longer issued — scarcity has a price, and it now attaches to permissions as much as to bricks. The rule for 2026 is simple and unromantic: buy the licence position, not the yield projection. Confirm what this address may legally be used for, model the return on that use, and treat any income above it as upside you never counted on.

Frequently asked questions

Can I still Airbnb a property I buy abroad in 2026?

In most markets yes — with a licence or registration number — and in a growing number of city centres, no. From 20 May 2026 EU Regulation 2024/1028 requires platforms to verify a host registration number and report your activity monthly to the state, so unregistered listings are removed. The real constraint is local: Lisbon, Barcelona and parts of Greece and Italy now restrict or refuse new short-let permits.

Can I rent out a Greek golden visa property on Airbnb?

No. Under Law 5100/2024, a property acquired for a Greek golden visa from 1 September 2024 cannot be short-let — lettings under 60 days without services beyond accommodation and bed linen — anywhere in Greece. The fine reaches €50,000 and the Ministry moves to revoke the permit. Long leases of 60 days or more are allowed, as is leasing to a hotel-type tourism business. Earlier and transitional purchases are not caught.

Does Spain still require a short-term rental registration number?

The national single registry was annulled by the Supreme Court on 21 May 2026 for lack of State competence, while the Digital One-Stop Shop for platform data was upheld. Nothing actually loosened: the regional tourist-licence regimes and municipal caps — which were always the strict layer — remain fully in force, and Barcelona's phase-out continues.

How is foreign Airbnb income taxed for a US citizen?

As worldwide income, on your US return, whether or not the money comes home. Most owners use Schedule E and depreciate the building over 30 years under ADS for foreign residential rental property, crediting foreign income tax on Form 1116. Provide substantial hotel-like services and the activity can become a Schedule C trade or business, exposing the profit to self-employment tax.

What is the seven-day rule for short-term rentals?

Under Treas. Reg. §1.469-1T(e)(3)(ii)(A), an activity is not a "rental activity" for passive-loss purposes if the average period of customer use is seven days or less — which a holiday let usually is. The automatic passive label therefore falls away, and if you materially participate, early-year losses can offset ordinary income instead of being suspended. It applies to a foreign property just as to a US one.
