Costa Rica Real Estate & Residency Guides

Lawyer-reviewed guides for Americans buying property or gaining residency in Costa Rica — the legal and tax reality, not the dream.

Costa Rica lets foreigners own property on exactly the same terms as citizens — full fee-simple title, no residency required — and it's one of the lowest-friction markets in the Americas. Why Americans look at Costa Rica

Costa Rica has been the soft landing for North American buyers for thirty years, and the appeal is easy to understand: a stable democracy with no standing army, a legal system that treats foreign buyers exactly like locals, and a climate that runs from cloud forest to Pacific surf town. A US buyer can hold full fee-simple title — propiedad, recorded in the National Registry — with the unrestricted right to sell, lease, mortgage, gift or pass the property to heirs. There's no foreign-buyer surcharge, no government approval for a residential purchase, and no minimum-stay requirement attached to ownership. You don't even need to set foot in the country to take title.

That ease is also where the danger lives. Because the front door is so open, buyers skip the diligence they'd never skip at home — and walk into the two places Costa Rica genuinely punishes the unprepared: beachfront land inside the Maritime Zone, and the well-meaning advice to "just put it in a Costa Rican corporation." The single most important early step is confirming the property is titled land rather than mere possession (derecho de posesión), which is far harder to defend, finance or resell. Our guide walks the whole transaction and flags both traps before they cost you.

Where to buy — the regions

Costa Rica's markets range from capital-city condos to jungle surf lots, each with its own buyer:

  • Central Valley (San José, Escazú, Santa Ana) — the year-round hub with the best healthcare, infrastructure and long-term rental demand. Best for buyers who want a practical primary base rather than a beach house.
  • Guanacaste (Tamarindo, Nosara, Papagayo) — the dry-season Pacific northwest, the most developed expat and vacation-rental region, with an international airport at Liberia. Strong short-let income, higher prices near the coast.
  • Central Pacific (Jacó, Manuel Antonio, Dominical) — surf towns and rainforest coast within a few hours of San José, popular for holiday homes and rental investment.
  • Southern Zone (Osa, Uvita) — wilder, greener and less developed, favoured by lifestyle buyers seeking privacy and nature over amenities.
  • Arenal & the northern lowlands — lake and volcano country, a quieter inland option for retirees and long-stay buyers.

Wherever you look near the coast, one rule overrides the postcard: a property advertised as "beachfront" may sit in the Maritime Zone as concession land you can never actually own. Always ask, in writing, whether a coastal property is titled or concession, and have your lawyer confirm it in the registry.

The line that catches Americans is the residency threshold's fine print: the investor route requires the property in your personal name — corporate ownership is not accepted — which directly contradicts the reflexive "put it in an S.A." advice. And the timing matters: the US$150,000 floor — and the Law 9996 duty-free import perks that come with it — lapses on 14 July 2026, with the application required to be filed by that date. Absent a renewal by the Legislative Assembly it reverts to US$200,000 from 15 July 2026, so if you are reading this after that date, budget for $200,000 unless your attorney confirms otherwise.

Who Costa Rica suits — and who should look elsewhere

Costa Rica fits Americans who want a low-tax, politically stable place to own a home in the Americas within easy reach of the US, and who value trivial holding costs (0.25% property tax), a flat 15% capital-gains regime with a primary-residence exemption, and a clean investor-residency path that a $150,000+ purchase can unlock. It also suits retirees, who can use the Pensionado or Rentista routes instead. See our guide to buying property in Costa Rica for the full title, tax and structuring walkthrough.

Costa Rica is a weaker fit if your heart is set on true beachfront — much of it is concession land inside the Maritime Zone that a non-resident foreigner can hold only up to 49% of without special structures. It's also riskier if you accept a Costa Rican S.A. without US tax advice: for a US person that "per se" foreign corporation triggers an annual Form 5471 ($10,000 minimum penalty), possible CFC/GILTI/PFIC exposure, and forfeits the Section 121 home-sale exclusion and step-up in basis at death. Costa Rica FAQ

Is Costa Rica a good place for Americans to buy property in 2026?

For a low-tax, politically stable home in the Americas — with a buyer who does the title and structuring diligence properly — it remains one of the most foreign-friendly markets in the world. Closing costs are low, annual property tax is trivial, capital gains are a flat 15% with a primary-residence exemption, and a purchase can double as a residency application — at $150,000 only for applications filed by 14 July 2026, and $200,000 after that absent a renewal.

Do Americans need residency or a visa to buy in Costa Rica?

No. Foreigners buy and fully own titled, fee-simple property with the same rights as citizens — to sell, lease, mortgage and inherit — with no residency or visa required, and you can buy remotely. Buying can, however, open the investor-residency door if $150,000+ is held in your personal name.

What's the catch with beachfront property?

The Maritime Terrestrial Zone (Ley 6043) governs the first 200 meters inland from the high-tide line: the first 50 meters are public and the next 150 are concession land leased from the municipality, not owned. A non-resident foreigner can hold no more than 49% of a concession, so a "beachfront" listing may sit on ground you can never actually own. Always confirm titled vs. concession in the registry.

Should I hold the property in a Costa Rican S.A.?

Usually not, if you're American. A sociedad anónima (S.A.) is a "per se" foreign corporation to the IRS: it triggers an annual Form 5471 ($10,000 minimum penalty), can create CFC/GILTI/PFIC exposure, and forfeits the Section 121 exclusion and step-up in basis at death. It also doesn't qualify for investor residency, which requires personal-name ownership. Get US tax advice before choosing a structure.

Will I owe US tax on a Costa Rican property?

Possibly. Directly-held foreign real estate is not FBAR or Form 8938 reportable, but the US taxes worldwide income, so rental income (Schedule E, depreciated over 30 years) and a future sale are reportable. You can generally credit Costa Rican capital-gains and rental tax you've paid, and Section 121 can shelter a true primary-residence gain — but watch the Section 988 currency angle on a colón mortgage.
