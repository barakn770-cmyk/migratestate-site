Buying Property in Thailand as an American (2026)

MS By The MigrateState Editorial Team · Reviewed by a licensed international real-estate attorney  ·  Updated June 2026  ·  11 min read
A foreigner can own a Thai condominium outright — but never the land under a house. Condo freehold is capped at 49% of a building's floor area; everything else is leasehold. Budget a 2% transfer fee plus seller taxes, and know the two traps that sink Americans: the FET form you must obtain to register freehold, and the nominee company sold as a way to "own" land — which is illegal and now actively prosecuted.

Thailand is one of the easiest places in Asia for a foreigner to actually own a home — and one of the easiest to get badly wrong. The good news is real: an American can buy a beachfront condo in Phuket or a city unit in Bangkok, hold true freehold title in their own name, and never need a visa or residency to do it. The bad news is that the rules are narrow and unforgiving in exactly the places where eager buyers cut corners. Thai law draws a hard line between what you can own (the apartment) and what you cannot (the land), and the "creative" structures brokers use to blur that line are not just risky — several are crimes that authorities are now prosecuting harder than ever.

This guide walks the whole transaction with 2026 figures and flags each trap before it costs you: the 49% quota that decides whether your condo is freehold or leasehold, the foreign-currency paperwork the Land Office demands, the leasehold renewal promise that isn't enforceable, and the nominee-company pitch that can void your title outright.

Can Americans own property in Thailand?

Yes — but only in specific legal forms, and the form matters more than anything else in the deal. A foreigner can own a condominium unit on a freehold basis, recorded in their own name with a unit title deed, provided the building is within its foreign-ownership quota. That is the cleanest, most secure way for an American to own Thai real estate, and it requires no residency, visa, or work permit.

What you cannot do is own land freehold. Under the Land Code, foreigners are, with very narrow and rarely-used exceptions, barred from holding title to land — which means the land beneath a house, villa, or townhouse is off-limits. You can own the building as a separate asset and lease the land long-term, but you will not get a land title in your name. This single distinction — condo freehold yes, land freehold no — drives every structuring decision below.

The 49% condo quota — and the FET form

The Condominium Act lets foreigners own up to 49% of the total saleable floor area of any condominium building on a freehold basis; the remaining 51% must stay in Thai hands. Two details trip people up. First, the quota is measured in square meters of floor area, not by the number of units — so a building can run out of "foreign quota" even while units sit empty. Second, when the foreign side of a building is already full, developers will offer the same unit on a registered leasehold instead of freehold, and the marketing rarely makes the downgrade obvious. Always confirm in writing whether your specific unit is being sold freehold within quota or leasehold outside it.

Here is the nuance most blogs skip, and it is the one that actually stops transfers at the Land Office: to register a condo freehold in a foreigner's name, the full purchase price must be remitted into Thailand from abroad in foreign currency. When that money lands, the receiving Thai bank issues a Foreign Exchange Transaction form (FET, the document that replaced the old "Tor Tor 3"), and the Land Office requires that FET to register the foreign-freehold title. If you fund the purchase from money already inside Thailand, or skip the FET, you can be blocked from registering freehold even though you are otherwise within quota. Plan the wire — in foreign currency, with the unit and buyer correctly stated — before you pay, not after.

Not sure if your unit is freehold within quota or leasehold? It changes your title, your taxes, and your resale. A MigrateState-matched Thai attorney will verify the building's foreign quota and structure the FET correctly before you wire a deposit.

A live 2026 wrinkle worth knowing: Thai regulators have been formally weighing whether to reduce the foreign condo quota, with proposals to amend the Condominium Act and bring the ceiling down to roughly 30–39%. As of mid-2026 the 49% quota remains fully in effect, and officials have signalled that any reduction would apply only to new transactions — existing freehold titles would be protected. It is a reason to verify the current rule on the day you buy, not a reason to panic.

Land, leasehold, and the renewal trap

Because foreigners cannot own land, the standard way to "buy a villa" is a 30-year registered lease of the land, often combined with foreign ownership of the structure on it. A registered lease is a legitimate, recordable interest — but its limits are widely misrepresented. The 30-year term is the maximum the law will register for residential land, and any "renewal" beyond it is purely contractual: it is a promise from the current landowner, not a property right that binds future owners or survives the lessor's death or sale. Thai courts have declined to enforce pre-packaged "30 + 30 + 30" renewal schemes as if they created a 90-year right. Treat a lease as worth its first 30 years and price it accordingly; anything beyond that is a hope, not a guarantee.

Other land-access tools you'll hear about — a usufruct (a registered lifetime right to use and profit from land) or a superficies (a right to own buildings on someone else's land) — are real and sometimes useful, but they are use-rights, not ownership, and they don't convert into a saleable land title. The honest summary: if you want true freehold in Thailand, buy a condo; if you want a house, accept that you are buying a building plus a time-limited interest in the land, and structure it with a lawyer who works for you, not the developer.

The nominee company — why it's illegal

Sooner or later a broker will offer the "solution" to the land problem: set up a Thai limited company, give it 51% Thai shareholders, and have the company buy the land while you control it. Marketed as routine, this is the most dangerous advice in the Thai market. Using Thai shareholders who hold their stake on a foreigner's behalf — nominees with no real economic stake — is illegal under the Foreign Business Act and the Land Code. It is not a grey area.

The consequences are severe and have teeth in 2026:

  • Criminal liability for both the foreigner and the Thai nominees — fines up to THB 1 million and imprisonment of up to three years, plus deportation for the foreigner.
  • Courts can void the land transaction and order the title cancelled — the Supreme Court has unwound deals where a Thai company was only a front for foreign control.
  • Forced dissolution of the company.
  • Enforcement has been escalating: since late 2025 the Department of Business Development has run an AI-driven analytics system that cross-checks corporate filings to flag suspected nominee structures, and from January 2026 new company registrations with foreign participation require the Thai shareholders to show three months of bank statements proving they actually funded their shares.

A Thai company that genuinely operates a business and genuinely needs the land can lawfully own it — that is different. But a shell whose only purpose is to park a foreigner's villa is exactly what the law targets, and "everyone does it" is not a defense when your title is cancelled. If a seller's whole plan depends on a nominee company, walk away or restructure as a proper lease.

Costs, taxes, and the buying process

Thailand's transaction taxes are collected at the Land Office on the day of transfer and are calculated on the higher of the sale price or the government appraised value. The headline charges:

Charge Typical 2026 rate Who usually pays
Transfer fee 2% of appraised value Often split 50/50 (negotiable)
Specific Business Tax (SBT) 3.3% (if seller owned <5 yrs or is a company) Seller
Stamp duty 0.5% (only if SBT does not apply) Seller
Withholding tax Progressive (individual) / 1% (company) Seller
Annual land & building tax ~0.02%–0.10% of assessed value Owner (ongoing)

A few practical points. SBT and stamp duty are mutually exclusive — a seller pays one or the other, never both — and SBT applies when the seller has held under five years or is a company. The Thai government has periodically cut the transfer fee to as low as 0.01% as a market stimulus, but those reduced rates are reserved for Thai nationals and capped property values; foreign buyers pay the standard 2%. Although these charges are allocated "by custom" between buyer and seller, all of it is negotiable and should be spelled out in the sale-and-purchase agreement. Legal fees and due-diligence costs are extra and modest, and you should engage your own lawyer rather than the developer's.

The process itself is straightforward once the structure is right: you sign a reservation agreement and pay a deposit, your lawyer runs title and quota due diligence (and checks for liens at the Land Office), you sign the sale-and-purchase agreement, remit the price in foreign currency to generate the FET, and complete the transfer and registration at the Land Office, where ownership passes. For a pre-construction unit, use an escrow arrangement or staged payments and confirm the developer's foreign quota is genuinely available for your unit, not just the project overall.

The US tax angle

For a US citizen or green-card holder, owning a Thai condo directly is relatively clean on the US side. Directly-held foreign real estate is not reportable on the FBAR (FinCEN 114) or Form 8938 — those cover financial accounts and assets, not a home. But the Thai bank account you'll almost certainly open to receive rent or pay maintenance is a foreign financial account: once your non-US accounts together exceed $10,000 at any point in the year, the FBAR is required, and Form 8938 may be too depending on your filing status and where you live.

Rental income goes on Schedule E at ordinary rates (the Foreign Earned Income Exclusion does not shelter rental income), with the building depreciated over 30 years under the Alternative Depreciation System that US law mandates for property used predominantly abroad — versus 27.5 years for a US rental. You can generally claim a foreign tax credit for Thai tax paid, and on a true primary residence the Section 121 exclusion can shelter up to $250,000/$500,000 of gain.

The nuance most blogs miss is the currency point, and it actually cuts in your favor here. Thai property is priced in baht, and a baht-denominated mortgage can create a Section 988 "phantom" currency gain — US tax owed on an exchange-rate move even if the property's value never changed. But foreigners generally cannot get Thai mortgages (domestic banks rarely lend to non-residents for residential purchase), so most Americans buy cash — and a cash purchase with no foreign-currency debt sidesteps the Section 988 mortgage trap entirely. The flip side: if you do arrange any baht financing (or an offshore-bank product marketed to foreigners), get US tax advice first, because that is exactly where the currency surprise lives. And resist the broker's nominee-company pitch for a second reason beyond Thai law — a foreign company in the chain can drag a Form 5471 filing, CFC/GILTI exposure, and loss of the Section 121 exclusion into your US return.

Step-by-step: how to buy safely

  1. Decide condo vs. house early. If you want true freehold, target a condominium; if you want a house, accept a building-plus-land-lease and budget for the 30-year reality.
  2. Engage your own Thai attorney — independent of the developer, agent, and any "free" lawyer they offer.
  3. Verify the foreign quota for your specific unit in writing, and whether it's sold freehold-within-quota or leasehold.
  4. Run Land Office due diligence — title deed, encumbrances, liens, and (for a villa) the lease registration and landowner.
  5. Plan the FET. Remit the full price into Thailand in foreign currency so the bank issues the Foreign Exchange Transaction form the Land Office needs for freehold.
  6. Reject any nominee-company structure for land; if a house deal depends on one, restructure as a proper registered lease or walk away.
  7. Decide US ownership treatment with a cross-border advisor before closing, and keep every receipt for your US cost basis.
  8. Complete transfer and registration at the Land Office — ownership passes on registration, not on signing.

If you're not American: what changes

The Thai rules in this guide — condo freehold capped at the 49% foreign quota, the ban on foreigners owning land, the 30-year leasehold limit, the FET foreign-currency requirement, the 2% transfer fee and seller taxes, and the illegality of nominee companies — are set by Thailand and apply to every foreign buyer equally, regardless of passport. A British, German, Australian, or Israeli buyer faces the same quota, the same land ban, and the same FET paperwork as an American. What differs is only the tax wrapper back home.

The entire "US tax angle" section above — FBAR, Form 8938, Schedule E, the 30-year ADS depreciation, Section 121, and the Section 988 currency point — is US-specific and does not apply to non-Americans. If you're not a US person, swap those rules for your own and check three things with a tax adviser in your country of residence: (1) whether your home country taxes foreign rental income and capital gains; (2) whether you must report foreign assets, accounts, or holding companies at home; and (3) whether your country has a double-taxation treaty with Thailand that affects how that income is taxed. The nominee-company warning applies to everyone — it is Thai criminal law, not a US tax issue — and the FET requirement is the same for all foreign buyers seeking condo freehold.

Is it worth it in 2026?

For an American who wants a genuinely owned home in Asia — sun, low cost of living, strong rental demand in Phuket and Bangkok — a condo within the foreign quota is one of the most secure foreign-ownership setups available, and the US tax treatment of direct ownership is forgiving. Transaction taxes are moderate, annual property tax is trivial, and a cash purchase avoids the currency trap that bites in mortgage markets.

The ways buyers get hurt are entirely avoidable, and almost always involve trying to own what Thai law won't let foreigners own. Buying a villa through a nominee company can cost you the property and your liberty; treating a "renewable" 30-year lease as a 90-year right overpays for a promise that won't bind the next landowner; and skipping the FET can block your freehold registration after you've already paid. Buy the condo, obtain the FET, get an independent lawyer for the title and a US cross-border advisor for the structure, and Thailand stays exactly as easy as it looks at the front door.

Frequently asked questions

Can Americans own property in Thailand?

Yes, but only in certain forms. A foreigner can own a condominium unit freehold, in their own name, as long as the building hasn't exceeded its 49% foreign-ownership quota (measured by floor area). Foreigners cannot own land freehold, so a house or villa is bought as the building plus a long-term land lease. No residency or visa is required to buy a condo.

What is the 49% foreign quota in a Thai condo?

Under the Condominium Act, no more than 49% of a building's total saleable floor area may be owned freehold by foreigners; at least 51% stays Thai. It's measured in square meters, not units. If the foreign quota is full, a foreigner can only take the unit on a registered leasehold. In 2026 regulators were debating cutting the quota to 30–39%, but 49% remains in effect and any change would apply only to new transactions.

What taxes and fees apply when buying a condo in Thailand?

The main Land Office charge is a 2% transfer fee on the appraised value, commonly split between buyer and seller. The seller also pays either 3.3% Specific Business Tax (if they owned the unit under five years or are a company) or 0.5% stamp duty, plus withholding tax. Foreign buyers don't get the reduced Thai-national stimulus rates. Annual land and building tax on a residential condo is roughly 0.02%–0.10% of assessed value.

Can a foreigner own land in Thailand through a Thai company?

Not as a workaround. Using Thai nationals as nominee shareholders to hold land for a foreigner is illegal under the Foreign Business Act and the Land Code. Penalties include fines up to THB 1 million, up to three years' imprisonment, deportation, and courts voiding the transaction and cancelling the title. Enforcement tightened in 2025–2026, including AI flagging of nominee companies and bank-statement checks on Thai shareholders from January 2026.

Do Americans pay US tax on a Thai property?

Yes — the US taxes worldwide income. Rental income goes on Schedule E, with the building depreciated over 30 years under the ADS method for foreign property. Directly-held real estate isn't itself FBAR- or Form 8938-reportable, but a Thai bank account you open for the purchase often is, once your foreign accounts exceed $10,000. You can usually claim a foreign tax credit for Thai tax paid.
