Dominican Republic Real Estate & Residency Guides

Lawyer-reviewed guides for Americans buying property or gaining residency in the Dominican Republic — the legal and tax reality, not the dream.

The Dominican Republic is one of the most open real-estate markets in the Western Hemisphere for Americans — full freehold title, no residency required, and no foreign-buyer surcharge. Closing costs run about 4–8%, the transfer tax is 3%, and the annual IPI property tax is 1% but only on value above roughly US$182,000. The thing that decides your outcome here isn't the tax — it's the title. The guides below go deep on each; this page is the map.

Why Americans look at the Dominican Republic

For a US buyer, the Dominican Republic removes most of the barriers that other markets throw up. There are no nationality quotas, no approval boards, and no coastal-ownership ban of the kind you meet in Mexico or Costa Rica. An American can fly into Punta Cana, choose a condo, and take full freehold title to it — recorded in the national registry, with the unrestricted right to sell, lease, mortgage, and inherit — without ever becoming a resident or holding a visa. Even beachfront condominiums, the exact category restricted elsewhere, are freely and directly ownable here. Prices remain a fraction of comparable Caribbean coastlines, tourism-zone rental yields are strong, and a generous incentive called CONFOTUR can wipe out property tax for up to fifteen years.

The catch is that the wide-open front door is exactly why diligence matters more here than almost anywhere. Because the barriers to buying are so low, buyers skip the checks they would never skip at home — and the DR's weak spot is not its tax code, it is its land registry. Falsified title certificates and land that was never properly surveyed (the missing deslinde) are the two things that turn a dream purchase into a lawsuit. The only reliable protection is a direct check at the Registro de Títulos for a current certificate of legal status — never the seller's or developer's photocopy. Handled correctly, the openness is a genuine advantage all the way through the deal.

Where to buy — the regions

The DR behaves like several markets stacked on one island, each with a distinct buyer:

  • Punta Cana / Bávaro — the tourism engine of the country and the heart of CONFOTUR-approved development. This is where short-term rental demand is strongest and where most branded-resort condos sit. Best for buyers chasing yield and a hands-off holiday-let.
  • Santo Domingo — the capital and the only true year-round city, with a colonial-zone core, real local rental demand, and the professional infrastructure (lawyers, banks, hospitals) a full-time relocator wants. Suits those who value city life over beach life.
  • Cabarete / Sosúa — the north-coast watersports belt, long favoured by a mixed European and North American expat crowd. Gentler prices than Punta Cana, an established community, and a laid-back base for remote workers and active retirees.
  • Las Terrenas (Samaná) — the boutique, French- and Italian-inflected peninsula town, more upscale and low-density than the mega-resorts. Best for buyers who want charm and walkability over big-resort amenities.

One rule spans all of them: the region does not confer the tax break. CONFOTUR benefits attach to the approved project, not to the town — a resale home or a bare lot in Punta Cana or Las Terrenas does not qualify just because of its location. Always confirm the CONFOTUR resolution number and approval date in writing before you rely on it.

The line most buyers misjudge is the last two together. The 27% headline capital-gains rate looks steep beside Costa Rica's 15%, but the DGII inflation-indexes your cost basis, which on a property held several years can cut the taxable gain by half or more. And CONFOTUR — worth thousands at closing and potentially US$25,000-plus in avoided IPI over its life — only applies to registered tourism projects. Model the total both ways, and get the CONFOTUR paperwork verified, before you fall for a listing.

Who the Dominican Republic suits — and who should look elsewhere

The DR fits buyers who want a low-cost, low-holding-tax place to own in the Caribbean with strong rental demand; investors targeting CONFOTUR-exempt tourism condos; and anyone attracted to a real path to residency and even a second passport — a US$200,000 investment qualifies for investor residency under Decree 950-01, and property owners can naturalize on a reduced track (as little as six months of residency) while keeping US citizenship, since the DR permits dual nationality.

The DR is a weaker fit if you need the legal certainty of a European title system with title insurance, or if you are not prepared to pay for independent title and deslinde verification — the buyers who get hurt almost always trusted a photocopied certificate and skipped the registry check. It is also less compelling if an EU base or a fast EU passport is your real goal; for that, our best golden visas comparison is a better starting point.

Dominican Republic FAQ

Is the Dominican Republic a good place for Americans to buy property in 2026?

For a low-cost Caribbean base with full freehold ownership, strong tourism-rental demand, a genuine residency path, and one of the region's most generous tax incentives in CONFOTUR, it is hard to beat on paper. The main risk is specific rather than systemic: the title. Verify ownership at the Registro de Títulos and confirm the deslinde, and the open front door stays an advantage.

Do I need to be a resident to buy in the Dominican Republic?

No. There is no residency or visa requirement, no nationality quota, and no approval board for a standard residential purchase — including beachfront condos. Buying can, however, open the door to residency: a real-estate investment of at least US$200,000 qualifies for investor residency under the expedited Decree 950-01.

Which part of the Dominican Republic is best for a first purchase?

It depends on the goal: Punta Cana for rental yield and CONFOTUR condos, Santo Domingo for year-round city living and local rental demand, Cabarete/Sosúa for an established expat community and gentler prices, and Las Terrenas for boutique charm. Confirm CONFOTUR status project by project, not by town.

How risky is buying property in the DR compared with Europe?

The tax and ownership rules are straightforward; the registry is the weak point. Unlike France or Spain, there is no title insurance and no notaire-policed search you can lean on — funds security and title verification are on you. That makes an independent Dominican attorney and a current Certificate of Legal Status non-negotiable rather than optional.

Will owning DR property complicate my US taxes?

Directly-held foreign real estate is not itself FBAR- or Form 8938-reportable, but the Dominican bank account you open to pay IPI and fees can be, once your foreign accounts exceed US$10,000. Watch the capital-gains mismatch: the DR taxes your inflation-indexed peso gain at 27% while the US taxes your nominal dollar gain, so the foreign tax credit may not fully wash out. Model both with a cross-border advisor before you sell.
