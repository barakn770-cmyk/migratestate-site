Buying Property in Dubai as an American + Golden Visa: 2026 Guide

MS By The MigrateState Editorial Team · Reviewed by a licensed international real-estate attorney  ·  Updated June 2026  ·  8 min read
Yes — Americans can fully own property in Dubai, and it's one of the simplest markets in the world to buy in. US citizens can hold freehold title in 60+ designated areas with no residency required. Dubai charges zero property, income, and capital-gains tax, and a purchase of AED 2 million (~US$545K) earns a 10-year Golden Visa. The catch for Americans: the US still taxes your worldwide income.

While Europe tightens its residency-by-investment programs, Dubai has gone the other way — actively courting foreign buyers with full ownership, no tax, and one of the most generous property-to-residency deals available anywhere. For Americans wanting a high-yield, tax-light base in a fast-growing global hub, the UAE is now one of the strongest options on the board. Here's the 2026 reality.

Can Americans own property in Dubai?

Yes — fully and outright. Citizens of the US and every other country can buy freehold property in Dubai's designated zones, with the same ownership rights a local would have: you own the property and the land it sits on, in perpetuity. You do not need to be a UAE resident; a valid US passport is enough to complete the purchase, even remotely.

Freehold areas & how ownership works

Dubai's Freehold Law opened the market to foreign buyers in specific freehold areas — now 60+ communities. The most popular among international buyers include Dubai Marina, Downtown Dubai, Palm Jumeirah, and Jumeirah Village Circle (JVC). Outside freehold zones, foreigners can typically only buy leasehold (long-term lease) rather than full title — so confirming a property is genuinely freehold is the first due-diligence step. Ownership is registered with the Dubai Land Department (DLD), which issues the title deed.

The zero-tax advantage — and the US catch

This is Dubai's headline draw: zero property tax, zero income tax, and zero capital gains tax. There is no annual property tax bill. The main ongoing cost is service charges (building/community fees) of roughly AED 10–50 per square foot per year, depending on amenities.

But here is the nuance most guides aimed at Americans skip: UAE zero-tax does not free a US citizen from US tax. The United States taxes citizens on worldwide income regardless of where they live. So rental income from your Dubai apartment is reportable on your US return (Schedule E), and a future sale is a US capital gain. Because the UAE charges no tax, there's no foreign tax credit to offset it — meaning the income can be fully US-taxable. A UAE bank account (often needed for service charges) can also trigger FBAR and FATCA reporting. Plan this with a cross-border CPA before you buy.

The Golden Visa through property

Unlike Portugal, which scrapped its property route, the UAE rewards property buyers with residency. The tiers in 2026:

Property valueResidency
AED 2,000,000+ (~US$545K)10-year Golden Visa
Any completed freehold unit (sole owner; no minimum)2-year investor/property visa; joint owners each need AED 400,000

The AED 2M threshold can be met with a single property or several combined. There is no local sponsor or employer required, your family can be included, and — crucially — the residency stays valid even if you spend more than six months a year outside the UAE, making it a true flexible base. Eligibility is certified via a property status certificate from the DLD, which assesses the purchase price on the title deed rather than a later market valuation.

Mortgages for non-residents

Financing is available to non-residents, but on tighter terms than for residents:

FactorNon-resident (2026)Resident
Down payment40%–50%20%–25%
Interest rateFrom ~4% (variable)From ~4%

Many international buyers pay cash, both for speed and because the larger non-resident deposit narrows the leverage advantage. If you finance, note that a mortgaged property still counts toward the Golden Visa threshold based on its total value.

Purchase costs

Dubai's transaction costs are moderate and transparent. Budget for:

  • DLD transfer fee: 4% of the purchase price (the main cost), usually paid by the buyer.
  • Agency fee: typically 2% + VAT.
  • Registration and admin fees (trustee fee): AED 4,000 + 5% VAT for properties priced at AED 500,000 and above; AED 2,000 + 5% VAT below that threshold. Paid to the DLD-licensed trustee office that processes the transfer.
  • Mortgage registration (if financing): 0.25% of the loan.

Step-by-step: buying in Dubai as an American

  1. Confirm the area is freehold and the property is eligible for foreign ownership.
  2. Engage an independent buyer's lawyer (separate from the agent and developer).
  3. Sign the MOU (Form F) and pay a deposit, typically 10%.
  4. Obtain a No Objection Certificate (NOC) from the developer.
  5. Complete the transfer at the DLD, pay the 4% fee, and receive the title deed.
  6. Apply for the Golden Visa (if AED 2M+) using the DLD property status certificate.
  7. Set up US tax compliance — Schedule E, FBAR — before year-end.

Is buying property in Dubai worth it in 2026?

For Americans, the appeal is rare: full freehold ownership, strong rental yields, no local tax, and a flexible 10-year residency — in a hub between Europe, Asia and Africa. The discipline required is on the US side: the IRS still wants its share, and without a foreign tax credit the planning matters more, not less. Buy in a true freehold area, use independent legal counsel, and line up your US tax plan first — and Dubai is one of the most buyer-friendly markets an American can enter.

Frequently asked questions

Can Americans buy property in Dubai?

Yes. US citizens can fully own freehold property in Dubai's 60+ designated freehold areas, with full ownership rights, and you do not need to be a UAE resident — a valid US passport is enough.

Does buying property get you a Golden Visa?

Yes. A purchase of AED 2 million or more (~US$545K) qualifies for a 10-year Golden Visa; A sole owner of any completed freehold property now qualifies for a 2-year investor visa (the AED 750,000 floor was removed in April 2026); joint owners each need AED 400,000.

Is there property tax in Dubai?

No. Dubai has zero property, income and capital-gains tax. Buyers pay a one-time 4% DLD transfer fee plus annual service charges (~AED 10–50/sq ft). US citizens still owe US tax on worldwide income.

Can a US citizen get a mortgage in Dubai?

Yes, but as a non-resident expect a 40–50% down payment (vs 20–25% for residents). Non-resident rates in 2026 start from around 4%.

Do I pay US tax on a Dubai property?

Potentially yes. Despite UAE's zero tax, US citizens are taxed on worldwide income: rental income (Schedule E) and resale gains are US-reportable, and a UAE bank account can trigger FBAR/FATCA.
