Why Americans look at Spain

Spain is the most-searched European destination for American property buyers, and the draw is obvious: sun, a long coastline, big cities with deep expat roots, and prices that still undercut comparable spots in California or the US Northeast. The buying process is open and well-trodden. Spain places no restrictions on foreign ownership, so a US citizen can buy an apartment, a villa or land with the same legal rights as a Spanish national, hold it in their own name, and complete the purchase remotely through a lawyer under power of attorney. The one prerequisite is a NIE, the foreigner tax number.

What changed for 2026 is the politics around the purchase, not the purchase itself. Spain ended its residency-by-investment route, floated an eye-catching tax on non-EU buyers, and reformed parts of its transfer-tax scale. Separating what is actually law from what is political noise is where most guides fall down — and where a US buyer can save themselves a lot of needless worry. The short version: you still pay the same property taxes as a Spaniard, but you no longer get residency from a purchase, and you should keep one eye on a draft bill that may never pass.

Where to buy — the regions

Spain's regions differ not just in lifestyle but in the ITP transfer-tax rate, which each autonomous community sets itself:

  • Costa del Sol (Málaga, Marbella) — the prestige southern coast in Andalusia (~7% ITP), with the deepest international-buyer and luxury market.
  • Barcelona & Catalonia — a world city with strong rental demand; Catalonia uses progressive ITP brackets, so model the rate on your price.
  • Madrid — the capital, strong year-round rental demand and one of the lower ITP regimes among the big regions.
  • Valencia, Alicante & Costa Blanca — a popular value coast; the Valencian Community moved to 9% ITP on resale homes under €1M (11% above) from 1 June 2026, a regional change, not a national one.
  • Balearic Islands (Mallorca, Ibiza) — premium island market with high demand and higher price points.
  • Canary Islands — a year-round-climate option with its own tax quirks, popular with remote workers and second-home buyers.

Because ITP is regional, the same €400,000 home can carry a meaningfully different tax bill in Andalusia versus Catalonia — always confirm the rate for the exact community where the property sits.

Who Spain suits — and who should look elsewhere

Spain fits lifestyle and value buyers who want a liquid, well-regulated European market where foreigners pay the same taxes as locals, and who are comfortable budgeting the real 11%–15% entry cost. Start with the full guide to buying property in Spain, and if you intend to live there, read what replaced the investor route in the Spain Golden Visa update.

Spain is a weaker fit if you were counting on a purchase to deliver residency — that door closed in April 2025, and the remaining routes require you to genuinely reside and show income. It's also less appealing if the (still hypothetical) non-EU surcharge would derail your plans, or if you want the lowest possible transaction costs — Spain's all-in 11%–15% is among the higher entry costs in this comparison set. US citizens should also budget for the currency wrinkle: a strengthening euro can trigger a taxable "phantom" gain on a mortgage payoff.

Spain FAQ

Is Spain a good place for Americans to buy property in 2026?

For lifestyle and value it still earns its popularity — the buying process is clean, the market is liquid, and foreigners pay the same taxes as locals. The 2026 caveats are political rather than procedural: a purchase no longer buys residency, and there's a live (if unlikely) proposal to tax non-EU buyers more heavily. Buy with an independent lawyer, budget the real 11%–15%, and keep an eye on that draft bill.

Does buying property in Spain give me residency?

No. Spain ended its Golden Visa on 3 April 2025, so a purchase no longer grants any residence permit. Americans who want to live in Spain apply separately — most commonly through the non-lucrative visa (NLV) for those with passive income or savings, or the digital-nomad visa for remote workers.

Which region should a first-time buyer look at?

It depends on the goal and the ITP rate, which is set regionally. The Costa del Sol and Balearics lead for prestige and international demand, Madrid and Barcelona for year-round city rental, and Valencia, Alicante or the Canaries for value. Confirm your community's exact ITP rate before you offer.

How long does it take to buy?

A straightforward purchase typically completes in two to three months — getting your NIE, opening a bank account, running due diligence through an independent lawyer, signing the arras deposit contract, paying the purchase tax, then signing the escritura and registering the property.

Will I owe US tax on a Spanish property?

Possibly. The US taxes worldwide income, so Spanish rental income and a future sale are reportable, with the foreign tax credit relieving most double tax since Spain levies its own tax. A Spanish bank account can trigger FBAR/FATCA, and a stronger euro can create a taxable currency gain on a mortgage payoff.
