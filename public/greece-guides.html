Greece Real Estate & Residency Guides

Lawyer-reviewed guides for Americans buying property or residency in Greece.

Greece is the rare European market where an American can buy a home outright, pay one of the lowest purchase taxes on the continent, and still qualify for a genuine real-estate Golden Visa. The transfer tax is just 3.09%, all-in closing costs land around 7%–10%, and — unlike Spain and Portugal — the residency route still runs on property you actually own. The two things that decide your outcome: buying at the right Golden Visa threshold, and planning the US tax layer before you sign. The guides below go deep; this page is the map.

Why Americans look at Greece

For a US buyer, Greece quietly solves the affordability problem that the rest of the Mediterranean creates. Prices in Athens and on many islands still sit below comparable spots in Italy, France or Spain; the purchase tax is refreshingly light; and Greece places no general restriction on foreign ownership, so an American can buy an apartment, a villa or land in their own name and complete the whole transaction remotely through a lawyer holding power of attorney. The only true prerequisite is a Greek tax number — the AFM — which a lawyer can obtain for you in days.

The bigger draw is what Greece kept while its neighbours walked away. Spain scrapped its Golden Visa in 2025 and Portugal killed its real-estate route, but Greece still grants residency for a qualifying property purchase — the go-to option for Americans who want an EU footing tied to a tangible asset rather than a fund subscription. The catches are procedural rather than political: Greek title and the cadastre demand a careful independent lawyer, and the country's celebrated tax holidays can quietly leave an American fully exposed to the IRS. Those are the details our guides exist to get right.

Where to buy — the regions

Greece is a patchwork of very different markets, each with its own buyer and its own Golden Visa threshold:

  • Athens (Attica) — the capital and the deepest rental market, but a premium zone where the Golden Visa threshold jumps to €800,000. Best for buyers who want a real year-round city with strong long-let demand (short-letting is off-limits if the property is your Golden Visa investment — see below).
  • Thessaloniki — Greece's northern hub and university city, also in the €800,000 tier. A genuine urban economy with steadier local demand than the resort islands.
  • Mykonos & Santorini — the prestige islands and also €800,000-tier zones. Strong seasonal appeal and high entry prices — but be careful: holiday-letting is banned for a property acquired under the Golden Visa (Law 5100/2024), so the short-let income case only works if you buy without using the property for the visa.
  • The Peloponnese & mainland regions — most areas outside the premium zones sit at the €400,000 Golden Visa tier, offering the best value-to-residency math for Americans who don't need a marquee address.
  • Smaller islands & the Cyclades (under ~3,100 residents) — many fall in the €400,000 band, mixing lifestyle appeal with a lower entry point than Mykonos or Santorini.
  • Heritage & restoration projects anywhere — a listed building to restore, or a commercial-to-residential conversion, unlocks the €250,000 route regardless of location.

One rule catches many buyers off guard: the €400,000 and €800,000 tiers require a single property of at least 120 square metres of habitable area — you cannot stack several smaller units to hit the threshold. Only the €250,000 restoration route escapes the size minimum.

The most important — and most misread — line is capital gains. Greece has suspended CGT on individual property sales, which sellers cheer. But for an American it can be a trap: the US foreign tax credit only offsets tax you actually paid, so if Greece charges zero, the IRS taxes your entire gain (up to 20% federal, plus the 3.8% net investment income tax) with nothing to credit against it. Model the US exit tax before you buy, not when you sell.

Who Greece suits — and who should look elsewhere

Greece fits Americans who want an EU residency backed by a real, usable Mediterranean asset rather than a fund; buyers hunting value, especially in the €400,000 regions away from the premium zones; and families, since one qualifying investment can include a spouse, dependent children and dependent parents, with no minimum-stay requirement to keep the permit. If your priority is a tangible property-backed EU base, Greece is one of the strongest cases in Europe right now. See our guide to buying property in Greece and the Greece Golden Visa guide for the full mechanics.

Greece is a weaker fit if you want a fast route to an EU passport — this is residency, and citizenship is a separate, far longer road requiring 183+ days a year for seven years plus a language test. It is also less compelling if you're chasing the very highest rental yields, or if your budget sits below the €250,000/€400,000 levels where the thresholds and 120 sqm size rule make sense. For those goals, our best golden visas and Greece vs Portugal comparisons are a better starting point.

Greece FAQ

Is Greece a good place for Americans to buy property in 2026?

For value, lifestyle and EU mobility, it is one of the strongest cases in Europe right now: a 3.09% transfer tax, a temporary VAT break on new builds, prices that still undercut the western Mediterranean, and a real Golden Visa its neighbours have abandoned. The risks are specific rather than systemic — tangled title, cadastre mismatches, and a capital-gains holiday that offers Americans no US relief at sale.

Do Americans need residency or a visa to buy in Greece?

No. Greece imposes no general restriction on foreign ownership, so you can buy outright with the same rights as a Greek national and no need to live there. You only need a Greek tax number (AFM), which a lawyer can obtain remotely. Buying can, however, open the door to residency if you invest at the qualifying Golden Visa threshold.

Which region is best for a first purchase?

It depends on the goal. The €400,000 regions in the Peloponnese and mainland offer the best value-to-residency math; Athens and Thessaloniki give year-round long-let demand at the €800,000 tier; Mykonos and Santorini are lifestyle plays — but remember that a property used for the Golden Visa cannot be short-let at all under Law 5100/2024, so holiday-let income and the visa are mutually exclusive on the same unit. A genuine restoration project anywhere can unlock the €250,000 route.

Can I short-let a Greece Golden Visa property on Airbnb?

No. Under Law 5100/2024, a property acquired for the Golden Visa on or after 1 September 2024 cannot be let short-term (under 60 days, platform-listed or not) anywhere in Greece for as long as you hold the permit — with fines up to €50,000 and possible revocation of the permit. Long leases of 60 days or more are still allowed. Build your yield model on long-term rent.

How long does the whole process take?

Buying itself typically runs two to four months from offer to registered deed. The Golden Visa permit usually follows within a few months of the purchase, though wider processing backlogs can extend that timeline.

Will I owe US tax on a Greek property?

Possibly. The US taxes worldwide income, so Greek rent and a future sale are reportable, and a Greek bank account can trigger FBAR/FATCA. The twist: because Greece has suspended capital-gains tax on individual sales, there may be no Greek tax to credit — so the IRS can tax your entire gain. Plan the US side before you sign.
