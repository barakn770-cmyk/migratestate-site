Buying Property in Brazil as an American (2026 Guide)

MS By The MigrateState Editorial Team · Reviewed by a licensed international real-estate attorney  ·  Updated July 2026  ·  10 min read
Yes — an American can buy urban property in Brazil on the same legal footing as a Brazilian, with no visa or residency required. All you need first is a CPF tax ID. Can Americans buy property in Brazil?

For urban property — an apartment in Rio, a house in Florianópolis, a beach condo in Bahia, a city lot in São Paulo — the answer is a clean yes. Brazil places no nationality restriction on urban real estate. A US citizen buys in their own name, holds full freehold title, and is registered in the same public property registry as a Brazilian owner. There is no trust structure to build, no local partner to find, no minimum investment, and no permission to request. You do not need a visa, and you do not need to set foot in Brazil (a properly notarised and apostilled power of attorney lets a Brazilian lawyer sign for you).

What you do need is a CPF — the Cadastro de Pessoas Físicas, Brazil's individual taxpayer number. Nothing happens without it: you cannot sign a deed, open a bank account, connect utilities or pay tax without a CPF. It is free, and a US citizen can apply through a Brazilian consulate or the Receita Federal online portal, typically getting a number within one to three weeks. Get it before you start viewing, not after you've agreed a price.

The second thing worth internalising early: buying property in Brazil grants you no immigration status whatsoever. There is no Brazilian "golden visa" attached to residential real estate for ordinary buyers, and ownership does not extend your stay. Americans get a visa-free tourist entry, but living in Brazil requires a separate immigration route — an investor visa (which has its own thresholds and is not satisfied simply by buying a home to live in), a retirement visa on qualifying passive income, work, study or family. Treat the purchase and the right to live there as two entirely separate projects.

The rural-land trap — where the "no restrictions" story ends

This is the single biggest misunderstanding among American buyers, and it usually surfaces at the worst moment: at the cartório, when the deed is refused.

Brazil's openness applies to urban property. Rural land is governed by Lei 5.709/1971, and it is restrictive. The statute allows a foreign individual to acquire rural land only if that individual is resident in Brazil — and the deed itself must contain proof of residence in national territory. A non-resident American with a tourist entry stamp does not qualify, full stop. Even for a resident foreigner, holdings are capped by reference to the local module size (MEI), acquisitions above a threshold require authorisation from INCRA, the land-reform agency, and foreigners collectively may not hold more than 25% of the rural land in any one municipality. As of mid-2026 the core statutes — Lei 5.709/1971 and Lei 6.634/1979 — remain unamended, despite years of reform proposals.

Layered on top is the faixa de fronteira: a 150 km strip along Brazil's international borders where acquisition by foreigners requires clearance from the National Defense Council (CDN). That is a national-security process, not a real-estate one, and it is slow.

The problem for Americans is that "rural" is a legal classification, not a vibe. A property that looks like a country house with a few hectares — exactly the sort of thing sold to foreign buyers in Minas Gerais, the Chapada or the interior of Bahia — may well be registered as imóvel rural with an INCRA registration number. Before you commit to anything outside a clearly urban perimeter, have your lawyer pull the matrícula (the registry record) and confirm the property's classification and whether it sits in the border strip. Attempts to work around the rule by buying through a Brazilian company are scrutinised too: a company controlled by foreigners is treated as foreign for these purposes.

What it actually costs

Brazilian closing costs are moderate by international standards — well below Portugal or France — but they are front-loaded and non-negotiable, and the transfer tax must be paid before the notary will execute your deed.

Two practical notes. First, the ITBI is assessed on the municipality's own valuation if that exceeds the contract price — so an artificially low declared price does not save tax, it just creates a criminal-exposure problem and a smaller cost basis when you sell. Declare the real price. Second, Brazilian mortgage pricing makes leverage economically irrational for most Americans: at 18%–21% you are paying a rate no rental yield in Brazil supports. If you want leverage, borrow in dollars against US assets and buy in Brazil for cash.

The buying process, step by step

  1. Get a CPF. Free, through a Brazilian consulate or the Receita Federal portal. One to three weeks. Nothing proceeds without it.
  2. Engage an independent advogado. Not the seller's, not the developer's, not the agent's. Brazilian real-estate agents (corretores) represent the seller.
  3. Due diligence on the matrícula. Your lawyer pulls the registry record and the certidões: liens, mortgages, litigation against the seller, unpaid IPTU and condominium dues, and — critically — whether the property is classified urban or rural. Debts in Brazil attach to the property, so an unpaid condominium bill becomes yours.
  4. Signed purchase contract (compromisso de compra e venda). Deposit typically 10%–20%. Have it drafted or reviewed by your lawyer, and register it where possible.
  5. Open a Brazilian bank account and bring the funds in through a registered FX contract. See the next section — this is the step people get wrong.
  6. Pay the ITBI. The notary will not execute the deed until the municipality confirms payment.
  7. Sign the escritura pública at the tabelionato. Either in person or via an apostilled power of attorney. Non-Portuguese speakers require a sworn translator.
  8. Register the deed at the cartório de registro de imóveis. This — not the signing — is the moment you become the owner. Do not let this step drift.

Expect eight to twelve weeks end-to-end for a straightforward cash purchase, longer if the seller's paperwork is untidy or an inheritance sits in the chain of title.

Getting money in — and, more importantly, out

This is the nuance most guides skip, and it is the one that costs Americans the most. Brazil is not a free-flowing currency jurisdiction. Under the FX framework of Lei 14.286/2021, money you bring in to buy property must enter through a formal, contracted foreign-exchange operation at a licensed institution, and foreign capital invested in Brazil must be registered with the Banco Central do Brasil (the electronic declaratory registration for foreign direct investment). That registration is what documents, in the Central Bank's records, how many dollars you brought in and what they bought.

Why it matters: when you eventually sell and want to send the proceeds back to the United States, the bank executing the outbound FX contract will look for the paper trail showing the capital was properly brought in and registered. Buyers who wired money informally, brought cash, used a friend's account, or let a developer "handle the transfer" routinely discover — years later, at the exit — that they have a Brazilian asset they cannot cleanly repatriate the proceeds of without an expensive regularisation. The registration also underpins your documented acquisition cost, which is what your Brazilian capital-gains tax is calculated against. Getting this right at the entry point costs almost nothing; fixing it at the exit is a legal project.

Budget for IOF, Brazil's tax on financial operations, on the currency conversion. The rate depends on the type of operation and — this is the point — it is set by decree and was changed materially in 2025, with outbound transfer rates standardised sharply upward. Ask your bank or FX house for the exact current IOF rate for your specific operation on the day, and do not rely on a figure you read in a blog post, including this one.

Owning it: IPTU, rental income, and selling

IPTU is the annual municipal property tax, roughly 0.6%–1.4% of the municipality's assessed value, with discounts for paying in a single instalment early in the year. Condominium fees in the towers Americans typically buy in Rio, São Paulo and Florianópolis are a bigger line item than the tax.

Rental income earned by a non-resident from Brazilian property is subject to withholding at source — the paying tenant or the appointed local representative is responsible for withholding and remitting. Non-residents are required to have a Brazilian tax representative for this purpose. Short-term letting is popular and yields well in the coastal markets, but condominium bylaws increasingly restrict it, and municipalities are tightening; check the convenção do condomínio before you underwrite an Airbnb business case.

Selling. Capital gains on the sale of Brazilian property by a non-resident are taxed under progressive federal rates that begin at 15% and step up (17.5%, 20%, and 22.5% at the top of the brackets) on very large gains. The buyer — or your appointed representative — is legally responsible for withholding the tax at closing and remitting it to the Receita Federal, which is why sophisticated Brazilian buyers will insist on the mechanics being right. And note the sting: the exemptions that protect Brazilian residents (the single-property exemption, the small-value exemption, the reinvestment rollover) do not apply to non-residents. You get the rate, not the reliefs.

The US tax side — and the treaty that doesn't exist

The US taxes citizens on worldwide income, so Brazilian rental income goes on Schedule E and a future sale goes on Schedule D, regardless of where the money sits. Foreign residential rental property is depreciated over 30 years under ADS, not the 27.5 years you may be used to domestically.

Here is the point that genuinely distinguishes Brazil from Portugal, France or Spain: there is no income tax treaty between the United States and Brazil. None. (A totalization agreement on social security has been in force since 2018, but that is a different instrument and does nothing for income tax.) The practical consequences are real:

  • There is no treaty article to reduce Brazilian withholding on your rental income, and no treaty tie-breaker if you become resident in both countries.
  • Your only relief from double taxation is the foreign tax credit on Form 1116 — which means Brazilian tax must be properly assessed, properly paid, and properly documented, or you lose the credit and genuinely pay twice.
  • There is no treaty mechanism for resolving a dispute between the two tax authorities. Documentation discipline is not optional.

Two more traps. Currency gain: if you ever borrow in reais, the exchange-rate movement on the loan can create a separate taxable gain under Section 988 that is not sheltered by any home-sale exclusion — another argument against Brazilian debt. And reporting: the property itself, held directly in your own name, is not an FBAR or Form 8938 asset — but the Brazilian bank account you open to buy it and collect rent almost certainly is, once your foreign accounts exceed $10,000 in aggregate at any point in the year.

If you're not American: what changes

Everything above about Brazil itself is the same for you. The CPF requirement, the open urban market, the ITBI, the cartório process, the rural-land restriction, the Central Bank registration and the non-resident capital-gains rules apply to foreign buyers as a class — they do not turn on which passport you hold. What changes is only the tax wrapper back home.

If you are British, Canadian, Australian, Israeli, German or anything other than American, ignore the FBAR, Form 8938, Schedule E and Form 1116 sections above and substitute your own country's equivalents. Three questions to put to a tax adviser in your country of residence before you buy:

  • Does your home country tax foreign rental income and foreign capital gains? Most residence-based systems do. A few (and non-resident nationals of some countries) do not — which changes the arithmetic entirely.
  • What foreign-asset or foreign-account reporting applies to you? Many countries require disclosure of overseas accounts or assets above a threshold, with penalties for silence.
  • Does your country have a double-taxation treaty with Brazil? This is the big one, and it cuts both ways. Brazil has treaties with Canada, the UK (in force since 2025), France, Spain, Portugal, Italy, Israel, Japan, Mexico and many others — but, as noted, not with the United States. If your country has one, you may have relief and dispute-resolution routes that Americans simply do not. Read it before you assume.

The local Brazilian mechanics are the same for everyone. The relief you get on the other end is not.

Is buying in Brazil worth it in 2026?

Brazil rewards the buyer who is there for the asset, not for a passport. The upside is real: full freehold urban ownership open to foreigners, a public registry that gives genuine title certainty when you actually use it, closing costs around 5%–6% that undercut most of Europe, and prices — in dollar terms — that have made coastal and metropolitan Brazil one of the better-value lifestyle markets available to Americans. Rental yields in the strong coastal and urban markets are competitive with anything in southern Europe.

The downside is equally real, and it is administrative rather than legal. There is no residency in it. There is no useful mortgage. There is no US tax treaty, so your only shelter is a foreign tax credit that depends entirely on your paperwork. The currency is volatile, and the tax on taking your money out is set by decree and can change without warning. And the rural-land rule quietly closes off exactly the kind of countryside property many foreign buyers dream about.

If you are a cash buyer who wants an urban or coastal property, who does the Central Bank registration correctly on the way in, and who keeps clean records for the IRS — Brazil is one of the more attractive and under-rated markets an American can buy into in 2026. If you are looking for leverage, residency, or a farm, look elsewhere.

Brazil property FAQ

Can an American buy property in Brazil?

Yes. Urban property — apartments, houses and city lots — is open to foreign buyers on essentially the same terms as Brazilian citizens. No visa or residency is required. The only prerequisite is a CPF, Brazil's individual tax ID, which a US citizen can obtain free of charge through a Brazilian consulate or the Receita Federal portal, usually in one to three weeks. Rural land and land in the 150 km border strip are a different story and are heavily restricted.

How much are closing costs in Brazil?

Budget roughly 5% to 6% of the purchase price. The largest item is the municipal ITBI transfer tax, typically 2% to 3% (3% in São Paulo and Rio de Janeiro, about 2.5% in Florianópolis), which must be paid before the notary will execute the deed. Add notary fees of about 0.4% to 1%, registry fees of about 0.4% to 0.8%, and roughly 1% for independent legal due diligence. Agent commission is normally the seller's cost.

Can a foreigner get a mortgage in Brazil?

In theory yes, in practice almost never on terms an American would accept. Brazilian mortgage rates track the SELIC policy rate and ran at roughly 18% to 21% effective in 2026, and non-resident applicants face heavy documentation demands. Brazil is a cash market for foreign buyers: most Americans wire the full purchase price and, if they want leverage, borrow against US assets instead.

Can Americans buy rural land or farmland in Brazil?

Generally not as a non-resident. Under Lei 5.709/1971 only a foreign individual resident in Brazil may acquire rural land, and proof of residence must appear in the deed itself. Foreign ownership is further capped at 25% of the rural land in any one municipality, larger holdings require INCRA authorisation, and rural land inside the 150 km border strip additionally requires clearance from the National Defense Council. A Brazilian company controlled by foreigners is treated as foreign for this purpose.

Do I pay US tax on a property I buy in Brazil?

Yes, if it generates income or is sold. The US taxes citizens on worldwide income, so Brazilian rental income and any future gain are reportable to the IRS. Critically, there is no US–Brazil income tax treaty, so the foreign tax credit on Form 1116 — not a treaty — is your only relief from double taxation. The property itself, held directly, is not FBAR-reportable, but the Brazilian bank account you open to buy it very likely is.
