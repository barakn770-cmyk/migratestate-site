Malta Golden Visa (MPRP) 2026: Requirements, Costs & US Tax Guide

MS By The MigrateState Editorial Team · Reviewed by a licensed international real-estate attorney  ·  Updated July 2026  ·  10 min read
Malta's "Golden Visa" is the Permanent Residence Programme (MPRP) — permanent EU residence, not a passport. You qualify by buying a property from €375,000 or renting at €14,000 a year — the same threshold anywhere in Malta since Legal Notice 146 of 2025 abolished the cheaper Gozo and South Malta tiers — holding it for five years, showing at least €500,000 in assets, and paying a €60,000 administrative fee, a €37,000 government contribution and a €2,000 donation. There is no minimum stay. Malta's separate citizenship-by-investment scheme was struck down by the EU Court of Justice in April 2025, so there is no longer any route to buy a Maltese passport.

What the Malta Golden Visa actually is

The program marketed abroad as the "Malta Golden Visa" is formally the Malta Permanent Residence Programme (MPRP), administered by the Residency Malta Agency. It grants successful applicants — and their qualifying family members — the right to live in Malta permanently and to travel visa-free throughout the Schengen area. The residence card is issued for five years and renewed thereafter, but the underlying status is permanent as long as you keep meeting the conditions.

Two things make the MPRP distinctive among European residency-by-investment programs. First, it is a permanent residence program from day one, rather than a temporary permit that upgrades over time. Second — and this is the point almost every glossy brochure blurs — it is not a citizenship program and never converts automatically into one. We return to this below, because in 2025 it became the single most important thing an American needs to understand about Malta.

The MPRP is aimed squarely at non-EU nationals who want a European base, a Schengen residence card, and the optionality of relocating to an English-speaking, EU-member, euro-zone island without any obligation to actually move there. For Americans, that optionality — a plan-B EU residence you can activate later — is usually the real product being bought.

Who can apply

The MPRP is open to non-EU, non-EEA and non-Swiss nationals, which includes US citizens. To qualify you must:

  • Be at least 18 years old;
  • Hold assets of at least €500,000, of which at least €150,000 must be financial (liquid) assets — or, alternatively, €650,000 in total assets including at least €75,000 in liquid assets;
  • Have a clean criminal record and not be subject to sanctions or come from a sanctioned jurisdiction;
  • Not pose a reputational risk under Malta's four-tier due-diligence process;
  • Hold a valid health insurance policy covering Malta.

Applications cannot be filed directly with the government — they must be submitted through a licensed Maltese agent, who handles the due-diligence file, the property arrangements, and the government interface. The main applicant can include a spouse, financially dependent children, and dependent parents or grandparents in the same application.

The property routes: buy or rent

Every MPRP applicant must secure a qualifying residential property in Malta and hold it for at least five years. You can satisfy this by buying or by renting. Until mid-2025 the thresholds were lower for property in the south of Malta or on Gozo; Legal Notice 146 of 2025 (published 22 July 2025) abolished that regional discount and set a single national threshold.

After five years you may sell the property or end the lease, but you must continue to hold some residential address in Malta to keep your status. Most cost-conscious applicants take the rental route: renting at €14,000 a year for five years commits €70,000 to housing you don't own, but it avoids tying up €375,000 in a single illiquid Maltese apartment. Investors who genuinely intend to spend time on the island — or who see Maltese real estate as an asset in its own right — take the purchase route. The full cost breakdown

Beyond the property itself, the MPRP carries a set of government fees and a contribution. Malta rewrote this schedule in Legal Notice 146 of 2025 (22 July 2025), and a great deal of material online still quotes the old figures, so check any number you read elsewhere against the ones below — and confirm the exact amounts with your agent at the time you apply.

  • Administrative fee — €60,000 (non-refundable). Typically split, with a portion (around €15,000) due on submission and the balance after approval-in-principle. This is charged regardless of which property route you take.
  • Government contribution — €37,000. A single one-off figure since Legal Notice 146 of 2025. It no longer matters whether you buy or rent: the old split (€30,000 for buyers, €60,000 for renters) is gone. Spouses and minor children no longer attract a separate contribution.
  • Charitable donation — €2,000 to a registered Maltese non-governmental organisation (culture, sport, science, animal welfare, or similar).
  • Additional dependents — €7,500 each for adult dependents added to the application (the spouse is generally included without this surcharge).
  • Agent, due-diligence and legal fees — typically €15,000 and up, paid to your licensed agent and advisors.

Netted out, the rental route comes to about €169,000 before agent and due-diligence fees — €60,000 administrative fee + €37,000 contribution + €2,000 donation + €70,000 of rent over five years — or roughly €185,000–€200,000 once professional fees are added. The purchase route starts from around €474,000 all-in — a €375,000 property plus the €60,000 fee, €37,000 contribution and €2,000 donation — though most of that is a real-estate asset you retain rather than money spent. The single hard number to anchor on: nobody obtains Malta residence for less than about €150,000 in genuinely sunk cost.

The application process, step by step

  1. Engage a licensed agent. You cannot approach Residency Malta directly; a licensed agent files on your behalf and manages the four-tier due-diligence process.
  2. Compile the file. Passport, proof of the €500,000 (or €650,000) in assets, source-of-funds and source-of-wealth documentation, police clearance, health insurance, and family documents. Documents in English are accepted, which is one of Malta's practical advantages for Americans.
  3. Submit and pay the initial fee. The agent lodges the application and pays the first tranche of the administrative fee (around €15,000).
  4. Due diligence and approval-in-principle. Residency Malta runs enhanced background checks — typically completed within four to six months. On success you receive a Letter of Approval in Principle.
  5. Complete the qualifying conditions. Within the deadline in the approval letter, buy or lease the qualifying property, pay the balance of the administrative fee, make the government contribution, and complete the €2,000 donation.
  6. Biometrics and residence cards. Applicants visit Malta once to give biometrics; residence cards are then issued to the main applicant and all dependents.
  7. Maintain the conditions. Keep the property and required assets for five years, keep health cover in place, and renew the card as required. There is no minimum-stay obligation to satisfy.

The citizenship route is gone — and this is the fact most guides bury

For years, Malta was marketed as the only EU country where you could effectively buy a passport: alongside the MPRP residence program sat a separate citizenship-by-investment scheme, latterly the Malta Exceptional Investor Naturalisation (MEIN). That is over.

On 29 April 2025, the European Court of Justice ruled that Malta's investor-citizenship scheme violated EU law — holding that a member state cannot grant nationality (and, with it, EU citizenship) in exchange for predetermined payments or investments, because that reduces citizenship to a "commercial transaction." Malta formally ended the MEIN program in July 2025 and replaced it with a narrower, merit-based naturalisation framework reserved for people who make genuinely exceptional contributions to the country — not a route you can purchase.

The consequence for anyone researching Malta today is blunt: the MPRP gives you residence, and nothing about it leads to a Maltese passport. Because the MPRP has no physical-presence requirement, MPRP holders almost never accumulate the years of genuine, continuous residence that ordinary naturalisation demands. If your objective is a second EU citizenship, Malta is no longer the answer, and you should compare programs such as Portugal's five-year citizenship track — see our golden visa vs. second passport and best golden visa for Americans guides. If your objective is a permanent EU residence option with no obligation to move, the MPRP remains one of the cleaner products in Europe.

Malta's non-dom tax regime

Malta is attractive to internationally mobile people partly because of how it taxes residents who are not domiciled in Malta. Under the remittance basis, a resident non-dom is taxed as follows:

  • Maltese-source income and Maltese capital gains — taxable in Malta at normal rates.
  • Foreign-source income — taxable in Malta only to the extent it is remitted (brought into) Malta.
  • Foreign capital gains — outside the Maltese tax net entirely, even if you later remit the proceeds to Malta.

A minimum tax of €5,000 applies to a resident non-dom whose foreign income is at least €35,000 for the year and is not fully remitted. Malta has a double-taxation treaty with the United States and with more than 70 other countries, which can relieve double taxation where two countries tax the same income. For a genuinely relocating non-American with substantial foreign investment income kept offshore, this regime can be highly efficient. For an American, as the next section explains, most of that efficiency evaporates.

US tax rules for Americans in Malta

You remain a US taxpayer regardless

The United States taxes its citizens on worldwide income for life, on a "arising" basis — the moment income is earned, not when it is remitted anywhere. Holding Malta residence, or even becoming a Maltese tax resident, does not reduce or defer your US filing obligations. You continue to file a US Form 1040 every year.

The nuance most Malta guides skip: the remittance benefit is worthless to you

Malta's headline attraction for the internationally wealthy is the remittance basis — keep your foreign income and gains offshore and Malta largely won't tax them. But the entire mechanism is built around remittance, and the US tax system does not care about remittance at all. The IRS taxes a US citizen's foreign dividends, interest, rental income and capital gains as they arise, whether or not a single dollar ever reaches Malta. So the specific thing that makes Malta tax-efficient for a British or Middle Eastern investor — deferring tax by not remitting — delivers no benefit to an American, who is taxed by Washington on the same income immediately. Malta's non-dom regime is not a US tax shelter, and any advisor who implies otherwise is misreading how citizenship-based taxation works.

The practical planning point: Americans should evaluate Malta on residence, lifestyle and EU-access grounds, and should model Maltese tax purely as a possible additional layer (creditable against US tax via the foreign tax credit where Malta actually taxes the income), never as a way to lower the overall bill below the US floor.

FBAR and Form 8938

You will need a Maltese bank account to complete the process and manage local costs. Once the aggregate value of your foreign financial accounts exceeds $10,000 at any point in the year, you must file an FBAR (FinCEN Form 114) — filed with FinCEN, not the IRS, by 15 October with the automatic extension. The Supreme Court's 2023 Bittner decision confirmed that non-willful FBAR penalties are assessed per report (capping unintentional failures at $16,536 per year, 2026 inflation-adjusted), not per account. Form 8938 (FATCA) may also apply if your foreign financial assets exceed $200,000 at year-end for a single filer living abroad ($400,000 married filing jointly). Crucially, a directly-held Maltese property in your own name is not a financial account and is not reportable on either FBAR or Form 8938 — only bank, brokerage and investment accounts are. If you rent your Maltese property out, that rental income goes on Schedule E, and foreign residential rental property is depreciated over 30 years under the ADS system rather than the 27.5 years used for US property.

If you're not American: what changes

The MPRP itself — the €500,000 asset test, the €375,000 purchase or €14,000 rent thresholds, the €60,000 administrative fee, the €37,000 contribution, the five-year hold and the four-tier due diligence — is identical for every non-EU applicant regardless of passport. Nationality changes only your home-country tax wrapper, not the Maltese requirements.

If you are not American, Malta's remittance-basis regime may actually deliver the benefit it is famous for — but only after you check your own country's rules. Verify with a tax adviser where you are resident: (1) whether your home country taxes foreign income and capital gains on an arising basis (like the US) or lets you defer, and whether becoming Maltese-resident changes that; (2) whether your home country requires reporting of foreign accounts and assets — equivalents of FBAR and Form 8938 exist in the UK, Canada, Australia and elsewhere; (3) whether a double-taxation treaty exists between your home country and Malta and how it allocates taxing rights; and (4) whether leaving your current country of tax residence triggers an exit tax or deemed-disposal charge. The Maltese side is the same for everyone; the value you extract from it depends entirely on the tax system you are leaving behind.

Is it worth it in 2026?

The Malta Golden Visa in 2026 is a narrower, more honest product than it was two years ago. With the citizenship-by-investment route closed by the EU's top court, nobody can any longer sell Malta as a shortcut to an EU passport. What remains is a solid, permanent, English-language EU residence program with no minimum-stay obligation — genuinely useful for a family that wants a plan-B European base, Schengen mobility, and a foothold in the euro zone without relocating.

Whether it is worth roughly €150,000 (rental route) to €475,000+ (purchase route) depends on what you actually want. If you want a second citizenship, Malta is now the wrong country and you should look elsewhere. If you want tax savings and you are American, the non-dom regime will not help you, so the case has to stand on residence value alone. But if you want a durable, low-maintenance EU residence card that you can activate on your own timetable — and you can clear the asset test and due diligence — Malta remains one of the more credible and well-run programs in Europe, precisely because its due diligence is strict and its status is permanent. Go in for the residence, not the fantasy of the passport, and the math is far more likely to make sense.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does Malta's Golden Visa give you citizenship or an EU passport?

No. The MPRP grants permanent residence only — the right to live in Malta indefinitely and travel visa-free in Schengen. It is not a citizenship program and includes no passport. Malta's separate citizenship-by-investment scheme was ruled unlawful by the European Court of Justice on 29 April 2025 and formally ended in July 2025, so there is no longer any route to buy Maltese or EU citizenship. MPRP residence does not by itself put you on a naturalisation path, because the programme imposes no requirement to actually live in Malta.

How much does the Malta MPRP cost in 2026?

The core costs are a non-refundable €60,000 administrative fee, a €37,000 government contribution (unified by Legal Notice 146 of 2025 — it no longer differs between buying and renting), and a €2,000 NGO donation, plus €7,500 per additional adult dependent. You must also buy a qualifying property from €375,000 or rent at a minimum of €14,000 a year — the same figures anywhere in Malta, since the cheaper Gozo and South Malta thresholds were abolished — and hold it for five years. The rental route works out at roughly €169,000 before professional fees; the purchase route from about €474,000 all-in.

Do I have to live in Malta to keep MPRP residence?

No. There is no minimum-stay requirement. You must keep your qualifying property (owned or rented) for five years and maintain the required assets, but you are not obliged to spend any set number of days in Malta. This makes the MPRP a useful EU residence backstop, but it also means it does not build toward Maltese citizenship, which requires long periods of genuine physical residence.

How are US citizens taxed if they get Malta residence?

Malta taxes resident non-doms on the remittance basis: Maltese income and gains are taxable, foreign income only if remitted, and foreign capital gains never — with a €5,000 minimum tax if unremitted foreign income is at least €35,000. But US citizens are taxed by the IRS on worldwide income as it arises, regardless of remittance, so Malta's remittance advantage is largely worthless to Americans. A Malta bank account triggers FBAR once foreign accounts exceed $10,000, and Form 8938 may apply, though directly-held Maltese property is reportable on neither.

Can Americans apply for the Malta Golden Visa?

Yes. The MPRP is open to non-EU, non-EEA and non-Swiss nationals, including US citizens. You must be at least 18, have a clean criminal record, not be from a sanctioned country, and pass Malta's four-tier due diligence. Applications must go through a licensed Maltese agent — you cannot apply directly. Processing typically takes four to six months to approval, followed by issuance of residence cards.
