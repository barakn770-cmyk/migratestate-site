Buying Property in Japan as an American (2026)

MS By The MigrateState Editorial Team · Reviewed by a licensed international real-estate attorney  ·  Updated July 2026  ·  11 min read
Japan is one of the most open property markets in the world for a foreigner — you can own the land and the building outright, in your own name, with zero nationality restrictions. No visa, no residency, no minimum spend, no foreign-buyer surcharge. Budget about 7–10% in one-time costs. The two things to plan for in 2026: a new Bank of Japan filing for non-resident buyers, and the fact that a weak yen plus cheap "akiya" houses hide a 6× fixed-asset-tax trap.

Japan is the rare country where the headline is simply good news. Unlike Thailand, where foreigners can't own land; unlike Mexico, where the coast requires a bank trust; unlike Canada or Australia, which slap a surcharge on foreign buyers — Japan lets an American walk in and buy a house, the land under it, or a Tokyo apartment on exactly the same freehold basis as a Japanese citizen. Title is title. There is no residency test, no ownership cap, and no extra foreign-buyer tax. Combined with a yen that has spent 2024–2026 near multi-decade lows against the dollar, that openness is why American interest in Japanese property — from Tokyo condos to countryside "akiya" — has surged.

The catch is not whether you can buy; it's the details that Japanese sellers and agents rarely translate cleanly, and the US tax layer that no Japanese agent will mention at all. This guide walks the whole transaction with 2026 figures and flags each one — the new reporting rule that took effect this April, the assessed-value system that makes taxes look scarier than they are, the vacant-house tax trap, and the depreciation mismatch that can leave you paying US tax on a property that looks like a loss in Japan.

Can Americans own property in Japan?

Yes — with full freehold ownership of both land and building. Japan imposes no nationality-based restriction on real estate. A foreign national, whether living in Japan or buying entirely from abroad, can take clean title to a house, a condominium unit, or bare land in their own name. There is no visa requirement, no residency requirement, no minimum investment, and — importantly — no foreign-buyer stamp surcharge of the kind that exists in Canada, Australia, Singapore, or Hong Kong.

The flip side, and the point most "move to Japan" content blurs: buying property gives you nothing on the immigration side. There is no Japanese "golden visa," and owning a home does not grant, extend, or support a visa or residency. If you want to live in Japan long-term you need a separate qualifying status (work, spouse, business manager, and so on); the house is an asset, not a residency route. Own as much as you like — you still enter Japan on the same terms as any other American.

The 2026 change — the Bank of Japan filing

The one genuinely new thing for 2026 is a reporting obligation, not a restriction. Under the Foreign Exchange and Foreign Trade Act (FEFTA), from 1 April 2026 a non-resident who acquires real estate in Japan must file a post-transaction report — commonly the "Form 22" report to the Bank of Japan — generally within about 20 days of the acquisition. The report records the property type, location, area, acquisition date, and price. In practice your judicial scrivener (the licensed specialist who handles the title registration) or your real-estate agent files it for you as part of closing, so for most buyers it's a form to sign, not a hurdle.

Running alongside it, Japan is adding a nationality field to the real-estate registry from fiscal 2026, so new owners disclose their nationality when the transfer is recorded. Both measures are about visibility — the government wanting better data on foreign ownership — and neither changes your legal right to buy or the price you pay. The practical takeaway: engage a scrivener who is comfortable with foreign buyers, and confirm the FEFTA filing is on their closing checklist.

Buying from outside Japan? A non-resident purchase needs a judicial scrivener for the title registration, the FEFTA filing, and — if you'll rent it out — a Japanese tax representative. A MigrateState-matched adviser can line up the right professionals before you wire a deposit.

Costs, taxes, and the buying process

Japan's purchase costs look intimidating on paper until you understand one thing: most property taxes are charged on the assessed value (hyoka-gaku), which is typically only 50–70% of the market price. So a "4%" tax is really closer to 2–3% of what you actually paid. Here are the 2026 headline charges:

Charge Typical 2026 rate Notes
Real estate acquisition tax 3% residential (land & dwellings); 4% otherwise On assessed value; residential land gets a further reduction. One-time, billed a few months after purchase
Registration & license tax Land 1.5% (reduced, through Mar 2029); building ~0.3% reduced / 2% standard Paid at title registration
Stamp duty ~¥10,000–¥60,000 typical band On the sale contract; scales with price
Agent commission ~3% + ¥60,000 + 10% consumption tax Statutory maximum for higher-value deals
Judicial scrivener fee ~¥100,000–¥200,000 Handles registration + FEFTA filing
Annual: fixed asset + city planning tax ~1.4% + ~0.3% = ~1.7% of assessed value Ongoing; residential land gets large reductions

All-in, one-time buying costs run about 7–10% of the purchase price. A nuance worth flagging: consumption tax (10%) applies to the building portion when you buy from a business or developer (a new-build or a company-owned unit), but not on land and not on a private individual's resale — so a secondhand house bought from a private owner carries no consumption tax on the structure, while the same house from a developer does. It can move the total by several percent, so always ask whether the seller is a business.

The process is orderly and fast once you've chosen a property: you sign a purchase application, then the agent delivers the legally required "Important Matters Explanation" (juyo jiko setsumei) — a detailed disclosure a licensed agent must walk you through before you sign — you sign the sale-and-purchase contract and pay a deposit (commonly around 10%), and the judicial scrivener completes the balance payment, title transfer, and registration on the same day. From accepted offer to registered ownership is often just 4–8 weeks on a cash deal.

Want the real all-in number on a specific listing? Get a written closing-cost and annual-tax estimate — including whether the seller is a business (consumption tax) and the assessed value the taxes are actually based on — from a vetted Japanese professional before you commit.

The akiya boom — and the 6× tax trap

Japan has millions of akiya — vacant houses, many in the countryside, some listed for the price of a used car. They're real, foreigners can buy them freehold like anything else, and the weak yen makes the sticker prices look extraordinary to a dollar buyer. But there are three traps behind the romance.

First, the 6× tax trap. Residential land in Japan enjoys a large fixed-asset-tax reduction (up to a six-fold cut on the standard small-lot rate). Since a December 2023 revision of the vacant-house law, a property flagged as a "poorly managed" vacant house (kanri-fuzen akiya) can lose that reduction — so the annual fixed asset tax can jump to as much as six times what a maintained home pays. Leave an akiya to rot and the tax authority can strip the break. Second, renovation cost: many akiya need structural, plumbing, and earthquake-retrofit work that dwarfs the purchase price, and older wooden homes may not meet current seismic standards. Third, the ongoing-management reality for an overseas owner — you'll need someone in Japan for the mail, the utilities, and the tax representative role described below. An akiya can be a wonderful project; it is rarely the passive bargain it looks like from abroad.

Financing: why most foreigners pay cash

This is where non-residents hit the wall. Japanese banks are very reluctant to lend to non-resident foreigners for residential purchase — a domestic mortgage generally requires permanent residency (or a long-term visa with stable Japan-based employment and income), a Japanese guarantor or credit history, and Japanese-language paperwork. As a result, the large majority of overseas buyers purchase in cash, funding the deal with an international wire. A handful of foreign-facing lenders and some Japanese branches will consider PR holders, but for a buyer sitting in the US with no Japanese residency, plan on cash.

That has a hidden upside on the US side, covered next: buying cash with no yen-denominated mortgage avoids a currency-tax trap that catches Americans who finance abroad.

The US tax angle

For a US citizen or green-card holder, owning Japanese property directly is clean on the reporting side but has one genuine trap. Directly-held foreign real estate is not reportable on the FBAR (FinCEN 114) or Form 8938 — those cover financial accounts, not a house. But the Japanese bank account you'll open to pay property tax, utilities, or receive rent is a foreign financial account: once your non-US accounts together top $10,000 at any point in the year, the FBAR is required, and Form 8938 may be too depending on your filing status and where you live.

Rental income goes on Schedule E at ordinary rates (the Foreign Earned Income Exclusion does not shelter rental income). You can generally claim a foreign tax credit for Japanese tax paid, and on a true primary residence the Section 121 exclusion can shelter up to $250,000/$500,000 of gain.

The nuance most blogs miss is a depreciation and currency double-whammy. On the US return you must depreciate a foreign residential rental building over 30 years under the Alternative Depreciation System (versus 27.5 years for a US rental) — a slow write-off. But Japan's own tax system depreciates buildings much faster (a wooden house has a statutory useful life of just 22 years, and used-building rules can shorten it further), so the same house can be fully depreciated for Japanese purposes while barely started for US purposes. The result: you can show a loss in Japan and a taxable profit in the US on the identical property, which blunts the foreign tax credit and surprises people who assumed the two systems roughly offset. Add the Section 988 currency rule — a yen mortgage can create a "phantom" US tax gain on nothing more than an exchange-rate move — and the case for a cash purchase gets stronger. When you sell, Japan taxes the gain at 39.63% if you held five years or less and 20.315% if you held longer, and a buyer purchasing from a non-resident may have to withhold 10.21% of the price at closing; model all of this with a cross-border advisor before you buy, not at tax time.

Step-by-step: how to buy

  1. Decide the goal — Tokyo condo for rental yield, a resale house, or a countryside akiya project — because it drives price, financing, and tax.
  2. Assume a cash purchase unless you hold Japanese permanent residency; arrange the international wire and budget 7–10% in costs.
  3. Engage a bilingual agent and a judicial scrivener experienced with foreign buyers; confirm the FEFTA/Bank of Japan filing is on their checklist.
  4. Read the "Important Matters Explanation" disclosure carefully — building age, seismic standard, land rights, and any management or repair-fund arrears on a condo.
  5. Confirm whether the seller is a business (consumption tax on the building) and get the assessed value the annual taxes are based on.
  6. Appoint a Japanese tax representative (nozei kanrinin) if you'll be a non-resident owner — required to receive tax notices and file.
  7. Decide US treatment with a cross-border advisor before closing, and keep every receipt for your US cost basis.
  8. Complete transfer and registration with the scrivener — ownership passes on registration, and the FEFTA report follows within ~20 days.

If you're not American: what changes

The Japanese rules in this guide — full freehold for foreigners with no nationality restriction, no foreign-buyer surcharge, the FEFTA Form 22 filing for non-resident buyers, the 3%/4% acquisition tax on assessed value, the ~1.7% annual fixed-asset-plus-city-planning tax, the akiya 6× tax trap, and the difficulty of getting a mortgage as a non-resident — are set by Japan and apply to every foreign buyer equally, regardless of passport. A British, German, Australian, or Israeli buyer faces the same freehold rights and the same paperwork as an American. Only the home-country tax wrapper differs.

The entire "US tax angle" section — FBAR, Form 8938, Schedule E, the 30-year ADS depreciation and its mismatch with Japan's 22-year building life, Section 121, and the Section 988 currency point — is US-specific and does not apply to non-Americans. If you're not a US person, swap those rules for your own and check three things with a tax adviser in your country of residence: (1) whether your home country taxes foreign rental income and capital gains; (2) whether you must report foreign assets, accounts, or holding structures at home; and (3) whether your country has a double-taxation treaty with Japan that affects how that income and any Japanese capital-gains tax are credited. The Japanese FEFTA filing, consumption-tax-on-buildings point, and the akiya trap apply to all foreign buyers alike.

Is it worth it in 2026?

For an American who wants a genuinely owned home in a safe, low-crime, well-run country — with a currency that makes 2026 dollar prices look like a decade ago — Japan is one of the most legally straightforward foreign-ownership markets on earth. You get real freehold, no surcharge, moderate transaction costs, and trivial annual property tax once the residential reductions apply. A Tokyo or Osaka condo can produce solid rental yield; a resale house in a mid-size city can be remarkably cheap.

The ways buyers get hurt are avoidable and mostly financial rather than legal: overpaying for an akiya that needs more in renovation than it cost, getting caught by the 6× vacant-house tax, assuming a mortgage you won't actually be offered, or ignoring the US depreciation-and-currency mismatch until it produces a tax bill on a property that feels like it's losing money. Buy with clear eyes, use a scrivener who knows foreign buyers, appoint a tax representative, and get US cross-border advice before you wire — and Japan stays exactly as open as it looks at the front door.

Can you short-let it? The 180-night cap

If your yield model assumes nightly lettings, check this before you buy. Japan's minpaku regime (Housing Accommodation Business Act) requires a notification for private-home short-term letting and caps it at 180 nights per year — a hard national ceiling, and many municipalities layer on stricter local limits. Operating beyond it, or without notification, is illegal letting. The alternatives are a full hotel/inn licence (ryokan) or a special-zone (tokku minpaku) permit — and in 2026 Osaka City and most of its surrounding municipalities suspended new special-zone applications. Assume 180 nights, not 365, unless a licensed route is confirmed for that exact address. See our guide to short-term rental rules abroad.

Frequently asked questions

Can Americans buy property in Japan?

Yes, with full freehold rights. Japan places no nationality restriction on real estate — a US citizen, resident or not, can buy the land and the building outright in their own name, the same as a Japanese national. No visa, no residency, no minimum investment, and no foreign-buyer surcharge. Buying property does not grant any visa or residency in return.

What is the new 2026 rule for foreign buyers in Japan?

From 1 April 2026, a non-resident who acquires Japanese real estate must file a report (Form 22) with the Bank of Japan under the Foreign Exchange and Foreign Trade Act, generally within 20 days. Japan is also adding a nationality field to the property registry. Both are reporting requirements handled by your scrivener at closing — neither restricts your right to buy.

What does it cost to buy property in Japan?

Budget roughly 7–10% in one-time costs: acquisition tax (3% residential / 4% otherwise, on an assessed value that's usually only 50–70% of market), registration and license tax (a reduced 1.5% on land through March 2029), stamp duty, agent commission (~3% + ¥60,000 + consumption tax), and scrivener fees. Annual fixed asset plus city planning tax runs about 1.7% of assessed value.

Can foreigners buy akiya (vacant houses) in Japan?

Yes, on the same freehold basis as any property. The catch is the 2023 vacant-house law: a home classed as poorly managed can lose the residential-land tax break and see its annual fixed asset tax jump up to about six times. Akiya are usually cash purchases, often need major renovation, and a non-resident owner must appoint a Japanese tax representative for local tax notices.

Do Americans pay US tax on a Japanese property?

Yes — the US taxes worldwide income. Rental income goes on Schedule E, with the building depreciated over 30 years under the ADS method for foreign property. Directly-held real estate isn't itself FBAR- or Form 8938-reportable, but a Japanese bank account you open often is once your foreign accounts exceed $10,000. You can usually claim a foreign tax credit for Japanese tax, and Section 121 can shelter gain on a primary residence.
