Foreign Tax Credit on Property Abroad (Form 1116), 2026

MS By The MigrateState Editorial Team · Reviewed by a licensed international real-estate attorney  ·  Updated July 2026  ·  11 min read
The foreign tax credit stops you paying twice — but only on the right taxes. Income tax you pay another country on foreign rental profit and on the gain when you sell is creditable on Form 1116, dollar-for-dollar, inside the passive category basket. Annual property tax and transfer tax are not creditable. And on a capital gain the credit is capped at the US tax — often 15–20% — so foreign tax above that rate strands as carryover.

What the foreign tax credit actually relieves

The United States taxes its citizens and green card holders on their worldwide income, wherever they live and wherever the asset sits. Own an apartment in Lisbon, a house in Oaxaca or a flat in Tokyo, and the rent and the eventual sale gain are US income regardless of the fact that Portugal, Mexico or Japan has already taxed them first. Left there, you would pay twice on the same dollars. The foreign tax credit is the mechanism Congress built to stop that.

It works as a credit, not a deduction — a dollar of qualifying foreign tax cancels a dollar of US tax, rather than merely reducing the income the US tax is calculated on. That is far more valuable, and it is why the credit is almost always the right lever to reach for. But it is not unlimited and it is not automatic. It is claimed on Form 1116, it is capped by a formula, and it applies only to foreign taxes that meet a specific definition. Understanding where its edges are is the whole game.

Which foreign property taxes are creditable

This is the first place owners go wrong. They add up every euro or peso they handed a foreign government and assume it all counts. It does not. Only a foreign income tax — a levy on net income, gain or profit — is creditable on Form 1116. A tax on the mere ownership or transfer of property is not, no matter how large it was.

The distinction matters because the non-creditable taxes are frequently the biggest numbers. A transfer tax of 6–10% at purchase dwarfs the first year's income tax on the rent, but it never touches Form 1116. It is instead added to your dollar cost basis, where it quietly reduces the eventual capital gain — real value, but delayed by years and realised only on sale. Annual property tax, meanwhile, is a deductible rental expense on Schedule E while the property is let. For a personal residence abroad, note the harsher rule: the deduction for foreign real property taxes was suspended for individuals and remains disallowed for 2026, so those charges give a US owner no current relief at all.

The passive basket, and why the label matters

Form 1116 does not pool all your foreign taxes into one figure. It sorts foreign income — and the tax paid on it — into separate categories, usually called baskets, and computes a limit for each one in isolation. The two that matter to most people are the general category (active business and employment income) and the passive category (dividends, interest, rents and royalties). Rental income from a property abroad is passive. A gain on selling that property is passive too.

The reason this label is not a mere formality is that credits cannot cross baskets. Excess foreign tax generated in the passive basket can only ever offset US tax on other passive-basket foreign income. If you also run a foreign business and pay high tax there, that general-basket credit is walled off and can do nothing to relieve the US tax on your rental profit, and vice versa. When you have income in more than one category you file a separate Form 1116 for each. For a straightforward owner of one foreign let, everything lives in the single passive basket — but the walls still shape what happens to any credit you cannot immediately use.

The de minimis exemption trap

There is a well-known shortcut that lets small filers skip Form 1116 entirely and enter the credit straight onto Schedule 3. The threshold is generous-sounding: total creditable foreign tax of $300 or less for a single filer, or $600 or less on a joint return. Many owners of a modest foreign flat see those numbers, note that their foreign income tax on the rent was under the limit, and assume they qualify.

They usually do not, and the reason is a condition that is easy to miss. The exemption applies only when all your foreign income is passive and the foreign tax was reported to you on a qualified payee statement — a 1099 or an equivalent. Foreign rental income is never reported on a 1099. There is no US information return for the rent your Portuguese or Mexican tenant paid you. Because the tax on it fails the payee-statement test, the shortcut is closed, and an owner with foreign rental income generally has to file the full Form 1116 even for a trivial amount of foreign tax.

In plain terms: the $300 / $600 skip-the-form rule is built for people whose only foreign income is dividends and interest from a US brokerage, where the broker issues a 1099. It almost never rescues a landlord. If you have foreign rent, budget for Form 1116 regardless of how small the tax is.

The rate-differential adjustment that strands credit

This is the nuance that costs the most money and that almost no consumer guide explains. Even when your foreign tax is squarely creditable, the credit is capped at the US tax on that same foreign-source income under the Section 904 limitation. The cap is fine when both countries tax the income at similar ordinary rates. It becomes a trap on a long-term capital gain, because the US taxes that gain at a preferential rate — 0, 15 or 20% — while the foreign country may have taxed it as ordinary income at a much higher one.

To keep the credit honest, Form 1116 makes you shrink the foreign gain before it enters the limitation fraction, so the credit reflects only the reduced US tax the gain actually bears. You multiply the foreign-source gain by an adjustment factor: roughly 0.4054 if the gain was taxed at the US 15% rate, and 0.5405 if at the 20% rate. A gain taxed at the 0% US rate is left out of line 1a altogether. The effect is that only about half of the gain counts as foreign-source income in the formula — which halves the ceiling on the credit.

Read the bottom two rows together. You paid $28,000 abroad, the US would have taxed the gain at $20,000, and yet the credit allowed in the year is only around $10,810 because the adjustment shrank the foreign-source income the ceiling is built on. The rest is not refunded and not lost either — it becomes carryover. Countries whose gains tax runs well above the US preferential rate, which describes most of Europe, routinely produce this outcome. It is the single biggest reason a US owner ends up with unused foreign tax credit after selling abroad.

A related hazard sits next door. If you financed the purchase in the local currency, repaying the mortgage can trigger a separate Section 988 currency gain that is US-source ordinary income — and because it is US-source, no foreign tax was charged on it and no credit can shelter it. Carryback, carryforward and the ten-year clock

Foreign tax you cannot use in the year you paid it does not simply evaporate. Excess credit carries back one year to the immediately preceding return — where, if you had foreign-source income in the same basket, you can amend and recover tax already paid — and then carries forward up to ten years. After the tenth year, anything still unused is gone for good.

The catch, again, is the basket. A carryforward has value only if you later generate foreign-source income in the same category to absorb it. Passive-basket credit from a property sale needs future passive foreign income — foreign dividends, interest, or rent from another property abroad — to attach to. An American who sells their only foreign asset, repatriates the proceeds and invests them in US index funds has manufactured a ten-year carryforward with nothing to consume it. It expires quietly on a schedule almost no one tracks. If you expect to keep foreign-source income, the carryforward is a real asset; if you are exiting, treat it as probably lost and plan around that.

Credit or deduction — the once-a-year choice

Foreign income tax can instead be taken as an itemised deduction on Schedule A rather than a credit. This is almost always worse — a deduction reduces taxable income, a credit reduces tax itself — but it is occasionally useful, for instance in a year with so little US tax that a credit would be wasted while a deduction preserves other benefits, or where the paperwork of Form 1116 is not worth it for a tiny amount.

The important mechanical rule is that the choice is all-or-nothing for the whole year. You cannot credit the tax on your rental income and deduct the tax on your dividends in the same year — you elect one treatment for all your creditable foreign taxes. And electing to deduct forfeits the carryback and carryforward for that year's taxes entirely. For nearly every property owner the credit wins, but the election exists and it is annual, so it is worth a deliberate check rather than a default.

How to claim it, step by step

  1. Separate the taxes. Split what you paid abroad into creditable income taxes (on rent, on the sale gain) and non-creditable ones (property tax, transfer tax, VAT). Only the first group goes near Form 1116.
  2. Convert to dollars. Translate each foreign tax payment at the exchange rate on the date it was paid, or at the average rate if you elect and qualify to use it. Keep the receipts.
  3. Sort into baskets. Rent and property gains are passive category. File a separate Form 1116 for any income in a different category.
  4. Apply the rate-differential adjustment. For a long-term capital gain, scale the foreign-source gain down on line 1a using the factor for your US rate (about 0.5405 at 20%, 0.4054 at 15%) before the limitation is computed.
  5. Compute the Section 904 limit. The credit for the basket is capped at US tax multiplied by the ratio of adjusted foreign-source taxable income to total taxable income.
  6. Take the lower figure. Your allowed credit is the smaller of the foreign tax paid and that limit. The rest becomes carryover.
  7. Record the carryover. Track the excess by category and year on Schedule B of Form 1116 so the one-year-back, ten-year-forward clock is documented.
  8. Carry the result to Schedule 3. The allowed credit flows to Schedule 3 and onto your Form 1040.

If you're not American: what changes

The foreign tax you paid on the property is the same for every owner. The country where the property sits taxes the rent and the gain identically regardless of your passport. What is purely American is the relief wrapper described above — Form 1116, the passive basket, the rate-differential adjustment and the ten-year carryforward. Those are US rules for US taxpayers only.

If you are not a US person, ignore Form 1116 and look to how your country of tax residence relieves double taxation instead. Nearly every developed country offers a foreign tax credit or an exemption for foreign income already taxed at source, but the mechanics differ sharply. Check three things. First, whether your home country taxes foreign rental income and foreign gains at all, and how it credits the tax the property's country already took. Second, whether you have foreign asset or account reporting obligations at home covering the property, the rent and the proceeds. Third, whether a double-taxation treaty exists between your country of residence and the country where the property sits — immovable property is almost always taxable first where it is located and relieved second at home, but the treaty sets the order and the ceiling. A British, Canadian, Australian or Israeli owner runs the same underlying facts through their own credit system. Confirm the detail with a tax adviser where you are resident.

Is it worth the effort in 2026?

Yes — the foreign tax credit is usually the difference between paying tax once and paying it twice, and for a US owner of foreign property it is the central tool for keeping the total bill sane. On steady rental income, where both countries tax at broadly ordinary rates, it often works cleanly and wipes out the US tax on the rent almost entirely.

Where it disappoints is on the sale, and the disappointment is structural rather than a mistake you can avoid by filing better. The rate-differential adjustment means a gain taxed hard abroad and lightly in the US will leave foreign credit stranded, and the basket rules mean that stranded credit needs future foreign passive income to ever be worth anything. The owners who come out ahead are the ones who understand this before they act: they time a sale where possible, they keep enough foreign-source income in the passive basket to absorb the carryforward, and they never assume that "I already paid tax over there" translates automatically into a US credit. Sort the creditable from the non-creditable taxes early, run Form 1116's limitation honestly, and the credit does its job. Treat it as automatic and you will overpay one government or the other.

Frequently asked questions

Can I claim a US foreign tax credit for tax paid on my rental property abroad?

Yes, for the foreign income tax you paid on the rental profit and on any gain when you sell. You claim it on Form 1116 as a dollar-for-dollar credit against the US tax on that same income, which sits in the passive category basket. What you cannot credit is the country's annual property tax or its property transfer tax, because those are not income taxes. Those non-income taxes are instead deducted as rental expenses on Schedule E.

Are foreign property taxes creditable on Form 1116?

No. Only foreign income taxes are creditable. Annual municipal property taxes such as Portugal's IMI, Spain's IBI or a French taxe foncière, along with one-off property transfer or stamp taxes paid at purchase, are not income taxes and cannot go on Form 1116. For a rental property they are deducted as ordinary rental expenses on Schedule E. For a personal residence, the post-2017 rules disallow the deduction of foreign real property taxes entirely.

Do I have to file Form 1116 or can I skip it?

Most property owners must file it. The de minimis exemption that lets you skip Form 1116 applies only when all of your foreign income is passive and the foreign tax is reported to you on a qualified payee statement such as a 1099, with total foreign tax of $300 or less for a single filer or $600 or less for a joint return. Foreign rental income is not reported on a 1099, so it fails the payee-statement condition, which means owners with foreign rental income generally have to file Form 1116 even for small amounts.

Why can't I use all of the foreign tax I paid on a property sale?

Because of the rate-differential adjustment. The foreign tax credit is capped at the US tax on the foreign-source income, and a long-term capital gain is taxed at a preferential US rate of 0, 15 or 20 percent. Form 1116 requires you to scale the foreign gain down before it enters the credit limit — multiplying it by about 0.5405 if it was taxed at 20 percent. If the country taxed the same gain at a higher rate, the difference cannot be credited that year and becomes carryover.

What happens to foreign tax credit I cannot use this year?

Excess credit is not immediately lost. It carries back one year to the prior return and then forward up to ten years, but only within the same category. Passive-basket credit from property income or gains can only be absorbed by future passive foreign-source income, such as foreign dividends, interest or another property's rental profit. If you have no such income in the carry window, the credit expires unused after the tenth year.
