Can Americans Get a Mortgage in Mexico? (2026)

MS By The MigrateState Editorial Team · Reviewed by a licensed international real-estate attorney  ·  Updated June 2026  ·  9 min read
Yes — Americans can finance Mexican property, even as non-residents. Your three real options are a Mexican bank mortgage (best for residents), a cross-border USD loan (built for US buyers), or developer financing. Expect 9%–14% on peso loans, 5%–9% on USD cross-border loans, and a 35%–50% down payment as a non-resident. The biggest decision isn't the rate — it's the currency.

The myth that you must pay all cash for a home in Mexico is outdated. Financing exists for Americans in 2026 — through Mexican banks, a growing class of cross-border lenders, and developers — but the terms, the paperwork and above all the currency look nothing like a US mortgage. Picking the wrong structure can quietly cost you more than a couple of points of interest. Here's how to do it right.

Can Americans actually borrow in Mexico?

Yes. Foreign nationals are legally allowed to take a mortgage in Mexico, and both residents and non-residents can qualify depending on the lender and your financial profile. Access scales with your immigration status: a permanent resident (Residente Permanente) gets the best terms from Mexican banks; a temporary resident qualifies with select lenders; and a non-resident typically turns to specialized cross-border lenders or developer financing. Lenders will want US tax returns, bank statements, proof of income and a strong credit history — and they'll ask for more of it than a US bank would.

Rates, LTV and down payments

Mexican financing is more expensive and more conservative than a US mortgage. In early 2026:

TermPeso (Mexican bank)USD (cross-border)
Interest rate~9%–14%~5%–9%
Max LTV70%–80% (residents)60%–70%
Typical non-resident down payment35%–50%

Some fintech lenders stretch to 90% LTV for the very strongest profiles, but as a non-resident you should budget a 35%–50% deposit and stricter income documentation. Add closing costs of roughly 5%–8% on top.

Financing in the restricted (coastal) zone

If your dream home is on the beach or near a border — Tulum, Cabo, Puerto Vallarta — it sits in Mexico's restricted zone, where a foreigner holds title through a bank trust (fideicomiso). This does not block financing: lenders routinely mortgage trust-held property, and the bank that holds your fideicomiso is often involved. Just budget the trust's setup fee and annual fee on top of your loan costs. Full mechanics are in our Mexico property guide.

Peso vs USD — the decision that matters

This is where most buyers focus on the wrong number. A peso mortgage at 12% versus a USD loan at 7% looks like a simple rate comparison — but the currency you borrow in is a second bet layered on the property. Borrow in pesos and you owe in pesos: if the peso weakens against your dollar income, your effective cost falls, but if it strengthens, your dollar cost rises unpredictably. Borrow in USD and your dollar income matches your dollar debt — no FX risk, usually a lower rate, but a smaller pool of lenders. For a US earner buying a home to use (not a peso-income rental), a USD cross-border loan is usually the cleaner structure.

The US tax angle (and the currency trap)

Two US tax points every American borrower should know. First, the good news: mortgage interest on a foreign home can be deductible on your US return, within the usual qualified-residence limits, just like a US second home (the fideicomiso fees are not). Second, the trap almost nobody flags: IRS foreign-currency rules (Section 988). If you take a peso mortgage and later pay it off (or refinance) when the peso has weakened, the IRS can treat the difference as a taxable foreign-currency gain — you "saved" dollars repaying a cheaper-peso debt, and that saving is taxable income, even though you merely repaid a loan. There's no matching deductible loss if it moves the other way. This phantom gain catches peso borrowers off guard at payoff. It's the single strongest reason a dollar-earning American often prefers a USD loan: it sidesteps the Section 988 problem entirely. Model it with a cross-border CPA before you sign.

Step-by-step to a Mexican mortgage

  1. Confirm your status — permanent resident, temporary resident, or non-resident — which decides your lender pool.
  2. Get pre-qualified with a cross-border lender or Mexican bank; gather US tax returns, pay/income proof and bank statements.
  3. Choose your currency — USD for a dollar earner avoiding FX risk; pesos only with eyes open.
  4. Budget the deposit and costs — 35%–50% down as a non-resident, plus 5%–8% closing and (coastal) fideicomiso fees.
  5. Engage an independent Mexican lawyer for title, the trust (if coastal), and the loan documents.
  6. Order the appraisal (avalúo) and finalize the loan; the notario (notary) formalizes the deed and mortgage.
  7. Set up US compliance — FBAR for any Mexican account, and confirm interest deductibility and currency treatment with your CPA.

If you're not American: what changes

The Mexican lending side is identical for any foreign buyer. The lender routes, rates, LTV, down payments and the fideicomiso all apply the same whatever passport you hold. What changes is only the home-country tax treatment of your mortgage interest and any currency gain.

If you're not American, the Section 988 currency-gain trap and the US mortgage-interest deduction above are US-specific — they won't apply to you. The currency risk of a peso vs USD loan is real for everyone, but the tax consequences differ. Check three things in your country of residence: (1) whether mortgage interest on a foreign property is deductible at home; (2) whether your country taxes foreign-currency gains on debt repayment; and (3) whether you should borrow in your home currency, USD, or pesos to match your income. A Canadian or European earner may prefer a different currency mix than a US dollar earner. Confirm with a tax adviser in your country of residence.

Is financing in Mexico worth it in 2026?

For the right buyer, yes — financing lets you keep capital working at home while owning in Mexico, and rates on USD cross-border loans are reasonable for a dollar earner. The keys are choosing the currency that matches your income, budgeting a real 35%–50% deposit as a non-resident, and pricing the US currency-gain risk before you commit. Get those three right, line up an independent lawyer, and a Mexican mortgage is a sound, well-trodden path — not the cash-only minefield the myths suggest.

Frequently asked questions

Can an American get a mortgage in Mexico?

Yes. Foreign nationals, including non-residents, can finance Mexican property. Permanent residents get the best bank access; temporary residents qualify with select lenders; non-residents use cross-border USD lenders or developer financing — with more documentation and a bigger deposit.

What are mortgage rates in Mexico for foreigners in 2026?

Peso mortgages run about 9%–14%; cross-border USD loans for qualified US/Canadian buyers are roughly 5%–9%. The currency you borrow in matters as much as the rate.

How much down payment do Americans need to buy in Mexico?

Mexican banks lend ~70–80% to qualified residents; cross-border products cap at 60–70% LTV; non-residents often face 35–50% down. Some fintechs reach 90% LTV for the strongest profiles.

Can you finance property in Mexico's restricted (coastal) zone?

Yes. In the restricted zone you hold title through a fideicomiso (bank trust), and lenders routinely finance trust-held property. Budget the trust's setup and annual fees.

Is mortgage interest on a Mexican property tax-deductible in the US?

Often yes — interest on a qualified first or second home can be deductible within the US limits, even abroad. But a peso loan exposes you to IRS foreign-currency rules at payoff. Confirm both with a cross-border CPA.
