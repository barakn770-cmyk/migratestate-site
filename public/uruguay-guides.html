Uruguay Real Estate & Residency Guides

Lawyer-reviewed guides for Americans buying property or gaining residency in Uruguay — the legal and tax reality, not the dream.

Uruguay is the quiet, dollar-denominated, rule-of-law corner of South America — and foreigners buy on exactly the same terms as citizens. You take full freehold title with no residency required, plan on about 9–11% in closing costs, pay a 2% ITP transfer tax on the below-market cadastral value, and face a flat 12% capital-gains tax on the real gain when you sell. The trap isn't the local tax — it's assuming Uruguay's famous "tax holiday" shelters you. For an American, it largely doesn't. The guides below go deep on each; this page is the map.

Why Americans look at Uruguay

Uruguay has spent decades earning its reputation as the stable corner of the continent — the place Americans and Argentines park capital when they want the rule of law, a real land registry, and no drama. For a property buyer that reputation is largely deserved. There are no nationality restrictions on ownership, transactions are customarily denominated and settled in US dollars, and the whole conveyance runs through a notary system that is genuinely protective rather than a rubber stamp. You do not even need to set foot in the country; a purchase can be completed by power of attorney.

What trips people up is not the buying process — it's the tax story around it. Uruguay is marketed relentlessly as a "tax haven" with a multi-year holiday on foreign income, and that message collided with a 2026 law change that quietly quadrupled the price of entry. Under Law 20.446, effective 1 January 2026, the real-estate threshold for the tax-residency holiday jumped to roughly US$2 million, up from about US$590,000. Our guides walk the transaction with current numbers and then do the thing the glossy sites won't: explain exactly why that holiday is far less useful to an American than to almost anyone else.

Where to buy — the regions

Foreign money in Uruguay concentrates in a handful of well-defined markets, each with its own buyer:

  • Punta del Este — the marquee coastal resort and where most foreign capital lands. Beachfront apartments, luxury villas and a strong seasonal short-let market suit lifestyle buyers and higher-value investors; it is also the natural home for anyone weighing the tax-residency route.
  • Montevideo (Pocitos & Carrasco) — the capital's most sought-after neighborhoods. Pocitos offers walkable, sea-facing apartment living with genuine year-round rental demand; Carrasco is the leafy, upscale residential district favoured by families and long-term residents.
  • José Ignacio — the boutique, high-end coastal enclave east of Punta del Este. Quieter and more exclusive, it draws buyers after a design-led second home rather than rental yield.
  • Colonia (Colonia del Sacramento) — the historic, UNESCO-listed river town facing Buenos Aires. Lower prices, real charm and an easy ferry link to Argentina make it a value option for a slower-paced base.

The one genuine ownership restriction sits far from any of these: certain large rural (agricultural) landholdings may not be held anonymously through bearer-share companies under Law 18.092, a farmland-transparency rule. It has no bearing on buying an apartment in Montevideo or a house in Punta del Este.

The single most important distinction here is the last line: immigration residency and tax residency are two completely different things. You do not need to spend US$2 million, or buy anything at all, to earn the right to live in Uruguay — that is an income test. Conflating the accessible residency-by-income path with the expensive tax-holiday threshold is the most common and most costly mistake foreign buyers make here. And because the 2% ITP is charged on a cadastral value that typically sits 30–40% below market, the effective transfer-tax cost is often closer to 1.2–1.4% of what you actually pay.

Who Uruguay suits — and who should look elsewhere

Uruguay fits buyers who want a stable, dollar-priced, rule-of-law base in the Southern Hemisphere: a protective escribano system, no foreign-buyer penalties, easy income-based residency, and a genuinely strong passport (with broad visa-free access, including Schengen) after three to five years of residence. Because deals settle in dollars, the Section 988 currency-gain problem that bites euro- or peso-mortgage buyers largely doesn't arise.

Uruguay is a weaker fit for an American chasing the "tax haven" pitch. The US taxes citizens on worldwide income no matter where they live, and there is no US–Uruguay tax treaty and no totalization agreement, so the celebrated holiday exempts income at the Uruguayan level while your full US bill keeps running. It is also a weaker fit if you want low closing costs — 9–11% is on the higher side — or a fast citizenship timeline. Uruguay FAQ

Is Uruguay a good place for Americans to buy property in 2026?

For a stable, dollar-denominated, rule-of-law base with a protective notary system, no foreign-buyer penalties and easy income-based residency, it is one of the most reassuring places to own in the hemisphere. Closing costs are a touch high at 9–11%, but the process is clean and the title system trustworthy. The mistakes here are almost always tax mistakes, not property mistakes.

Do Americans need residency or a visa to buy in Uruguay?

No. Uruguay places no nationality restrictions, quotas or approval requirements on foreigners buying residential or urban property, and no residency or visa is required to buy. Americans take full freehold title with the same rights as citizens — to sell, lease, mortgage and inherit — and a purchase can even be completed by power of attorney without visiting.

Does buying property give me residency or the tax holiday?

These are separate. Legal (immigration) residency requires no property purchase at all — just proof of roughly US$1,500/month in stable income. The tax-residency "holiday" does have a property threshold, and under Law 20.446 it rose to about US$2 million (plus 60 days a year in Uruguay) on 1 January 2026, up from roughly US$590,000. Don't confuse the two.

How much are closing costs, really?

Budget 9–11% all-in on the buyer's side: the 2% ITP transfer tax (charged on the below-market cadastral value), escribano fees around 3% plus 22% VAT, agent commission around 3% plus VAT, and small registry and certificate costs. There is no foreign-buyer surcharge — the same rates apply to Uruguayans and foreigners.

Does Uruguay's tax holiday actually help American buyers?

Much less than the marketing suggests. The holiday exempts foreign-source income at the Uruguayan level, but the US taxes its citizens on worldwide income regardless of where they live, and there is no US–Uruguay tax treaty or totalization agreement. So an American who becomes a Uruguayan tax resident still files and pays US tax on that same income, with only the foreign tax credit or FEIE to lean on. The Uruguayan exemption doesn't erase the US bill.
