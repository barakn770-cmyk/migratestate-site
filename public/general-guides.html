General Guides: Buying Property & Residency Abroad

Cross-country guides on costs, taxes, banking, and how to choose the right destination.

Buying property abroad as an American is less about the country than about the layers most guides skip. Every purchase carries three cross-cutting realities: closing costs that run roughly 5–15% on top of the price, a US tax layer that follows the property home no matter where you buy, and a foreign bank account that can trigger FBAR reporting even when the real estate itself never appears on a US form. Get those right and the country almost chooses itself. These guides go deep on each; this page is the map.

Why buy property abroad

Americans buy abroad for four broad reasons, and being honest about which one drives you eliminates half the decision immediately. Some want rental yield and proximity — a dollar-friendly market a short flight away. Some want a vacation or retirement home in a lower-cost, warmer place. Some are chasing a residency or citizenship "Plan B" — a second base with EU access or a stronger passport. And some simply want to diversify assets out of a single currency and market. The mistake is treating "best place to buy abroad" as one question when it is really four, each with a different answer.

What unites all four is that the sticker price is never the real price. A US buyer has to look past sunshine and headline cost to three filters most lists ignore: whether you can own cleanly, whether you can exit without a tax ambush, and whether financing adds a currency bet on top of a property bet. Those filters — not the view from the balcony — decide whether a purchase actually serves you.

Choosing a country

The right country flows from three practical tests, drawn from our full country comparisons:

  • Ownership friction — can you hold freehold in your own name, or are there restricted zones, trusts or permits? Mexico's coastal fideicomiso bank trust and the Philippines' outright land ban for foreigners are the classic examples of "yes, but" markets.
  • Residency upside — in 2026, only a few markets tie residency to the purchase itself. Greece grants a golden visa from €400K in most regions — €800K in Athens, Thessaloniki, Mykonos, Santorini and the larger islands, and €250K only for restorations/conversions — and Turkey offers citizenship at USD 400,000. Spain and Portugal both ended their real-estate residency routes.
  • The combined tax position — a "high-tax" country can cost an American less overall than a zero-tax one, because foreign tax you actually pay can be credited against your US bill, while a local tax holiday leaves you fully exposed to the IRS.

Lead with your use case, set a true all-in budget (price plus 5–15% costs plus ongoing taxes), check ownership friction for the exact property, and only then rank on tax. Our best countries and best golden visa guides score the majors this way.

The costs & US tax every buyer faces

Wherever you buy, the money splits into three buckets: what you pay to acquire, what you pay to hold, and what the US expects regardless of local rules. Here is how the popular markets compare on the headline figures.

The interpretive point most lists miss: the cheapest sticker price is rarely the cheapest after US tax. Because the US taxes citizens on worldwide income and the foreign tax credit only offsets tax you actually paid abroad, the zero-tax darlings — Dubai, or countries after their capital-gains holidays — give you nothing to credit, so the IRS taxes your rent and gains in full. Layer in currency risk (a euro or peso mortgage can manufacture a taxable dollar gain on payoff), and the real ranking depends on your combined US-plus-local position. Foreign rental income runs on Schedule E with a slower 30-year depreciation; a future sale is a US capital gain; and inheritance can trigger both US estate tax and local forced-heirship rules. The property itself is not FBAR-reportable — but the foreign bank account you open to buy it usually is.

Is buying abroad right for you

Buying abroad fits the buyer who leads with a clear use case, budgets for the full 5–15% of costs plus ongoing tax, engages an independent local lawyer, and lines up a cross-border CPA before — not after — closing. If you can hold freehold cleanly in your target market, exit without a tax surprise, and finance without betting the house on a currency move, the upside is real.

Buying abroad is a weaker fit if you are seduced by a headline rate without modelling the combined tax bill, if you plan to lean on a local "tax holiday" that does nothing for a US citizen, or if you would improvise around ownership rules (a nominee, an undisclosed trust) rather than buy only what you can cleanly title. The buyers who get hurt are almost never the ones who did it by the book — they are the ones who let sunshine and a sticker price drive a six-figure decision. General FAQ

How much does it cost to buy property abroad beyond the price?

Plan for roughly 5–15% on top of the purchase price, depending on the country. That covers transfer or stamp tax, notary and legal fees, registration, and agent commission where the buyer pays it. Greece (~7–10%) and Dubai (~7–8%) sit at the low end; Spain and Italy (~11–15%) at the high end. Add ongoing costs — annual property tax, and for Mexico's coastal zone, the fideicomiso trust fee.

Do Americans always owe US tax on property bought abroad?

The purchase itself usually creates no US tax event, but earning from or selling the property does. The US taxes worldwide income, so foreign rent goes on Schedule E and a future sale is a US capital gain, wherever the property is. Treaties and the foreign tax credit relieve most double tax — but a local "tax holiday" gives you nothing to credit, leaving you fully exposed to the IRS.

Which country gives residency when you buy property?

Fewer than buyers expect. In 2026, Greece ties a golden visa to a purchase from €400K in most regions (€800K in Athens, Thessaloniki, Mykonos, Santorini and larger islands; €250K only for restorations/conversions), Turkey offers citizenship at USD 400,000, and Dubai links a renewable investor visa to freehold ownership. Spain and Portugal both ended their real-estate residency routes, and Mexico and Italy grant residency through separate visa programs rather than the purchase.

Do I have to report a foreign property to the IRS?

Directly held foreign real estate is not itself reportable on the FBAR or Form 8938 — but the foreign bank account you open to buy and run it usually is. The FBAR is triggered when your foreign accounts together top $10,000 at any point in the year, and Form 8938 by its own higher thresholds. Holding property through a foreign company changes this and can pull in complex reporting, so structure carefully.

What is the biggest mistake Americans make buying abroad?

Ranking on the local sticker price and ignoring the combined US-plus-local tax math. A high-tax country can cost an American less overall, because foreign tax paid credits against the US bill, while a zero-tax market leaves the full US liability standing. The second-biggest is improvising around ownership rules instead of buying only what can be cleanly titled — and skipping an independent local lawyer and cross-border CPA.
