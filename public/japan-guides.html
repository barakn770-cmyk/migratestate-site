Why Americans look at Japan

Japan does something almost no other country does: it lets a foreigner — resident or not — buy land and buildings freehold, in their own name, with no permit, no nationality restriction, no minimum investment and no foreign-buyer surcharge. Combine that with a weak yen, a functioning legal registry, and a stock of cheap rural akiya (vacant houses), and it is easy to see why American buyers keep looking. Tokyo and Osaka offer genuinely liquid urban markets; Niseko and Hakuba offer ski property with real rental demand.

The catch is that openness is not the same as ease. Buying grants no residency of any kind. Financing is effectively unavailable to non-residents, so Japan is a cash market for most foreign buyers. Buildings depreciate hard in Japanese valuation practice — the land holds value, the structure often does not — so capital-growth assumptions imported from the US market tend to be wrong. And from 1 April 2026 a non-resident buyer must file a post-transaction report with the Bank of Japan under FEFTA, generally within about 20 days of acquisition; it is a reporting obligation, not a restriction, and usually handled by your judicial scrivener at closing.

Who Japan suits — and who should look elsewhere

Japan fits the cash buyer who wants unrestricted freehold ownership in a rule-of-law market, who values a Tokyo or Osaka income asset or a Niseko ski property, and who understands that the land — not the building — is what holds value. Japan FAQ

Can an American buy property in Japan?

Yes — and unusually freely. Japan places no nationality restriction on real estate. A foreigner, resident or not, can buy land and buildings freehold in their own name, with no visa, no residency requirement, no minimum spend and no foreign-buyer surcharge.

What are the costs of buying in Japan?

Budget roughly 7%–10% of the price in one-time costs: real-estate acquisition tax (about 3% for residential, with a reduced land rate running through March 2029), registration and stamp duty, judicial scrivener fees, and agent commission on the standard 3% + ¥60,000 + consumption tax formula. The annual fixed-asset tax is about 1.4% of assessed value, plus a city planning tax of roughly 0.3%.

What is the new Bank of Japan filing in 2026?

From 1 April 2026, a non-resident who acquires Japanese real estate must file a post-transaction report to the Bank of Japan under the Foreign Exchange and Foreign Trade Act (FEFTA), generally within about 20 days. It is a reporting obligation, not a restriction, and your judicial scrivener or agent normally files it as part of closing. Japan is separately adding a nationality field to the property registry from fiscal 2026.

Are cheap akiya houses a good deal?

Sometimes — but price the traps first. Residential land in Japan gets a large fixed-asset-tax reduction, and losing it (for example, if the property is deemed a neglected vacant house) can raise the land tax by up to six times. Add renovation costs, rural depreciation of the structure, and the practical difficulty of managing a countryside property from the US.

Does buying property in Japan give me a visa?

No. Ownership grants no immigration status and no path to residency or citizenship. Living in Japan requires a separate visa route — work, business manager, spouse, or another qualifying category.
