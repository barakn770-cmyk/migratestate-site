Best Countries for Americans to Buy Property Abroad in 2026

MS By The MigrateState Editorial Team · Reviewed by a licensed international real-estate attorney  ·  Updated June 2026  ·  11 min read
There's no single "best" — there's a best for your goal. For proximity and rental yield, Mexico. For low costs plus residency, Greece (3.09% transfer tax + golden visa). For an EU passport path, Portugal. For value and lifestyle, Italy. For zero local tax, Dubai. The trap most lists miss: the cheapest sticker price isn't the cheapest after US tax and currency risk.

Every "best places to buy abroad" list ranks sunshine and sticker price. For an American, those are the wrong filters. The countries that actually serve a US buyer are the ones where you can own cleanly, exit without a tax ambush, and finance without betting the house on a currency move. Here's the 2026 shortlist scored the way a US buyer should score it — with a link to a full guide for each.

How to actually rank a country

Three filters most lists skip decide whether a country is genuinely good for an American:

  • Ownership friction — can you hold freehold in your own name, or are there restricted zones, trusts or permits? (Mexico's coastal fideicomiso, Greece's border zones.)
  • The US-tax reality — a local "tax holiday" that gives you nothing to credit can leave you more exposed to the IRS, not less.
  • Currency & financing — a euro or peso mortgage adds a currency bet on top of a property bet, and can create "phantom" US tax on the payoff.

Mexico — closest & highest yield

For most Americans, Mexico is the default first move abroad: a short flight, a dollar-friendly rental market in spots like Tulum, Puerto Vallarta and Mexico City, and closing costs around 5–10%. The one quirk is the restricted zone (within 50 km of the coast or 100 km of a border), where foreigners hold property through a bank trust (fideicomiso) rather than directly — routine, but a real annual cost. Outside that zone you own outright. Full detail in our Mexico property guide and Mexico mortgage guide.

Greece — cheapest entry + residency

Greece is the value champion of 2026: a transfer tax of just 3.09%, all-in costs of 7–10%, prices below the western Mediterranean, and — uniquely among the majors — a real-estate golden visa from €250K. If you want a home and EU residency in one purchase, nowhere else on this list does both. Read the full Greece property guide.

Portugal — the EU-passport play

Portugal lost its real-estate golden visa, so a purchase no longer buys residency. But the market stays popular for lifestyle and rental, costs are moderate (~7–8%), and the country still offers the clearest route to an EU passport via its fund-based golden visa. Buy here for the home and the community; pursue residency separately. See the Portugal property guide.

Italy & Spain — lifestyle and liquidity

Italy wins on romance and, thanks to the cadastral-value tax base, is cheaper to buy than its 9% headline implies — though costs land at 10–15% all-in. Spain is the most liquid market here, with deep expat infrastructure, but the highest entry costs (11–15%) and a scrapped golden visa. Both let you own freehold cleanly. Compare the Italy guide and the Spain guide.

Dubai — zero local tax

Dubai is the outlier: no annual property tax, no income tax, no capital-gains tax locally, full freehold in designated areas, and a residency visa tied to your purchase. Costs are low (~7–8%, mostly the 4% DLD fee). The catch is entirely on the US side — with no local tax to credit, your rental income and gains fall fully under the IRS. See the Dubai property + golden visa guide.

The US-tax filter that reshuffles the list

Here's the nuance that rearranges every "cheapest country" ranking for an American. Sites score countries on local cost and local tax. But a US citizen pays US tax on worldwide income, and the foreign tax credit only offsets tax you actually paid abroad. So the zero-tax darlings — Dubai, and Greece or Italy after their capital-gains holidays — give you nothing to credit, meaning the IRS taxes your rental income and sale gain in full. A "high-tax" country like Spain can, paradoxically, cost an American less in total, because the Spanish tax you pay credits against your US bill instead of stacking on top of it. Layer in currency risk — a euro or peso mortgage can manufacture a taxable dollar gain on payoff even if the local price never moved — and the real ranking depends on your combined US-plus-local position, not the local sticker. Run that math, country by country, before you fall for a headline rate.

How to choose your country

  1. Lead with use case — rental income, a vacation home, retirement, or a residency/Plan B. It eliminates half the list immediately.
  2. Set a true all-in budget — price plus 5–15% costs, plus ongoing taxes and (for Mexico) trust fees.
  3. Check ownership friction — freehold vs trust vs restricted zone for the exact property.
  4. Decide if residency matters — only Greece and Dubai tie it to the purchase in 2026.
  5. Model the combined US + local tax with a cross-border CPA, including currency exposure.
  6. Engage an independent local lawyer in the winning country before you place a deposit.

If you're not American: what changes

The country-by-country mechanics are identical for any foreign buyer. The closing costs, ownership rules, restricted zones and residency routes above apply the same whatever passport you hold. Only the home-country tax filter — the third lens in this guide — is different for you.

If you're not American, keep the ownership, cost and residency comparisons exactly as written; they don't change with nationality. What flips is the tax-ranking logic. The "foreign tax credit" warning above is US-specific — most other countries tax on residency, so if you're not tax-resident in a high-tax home country, a zero-tax market like Dubai can be genuinely tax-efficient for you in a way it never is for an American. Before ranking on tax, check three things in your country of residence: (1) whether it taxes foreign rental income and capital gains; (2) whether you must report foreign property and accounts at home; and (3) whether it has a double-taxation treaty with the country you're buying in. Confirm with a tax adviser in your country of residence.

So where should an American buy in 2026?

If you want yield and proximity, Mexico. If you want the cheapest entry plus residency, Greece. If you want an EU passport eventually, buy where you love and pursue Portugal's route separately. If you want lifestyle value, Italy; liquidity, Spain; zero local tax (eyes open on the US side), Dubai. Score on use case, ownership friction, and the combined US-plus-local tax math — not on sunshine and a sticker price — and the right country for you becomes obvious.

Frequently asked questions

What is the best country for Americans to buy property abroad in 2026?

It depends on your goal. Mexico for proximity and yield, Greece for low costs plus a golden visa, Portugal for an EU-passport path, Italy for value, Dubai for zero local tax, Spain for liquidity. Match it to budget, use case and visit frequency.

Where is the cheapest place for Americans to buy property abroad?

On closing costs, Greece (~7–10%, 3.09% transfer tax) and Dubai (~7–8%) are lowest. Italy's 9% headline drops on the cadastral value. Mexico runs ~5–10%; Spain is higher at ~11–15%.

Can Americans own property abroad outright?

In most markets, yes — Greece, Portugal, Italy, Spain and Dubai allow full freehold. Mexico requires a bank trust (fideicomiso) only in the restricted coastal/border zone; elsewhere you own outright.

Do Americans pay US tax on property bought abroad?

Potentially. The US taxes worldwide income, so foreign rental income and a sale are IRS-reportable anywhere, and a foreign account can trigger FBAR/FATCA. Treaties and the foreign tax credit help, but local tax holidays can leave you fully exposed.

Which country abroad gives residency when you buy property?

Greece (golden visa from €250K) and Dubai (renewable visa, plus a 10-year Golden Visa from AED 2M). Spain and Portugal ended their real-estate routes; Mexico and Italy grant residency through separate visas.
