Capital Gains Tax on Property Abroad (2026)

MS By The MigrateState Editorial Team · Reviewed by a licensed international real-estate attorney  ·  Updated June 2026  ·  11 min read
Selling property abroad is a US taxable event, even if it was taxed where it sits. A long-term gain is taxed at 0%, 15% or 20%, plus the 3.8% NIIT for higher earners. A foreign principal residence still gets the Section 121 exclusion ($250k single / $500k joint), and a foreign tax credit offsets foreign tax paid. The two traps: the gain is computed in US dollars, so a weak dollar inflates it, and the credit on a long-term gain is cut down by a rate adjustment — so a US bill often survives.

Buying abroad is the exciting part; selling is where the US tax actually lands. The day you close on that apartment in Lisbon or villa in Tulum, the IRS treats it exactly like selling a house in Ohio — because as a US citizen or green-card holder you are taxed on your worldwide gains, wherever the dirt happens to be. The good news is that the same reliefs apply: the preferential long-term rates, the Section 121 home-sale exclusion, and the foreign tax credit. The bad news is that two quiet mechanics — a gain measured in dollars rather than local currency, and a credit that shrinks on long-term gains — routinely leave Americans with a US bill they didn't expect. Here is the 2026 map of how the sale is taxed, what relief you can claim, and where the surprises hide.

How the US taxes a foreign sale

The mechanics are unremarkable and that is the point: a foreign property sale flows onto Form 8949 and Schedule D like any capital asset. Hold the property for more than one year and the gain is long-term, taxed at the preferential 0/15/20% rates; sell inside a year and it is short-term, taxed at your ordinary income rate. On top of the rate sits the 3.8% Net Investment Income Tax (NIIT) once your modified AGI passes $200,000 (single) or $250,000 (married filing jointly). The sale is reportable even if you owe nothing after credits or exclusions — the only common exception is a principal residence whose entire gain is excluded under Section 121 and for which you received no Form 1099-S. Note that FIRPTA, the withholding regime people often half-remember, runs the other way: it applies to foreigners selling US property, not to Americans selling abroad.

Why the gain is figured in dollars

This is the trap that catches sellers who did everything else right. Your gain is not computed in euros, pesos or pounds — it is computed in US dollars, twice over. You translate the purchase price (plus capital improvements and buying costs that form your basis) into dollars at the exchange rate on the day you bought, and you translate the sale proceeds into dollars at the rate on the day you sold. The difference between those two dollar figures is your taxable gain.

The consequence is unforgiving: if the dollar weakened against the local currency between purchase and sale, you can show a meaningful dollar gain even on a property that barely moved in local-currency terms — and you owe US tax on that phantom appreciation. The reverse also bites: if the dollar strengthened, a property that rose locally can convert into a smaller dollar gain or even a loss for US purposes. Sellers who only watch the local price tag are routinely blindsided. The discipline is to run the dollar math at both ends before you sell, because the exchange rate on closing day is part of your tax bill whether you planned for it or not.

The Section 121 exclusion abroad

Here is the relief most people don't realize travels with them: the Section 121 principal-residence exclusion applies to a foreign home exactly as it does to a US one. If you owned and used the property as your main home for at least 730 days (two years) within the five years ending on the sale — and the two years need not be continuous — you can exclude up to $250,000 of gain if single, or $500,000 if married filing jointly. You also can't have used the exclusion on another home sale in the prior two years.

Two qualifiers matter for property bought as an investment and later lived in (or vice versa). First, periods the home was a rental after 1 January 2009 count as non-qualified use and proportionally reduce the exclusion. Second, any depreciation you claimed while it was a rental is not covered by Section 121 — it is recaptured separately (see below). For a clean expat case — you bought a flat abroad, lived in it for years, and sold — the exclusion can erase most or all of the gain. But note the sting in the tail: if the local country also exempts your residence (as several do), you'll have no foreign tax to credit, so any gain above the $250k/$500k line is taxed by the US at full rate with nothing to offset it.

The foreign tax credit's hidden shortfall

Most guides wave at the foreign tax credit (Form 1116) and imply it cancels your US capital gains tax. Often it doesn't, and this is the genuine nuance worth understanding before you sell. Three forces conspire against a clean offset.

First, the rate-differential haircut. Because the US taxes your long-term gain at a preferential rate (15% or 20%), Form 1116 requires you to shrink the foreign-source gain in the credit limitation before claiming it — you multiply the gain by roughly 0.4054 if it's taxed at 15%, or 0.5405 at 20%. The logic is that the credit should only relieve the US tax actually imposed on that income, not a hypothetical full-rate tax. The practical effect is that even when you paid solid foreign tax, the credit you're allowed to use against the gain is materially smaller than the foreign tax you actually paid.

Second, the passive-basket trap. A property gain falls in the passive credit category. Any foreign tax beyond what the haircut lets you use becomes an excess credit that can only offset other passive foreign income — future dividends or another foreign sale. If you have none, the excess carries for a limited window and then simply expires unused. Many one-time sellers never recover it.

Third, timing and zero-tax countries. The foreign country may tax the gain in a different tax year than the US recognizes it, breaking the year-to-year match unless you elect to claim credits on the accrual basis — the IRS flags this mismatch frequently. And where the country exempts a long-held or primary residence (common in parts of Europe), there is simply no foreign tax to credit at all, leaving the full US tax standing. The honest planning rule: don't assume the credit zeroes you out. Model it.

Depreciation recapture if you rented

If the property was ever a rental and you (correctly) claimed depreciation on Schedule E, the IRS wants that benefit back at sale. Unrecaptured Section 1250 gain — the portion of your gain attributable to depreciation taken — is taxed at a maximum rate of 25%, higher than the 15/20% long-term rate, and it sits outside the Section 121 exclusion. Worse, recapture applies to depreciation you were entitled to take whether or not you actually claimed it, so skipping depreciation while renting doesn't dodge the recapture — it just wastes the deduction. Foreign residential property depreciates over 30 years (not the US 27.5), which slightly lowers the annual figure and therefore the eventual recapture, but the principle is unchanged: rent it, and a slice of your gain is carved out for ordinary-leaning treatment.

Step-by-step at sale

  1. Rebuild your dollar basis — translate purchase price, buying costs and capital improvements into US dollars at the exchange rate on each original date.
  2. Translate the proceeds — convert the sale price to dollars at the closing-day rate; the gap from your dollar basis is the gain.
  3. Apply Section 121 if it was your principal residence — confirm the 730-day test and reduce for any post-2008 rental (non-qualified) use.
  4. Carve out depreciation recapture — tax the unrecaptured Section 1250 portion at up to 25%, separate from the exclusion.
  5. Classify the rest — long-term (0/15/20%) plus 3.8% NIIT if your income clears the threshold.
  6. Compute the credit properly — file Form 1116, apply the rate-differential adjustment, and check whether excess passive credits will actually be usable.
  7. Report even at zero — file Schedule D / Form 8949, and don't forget the FBAR and Form 8938 on the accounts that receive your proceeds.

If you're not American: what changes

The property side is identical for any foreign owner. The country where the property sits will tax the gain (or exempt it) under the same local rules regardless of your passport, and the local sale process, fees and notary costs don't change. What's purely American is the home-country wrapper — US capital gains rates, the Section 121 exclusion, the dollar-basis calculation, and the Form 1116 credit.

If you're not a US person, drop the IRS layer above and substitute your own country of tax residence. Check three things: (1) whether your home country taxes a foreign capital gain on worldwide basis — most do, though many give relief or an exemption for a main residence (the UK and others) or after a holding period; (2) whether you must report the disposal or the proceeds at home, for example on Canada's T1135 or via UK self-assessment; and (3) whether a double-taxation treaty exists between your country and the country where the property sits, so the local tax you pay is credited rather than stacked. A Canadian, Brit or Australian computes the gain in their own currency and under their own reliefs, not the US dollar-basis rule. Confirm the specifics with a tax adviser where you're resident.

Is selling smart in 2026?

Selling foreign property in 2026 is entirely manageable — but only if you treat the tax as part of the deal, not an afterthought. The reliefs are real: hold over a year for the preferential rate, claim Section 121 if you lived there, and credit the foreign tax you paid. The losses happen when sellers assume the credit erases everything and ignore the two quiet mechanics — the gain measured in dollars, which a weak dollar can inflate into phantom income, and the rate-differential haircut that leaves a US bill standing even after foreign tax. Run both calculations before you sign, time the sale to land in a favorable bracket where you can, and keep every receipt and exchange-rate record. Do that, and the sale is a clean exit rather than an April surprise. The math rewards the seller who plans the closing date as carefully as the purchase.

Frequently asked questions

Do US citizens pay capital gains tax when they sell property abroad?

Yes. You're taxed on worldwide income, so a foreign property gain goes on Schedule D and Form 8949 like a US sale, even if the host country taxed it too. A long-term gain (held over a year) is taxed at 0/15/20% plus 3.8% NIIT for higher earners. A foreign tax credit usually softens, but does not always erase, the US bill.

Does the Section 121 home-sale exclusion apply to a foreign home?

Yes — the IRS applies Section 121 to a foreign principal residence the same way as a US one. Own and use it as your main home for 730 days within the five years before sale and you can exclude up to $250,000 (single) or $500,000 (joint). Post-2008 rental periods are non-qualified use and reduce the exclusion, and depreciation is recaptured separately.

How is the gain on foreign property calculated for US tax?

Entirely in US dollars. You translate the purchase price and improvements at the exchange rate on the purchase date, and the sale proceeds at the rate on the sale date. Currency movement alone can create or enlarge a taxable gain even when the property barely rose locally — a frequent surprise in years the dollar weakened.

Will the foreign tax credit cancel my US capital gains tax?

Not always. The country may exempt a long-held or primary residence (no foreign tax to credit), the credit on a preferential long-term gain is shrunk by the Form 1116 rate-differential adjustment, and it sits in the passive basket, so excess often can't be used and expires. With a low or zero local rate, expect a US check.

Do I have to report the sale if I owe no US tax?

Usually yes — the sale is reportable even if a credit or Section 121 drops your tax to zero. The common exception is a principal residence fully excluded under Section 121 where you got no Form 1099-S. Separately, remember FBAR and Form 8938 on the accounts receiving the proceeds, which apply regardless of tax due.
