Buying Property in Spain as an American (2026 Guide)

Spain is the most-searched European destination for American property buyers — sun, coastline, big cities with deep expat roots, and prices that still undercut comparable spots in California or the US Northeast. Buying is open and well-trodden. What changed for 2026 is the politics around it: Spain ended the residency-by-investment route, floated an eye-watering tax on non-EU buyers, and reformed its transfer-tax scale. Separating what's actually law from what's political noise is where most guides fall down. Here's the grounded version.

Can Americans buy property in Spain?

Yes — fully and without restriction. Spain imposes no limits on foreign ownership of real estate. As a US citizen you can buy an apartment, a villa, or land with the same legal rights as a Spanish national, hold it in your own name, and complete the purchase remotely through a lawyer with power of attorney. You do not need to be a resident or hold a visa to own. The one prerequisite is a Spanish foreigner tax number, the NIE.

What you need before you buy

Four essentials turn an American into a "ready" buyer in Spain:

  • A NIE (Número de Identificación de Extranjero) — your foreigner tax number, required for the purchase, taxes, utilities and a bank account. It takes anywhere from a few days to about three weeks.
  • A Spanish bank account — to pay the deposit, taxes and ongoing bills. Expect FATCA checks: the bank will likely ask for an IRS W-9.
  • An independent lawyer (abogado) — separate from the agent and developer, to run due diligence on title, debts, planning permissions and community fees.
  • Funds documentation — Spanish notaries and banks apply anti-money-laundering checks on the source of your funds.

The purchase taxes: ITP, IVA & AJD

Which tax you pay depends on whether the home is resale or new-build — and, for resale, on the autonomous community (region) it sits in.

Resale homes pay ITP (Impuesto de Transmisiones Patrimoniales), a transfer tax set regionally, generally 6%–11%. ITP is set by each autonomous community — there is no single national rate. Regional rates in 2026 range from approximately 6% to 11% depending on location and price. Andalusia charges ~7%; Murcia ~7.75%; Catalonia uses progressive brackets. The Valencian Community (Valencia, Alicante and Castellón provinces) reduced its rate from 10% to 9% on resale homes under €1,000,000 (11% above €1M) effective 1 June 2026 under Ley 5/2025 de la Comunitat Valenciana — a regional law, not a national Spanish reform. If your property is in Valencia, Alicante or Castellón, that 9% rate applies; buyers elsewhere pay their own region's rate. Always confirm the ITP rate for the specific autonomous community where your property sits.

New-build homes pay IVA (VAT) instead of ITP — a flat 10% on residential property — plus AJD stamp duty of about 1%–2%, also set regionally.

Crucially: there is no foreigner surcharge. A non-resident American currently pays the same ITP or IVA as a Spanish buyer on the same property (more on the proposed change below).

The Golden Visa is gone — and the "100% tax" truth

This is the section that separates an informed buyer from a panicked one, and it's where the genuine 2026 nuance lives.

1) The Golden Visa is dead. On 3 April 2025, Organic Law 1/2025 repealed Spain's residency-by-investment scheme after twelve years. Buying property in Spain no longer grants any residence permit. If your goal is to actually live in Spain, the routes in 2026 are the non-lucrative visa (NLV) — for those who can show sufficient passive income/savings and won't work locally — or the digital-nomad visa for remote workers. Both require you to genuinely reside; neither is triggered by a purchase.

2) The "100% tax" is a proposal, not a law — and may never be one. In January 2025 the government floated a headline-grabbing idea: a tax of up to 100% of the property value on purchases by non-EU, non-resident buyers (Americans included). A draft bill was submitted to Parliament in May 2025. But here is what scare-stories omit: as of 2026 it has not been enacted, the governing coalition lacks the parliamentary majority to pass it cleanly, and tax and EU-law specialists widely judge it vulnerable to challenge because singling out non-EU buyers brushes against the EU principle of free movement of capital (which protects capital flows to and from third countries, not just within the EU). Translation for an American buyer today: you pay the same ITP/IVA as a local, but you should build the possibility of a future surcharge into your timing and have your lawyer track the bill's progress before you commit to a multi-year plan.

Mortgages for US buyers

Spanish banks lend to non-resident Americans, on more conservative terms than to residents. Expect a loan-to-value of about 60%–70% (so a 30%–40% down payment), rates linked to the Euribor benchmark, and a request for US tax returns, pay/income proof and bank statements. Financing a new-build also means AJD stamp duty on the mortgage deed. Many American buyers pay cash for speed and negotiating power, then consider refinancing later.

Step-by-step: buying property in Spain as an American

  1. Get your NIE — in person at a Spanish consulate in the US, or in Spain, or via your lawyer under power of attorney.
  2. Open a Spanish bank account (expect a W-9 / FATCA check) for the deposit, taxes and bills.
  3. Engage an independent lawyer to verify title, debts, community fees, and that licenses/planning are in order.
  4. Sign the reservation + contrato de arras (deposit contract), typically 10%. Under the standard arras, if you back out you forfeit the deposit; if the seller backs out they repay double.
  5. Pay the purchase tax — ITP (resale) or IVA + AJD (new build) — around completion.
  6. Sign the escritura (title deed) before a notary, in person or via your lawyer.
  7. Register the property at the Land Registry in your name.
  8. Set up US tax compliance — FBAR for the bank account, and plan for rental/resale reporting.

Start to finish, the typical timeline is two to three months.

The US tax rules that still apply

Your Spanish lawyer handles Spain; the IRS is your problem. The United States taxes citizens on worldwide income, so rental income from your Spanish home is reportable on Schedule E, and a future sale is a US capital gain. Because Spain levies its own taxes, you can usually use the foreign tax credit to offset most of the US bill — but you still file in both countries. A Spanish bank account can trigger FBAR (FinCEN Form 114) and possibly FATCA (Form 8938) once balances cross the thresholds. The wrinkle nobody warns Americans about: currency gains. If the euro strengthens against the dollar between purchase and sale, the IRS can tax a "phantom" gain on the mortgage payoff measured in dollars — even if the property's euro value didn't move. Is buying property in Spain worth it in 2026?

For lifestyle and value, Spain still earns its popularity — the buying process is clean, foreigners pay the same taxes as locals, and the market is liquid. The 2026 caveats are political, not procedural: a purchase no longer buys you residency, and there's a live (if unlikely) proposal to tax non-EU buyers far more heavily down the road. Buy with an independent lawyer, budget the real 11%–15%, line up your US tax plan first, and keep an eye on that draft bill — and Spain remains one of the most rewarding places an American can own a home in Europe.

Frequently asked questions

Can a US citizen buy property in Spain?

Yes. Spain places no restrictions on foreign ownership. US citizens can buy outright with the same rights as a local; you only need a NIE (foreigner tax number), not residency or a visa.

How much does it cost to buy property in Spain as an American?

Plan for 11%–15% on top of the price. Resale homes pay ITP transfer tax (~6%–11% by region); new builds pay 10% VAT plus 1%–2% stamp duty (AJD). Lawyer, notary and registry add ~1.5%–3%.

Does buying property in Spain give you residency or a Golden Visa?

No. Spain ended its Golden Visa on 3 April 2025, so a purchase no longer grants residency. To live in Spain, Americans typically use the non-lucrative visa (NLV) or the digital-nomad visa.

Is there a 100% tax on non-EU buyers in Spain?

Not as law. It was proposed politically in early 2025 and a draft bill was submitted, but it hasn't been enacted, lacks a clear majority, and likely conflicts with EU free-movement-of-capital rules. In 2026 Americans pay the same property taxes as Spaniards.

Do I pay US tax on a property I buy in Spain?

Potentially. The US taxes worldwide income, so Spanish rental income and a future sale are IRS-reportable. You can usually credit Spanish tax via the foreign tax credit, and a Spanish bank account can trigger FBAR/FATCA reporting.
