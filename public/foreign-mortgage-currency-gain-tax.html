The Foreign Mortgage Phantom Gain (Section 988), 2026

MS By The MigrateState Editorial Team · Reviewed by a licensed international real-estate attorney  ·  Updated July 2026  ·  10 min read
Your foreign mortgage is a second taxable transaction, separate from the house. Under IRC Section 988, if the dollar strengthens between the day you borrow and the day you repay, the dollars you saved extinguishing the debt are taxable ordinary income — up to 37% federal in 2026. It is triggered on payoff, sale or refinance, no cash ever changes hands, and the Section 121 home-sale exclusion does not shelter it.

What Section 988 actually taxes

Almost every American who finances a property abroad thinks about the mortgage in one currency and one currency only: the local one. You borrow €300,000 to buy in Portugal, you pay it back in euros, and when you eventually sell you assume the only US tax question is the gain on the house. That assumption is wrong, and it is expensive.

Section 988 of the Internal Revenue Code governs "certain foreign currency transactions." Borrowing in a currency that isn't your functional currency — and for an individual US taxpayer, the functional currency is always the US dollar — is one of them. The statute is blunt about the consequence: any foreign currency gain or loss on a Section 988 transaction is computed separately from the underlying deal and is treated as ordinary income or loss.

In plain English: when you take out a euro mortgage, the IRS considers that you have effectively taken a short position against the euro. You owe a fixed number of euros. If the euro falls against the dollar before you repay, buying back those euros costs you fewer dollars than the dollars you notionally received. You have made money on the currency, and the IRS wants tax on it — in the year you close out the position, at ordinary rates, whether or not a single dollar of profit ever appeared in your account.

Why the IRS splits the house from the loan

The instinct is to treat a mortgaged purchase as one transaction. Tax law does not. The property is a capital asset; the debt is a financial instrument. They have different holding periods, different character on disposal, and — critically — different reliefs.

That separation is what makes the phantom gain possible. The house is measured against its dollar basis on the purchase date. The loan is measured against the dollar value of the amount borrowed on the day it was drawn. Those two clocks can run in opposite directions. A property can be flat or down in local terms while the debt generates a large taxable gain, or the house can boom while the loan produces nothing at all. There is no netting between them.

A worked example in euros

Suppose you bought in 2021 with a €300,000 mortgage when the euro was worth $1.20. You sell in 2026 and pay the remaining balance off when the euro is worth $1.08. Assume for simplicity you had repaid €40,000 of principal and are extinguishing €260,000 at closing.

Nothing about that $31,200 is economic profit you can spend. You did not receive it. You cannot point to it in a bank statement. It exists because the same euro obligation was worth fewer dollars on the day you settled it than on the day you took it on. At a 37% marginal rate the same facts produce roughly $11,544 of federal tax, before any state tax.

Scale matters here. A €300,000 loan and a twelve-cent move in the euro produce a five-figure bill. A €900,000 loan through a larger swing produces a six-figure one. This is not a rounding error, and it is the single most common item missed on the US return of an American who financed a home in Europe.

Why Section 121 doesn't save you

This is where most people are caught. Section 121 lets you exclude up to $250,000 of gain ($500,000 filing jointly) on the sale of a principal residence, and — as we cover in our guide to capital gains on property abroad — the IRS applies it to a foreign home the same way it applies to a home in Ohio, provided you meet the 730-day ownership and use tests within the five years before sale.

But Section 121 excludes gain from the sale of a residence. The Section 988 gain is not gain from the sale of a residence. It is ordinary income arising on the disposition of a debt instrument that happened to be secured by that residence. Two separate transactions, two separate characterisations, and the exclusion reaches only one of them.

The practical result is jarring and entirely routine: a couple sells a Lisbon apartment, excludes every dollar of the $400,000 house gain under Section 121, files a return showing zero capital gain — and owes ordinary income tax on a $60,000 currency gain from the mortgage they were delighted to finally pay off. Their US return shows tax due on a sale they were told would be tax-free.

The gain is taxable; the loss often isn't

Here is the nuance almost no guide mentions, and it is the one worth reading twice. Section 988 is not symmetrical for homeowners.

If the currency moves against you — the euro strengthens, and repaying costs you more dollars than you notionally received — you have a Section 988 loss. You might reasonably expect to deduct it against ordinary income. Frequently you cannot. Where the borrowing is personal in nature, meaning a mortgage on a home you occupy rather than an investment or business asset, the resulting loss is generally treated as a personal loss and disallowed under the general rule that personal losses are not deductible. The gain, meanwhile, is fully taxable in every scenario.

The asymmetry flips if the property is a genuine rental or business asset, where the debt is incurred in a profit-seeking activity and the loss is far more likely to be deductible. That difference — personal residence versus rental — can be worth tens of thousands of dollars, and it turns on how the property is actually used and documented over the life of the loan, not on what you called it at the closing table. What triggers it — and what doesn't

The gain crystallises on disposition of the debt, not on the passage of time. Exchange-rate movement while you simply hold the mortgage is not taxable — there is no annual mark-to-market for an individual's personal foreign mortgage.

EventSection 988 consequence
Selling the property and paying off the loanTriggered in full on the balance extinguished
Paying off the mortgage without sellingTriggered — the sale is irrelevant; the debt is what matters
Refinancing into a new loanGenerally triggered where the old debt is legally extinguished and replaced
A large lump-sum principal prepaymentPartial disposition — commonly computed pro rata on the amount repaid
Ordinary monthly amortisationTechnically a partial disposition; widely treated as de minimis in practice, but that is convention, not statute
Currency moves while you keep payingNot triggered — no disposition, no gain
Switching lenders in the same currencyDepends on whether the original obligation is extinguished — get it reviewed
Interest paymentsTranslated at the spot rate when paid; separate from the principal calculation

Two points deserve emphasis. First, the trigger is the debt, not the property — an American who inherits cash and simply clears a Mexican peso mortgage without ever selling the house has a taxable event. Second, refinancing to chase a better rate is not a neutral act for US purposes. A refinance can accelerate an entire accumulated currency gain into a single tax year in which you received no proceeds to pay it with. If you are considering one, read it alongside our guide to foreign mortgages for US citizens.

The records you need from day one

The computation is arithmetic. The difficulty is evidence — and it is usually needed years after the fact, when the bank has purged its records and the closing file is in a box.

Keep, from the moment you draw the loan: the loan agreement showing the exact principal in the foreign currency and the drawdown date; a documented spot rate for that date from a defensible published source; an amortisation schedule separating principal from interest for every year; dated records of any prepayment; and the payoff statement showing the exact balance extinguished and the date. Use a consistent rate source — the US Treasury reporting rates or your bank's documented rate — and do not switch between sources mid-loan.

How to compute it, step by step

  1. Fix the borrowing rate. Take the spot exchange rate on the date the loan was drawn and record the dollar value of the full principal.
  2. Identify the disposition. Determine what portion of the foreign-currency principal is being extinguished and on exactly what date — payoff, sale, refinance or prepayment.
  3. Translate the repayment. Convert that same foreign-currency amount at the spot rate on the disposition date.
  4. Compare like with like. The gain (or loss) is the dollar value of that principal when borrowed minus its dollar cost when repaid. Never compare the whole loan against a partial repayment.
  5. Characterise it. A gain is ordinary income, reported as other income — not on Schedule D, not on Form 8949, and not eligible for capital rates. A loss requires a personal-versus-business analysis before you claim anything.
  6. Run the property calculation separately. Compute the house gain in dollars, apply Section 121 and any depreciation recapture, and claim the foreign tax credit on Form 1116 for foreign tax actually paid.
  7. Check the credit actually helps. Foreign tax on a property sale sits in the passive basket, and a foreign country that taxed the house does not tax your mortgage — so there is typically no foreign tax to credit against the Section 988 gain at all.

That last point is the quiet sting. The currency gain is a purely American invention. No other country taxes it, which means no foreign tax credit exists to offset it. It is one of the very few items in cross-border property tax where the US bill stands entirely alone.

If you're not American: what changes

The mortgage itself is the same for any foreign buyer. Lender criteria, loan-to-value limits, the local notary process, the rates on offer and the country's own purchase taxes do not change with your passport. What is purely American is the tax wrapper described above — Section 988, the ordinary-income characterisation, and the dollar-basis measurement of the debt.

If you are not a US person, discard the IRS layer and substitute the rules of your own country of tax residence. Check three things. First, whether your home country taxes foreign exchange gains on personal borrowing at all — many, including the UK for individuals in most ordinary cases, simply do not treat a personal foreign-currency mortgage as producing taxable forex income, which makes this an unusually American problem. Second, whether you have foreign asset or account reporting obligations at home covering the property, the loan or the account servicing it. Third, whether a double-taxation treaty exists between your country and the country where the property sits, so that local tax on any eventual sale is credited rather than stacked. A Canadian, Australian or Israeli buyer computes everything in their own currency under their own reliefs. Confirm the specifics with a tax adviser where you are resident.

Is a foreign mortgage still worth it in 2026?

Usually, yes — but with your eyes open. Local financing still does things cash cannot: it preserves dollars for other uses, it gives you a natural hedge because the debt sits in the same currency as the asset, and in much of Europe the rates on offer remain attractive relative to US mortgages. None of that is undone by Section 988.

What Section 988 changes is the exit. The currency gain is not a reason to avoid borrowing abroad; it is a reason to know, before you sign, roughly what the loan will cost you at payoff under a strong-dollar scenario, and to plan the timing of a sale or refinance with that number in front of you. The Americans who get hurt are not the ones who borrowed — they are the ones who discovered the rule the April after they sold. Model it at the start, keep the exchange-rate records as carefully as you keep the deed, and treat the mortgage as the second taxable asset it legally is. Do that and the phantom gain is a line item you planned for, not a bill that ambushes you.

Frequently asked questions

What is a Section 988 phantom gain on a foreign mortgage?

It is a US tax gain created purely by exchange-rate movement on foreign-currency debt. The IRS treats your euro or peso mortgage as a separate transaction from the house. If the dollar strengthens between the day you borrow and the day you repay, it costs fewer dollars to extinguish the same debt, and Section 988 treats that saving as taxable ordinary income — even though no money ever reached your bank account.

Is the Section 988 gain taxed as capital gain or ordinary income?

Ordinary income. Section 988(a)(1)(A) says foreign currency gain or loss is computed separately and treated as ordinary. It does not qualify for the 0/15/20% long-term capital gains rates, so it can be taxed at your marginal rate — up to 37% federal in 2026 — no matter how long you held the property or the loan.

Does the Section 121 home-sale exclusion cover the currency gain?

No. Section 121 excludes up to $250,000 (single) or $500,000 (joint) of gain from the sale of a principal residence — the property. The Section 988 gain arises on the debt, a legally separate instrument, and is ordinary income rather than gain from the sale of a residence. Many sellers exclude the entire house gain and still owe tax on the mortgage.

Can I deduct a Section 988 loss if the dollar weakened instead?

Often not, and the asymmetry is the cruel part. A currency gain on the debt is always taxable, but where the borrowing is personal — a mortgage on a home you live in rather than an investment property — the corresponding loss is generally treated as a nondeductible personal loss. Debt on a genuine rental or business property is usually in a better position. Confirm with a cross-border CPA before claiming anything.

When exactly is the Section 988 gain triggered?

On disposition of the debt: full payoff, payoff funded by a sale, or a refinance that legally extinguishes and replaces the old loan. Technically each principal repayment is a partial disposition, and lump-sum prepayments are commonly computed that way. Ordinary monthly amortisation is frequently treated as de minimis in practice, but that is a practitioner convention, not a statutory exemption.
