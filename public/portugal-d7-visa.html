Portugal D7 Visa for Americans: The 2026 Guide

MS By The MigrateState Editorial Team · Reviewed by a licensed international real-estate attorney  ·  Updated July 2026  ·  10 min read
The D7 is Portugal's passive-income residence visa — the route most American retirees, pensioners, and people living off investments use to move to Portugal. In 2026 a single applicant must show stable passive income of at least €920 per month (about €11,040 a year, pegged to the Portuguese minimum wage), plus savings of roughly the same amount held in a Portuguese bank account. It leads to permanent residence after five years and, eventually, citizenship — but the famous NHR tax break that once made it irresistible no longer exists.

For Americans who want to actually live in Portugal — not just hold an investment-linked permit — the D7 is the workhorse visa. It is cheaper than the Golden Visa by an order of magnitude, it does not require you to buy property or park half a million euros in a fund, and it has carried tens of thousands of retirees, remote-income earners, and financially independent families into Portuguese residence over the last decade. It also comes wrapped in a great deal of outdated information, because the two things that historically made it so attractive — a low income bar and a near-total tax exemption on foreign pensions — have both changed. This guide gives you the 2026 reality.

What is the D7 visa?

The D7, formally the residence visa for retirees and holders of passive/own-means income, is a Portuguese national long-stay visa created under Portugal's immigration law for non-EU/EEA nationals who can support themselves from income they do not have to work for. "Passive" is the operative word: pensions (including US Social Security and 401(k)/IRA distributions), rental income, dividends, interest, royalties, and annuity payments all qualify. It is often marketed as the "retirement visa" or "passive income visa," and while retirees are the classic case, anyone with stable, ongoing, non-employment income from outside Portugal can use it.

The D7 is a genuine residence pathway, not a long tourist permit. It makes you a legal resident of Portugal, gives you access to the national health service (SNS) and the right to enrol children in school, lets you travel visa-free across the Schengen area, and — crucially — counts toward permanent residence and, in time, citizenship. That is the structural difference from a Golden Visa: the D7 expects you to live in Portugal, whereas the Golden Visa was designed for investors who wanted residence rights without relocating.

Who qualifies — and the income rule

There are three core requirements: enough qualifying passive income, somewhere to live in Portugal (a twelve-month rental contract or a property you own, with the lease registered with the tax authority), and a clean criminal record. The income test is the one that trips people up, because it is pegged to the Portuguese minimum wage, which rose for 2026.

As of 1 January 2026, the minimum monthly passive income for a single main applicant is €920 — the 2026 Portuguese minimum wage — which works out to roughly €11,040 per year. The requirement scales up for family members joining you:

These are minimums, and treating them as targets is a mistake. Consulates exercise discretion, and an application that clears the bar by only a few euros, or that relies on income a reviewing officer considers unstable, can be refused. Most immigration lawyers advise demonstrating comfortably above the threshold — and showing that the income is durable, documented, and likely to continue (a pension award letter, several months of statements showing the deposits, and your most recent tax return are the standard evidence package).

The Portuguese-bank savings deposit

Income is only half the financial test. In practice, most Portuguese consulates also want to see savings — typically twelve months of the required income — sitting in a Portuguese bank account in your name before you submit the application. For a single applicant in 2026 that is about €11,040; for a couple, roughly €16,560. This is the requirement that surprises American applicants most, because it forces you to set up the Portuguese side of your financial life before you have a visa.

To open that account you first need a Portuguese NIF (tax number), which a fiscal representative or lawyer can obtain for you remotely. A few important practical points: the funds must usually be in an actual Portuguese bank — Millennium BCP, Caixa Geral de Depósitos, Novo Banco, Santander Portugal and similar — and consulates generally do not accept Wise, Revolut, or other e-money institutions as a substitute. The savings buffer demonstrates that you can sustain yourself in Portugal even if income is briefly interrupted, and it is read alongside, not instead of, the monthly income figure.

The single most common error is a remote employee trying to use the D7. If your income is a salary or active freelance billing — work you perform — it is not passive, and the correct route is the D8 with its higher income bar. The D7 is for money that arrives whether you work or not.

The application process and timeline

The D7 is a two-stage process, and understanding the split matters. First you apply for the visa at the Portuguese consulate (or VFS Global centre) responsible for your US state of residence. That visa, once granted, is valid for 120 days and allows two entries — it is essentially your ticket to enter Portugal and complete the second stage. Inside Portugal you then attend an appointment with AIMA (the Agency for Integration, Migration and Asylum, which replaced SEF) to collect your actual residence permit, the card that makes you a resident.

Consular processing typically runs 60 to 90 days, though it varies by consulate and by how complete your file is. Build in extra time for the steps that come before you can even submit: getting a NIF, opening the Portuguese bank account and funding it, and signing and registering a twelve-month accommodation contract. Realistically, from "decision to apply" to "holding a residence card," a well-organised D7 takes six to nine months.

Renewal, minimum stay, permanent residence and citizenship

The first residence permit is issued for two years and is then renewable for a further three, taking you to the five-year mark. To keep the permit valid you must genuinely live in Portugal: broadly, you cannot be absent for more than six consecutive months, or eight non-consecutive months, across the validity of the permit, and the rule of thumb for the first two-year cycle is around sixteen months of physical presence in Portugal. The D7 is not compatible with treating Portugal as a part-time base — that is what the Golden Visa was for.

After five years of legal residence you can apply for permanent residence. Citizenship is where the law changed dramatically in 2026. Portugal's revised Nationality Law — Lei Orgânica 1/2026, published 18 May 2026 and in force from 19 May 2026 — extended the general naturalisation period from five years to ten years for most non-EU nationals (seven years for nationals of EU and Portuguese-speaking/CPLP countries), and changed the start of the clock so that the qualifying period now runs from the date your residence permit is issued, not from when you first applied. Transitional rule: nationality applications filed with the IRN on or before 18 May 2026 are still processed under the previous five-year regime. New applicants also face a Portuguese-language requirement and a civic/cultural knowledge test. In short: the D7 still leads to a Portuguese (and therefore EU) passport, but the timeline for Americans is now roughly twice as long as the headline figure that older guides quote.

The tax catch: NHR is gone

This is the genuine nuance that most D7 articles still get wrong, and it can be a five-figure annual difference. For years the D7 was sold as a package deal with Portugal's Non-Habitual Resident (NHR) regime, which let new tax residents receive most foreign pension income at a flat 10% — or, in the earliest version, tax-free. That combination is what made Portugal the retirement destination of the 2010s.

NHR closed to new applicants on 1 January 2025. Its replacement, IFICI (the Tax Incentive for Scientific Research and Innovation, widely called "NHR 2.0"), is a different animal: it is aimed at qualified professionals in research, higher education, technology, and certain high-value-added activities, it grants a flat 20% rate on qualifying Portuguese-source employment or self-employment income, and — decisively for D7 retirees — it does not exempt foreign pensions at all. A typical American retiree on a D7 will not qualify for IFICI, because their income is a pension, not qualifying professional activity.

The practical consequence: once you spend 183 days in Portugal in a calendar year (or make it your habitual home), you become a Portuguese tax resident and Portugal taxes your worldwide income — including your US pension and investment income — under its normal progressive rates, which reach 48%. Holding a D7 permit does not by itself make you a tax resident; physical presence does. This does not mean you are doomed to double taxation — the US–Portugal tax treaty and foreign tax credits coordinate the two systems — but it does mean the old "move to Portugal and pay almost nothing on your pension" pitch is dead. Anyone choosing the D7 for tax reasons in 2026 needs a current, individualised projection, not a blog post written in the NHR era.

US tax: what Americans actually owe

Becoming a Portuguese resident does not release you from the US system. The United States taxes its citizens on worldwide income wherever they live, so as a D7 holder you will keep filing a US return every year in addition to any Portuguese return. The mechanics that matter:

Your US pension, Social Security, and investment income remain reportable to the IRS. Once you are also a Portuguese tax resident, the same income is taxable in Portugal, and you use the foreign tax credit (Form 1116) and the US–Portugal treaty to avoid being taxed twice on the same euro — generally you pay the higher of the two countries' rates in total, not both stacked. Your Portuguese bank account — the one you had to open and fund for the D7 — becomes an FBAR account the moment your aggregate foreign accounts exceed $10,000 at any point in the year, and may also trigger Form 8938 depending on your filing status and where you live. Note the distinction MigrateState makes throughout: a directly-owned Portuguese home is not an FBAR or Form 8938 asset, but the bank account attached to it is. Currency matters too — gains created purely by euro-dollar movement can be taxable in the US even when, in euros, nothing changed. A cross-border accountant who handles both US and Portuguese filings is not a luxury here; it is the cost of doing this correctly.

Step-by-step checklist: D7 from decision to residence card

  1. Confirm your income qualifies — total your stable passive income and check it clears €920/month for a single applicant, plus the family uplifts. Gather pension award letters and statements proving it.
  2. Get a Portuguese NIF — remotely, through a fiscal representative or lawyer, before anything else.
  3. Open and fund a Portuguese bank account — deposit roughly twelve months of the required income. Use a real Portuguese bank, not an e-money app.
  4. Secure twelve-month accommodation — a registered rental contract or proof of owned property in Portugal.
  5. Assemble the document file — passport, FBI background check (apostilled), proof of income and savings, accommodation proof, health insurance, and certified Portuguese translations.
  6. Apply at your consulate / VFS centre — for the US state where you live. Expect 60–90 days for the visa.
  7. Enter Portugal on the 120-day visa — then attend your AIMA appointment to collect the residence permit.
  8. Plan your tax position — model your residency carefully around the 183-day line and get a cross-border tax projection before, not after, you move.
  9. Renew and accumulate residence — renew at year two for three more years, meet the minimum-stay rules, and track the clock toward permanent residence and citizenship.

If you're not American: what changes

The D7 itself works the same for every non-EU/EEA national. A Briton, Canadian, Australian, South African or Brazilian follows the identical Portuguese process — same €920/month income test, same savings deposit, same two-stage consular and AIMA route, same minimum-stay and permanent-residence rules. What changes is the tax wrapper from your home country and, for some, the citizenship timeline.

Non-US readers should swap the US-specific analysis above for their own country's rules and check three things: (1) whether your home country taxes worldwide income or only domestic income once you leave — unlike the US, most countries (UK, Canada, Australia) tax on residence, so becoming a Portuguese resident may take you out of your home tax net rather than leaving you in two systems; (2) your home country's foreign-asset and foreign-account reporting duties, which differ from the US FBAR/Form 8938 regime; and (3) the double-taxation treaty between your country and Portugal, which determines how pension and investment income is allocated. One citizenship point also differs: nationals of Portuguese-speaking (CPLP) countries face a seven-year naturalisation clock under the 2026 nationality law rather than the ten years applied to most other non-EU nationals. Always confirm the tax consequences with an adviser in your country of residence.

Is the D7 worth it in 2026?

For the right person, very much so. If your goal is to genuinely relocate to Portugal on stable passive income, the D7 remains the most cost-effective legal route by a wide margin — a few hundred euros in fees and a modest income bar, against half a million for the Golden Visa. It gives you full residence rights, healthcare access, Schengen mobility, and a real, if now longer, path to an EU passport.

Where 2026 demands clear eyes is on the two changes this guide has flagged. The tax fantasy is over: budget to pay Portuguese tax on your worldwide income once you become resident, and price that in honestly rather than relying on the NHR pension exemption that no longer exists. And the citizenship horizon has roughly doubled — ten years for most Americans, counted from permit issuance — so if a passport is the objective, plan for the long game. The D7 is still an excellent visa; it is simply a relocation-and-lifestyle decision now far more than a tax play. Treat it as the former, structure the tax and timeline correctly from the start with a Portuguese lawyer and a cross-border accountant, and it does exactly what it promises.

Frequently asked questions

How much income do you need for the Portugal D7 visa in 2026?

A single applicant needs stable passive income of at least €920/month — about €11,040/year, tied to the 2026 Portuguese minimum wage. Add 50% for a spouse (€460/month) and 30% per dependent child (€276/month). A couple needs roughly €1,380/month. Apply comfortably above the minimum; consulates can refuse borderline cases.

Do I need money in a Portuguese bank account for the D7?

Yes — most consulates want about twelve months of the required income (~€11,040 single) deposited in a Portuguese bank before you submit. You need a NIF to open the account, and Wise/Revolut and similar e-money apps are usually not accepted in place of a Portuguese bank.

Can D7 holders still get the NHR tax break on pensions?

No. NHR closed to new applicants on 1 January 2025. Its replacement, IFICI, targets qualifying professionals and does not exempt foreign pensions. A D7 retiree who becomes a Portuguese tax resident (183+ days) is taxed on worldwide income at normal progressive rates, with the US–Portugal treaty and foreign tax credit preventing double taxation.

How long until D7 residence becomes citizenship?

Permanent residence is available after five years. Under the nationality law that took force in 2026, naturalisation now requires ten years for most non-EU nationals (seven for EU/CPLP nationals), counted from the date your residence permit is issued, plus a language and civics test.

What is the difference between the D7 and the D8 digital nomad visa?

The D7 is for passive income (pensions, rent, dividends) at ~€920/month. The D8 is for active remote-work income from outside Portugal and requires around four times the minimum wage. Retirees and investors use the D7; salaried remote workers and freelancers use the D8.
