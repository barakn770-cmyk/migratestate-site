Philippines Real Estate & Residency Guides

Lawyer-reviewed guides for Americans buying property in the Philippines — the legal and tax reality, not the dream.

Foreigners cannot own land in the Philippines — full stop — but they can own condominium units outright and lease land for the long term. The hard limit is the 40% foreign cap on any condo project; the newest opening is the 99-year investor land lease under RA 12252, live since its implementing rules took effect on 4 January 2026. Plan on 3–5.5% in buyer closing costs and a 6% capital-gains tax at sale. Why Americans look at the Philippines

The Philippines is one of the most emotionally magnetic destinations for a US buyer. For millions of Filipino-Americans it is home, or a parent's home; for retirees drawn by warm weather, English fluency and a low cost of living, it is a genuine landing spot. And yet it is also one of the most legally restrictive property markets an American can walk into — restrictive in a way that catches people who assume that "I'm married to a Filipina" or "I have money to invest" is enough to buy a beachfront lot. It usually isn't, and the mistake can be expensive.

The rules are not impossible; they are just specific. Get them right and Americans buy condominiums in Makati or Cebu with clean, registered title on a Condominium Certificate of Title. Get them wrong — by parking land in a relative's name, or trusting a developer who can't actually register a foreign buyer — and you can lose the property outright. The single most important fact to internalize: the building yes, the land no. Article XII of the 1987 Constitution reserves land ownership to Filipino citizens and to companies at least 60% Filipino-owned, and that prohibition does not bend for a visa, a marriage or the size of your bank account.

Where to buy — the regions

Because titled ownership runs through condominiums, the markets that matter to a foreign buyer are the ones with real condo supply:

  • Metro Manila (Makati & BGC) — the country's financial core and the deepest condo market. Makati and Bonifacio Global City offer the most liquid resale and rental demand, with clean CCT title and international-standard developments — the default for an investor after yield.
  • Cebu — the strong second city, blending a business hub with beach access. A growing condo pipeline, lower prices than Manila, and both urban and resort demand make it a favourite for buyers who want lifestyle and rental in one.
  • Boracay — the marquee island resort. Suited to holiday-let condo buyers chasing tourism income, though supply is tighter and seasonality real; verify a project's foreign capacity carefully here.
  • Palawan — the frontier lifestyle pick (Puerto Princesa, El Nido, Coron). More about a long-horizon second home or a leased land-and-house project than a liquid condo market; the 99-year lease is especially relevant here.
  • Davao — the orderly southern metropolis, popular with retirees for its low cost of living and calm pace. Condo stock is thinner than Manila or Cebu but growing, with genuine local demand rather than tourist reliance.

Wherever you look, the 40% rule governs: foreign nationals combined may own no more than 40% of a condominium project by floor area, enforced at the project level. If a building is already at that cap, the Registry of Deeds will not register your title even with a signed contract and a paid deposit — so get written confirmation of remaining foreign capacity before you sign.

The single most important line for a US buyer is the capital-gains row. The Philippine "6% capital gains tax" behaves like a transfer tax: it is charged on the whole sale price, not your gain, so you owe it even if you sell at a loss. Because the US taxes only your actual gain and the foreign tax credit can only offset US tax on the same category of income, a low-profit sale can leave you with a Philippine tax you can't fully credit — a real, non-recoverable exit cost to plan around, not a wash.

Who the Philippines suits — and who should look elsewhere

The Philippines fits a narrower buyer than the brochures suggest: someone who wants a titled condominium in a major city, or who has Filipino roots. Former natural-born Filipinos can own limited land outright (up to 1,000 m² urban or 1 hectare rural for residential use under Batas Pambansa 185), and reacquiring citizenship under RA 9225 lifts the land ban entirely — often the cleanest answer of all. The 2026 arrival of the 99-year investor lease under RA 12252 also makes larger, land-based projects far more bankable than a year ago.

The Philippines is a weaker fit if your dream is to own a beachfront lot outright as a foreigner — the Constitution simply won't allow it, and every workaround that promises otherwise is either a lease dressed up as ownership or an Anti-Dummy Law liability waiting to detonate. Using a Filipino nominee to hold land is illegal, and the Supreme Court has repeatedly held that a foreigner who funds such a purchase cannot even recover the money. Philippines FAQ

Is the Philippines a good place for Americans to buy property in 2026?

For the right buyer, yes — but that buyer is narrower than the brochures suggest. If you want a titled condominium in a major city, or you have Filipino roots that unlock land ownership or citizenship, it is a warm, English-speaking, low-cost place to own with a clean CCT and cheap annual taxes. If your dream is a beachfront lot owned outright as a foreigner, the honest answer is that the Constitution won't allow it.

Can Americans actually own anything in the Philippines?

Yes — the building, not the land. Americans can own condominium units in their own name (subject to the 40% foreign cap), own a building separately from the land beneath it, and lease land long-term — up to 99 years for qualifying investors under RA 12252, or 25+25 years under the older law. Former natural-born Filipinos can own limited amounts of land outright.

What is the 40% condo rule?

Under the Condominium Act, foreign nationals combined may own no more than 40% of the units by floor area in any single condominium project. The quota is enforced at the project level, so a building can be at its foreign limit even if the specific unit you want is unsold — and if it is, the Registry of Deeds will not register your title no matter how much you've paid. Always get written confirmation of remaining foreign capacity before signing.

How much does it cost to buy, and what about residency?

Budget 3–5.5% on the buyer's side (1.5% documentary stamp tax, ~0.5–0.75% transfer tax, ~0.25% registration, 1–2% notarial); developer sales above ~₱3.6M add 12% VAT. Buying grants no residency — the SRRV retirement visa is the usual route, now with a minimum age of 40, a US$1,500 fee, and a bank deposit that scales with age and pension status.

Do Americans pay US tax on Philippine property?

Yes. The US taxes worldwide income: rental income goes on Schedule E with 30-year depreciation, a Philippine bank account over $10,000 triggers an FBAR, and a sale is US-taxable with a foreign tax credit for the 6% Philippine tax. The trap is that the 6% is charged on the gross price, not your gain — so a low-profit sale can leave you with foreign tax you cannot fully credit against a US gain you don't have.
