Bali & Indonesia Real Estate Guides

Lawyer-reviewed guides for Americans buying property in Bali — the land law, the structures, and the traps behind the villa brochures.

No foreigner can own freehold land in Indonesia — Hak Milik title is reserved for Indonesian citizens. Americans buy through one of three legal routes: a leasehold (25–30 years, extendable), Hak Pakai right-to-use title (up to 80 years, but it requires an Indonesian residence permit), or a PT PMA company holding HGB — the only licensed route for a rental business. Budget 8–12% in transaction costs. The "nominee" freehold your agent may offer is illegal and void.

Why Americans look at Bali

Price, yield and lifestyle — in that order. A villa in Canggu, Uluwatu or Ubud can cost a fraction of a US coastal property, tourism demand is deep and year-round, and quoted gross rental yields of 8–12% are far above anything on offer in a mature Western market. Bali also has a genuine expat and digital-nomad economy, so the rental market isn't purely seasonal.

What the brochures blur is what you are actually buying. Indonesia's Basic Agrarian Law reserves the strongest title, Hak Milik, for Indonesian citizens. Every legitimate foreign purchase is therefore a lesser right — a lease, a registered right-to-use, or shares in an Indonesian company. Those are all real, workable assets. But a lease decays, Hak Pakai requires a residence permit, and a PT PMA drags a US citizen straight into foreign-corporation reporting. Buy the structure first; the villa second.

Who Bali suits — and who should look elsewhere

Bali fits the cash buyer who wants a lifestyle asset with real rental demand, who is comfortable holding a long lease or a right-of-use title rather than a US-style deed, and who will pay for proper due diligence on zoning, permits and the certificate. Read the full guide to buying property in Bali for the structures, the costs and the US tax layer.

Bali is a weaker fit if you need title in your own name (look at Japan, which allows unrestricted freehold), if you want residency from the purchase (it grants none — see the best golden visas for Americans), if you need a mortgage, or if your return only works when you ignore the fact that a leasehold burns down every year you hold it.

Bali FAQ

Can an American buy property in Bali?

Not freehold. Hak Milik title is reserved for Indonesian citizens. Foreigners buy via a leasehold (Hak Sewa, typically 25–30 years plus extensions), Hak Pakai (a registered right-to-use title of 30 + 20 + 30 years, up to 80 years, requiring an Indonesian residence permit), or a PT PMA company holding HGB, which is the licensed route if you intend to rent the property out.

Are nominee structures legal?

No. Holding land through an Indonesian nominee with side agreements breaches the Basic Agrarian Law and Indonesian courts have repeatedly annulled these arrangements — leaving the registered Indonesian owner as the legal owner. Around 10,500 Bali properties, some $10.4 billion of value, are estimated to sit in these structures.

What does it cost to buy in Bali?

Roughly 8–12% of the price. The buyer pays BPHTB acquisition duty of 5% of the assessed acquisition value after the non-taxable allowance, plus around 1% in notary/PPAT fees and legal due diligence. The seller pays 2.5% PPh Final, usually priced into the deal. Annual PBB tax is low, commonly 0.1%–0.3% of assessed value.

Can I get a mortgage in Bali?

Realistically, no. Indonesian banks do not lend to non-resident foreigners for residential property in any practical way, so Bali is a cash market for most American buyers. Some developers offer instalment plans — treat those as unsecured credit and have a lawyer read the terms.

Does buying property in Bali give me a visa?

No. Ownership grants no immigration status. Living in Indonesia requires a separate visa route, and Hak Pakai title in fact runs the other way round — you need a valid residence permit (KITAS/KITAP) before you can hold it.
