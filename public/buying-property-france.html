Buying Property in France as an American (2026 Guide)

France is the second-home dream that actually lets you buy it: a Paris pied-à-terre, a Provence farmhouse, a ski apartment in the Alps, a stone house in the Dordogne. Unlike many countries, France places no restrictions on foreign ownership — an American buys on the same legal footing as a French citizen. The friction isn't permission; it's the notaire-led process, a transfer tax that quietly rose in 2025, a stingy mortgage cap for non-EU buyers, and a wealth tax most US buyers underestimate. Here's the grounded 2026 version, with the legal and tax nuances the listing portals leave out.

Can Americans buy property in France?

Yes — fully, and with no special approval. France has no foreign-ownership restrictions on residential property, so as a US citizen you can buy an apartment, a house or land in your own name (or through a French SCI company) whether or not you live in France. You do not need a visa, residency card or permit simply to own. The one non-negotiable is the notaire — a state-appointed public official who, by law, drafts the deed, verifies title, collects the taxes and registers the transfer. The notaire is neutral between buyer and seller; on a significant purchase you may still want your own English-speaking lawyer for due diligence.

One thing buying does not do: it does not grant you the right to live in France. As a non-resident American you remain bound by the 90 days in any 180 Schengen tourist limit. To stay longer you need a long-stay visa (such as the visiteur visa), which is a separate process from the purchase.

What you need before you buy

Four things turn an American into a "ready" buyer in France:

  • A French bank account — to pay the deposit, the notaire and the ongoing utility and tax bills. Expect a FATCA check and a request for your US tax details; not every bank will onboard a US person, so you may need a FATCA-friendly lender.
  • Proof of funds and source of funds — French notaires apply strict anti-money-laundering checks and will want to see where your money came from.
  • A mortgage agreement in principle (if borrowing) — arranged early, because non-EU buyers face a lower loan cap and a longer approval timeline.
  • An independent adviser — optionally your own lawyer, and for a cross-border purchase a US tax professional, lined up before you sign, not after.

Frais de notaire and the transfer tax

The headline cost most Americans miss is frais de notaire — "notaire fees," which is a misleading name, because the notaire's own fee is only a small slice. The bulk is droits de mutation: government transfer tax (stamp duty) that the notaire collects on the state's behalf. The total runs very differently depending on the property's age:

Property typeFrais de notaire (2026)What it mostly is
Existing / resale home~7%–8% of priceTransfer tax (droits de mutation) up to 5%, plus registration, notaire fee and disbursements
New-build (under 5 years, from developer)~2%–3% of priceVAT (TVA, 20%) is already in the price; only reduced registration duties apply

The transfer tax itself changed recently. From 1 April 2025, French departments were allowed to raise the droits de mutation by up to half a percentage point, lifting the ceiling from 4.5% to 5.0% of the price. Most departments adopted the increase, and the 5% cap is expected to hold through 2026. There is one carve-out: first-time buyers of a principal residence — and anyone who hasn't owned a home in the previous two years — can keep the lower 4.5% rate for a transitional period. Most American second-home buyers will not qualify and should budget the full 5%.

⚠️ "Notaire fees" ≠ the notaire's pay. On a €500,000 resale home, frais de notaire of roughly €37,500 break down as mostly transfer tax to the government, a few thousand in disbursements and a regulated notaire fee of only around 0.8%. Always ask for a written simulation of frais de notaire before you sign — and remember it is the buyer who pays it.

Total acquisition costs

On top of frais de notaire, the big variable is the agent commission — which in France is usually baked into the advertised price (commission "FAI", frais d'agence inclus). For a typical resale purchase in 2026:

Financing as a non-resident American

France has one of the most borrower-friendly mortgage markets in Europe — long fixed terms, no early-repayment trap, and rates that in early 2026 ran roughly 3.5%–4.25% on 20–25 year fixed loans. The catch for Americans is the loan-to-value cap. As non-EU nationals, US buyers are typically limited to about 50% LTV — meaning a 50% deposit — versus 80%+ for residents. Lenders also commonly set a minimum loan of around €250,000 for US and Canadian buyers, require total debt payments to stay under 35% of gross income, and will only process a US-person file through a FATCA-compliant bank. Arrange your agreement in principle early; the non-resident underwriting timeline is slower.

Annual taxes: foncière, d'habitation and IFI

Owning in France brings three recurring taxes a US buyer should price in:

  • Taxe foncière — the annual land/ownership tax, paid by every owner, resident or not. It varies widely by commune and has risen sharply in recent years.
  • Taxe d'habitation — abolished on primary residences, but it still applies to second homes, and many high-demand communes (Paris, the Côte d'Azur, Atlantic resorts) add a surcharge of 5%–60%. As a non-resident, your French home is a second home, so you pay it.
  • IFI — the real-estate wealth tax — Impôt sur la Fortune Immobilière applies to anyone, of any nationality, whose net French real estate exceeds €1.3 million on 1 January. Non-residents are taxed only on French property; the rate is progressive from 0.5% up to 1.5%. (Note: 2026 budget proposals to broaden IFI into a wider "unproductive wealth" tax have been discussed but should be confirmed before relying on them.)

The US tax rules that still apply

Your notaire handles France; the IRS is still your job. The United States taxes citizens on worldwide income, so rental income from your French home goes on Schedule E and a future sale is a US capital gain. France taxes non-residents on rental income (20%–30% income tax plus 17.2% social charges) and on gains at 19% plus 17.2% social charges, with taper relief building to a full income-tax exemption after 22 years and full social-charge exemption after 30 years of ownership (France's 2026 finance law has been reported to accelerate the income-tax taper — confirm the current schedule with your adviser). A French bank account can trigger FBAR (FinCEN Form 114) and FATCA (Form 8938) once balances cross the thresholds.

Here is the nuance most American buyers — and most blogs — get wrong, in both directions. First, the good news: for years the IRS denied any credit for France's 17.2% social charges (CSG/CRDS), treating them as social-security taxes. That changed in 2019, when the US and France agreed they are income taxes, not social taxes — so CSG/CRDS on your French rental income and property gains is now creditable on Form 1116, and Americans who paid them earlier can still amend. Now the trap: the IFI wealth tax is the opposite — because it is a tax on net worth, not income, it generally does not qualify for the US foreign tax credit. Plenty of articles wrongly imply IFI is creditable; for a high-value French property it is usually a real, unrelievable annual cost stacked on top of your US bill. Model both before you buy, not after.

The €1.3M wealth tax usually isn't creditable in the US.

Step-by-step: buying property in France as an American

  1. Get your financing lined up — open a FATCA-friendly French bank account and, if borrowing, secure a mortgage agreement in principle (remember the ~50% cap).
  2. Make an offer (offre d'achat); once accepted the parties move to a preliminary contract.
  3. Sign the preliminary contract (compromis de vente) with a deposit of usually 5%–10%. You then have a 10-day statutory cooling-off period to withdraw without penalty.
  4. The notaire runs checks — title, the seller's mandatory diagnostics (asbestos, lead, energy, termites), planning and any pre-emption rights. This phase typically takes two to three months.
  5. Pay the balance and frais de notaire into the notaire's account ahead of completion.
  6. Sign the deed (acte de vente) before the notaire, who registers the transfer and hands over the keys.
  7. Set up US compliance — file FBAR for the bank account and plan your rental/resale and IFI reporting with a cross-border CPA.

Start to finish, the typical timeline is three to four months.

If you're not American: what changes

The French side is identical for any foreign buyer. No-restriction ownership, the mandatory notaire, the 7%–8% frais de notaire on a resale home, the droits de mutation, the IFI wealth-tax threshold of €1.3 million and the taxe foncière / taxe d'habitation rules apply the same whether you hold a US, UK, Canadian, Australian or Israeli passport. Only the home-country tax wrapper on top is different — and EU/EEA buyers usually get better mortgage terms.

If you're not American, keep everything above on the French process exactly as written — France welcomes foreign buyers and the mechanics don't change with your passport. What you swap out is the US-specific tax layer (worldwide-income filing, FBAR, FATCA, the CSG/CRDS credit). In its place, check three things in your own country of residence: (1) whether your home country taxes foreign rental income and capital gains earned in France; (2) whether you must report foreign property, bank accounts or assets at home; and (3) whether your country has a double-taxation treaty with France that lets you credit French tax — including the 17.2% social charges — against your home bill. EU/EEA citizens should also note they typically qualify for higher mortgage LTVs than the ~50% non-EU cap. Confirm the specifics with a tax adviser in your country of residence before committing.

Is buying property in France worth it in 2026?

For lifestyle, liquidity and legal security, France is one of the safest places an American can own a home — open ownership, a notaire-policed title system, no title-insurance racket, and a mortgage market built around long fixed rates. The costs are real but predictable: a 5% transfer tax in most departments, 7%–8% all-in frais de notaire, a 50% deposit if you borrow, and ongoing taxe foncière, second-home taxe d'habitation and — above €1.3 million — the IFI wealth tax that the US won't credit. Buy with eyes open, get a written frais-de-notaire simulation, line up your US tax plan first, and France remains a genuinely rewarding place to own.

Frequently asked questions

Can a US citizen buy property in France?

Yes. France places no restrictions on foreign ownership, so US citizens buy houses, apartments or land in their own name with no residency, visa or permit. A notaire is legally required to complete the sale, and owning property doesn't grant the right to live in France beyond the 90-day Schengen limit.

How much are the fees to buy a house in France?

Budget about 7%–8% in frais de notaire on a resale home and 2%–3% on a new-build. Most of that is the droits de mutation transfer tax, which many departments raised to a 5% ceiling from April 2025. Agent commission (~4%–8%) is usually already inside the advertised price.

Can an American get a mortgage in France?

Yes, but on tighter terms. As non-EU nationals, US buyers are usually capped near 50% loan-to-value — a 50% deposit — and must use a FATCA-compliant bank. Non-resident fixed rates ran about 3.5%–4.25% in early 2026, with total debt kept under 35% of gross income.

Do Americans pay the French wealth tax (IFI)?

Possibly. IFI applies to non-residents whose net French real estate exceeds €1.3 million on January 1, at progressive rates up to 1.5%. Because it's a wealth tax rather than an income tax, it generally can't be claimed as a US foreign tax credit — so it's a real added cost for high-value buyers.

Do I pay US tax on a property I buy in France?

Potentially. The US taxes worldwide income, so French rental income and a future sale are IRS-reportable. The US–France treaty and foreign tax credit usually prevent double taxation, and since 2019 the IRS accepts the 17.2% CSG/CRDS social charges as creditable. A French bank account can trigger FBAR/FATCA.
