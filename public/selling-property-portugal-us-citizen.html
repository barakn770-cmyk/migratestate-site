Selling Property in Portugal as a US Citizen, 2026

MS By The MigrateState Editorial Team · Reviewed by a licensed international real-estate attorney  ·  Updated July 2026  ·  11 min read
Portugal taxes only half your gain — and that is exactly why the US bill surprises Americans. A non-resident selling Portuguese property in 2026 is taxed on 50% of the net gain at progressive rates, an effective ~6.5%–24% on the whole gain. Because that Portuguese tax is often smaller than the US tax on the full gain, the foreign tax credit covers only part of your IRS bill — and Portuguese reinvestment relief, which drops the tax toward zero, can leave you paying the US in full.

How Portugal taxes your 2026 sale

For years the rule for a non-resident selling Portuguese property was blunt: a flat 28% tax on the entire gain. That regime is gone. After a line of European Court of Justice rulings held it discriminatory to tax non-residents worse than residents, Portugal aligned the two from January 2023. A non-resident now benefits from the same 50% inclusion a resident gets: only half the net gain is taxable, and that half is taxed at Portugal's progressive IRS rates rather than a flat charge.

The practical effect is a much softer number. The included half is added to your worldwide income to determine which bracket applies — the 2026 IRS scale runs from about 13% at the bottom to 48% at the top — so the effective rate on the full gain generally lands between roughly 6.5% and 24%. High earners also face a solidarity surcharge of 2.5% on taxable income above €80,000 and 5% above €250,000.

The gain itself is not just sale price minus purchase price. Portugal lets you add to your acquisition cost the IMT and stamp duty you paid when you bought, notary and registration fees, estate-agent commission on the sale, and documented capital improvements made in the last 12 years. For any property held more than 24 months, your acquisition cost is also multiplied by an official monetary devaluation coefficient that indexes it for inflation — a genuine, and frequently overlooked, reduction in the taxable gain.

Why half-taxing the gain traps Americans

Every US citizen carries the US tax system abroad. The United States taxes its citizens and green-card holders on worldwide gains, and Portugal's generosity does not change that. The instinct — a correct one — is to lean on the foreign tax credit on Form 1116, which credits foreign tax paid against the US tax on the same income so the gain is not taxed twice. On a Portuguese sale that credit is available, because gain on foreign real property is foreign-source income.

The catch is arithmetic. The foreign tax credit can only offset up to the amount of foreign tax you actually paid. Portugal taxed only half your gain; the US taxes all of it. When the US tax on the whole gain exceeds the Portuguese tax on half of it — which is common, especially at the US 20% long-term rate plus the 3.8% net investment income tax — the credit runs out before the US bill does, and you pay the IRS the shortfall.

The half-credit gap: imagine a $200,000 gain. Portugal taxes 50% — $100,000 — at, say, an effective 24%, roughly $24,000. The US taxes the full $200,000 at 23.8%, roughly $47,600. Form 1116 credits the $24,000 of Portuguese tax, leaving about $23,600 owed to the IRS. Portugal's low headline rate does not make the sale cheap for an American; it just moves the tax from Lisbon to Washington.

None of this is a reason to avoid selling — it is a reason to model both sides before you pick a closing date. Where the Portuguese tax is genuinely high (a big gain taxed near the top bracket with the solidarity surcharge) the credit can wipe out most of the US bill. Where Portugal's reliefs push its tax low, the US collects the difference. The only way to know which case you are in is to run the two calculations together.

The reinvestment-relief backfire

Portugal offers a powerful domestic relief: if you sell your own permanent home and reinvest the proceeds in another main residence anywhere in the EU or EEA within the statutory window, the gain is deferred — often to zero Portuguese tax. For a Portuguese resident trading up, this is excellent. For an American, it can be a trap that mirrors the one non-resident sellers hit in Greece.

The reason is the same credit arithmetic in reverse. Reinvestment relief works by making the Portuguese tax disappear. But Section 121 aside, the US does not recognise Portuguese reinvestment relief, and a Portuguese tax of zero means a foreign tax credit of zero. So the US taxes your entire gain with nothing to offset it. An American who claims reinvestment relief in Portugal and cannot fully shelter the gain under Section 121 can end up paying the IRS more than one who simply paid the Portuguese tax and credited it.

The counterintuitive result: for a US person, deferring Portuguese tax through reinvestment relief is frequently the worse move on a gain that Section 121 does not cover. Paying a creditable Portuguese tax and claiming Form 1116 can beat paying nothing in Portugal and facing an uncredited US gain. The relief that helps a Portuguese neighbour most is the one that can cost an American most.

How the US taxes your Portuguese sale

The US result turns on three questions: was the property your principal residence, how did the dollar move against the euro, and did you ever rent it out. Here is the whole picture on one page.

The dollar-basis rule is the one that turns a modest euro profit into a large US gain — or the reverse. The IRS does not care what your apartment did in euros. Your basis is the purchase price translated at the exchange rate on the day you bought; your proceeds are translated at the rate on the day you sold. If the dollar weakened against the euro in between, a flat sale in euros can still throw off a five-figure US gain out of nothing but currency. If the dollar strengthened, you can show a US loss on a euro profit — but a loss on a personal residence is not deductible, so that phantom loss buys you nothing.

The Section 121 exclusion is your strongest line of defence, and it works on a Portuguese home exactly as on a house in Ohio: up to $250,000 of gain excluded for a single filer, $500,000 filing jointly, provided you owned and lived in it as your principal residence for at least 24 of the 60 months before the sale. A pure holiday home or a Golden-Visa buy-to-let that was never your main residence does not qualify, and the whole gain there stays taxable. Note the interaction with the reinvestment problem above: where Section 121 fully covers the gain, the missing foreign tax credit does not matter, because there is no US tax to offset in the first place. For the detail, see our guide to the Section 121 exclusion on a foreign home sale.

The US–Portugal tax treaty does not rescue you from any of this. Like almost every treaty, it lets the country where the property sits — Portugal — tax the gain first, and leaves the US to tax its citizens and relieve double taxation through the foreign tax credit. The treaty confirms the credit mechanism; it does not expand it beyond the Portuguese tax actually paid.

The mortgage twist: Section 988

If you financed the Portuguese purchase with a euro mortgage, the loan has its own separate US tax life. Under Section 988, repaying foreign-currency debt when the dollar is stronger than it was at origination produces a currency gain on the debt itself, taxed as ordinary income at rates up to 37%. It is computed independently of the house, and — crucially — Section 121 does not touch it. You can fully exclude the gain on the residence and still owe ordinary-income tax on the mortgage payoff.

This catches Americans who assume the home-sale exclusion cleans up the entire transaction. It does not. The residence and the loan are two calculations, and the loan is the one people forget. We walk through the mechanics in our guide to the foreign mortgage phantom gain.

Selling step by step

  1. Model both sides first. Compute the Portuguese tax on 50% of the gain after the inflation coefficient and deductible costs, then the US tax on the full gain, then see how much of the US bill the Form 1116 credit actually covers.
  2. Decide on reinvestment relief deliberately. If the property was your permanent home, weigh Portuguese reinvestment relief against the credit you would lose — do not claim it by reflex.
  3. Get the certificate ready. Commission the Energy Performance Certificate early; it is required before the property can be marketed.
  4. Clear your Portuguese taxes. Settle any outstanding IMI and confirm your NIF is active; a notary cannot complete the deed until the property is tax-clear.
  5. Appoint an independent lawyer. Use counsel who does not also act for the buyer, and grant a power of attorney if you are handling the sale from the US.
  6. Sign the notarial deed (escritura). The notary executes the contract; the buyer pays the IMT and stamp duty and the balance of the price, and any mortgage is discharged at completion.
  7. Handle the currency separately. If a euro mortgage is being repaid, run the Section 988 calculation on its own and report any gain as ordinary income.
  8. Report on both returns. Declare the gain on the following year's Portuguese IRS return, and in the US file Form 8949 and Schedule D, claim Section 121 with adjustment code H if it applies, and claim the Form 1116 credit for the Portuguese tax paid.

If you're not American: what changes

The Portuguese side of the sale is identical for any foreign seller. The 50% inclusion, the progressive rates, the inflation coefficient, the buyer-paid IMT and stamp duty, the agent commission, the NIF and IMI-clearance requirements do not change with your passport. What is purely American is the wrapper: worldwide taxation, Section 121, the dollar-basis measurement, Section 988 on a euro mortgage, and Form 8949.

If you are not a US person, drop the IRS layer and substitute the rules of your own country of tax residence. Check three things. First, whether your home country taxes gains on foreign property at all, and whether it offers its own main-residence relief — the UK's private residence relief, Canada's principal residence exemption and Israel's single-dwelling exemption each have their own tests. Second, whether you have foreign asset or account reporting obligations at home covering the Portuguese property and the sale proceeds. Third, whether a double-taxation treaty exists between your country and Portugal, remembering that immovable property is almost always taxable first where it sits and relieved second at home. The Portuguese reinvestment-relief trap can bite any foreign seller whose home country taxes the gain but ignores Portugal's deferral — confirm the specifics with a tax adviser where you are resident.

Is it worth selling in 2026?

For a non-American, 2026 is a comfortable time to sell: the post-2023 alignment means only half the gain is taxed, the inflation coefficient softens it further, and a market that has climbed steeply since the mid-2010s makes for healthy proceeds. For a US citizen, the answer is more nuanced, and it turns almost entirely on Section 121. If the Portuguese property was your principal residence and the dollar gain fits inside the $250,000 or $500,000 exclusion, you can exit essentially clean on both sides — the credit gap never arises because there is no US tax to cover. If it was a rental or a holiday home, or the gain runs past the exclusion, Portugal's low effective rate quietly shifts tax to the IRS, and reinvestment relief can make that worse rather than better.

The sellers who do well are the ones who run the numbers in dollars months before listing — basis translated properly, the inflation coefficient applied on the Portuguese side, Section 121 tested honestly, the Section 988 position on any euro mortgage computed, depreciation recapture identified if the place was ever rented, and reinvestment relief chosen on the merits rather than by default. Do that, and a Portuguese sale can be genuinely efficient. Discover the half-credit gap the following April, and you will have handed the IRS a bill you could have planned around.

Frequently asked questions

How much capital gains tax does a non-resident pay when selling property in Portugal in 2026?

Since January 2023 a non-resident is taxed on only 50% of the net gain, at Portugal's progressive IRS rates rather than the old flat 28% on the whole gain. The included half is added to your worldwide income to set the bracket, so the effective rate on the full gain typically lands between roughly 6.5% and 24%. The gain is reduced by the IMT and costs you paid on purchase, documented improvements, selling costs, and — for property held more than 24 months — an inflation coefficient that lifts your acquisition cost.

Does a US citizen owe US tax on a Portuguese property sale even after paying Portuguese tax?

Usually yes, at least in part. The US taxes citizens on worldwide gains and gives a foreign tax credit on Form 1116 for the Portuguese tax paid. But because Portugal taxes only half the gain, the Portuguese tax is often smaller than the US tax on the full gain, so the credit only partially offsets the US bill and you pay the IRS the difference. The Section 121 exclusion, if the property was your main home, is usually the more powerful shield.

Can I use the US Section 121 exclusion on a home in Portugal?

Yes, if it was genuinely your principal residence. Section 121 has no geographic limit, so a US citizen who owned and used a Portuguese property as their main home for at least 24 of the 60 months before the sale can exclude up to $250,000 of gain, or $500,000 filing jointly. A holiday home or a Golden-Visa buy-to-let that was never your main residence does not qualify, and that gain stays fully taxable in the US.

Does Portuguese reinvestment relief help a US seller?

It can backfire. Portugal lets you defer tax on the gain from your own permanent home if you reinvest the proceeds in another main residence in the EU or EEA. That drops your Portuguese tax toward zero — but a zero Portuguese tax means no foreign tax credit, so the US taxes the whole gain with nothing to offset it. An American who qualifies for reinvestment relief can end up paying more in total than one who simply paid the Portuguese tax and credited it.

What are the seller's costs when selling property in Portugal?

The seller typically pays estate agent commission of about 3%–5% plus 23% VAT, legal fees of roughly 1%, and an Energy Performance Certificate of €150–€500. The IMT transfer tax and stamp duty are paid by the buyer, not the seller. You must also be clear of any outstanding IMI (annual property tax) and hold a valid Portuguese tax number (NIF) to complete the deed.
