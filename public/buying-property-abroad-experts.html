Buying Property Abroad for a Golden Visa: The Complete Expert Guide (2026)

For many people around the world, buying real estate abroad has shifted from a vacation-home aspiration into a deliberate long-term strategy. Whether the goal is a lifestyle change, a family relocation, or securing legal residency through a Golden Visa program, international property offers real opportunities: residency rights, a potential path to a second passport, and genuine geographic diversification. From the historic streets of Europe to the tropical shores of Latin America, the programs are real and accessible.

But when a property purchase is directly tied to your immigration rights or visa status, the stakes are categorically different from buying at home. A single oversight — the wrong zone, the wrong ownership structure, an undisclosed lien — will not just cost you money. It can permanently disqualify your visa application or invalidate your investment for immigration purposes entirely.

One truth every foreign buyer must internalize before wiring money abroad: hiring independent, local experts is not a luxury. It is the minimum viable protection for your capital and your residency rights.

Why the Stakes Are Higher Than a Domestic Purchase

Many buyers assume that experience purchasing property at home translates to international markets. This is a dangerous starting point. Foreign real estate markets operate under entirely different legal frameworks, buyer protections, and disclosure requirements — and Golden Visa regulations are notoriously strict and constantly evolving.

Consider what changed in the past 18 months alone: Spain abolished its entire Golden Visa program in April 2025. Portugal removed real estate from its program entirely, shifting to investment funds. Greece raised its minimum investment thresholds to €800,000 in prime zones. Italy raised its flat-tax regime for new residents to €300,000 per year — up from €200,000 (the rate for entrants after 10 August 2024), itself doubled from the original €100,000. A buyer relying on advice from 12 months ago is already operating on obsolete information.

The Hidden Traps Foreign Buyers Fall Into

Visa disqualification from a zoning or threshold error. Most Golden Visa programs define eligible properties by geographic zone, property type, and minimum net investment amount — and “net” typically means after local transfer taxes and transaction fees are excluded. A purchase price that meets the headline threshold on paper may fall below it once the government calculates the eligible amount. Without local legal verification before signing, a buyer can complete a purchase and still be ruled ineligible.

The Golden Visa premium. In markets where a specific investment threshold unlocks residency, developers routinely price “visa-eligible” properties 20%–40% above comparable non-eligible stock, knowing that buyers are anchored to the program minimum rather than market value. An independent appraisal and legal review is the only reliable protection against overpaying.

Home-country tax exposure that local advisers won’t mention. Local attorneys and notaries handle the purchase correctly under local law — and stop there. They are not licensed to advise on your home country’s tax rules. Depending on your nationality, purchasing abroad may trigger reporting obligations, wealth tax declarations, controlled foreign corporation rules, or foreign income reporting requirements in your country of residence or citizenship. This advice must come from a tax professional qualified in your home jurisdiction.

Structural problems no seller will disclose. In many foreign markets, mandatory seller disclosure laws and standardized building codes simply do not exist in the way they do in the UK, Canada, Australia, or the US. Purchasing without an independent physical inspection can leave you discovering structural defects, illegal construction, or unpermitted modifications after closing — with limited or no legal recourse.

Double taxation without a treaty strategy. Owning foreign property typically generates taxable events in two jurisdictions simultaneously — the country where the property is located, and your home country. Without advance planning, you may pay full tax in both. Most countries have bilateral tax treaties that allow credits or exemptions, but these must be actively applied with proper advice, not assumed.

The Experts You Must Have in Your Corner

1. Independent Local Immigration & Real Estate Attorney

This is the most critical hire — and the word “independent” is not optional. You need a lawyer who represents you, not the seller, the developer, or the introducing agent. They will confirm the property is legally eligible under the specific Golden Visa rules, verify the title is clean and free of encumbrances, and ensure the transaction structure satisfies local immigration law. For Greece, this means confirming zone tier eligibility. For Portugal, it means verifying the fund is on the approved list. For Italy, it means confirming the startup or company structure qualifies under Investor Visa requirements.

2. Cross-Border Tax Adviser

A tax professional qualified in both the host country and your home country is essential. They will help you navigate:

  • Ownership structure: Whether to buy in your own name, through a holding company, a trust, or a foreign entity — each has different tax implications in the host country and at home.
  • Rental income taxation: Foreign rental income is typically taxable in both the source country and your home country. A tax adviser identifies which credits apply and how to avoid paying twice.
  • Capital gains on a future sale: Gains on foreign property are taxable events in most jurisdictions. The rate, calculation method, and any available exemptions vary widely.
  • Wealth and asset reporting: Many countries require residents to declare foreign assets above certain thresholds. Failure to report can result in significant penalties regardless of whether tax is owed.
  • Tax residency implications: Securing a Golden Visa does not automatically make you a tax resident of the host country — but spending time there might. Understanding the threshold before you cross it is critical.

3. Independent Property Inspector or Surveyor

Buyers from countries with robust building codes and mandatory disclosure regimes are often surprised to find these do not translate globally. An independent inspector physically evaluates the structural integrity, plumbing, electrical systems, roof condition, and whether any construction is properly permitted. On a property tied to a visa application, a structural defect that delays closing by several months can cascade into missed application deadlines and additional costs.

4. Vetted Local Property Manager

Most Golden Visa programs require little or no physical presence in the host country — Greece and Italy require none, Portugal just 7 days per year. If you are not relocating immediately, you cannot manage a foreign property from abroad. A reputable local property management company handles tenant sourcing, rent collection, maintenance, and local regulatory compliance, converting your visa investment into genuinely passive income. This is also relevant for tax planning: a well-documented rental history simplifies reporting on both sides of the border. See our guide on capital gains on foreign property.

What This Means Program by Program in 2026

The specific risks depend on which program you are pursuing. Here is what independent expert guidance means in practice for each active program:

  • Greece (€400,000–€800,000 real estate): Attorney must confirm the property falls in the correct tier zone and that the net eligible amount meets the threshold after transfer tax is excluded. Physical inspection is critical in older properties. No minimum stay, but capital is locked in an illiquid asset.
  • Portugal (€500,000 investment funds): Attorney confirms the specific fund is on the approved Golden Visa list. Tax adviser must address how fund distributions and gains are treated in your home country — rules vary significantly by nationality.
  • Hungary (€250,000 real estate investment funds): Attorney verifies fund approval by the National Bank of Hungary. Tax adviser should model how distributions flow back to your home country under any applicable tax treaty.
  • Italy (€250,000+ startup / €500,000 company / €1M donation): Attorney verifies the company or startup structure qualifies under Investor Visa rules. Tax adviser should model Italy’s €300,000/year flat-tax cost — raised from €200,000 for entrants from 1 January 2026, and from €100,000 originally — against your actual foreign-source income before committing.
  • Spain: Program closed. Spain abolished its entire Golden Visa program in April 2025. All investor visa routes — real estate, bonds, equity, business creation — are closed to new applications. If you see current marketing for a Spanish Golden Visa, verify with a licensed attorney before taking any action.

Special Considerations for US Citizens

If you hold US citizenship or a US Green Card, the above guidance applies in full — and you face a significant additional layer of complexity that buyers from most other countries do not. The United States taxes its citizens and permanent residents on worldwide income, regardless of where they live or invest. A Golden Visa in another country does not change your US filing obligations in any way.

  • FBAR (FinCEN Form 114): Required if any foreign bank or investment account exceeds $10,000 at any point during the year. Penalties for non-filing start at $10,000 per violation and can reach the account balance itself for wilful failures.
  • FATCA (Form 8938): Applies to specified foreign financial assets above threshold amounts. Investment fund holdings used for a Golden Visa frequently qualify.
  • PFIC rules for fund investors: Most Portuguese and Hungarian Golden Visa investment funds are classified as Passive Foreign Investment Companies (PFICs) under US tax law. Without a Qualified Electing Fund (QEF) or mark-to-market election filed in Year 1, gains are taxed at ordinary income rates plus a compound interest charge going back to acquisition — effectively converting what looks like a capital gain into a much larger ordinary income bill. This election must be made with a cross-border CPA before your first US tax year of ownership ends.
  • Exit tax: If your long-term plan involves renouncing US citizenship after obtaining a second passport through a Golden Visa program, the US imposes an exit tax on unrealized gains above roughly $910,000 (2026, approximate). This must be planned years in advance with qualified legal and tax counsel. See our Golden Visa vs. Second Passport guide for detail.

US citizens should always add a US cross-border CPA to the expert team described above — not as a substitute for local tax advice, but in addition to it. For a full breakdown of US reporting on foreign accounts and assets, see our guide on FBAR & FATCA for Americans abroad.

The Bottom Line

Buying property abroad for a Golden Visa is a serious strategic move that can unlock geographic freedom, a future second passport, and long-term optionality for your family. The opportunity is real — but so is the complexity. Foreign markets do not come with the buyer protections most people are used to at home, Golden Visa rules change rapidly, and the tax implications in your home country will not be handled by local advisers who only know local law.

The professionals who protect you from the downside are not a cost overhead. They are the difference between a purchase that qualifies for residency and one that costs you six figures and leaves you with an illiquid foreign asset and a rejected visa application. Build your team before you make an offer — not after.

Frequently asked questions

Do I need a local attorney to buy property abroad for a Golden Visa?

Yes — and it must be independent, not the seller’s or developer’s lawyer. They verify zone eligibility, confirm the net investment meets the threshold, check title, and ensure the transaction structure satisfies local immigration law.

What is the biggest mistake foreign buyers make when buying for a Golden Visa?

Purchasing in a zone or property category not approved under the program rules, or investing below the net threshold once taxes and fees are deducted. A close second: assuming buyer protections from home — disclosure laws, building codes, title guarantees — apply in the same way abroad.

Does buying property abroad reduce my home-country taxes?

Not automatically. A Golden Visa grants residency rights, not automatic tax savings. Whether you can reduce your tax burden depends on your home country’s rules, whether you establish genuine tax residency in the host country, and whether a bilateral tax treaty applies. Always get home-country tax advice before investing.

Is Spain still a Golden Visa option in 2026?

No. Spain abolished its entire Golden Visa program in April 2025, including all investment routes. No new applications are being accepted.

What extra steps do US citizens need compared to other buyers?

US citizens face citizenship-based taxation — the IRS taxes worldwide income regardless of residency. This means FBAR and FATCA reporting for foreign accounts, PFIC rules on foreign fund investments (critical for Portugal’s fund route), and potential exit tax exposure if they later renounce citizenship. A US cross-border CPA is required in addition to local legal and tax counsel.
