Buying Property in Colombia as an American (2026 Guide)

Yes — Americans can buy property in Colombia 100% in their own name, with no residency, visa or permit required. Closing costs are low, around 1.5%–2.5% for the buyer. Colombia has become one of the most talked-about property markets in the Americas — Medellín's El Poblado and Laureles, Bogotá's Chicó, the Caribbean walls of Cartagena. Prices are a fraction of comparable US cities, the peso has made dollars go further, and — unlike Mexico's coastal restricted zone or Bali's leasehold-only reality — a foreigner in Colombia can own real estate outright, freehold, in their own name. The friction here is not permission. It is a quiet currency-control step most buyers don't hear about until they try to sell and discover they can't legally move the money home. Here is the grounded 2026 version, with the legal and tax nuances the listing portals leave out.

Can Americans buy property in Colombia?

Yes — fully, and without any special approval. Colombian law treats a foreign buyer exactly like a Colombian one: you can buy an apartment, a house or land in your own name, hold it freehold, rent it out and resell it, with the same rights and the same protections. You do not need residency, a visa or a permit to buy, which means you can legally purchase while in the country on a standard 90-day tourist stamp, or even by power of attorney without setting foot in Colombia.

There are only two narrow restrictions, and they apply to every non-citizen rather than singling out Americans: vacant land within roughly 100 kilometers of an international border, and certain protected environmental zones (national parks, indigenous reserves, some coastal and island areas). Ordinary city apartments and houses — which is what nearly every foreign buyer is after — are entirely unaffected. One thing buying does not do: it does not grant you the right to live in Colombia beyond your tourist allowance. Residency is a separate process (see below).

What you need before you buy

Four things turn an American into a "ready" buyer in Colombia:

  • A passport (and usually a Colombian tax ID) — you can transact on your passport, but for utilities, ongoing taxes and an eventual sale you will want a Colombian NIT/RUT tax registration, and many buyers obtain a cédula de extranjería once they have a visa.
  • A lawyer and an independent title study — Colombia has no title insurance. Your protection is a lawyer pulling the Certificado de Tradición y Libertad (the property's full ownership and lien history) and verifying the chain of title before you sign. Do not skip this.
  • Proof and source of funds — notaries and banks apply strict anti-money-laundering checks, and Colombia and the US share financial data under FATCA and a tax-information-exchange agreement.
  • The right money channel — this is the one most buyers get wrong. How you bring the money in determines whether you can ever take it back out. More on this next.

The foreign-exchange registration that protects your money

This is the single most important sentence in this guide: if you bring your purchase funds from abroad, you must channel them through Colombia's regulated foreign-exchange market and register the investment with the Banco de la República. Under Decreto 2080 of 2000, foreign investment in real estate is registered by filing Form 4 (Formulario No. 4) through an authorized exchange-market intermediary (an IMC — typically a Colombian bank or licensed broker) when the money enters, generally within three months of the investment.

Why it matters so much: the registration is what gives you the legal right to repatriate — to convert pesos back to dollars and send home both your original capital and any profit when you sell. Wire money in informally, carry cash, or use an unregistered channel, and you keep the apartment but can lose the documented right to move the sale proceeds out of the country at the official rate. The same registration is also a prerequisite for the property-based investor visa. Most blogs mention "register with the central bank" in a single line; the nuance they miss is that the registration is welded to the FX channel the money travels through — you cannot reliably bless an informal transfer after the fact. Set this up before the first wire, with your lawyer and bank, not after closing.

⚠️ The repatriation trap. A buyer who wires US$200,000 through a registered IMC and files Form 4 can later sell, reconvert and send the full proceeds home legally. A buyer who brought the same money in "off the books" may be stuck converting at unfavorable rates — or unable to expatriate the capital at all. The registration costs almost nothing; skipping it can cost you the exit.

Closing costs and who pays what

Colombia is refreshingly cheap to transact in compared with Europe. A buyer should budget roughly 1.5% to 2.5% of the price in statutory closing costs. The transaction is formalized in a Promesa de Compraventa (a binding promise-to-buy, usually with a 10%–30% deposit), then completed by signing the Escritura Pública (public deed) before a notaría, and finally registered at the Oficina de Registro de Instrumentos Públicos — ownership only transfers on registration.

Two honest cautions. First, the headline notary and registration figures are percentages of the declared deed value — under-declaring to save tax is common in the market and is exactly what creates title and capital-gains problems later, so insist on declaring the true price. Second, departmental registration rates and the annual property tax are set locally, so your all-in number varies by city and department. All told, a cash buyer should plan for about 3% to 5% on top of the price once the lawyer is included.

Annual taxes: predial and the wealth tax

Owning in Colombia brings two taxes a US buyer should price in:

  • Predial — the annual municipal property tax, charged on the property's avalúo catastral (cadastral value). Rates are set by each municipality and commonly run from about 0.1% up to 1.6% (roughly 1 to 16 per mil) of cadastral value, paid every year by every owner, resident or not. Many cities give a discount for early payment.
  • Impuesto al Patrimonio (wealth tax) — a net-worth tax that bites only at the top. It applies when net assets on 1 January exceed 72,000 UVT (about COP 3.77 billion, roughly US$900,000–$1,000,000), at progressive rates from 0.5% to 1.5%. Crucially for foreigners, a non-resident is taxed only on Colombian-located assets — so only a very high-value Colombian property (plus other Colombian assets) over that threshold triggers it. (Note: a 2026 proposal to lower the filing threshold and raise rates was under Constitutional Court review at the start of the year — confirm the current rule before relying on it.)

Turning a purchase into residency

Buying property does not by itself let you stay, but it can be the basis for a visa. The M-10 real-estate investor visa is granted to foreigners who own registered Colombian property worth at least 350 monthly minimum wages (SMMLV). With the 2026 minimum wage at COP 1,750,905, that threshold is about COP 613 million — roughly US$155,000 at early-2026 rates (the exact dollar figure moves with the peso). It is a Migrant (M) category visa, so time on it counts toward permanent residency: hold the M-10 for five consecutive years and you can apply for a Resident (R) visa. Two traps to respect — the property investment must be registered with the Banco de la República (the same Form 4 above), and an absence of more than 180 consecutive days cancels an M visa.

The US tax rules that still apply

Your Colombian lawyer handles Colombia; the IRS is still your job. The United States taxes citizens on worldwide income, so rental income from your Colombian apartment goes on Schedule E — depreciated over the 30-year ADS schedule that applies to foreign residential rental — and a future sale is a US capital gain. Colombia taxes a long-held property's gain as ganancia ocasional at 15% (for property owned two years or more), with that 1% retención at sale acting as an advance. A Colombian bank account can trigger FBAR (FinCEN Form 114) once your foreign accounts cross US$10,000, and FATCA (Form 8938) at higher thresholds — but the directly-held real estate itself is not FBAR- or 8938-reportable.

Here is the nuance most American buyers — and most blogs — get wrong. There is no income tax treaty between the United States and Colombia. A convention was signed years ago but has never been ratified by the US Senate, so it has no legal effect. Practically, that means three things: you cannot lean on any treaty "tie-breaker" or reduced-withholding article, your only tool against double taxation is the foreign tax credit on Form 1116 (crediting the 15% Colombian gains tax and Colombian income tax against your US bill), and — because the two countries do have a tax-information-exchange agreement plus FATCA — Colombia's tax authority (DIAN) and the IRS share account and income data, so under-reporting on either side is visible to both. Plenty of articles loosely reference a "US–Colombia treaty"; for tax purposes, treat it as if it does not exist, because legally it does not.

Step-by-step: buying property in Colombia as an American

  1. Engage a lawyer and get your tax ID — line up an independent Colombian real-estate attorney and obtain a NIT/RUT for the transaction and ongoing taxes.
  2. Run the title study — your lawyer pulls the Certificado de Tradición y Libertad, confirms clean title, no liens and that the seller can convey.
  3. Sign the Promesa de Compraventa — the binding promise-to-buy, typically with a 10%–30% deposit and agreed closing date.
  4. Bring the money in through a registered FX channel — wire via an authorized intermediary (IMC) and file Form 4 with the Banco de la República to register the foreign investment.
  5. Sign the Escritura Pública — execute the public deed before the notaría and pay the notary and registration costs.
  6. Register the deed at the Oficina de Registro de Instrumentos Públicos — ownership legally transfers only here.
  7. Set up US compliance — file FBAR for any Colombian bank account and plan your rental, resale and (if relevant) investor-visa reporting with a cross-border CPA.

Start to finish, a straightforward purchase typically takes one to two months.

If you're not American: what changes

The Colombian side is identical for any foreign buyer. The 100% freehold ownership, the notary-and-registration process, the 1.5%–2.5% buyer closing costs, the predial property tax, the wealth-tax threshold and — critically — the Banco de la República foreign-investment registration all apply the same whether you hold a US, UK, Canadian, Australian or Israeli passport. Only the home-country tax wrapper on top is different.

If you're not American, keep everything above on the Colombian process exactly as written — and pay special attention to the Form 4 foreign-exchange registration, which protects every foreign buyer's right to repatriate, regardless of nationality. What you swap out is the US-specific tax layer (worldwide-income filing, FBAR, FATCA, Form 1116). In its place, check three things in your own country of residence: (1) whether your home country taxes foreign rental income and capital gains earned in Colombia; (2) whether you must report foreign property, bank accounts or assets at home; and (3) whether your country has a double-taxation treaty with Colombia — unlike the US, many countries (Spain, the UK, Canada and others) do, which can change how Colombian tax is credited. Confirm the specifics with a tax adviser in your country of residence before committing.

Is buying property in Colombia worth it in 2026?

For value, openness and legal security of ownership, Colombia is one of the more attractive places an American can buy — genuine freehold, low transaction costs, a public registry that gives clear title, and a property-based path to residency. The risks are not about whether you can own; they're about process discipline: declare the true price, run a real title study because there's no title insurance, and above all bring your money in through a registered exchange channel and file Form 4 so you can take it back out. Do those three things, line up your US tax plan first (remembering there's no treaty to fall back on), and Colombia is a genuinely rewarding market to own in.

Frequently asked questions

Can a US citizen buy property in Colombia?

Yes. Foreigners own property in Colombia 100% in their own name, on the same footing as citizens, with no residency, visa or permit required — you can buy on a 90-day tourist stamp. The only limits are vacant land within ~100 km of a border and certain protected zones, which apply to all non-citizens.

What are the closing costs to buy property in Colombia?

A buyer typically pays about 1.5%–2.5% of the price: notary fees near 0.3% (split with the seller) and the departmental registration tax of around 1%. The seller separately pays a 1% retención en la fuente. With a lawyer included, budget roughly 3%–5% on top of the price.

Do I have to register my money with the Banco de la República?

If you bring funds from abroad, yes — register the foreign investment via Form 4 under Decreto 2080 of 2000, through an authorized exchange intermediary, generally within three months. It protects your legal right to repatriate your capital and profits and is required for the investor visa.

Can buying property in Colombia get me residency?

Yes, via the M-10 real-estate investor visa: in 2026 a registered property investment of at least 350 minimum wages (about COP 613 million, roughly US$155,000). It's a Migrant-category visa; hold it five years to apply for a Resident visa, and don't be absent more than 180 consecutive days.

Do I pay US tax on a property I buy in Colombia?

Potentially. The US taxes worldwide income, so Colombian rent goes on Schedule E and a sale is a US capital gain. There is no US–Colombia tax treaty, so you rely on the Form 1116 foreign tax credit. The property isn't FBAR-reportable, but a Colombian bank account is once you cross US$10,000.
