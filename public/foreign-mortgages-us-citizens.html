Foreign Mortgages for US Citizens: Country-by-Country Guide

A foreign mortgage is not just a loan — for an American it's a currency position bolted onto a house, wrapped in another country's underwriting and another layer of US tax rules. The good news: lenders across Europe and the Americas do finance non-resident US buyers in 2026, often at rates that shame a US mortgage. The catch is everything around the rate. Here's the country-by-country reality, plus the two US-tax facts that decide how you should borrow.

Can Americans borrow abroad?

Yes — many countries lend to non-resident Americans, just cautiously. Lenders want US tax returns, proof of income, bank statements and a clean credit history, and they offset the cross-border risk with lower loan-to-value and larger deposits than a local borrower faces. Accessibility varies widely: Portugal, Spain and France are among the friendliest to non-residents, while Italy underwrites conservatively and the UAE caps non-resident lending tightly.

Europe: Portugal, Spain, France, Italy

Portugal is one of the most accessible eurozone markets — no restrictions on foreign borrowers, ~60–75% LTV, competitive ~3–4% rates and terms up to 30 years. Spain lends ~60–70% to non-residents at attractive fixed rates (~2.8–3.5%) but adds the mortgage's own stamp duty (AJD). France is surprisingly open to US buyers for residential property, ~60–75% LTV. Italy is the strictest of the four: non-resident mortgages exist but underwriting is detailed and LTV often 50–70%, so many buyers there pay cash. See the country guides for Portugal, Spain and Italy.

Mexico & Dubai

Mexico offers the most US-friendly structure on this list: dedicated cross-border USD lenders at ~5–9%, alongside peso bank loans at 9–14% and developer financing — full detail in our Mexico mortgage guide. Dubai lends to non-residents but conservatively, typically ~50–60% LTV at ~6–7%, with property-linked residency upside; the Dubai guide covers it. Because the UAE has no local income or capital-gains tax, a dirham mortgage there carries the same US-only tax exposure described below.

The currency question

Before the rate, decide the currency. Borrow in local currency (euro, peso, dirham) and your debt moves with the exchange rate against your dollar income — great if it weakens, painful if it strengthens. Borrow in US dollars (where available, e.g. Mexican cross-border loans) and your dollar income matches your dollar debt, eliminating FX risk. The rule of thumb: match the loan currency to the income that will repay it. A euro-rental that covers a euro mortgage is naturally hedged; a dollar earner servicing a euro loan from US income is carrying a currency bet whether they realize it or not.

US tax: the deduction and the Section 988 trap

Two US-tax facts shape every foreign-mortgage decision. First, the upside: interest on a foreign home loan can be deductible on your US return, within the qualified-residence mortgage-interest limits, exactly like a US second home. Second, the trap that catches almost everyone: IRS Section 988 foreign-currency rules. When you repay or refinance a foreign-currency mortgage after the currency has weakened against the dollar, the IRS can treat your "saving" as a taxable foreign-currency gain — ordinary income — even though you simply paid off a loan. Worse, the rule is asymmetric: a currency loss on a personal mortgage generally isn't deductible. So a euro or peso mortgage can hand you a US tax bill at payoff that no one mentioned at signing. This single rule is the strongest argument for either borrowing in USD or borrowing against your US home instead.

The alternative: borrow against your US home

Here's the option most "foreign mortgage" articles never mention. For many American buyers, the smartest financing isn't a foreign mortgage at all — it's a HELOC or cash-out refinance on your US home. You borrow in dollars, against US equity, with familiar underwriting and no foreign lender to satisfy — then buy the property abroad in cash. That sidesteps the FX risk, the Section 988 trap, the larger foreign deposit, and the cross-border paperwork in one move, and it often beats the all-in cost of a non-resident foreign loan. The trade-off: you encumber your US home and forgo a natural local-currency hedge if the property earns local-currency rent. For a vacation home a US dollar buyer will use, the US-equity route frequently wins; for a euro- or peso-income rental, a matched local loan can be better. Compare both — don't default to the foreign mortgage just because the property is foreign.

Step-by-step

  1. Compare both paths first — a foreign mortgage vs a USD HELOC/cash-out on your US home.
  2. Pick the loan currency to match the income that will repay it.
  3. Get pre-qualified locally with an international broker; prepare US tax returns, income proof and statements.
  4. Budget the deposit and costs — 25%–50% down plus local mortgage taxes/fees (e.g. Spain's AJD).
  5. Engage an independent local lawyer for title and the loan documents.
  6. Model the US side — interest deductibility and Section 988 currency exposure — with a cross-border CPA.
  7. Close, register, and set up FBAR/FATCA reporting for any foreign account.

If you're not American: what changes

The lending mechanics are identical for any foreign buyer. The country-by-country LTV, rates, deposits and currency-matching logic apply the same whatever passport you hold. What's US-specific is the tax layer — the mortgage-interest deduction and the Section 988 currency-gain trap.

If you're not American, ignore the Section 988 discussion and the US-equity-vs-foreign-loan framing built around US rules — though the underlying currency risk is universal. Instead check three things in your country of residence: (1) whether mortgage interest on a foreign property is deductible at home; (2) whether your country taxes foreign-currency gains on debt; and (3) whether borrowing against home equity in your country is cheaper than a non-resident foreign mortgage. A UK, Canadian or European buyer will run different numbers than a US dollar earner. Confirm with a tax adviser in your country of residence before borrowing.

Is a foreign mortgage worth it in 2026?

Sometimes — and sometimes a dollar loan against your US home beats it outright. Eurozone rates are genuinely attractive, and a local mortgage that matches local rental income is a clean, hedged structure. But for a US dollar earner buying a home to use, the foreign loan's larger deposit, FX risk and the Section 988 trap often tip the math toward borrowing in dollars. Decide on the currency first, compare the US-equity alternative, and price the US tax before the rate — then a foreign mortgage becomes a tool, not a tripwire.

Frequently asked questions

Can US citizens get a mortgage abroad?

Yes. Many countries lend to non-resident Americans on conservative terms. Portugal, Spain and France are most accessible (~60–75% LTV); Italy and the UAE are stricter. You'll provide US tax returns, income proof and statements, and accept a larger deposit.

How much can an American borrow on a foreign property?

Typically 50–75% of value (a 25–50% deposit). Portugal and France ~60–75%, Spain 60–70%, Italy 50–70%, Mexico 60–70% cross-border, UAE ~50–60%.

What are foreign mortgage rates for US citizens in 2026?

Eurozone rates are ~2.8–4% (often fixed) in Spain, Portugal, France and Italy. Mexican cross-border USD loans run ~5–9% and peso loans 9–14%. Rates depend on term, deposit and currency.

Is interest on a foreign mortgage tax-deductible in the US?

Often yes — interest on a qualified first or second home is deductible within the US limits, even abroad. But borrowing in a foreign currency exposes you to IRS Section 988 at payoff. Confirm both with a cross-border CPA.

Should an American borrow abroad or against their US home?

It depends. A USD HELOC or cash-out on your US home avoids FX risk and the Section 988 trap, often with simpler underwriting. A local foreign mortgage hedges local-currency rental income and keeps US equity intact. Compare both.
