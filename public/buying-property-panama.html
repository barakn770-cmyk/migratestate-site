Buying Property in Panama as an American (2026)

MS By The MigrateState Editorial Team · Reviewed by a licensed international real-estate attorney  ·  Updated June 2026  ·  11 min read
Foreigners buy Panamanian property on the same terms as citizens — full titled ownership, no residency required. Plan on roughly 2-5% in buyer closing costs, a 2% transfer tax (seller pays), and 10% capital-gains tax on sale. The real traps are untitled "Rights of Possession" land, the 10-kilometer border rule, and the Panamanian corporation that quietly wrecks your US taxes.

Panama has quietly become the default landing pad for Americans who want a foothold abroad without the friction. The reasons are practical rather than romantic: the country runs on the US dollar, the residency programs are unusually open to US citizens, the tax system is territorial, and a foreign buyer takes title to an apartment in Panama City or a hill town in Boquete with the same rights a Panamanian has. You do not need to be a resident, hold a visa, or even visit to buy.

That openness is also where unprepared buyers get hurt. Because the front door is wide, people skip the diligence they would never skip at home — and walk into the three places Panama genuinely punishes the careless: untitled "Rights of Possession" land sold as if it were titled, the constitutional ban on foreign ownership near the borders, and the broker's reflexive advice to "just put it in a Panama corporation." This guide walks the whole transaction with the 2026 numbers and flags each trap before it costs you.

Can Americans own property in Panama?

Yes — with two narrow constitutional exceptions. Article 47 of the Panamanian Constitution grants foreign nationals the same property rights as citizens. You can hold full titled (fee-simple) ownership, recorded in the Public Registry (Registro Público), with the unrestricted right to sell, lease, mortgage, gift, or pass property to your heirs. There is no foreign-buyer surcharge and no government approval needed for a residential purchase.

The first exception is the 10-kilometer border rule: foreigners — and even Panamanian companies that have any foreign capital — cannot own land within 10 km of a national border. In practice that affects parts of Chiriquí, Bocas del Toro, and Darién near the Costa Rica and Colombia frontiers. The corporate workaround does not get you past it. The second exception is the public coastal strip: no one, Panamanian or foreign, can title land within 22 meters of the high-tide line on the Pacific coast or 10 meters on the Atlantic. (The old blanket ban on foreigners owning island property was repealed in 2006, so islands are fine unless they fall inside the 10 km border zone.)

Titled land vs. Rights of Possession

This is the single most important diligence point in Panama, and the one that catches the most Americans. Panamanian property comes in two very different forms. Titled property (propiedad titulada) is registered in the Public Registry with a clean, defensible legal title — this is what you want. Rights of Possession (derecho posesorio, or "ROP") is something else entirely: it is an unregistered, possessory claim to government-owned land. There is no title, the "owner" cannot give you one, and the claim is far harder to defend, finance, or resell.

ROP land is common in rural areas and especially in parts of Bocas del Toro, where beautiful beach and island parcels are routinely marketed to foreigners at tempting prices — without making clear that no registered title exists. Some ROP land can be converted to title, but the process is slow, uncertain, and occasionally impossible. The rule for a foreign buyer is simple: insist on titled land verified in the Public Registry, and treat any "Rights of Possession" deal as a specialist project to be avoided unless you have independent counsel who has confirmed exactly what you are buying. Your lawyer should pull a current registry certificate (certificado del Registro Público) showing the registered owner, the finca number, the boundaries, and that no mortgages or liens are attached.

Closing costs and the buying process

Panama's transaction costs are moderate and, helpfully, denominated entirely in US dollars — there is no currency conversion to budget for. The headline taxes fall on the seller: a 2% transfer tax (on the higher of the sale price or the registered cadastral value) and a 3% capital-gains advance (more on that below). As the buyer, your own costs are the predictable ones:

Cost Typical 2026 rate Who pays
Legal / attorney fees ~1–1.5% Buyer — engage your own, independent of the seller
Public Registry inscription Tiered (a few hundred to ~$2,000+) Buyer — scales with property value
Notary fees ~$30–$300 Buyer — modest, on a statutory scale
Escrow fee ~$500–$1,500 Buyer — strongly recommended
Transfer tax 2% Seller (by law)
Capital-gains advance 3% of price Seller (credited against 10% CGT)

The process is title-driven. You sign a promise-to-buy contract (contrato de promesa de compraventa) with a deposit held in escrow, your attorney runs the registry diligence, and the transfer is completed by a public deed (escritura pública) before a notary and then registered in the Public Registry — and it is that registration, not the signing, that makes you the legal owner. Always move funds through a regulated escrow agent: Panama's anti-money-laundering rules require source-of-funds documentation, and a compliant escrow protects both your deposit and your future ability to repatriate sale proceeds cleanly. Note that new construction sold by a developer is generally exempt from the 2% transfer tax but carries its own tax treatment, so confirm the figures on a pre-construction purchase.

Property, capital-gains and rental taxes

Annual property tax in Panama (the IBI, Impuesto de Bienes Inmuebles) is genuinely low for owner-occupiers thanks to Law 66 of 2017. If your home is registered as a Primary Residence or Family Tax Patrimony (Patrimonio Familiar Tributario), the scale is:

Registered value Annual rate (primary residence)
US$0 – $120,0000% (exempt)
$120,001 – $700,0000.5%
Above $700,0000.7%

That exemption is not automatic — you must register the property as a Primary Residence or Family Tax Patrimony with the tax authority (DGI), which has up to three months to approve the filing. Second homes and investment property use a separate, less generous scale (exempt only to $30,000, then a progressive 0.6%–1.0%), so a rental condo will not get the $120,000 break. When you sell, capital-gains tax is a flat 10% of the net gain. The mechanics catch people out: at closing the seller pays a 3% advance calculated on the gross sale price (or cadastral value, whichever is higher). That 3% is then reconciled against the real 10%-of-gain figure — if the true tax is lower, you can either treat the 3% as a final tax or file to claim a refund of the difference. Rental income is Panama-source income taxed on the progressive individual scale (0% up to roughly $11,000, rising to 25%), after deductible expenses.

Does buying get you residency?

This is where Panama shines for Americans, because property can directly buy you residency through more than one route. The Qualified Investor Visa (Panama's "golden visa") grants permanent residency in roughly three months for a real-estate investment of US$300,000, held for at least five years. Crucially, that $300,000 threshold is scheduled to rise to US$500,000 after October 15, 2026, so timing matters if this is your route. Equity counts: a $500,000 property with a $200,000 mortgage and $300,000 of your own equity qualifies.

The Friendly Nations Visa — for which US citizens are eligible — accepts a US$200,000 real-estate purchase as the qualifying economic tie (a Panamanian job offer or a $200,000 bank deposit also work). Since the 2021 reform it grants two-year provisional residency first, converting to permanent residency afterward, rather than the instant permanent status it once offered. Retirees have a third path that needs no purchase at all: the celebrated Pensionado visa, available with just US$1,000/month of lifetime pension income (plus $250 per dependent), which also unlocks Panama's well-known discounts for retirees. All these routes can lead to citizenship after five years of permanent residency, though naturalization in Panama is discretionary and document-intensive.

The US tax angle — and the dollar twist

For a US citizen or green-card holder, owning Panamanian real estate directly is refreshingly clean on the US side. Directly-held foreign real estate is not reportable on the FBAR (FinCEN 114) or on Form 8938 — those cover foreign financial accounts and assets, not a house. You will report rental income on Schedule E (with foreign residential property depreciated over 30 years under the ADS method), and any gain when you sell, but you can generally claim a foreign tax credit for the Panamanian 10% capital-gains and rental tax you've paid, and the Section 121 exclusion can shelter up to $250,000/$500,000 of gain on a true primary residence.

Here is the nuance most blogs skip — and it cuts both ways. Because Panama's economy runs on the US dollar (the balboa is pegged 1:1 and US notes are legal tender), the Section 988 currency trap simply does not exist here. In Mexico, Costa Rica, or Europe, a US owner can owe US tax on a phantom dollar "gain" created purely by exchange-rate moves on a peso- or euro-denominated mortgage — even if the property's dollar value never changed. In dollarized Panama there is no second currency to move, so that trap is gone. But the flip side, which the brochures never mention, is that Panama's famous territorial tax system does nothing for a US citizen: the US taxes its citizens on worldwide income regardless of where they live, so the "Panama doesn't tax foreign income" pitch is irrelevant to your IRS bill.

The classic structural mistake is the same one Costa Rica and Mexico produce: Panamanian lawyers and brokers reflexively recommend holding property in a sociedad anónima (S.A.) — it is the cultural default. For a US person this is often costly. A Panamanian S.A. is a "per se" foreign corporation to the IRS, which means:

  • An annual Form 5471 is required — the penalty for not filing starts at $10,000 per year.
  • The structure can trigger CFC and GILTI taxation, or be treated as a PFIC if it holds passive assets — both expensive and complex.
  • You lose the Section 121 home-sale exclusion and the step-up in basis at death, leaving your heirs a built-in gain.
  • It does not bypass the 10-kilometer border rule, because the Constitution restricts companies with foreign capital too.

You can elect pass-through treatment with a Form 8832 "check-the-box" election, but it must generally be filed within 75 days of forming the entity and has its own consequences. Corporate ownership is occasionally the right call — for genuinely passing the property between owners, or for estate planning done with US advice — but it is a US tax decision to make with a cross-border advisor before closing, not a default a broker selects for you.

Step-by-step: how to buy safely

  1. Engage your own Panamanian attorney — independent of the seller, the broker, and any notary they propose.
  2. Confirm titled vs. ROP land and that the parcel is outside the 10 km border zone and the public coastal strip, via a current Public Registry certificate.
  3. Sign a promise-to-buy contract (contrato de promesa de compraventa) with a deposit held in a regulated escrow account.
  4. Decide the ownership structure — personal name vs. entity — with a US cross-border tax advisor, before you commit.
  5. Fund through escrow with full source-of-funds documentation to satisfy AML rules.
  6. Execute the public deed (escritura pública) before a notary and register the transfer in the Public Registry — ownership passes on registration.
  7. Register the Primary Residence / Family Tax Patrimony exemption with the DGI if you'll live there, and keep every receipt for your US cost basis.

If you're not American: what changes

The Panamanian rules in this guide — titled ownership for foreigners, the 10-kilometer border rule, the Rights-of-Possession trap, the 2% transfer tax, ~2-5% buyer closing costs, the IBI property-tax scale, and the 10% capital-gains tax — are set by Panama and apply to every foreign buyer equally, regardless of passport. A Canadian, British, German, or Israeli buyer pays the same transfer tax and faces the same border and title limits as an American. What differs is only the tax wrapper back home.

The entire "US tax angle" section above — FBAR, Form 8938, Form 5471, Section 121, the 30-year ADS depreciation, and the Section 988 dollar point — is US-specific and does not apply to non-Americans. Note especially that the "no currency trap" advantage is unique to US persons, since for them Panama is already in their home currency; a euro- or sterling-based buyer still has their own currency moving against the dollar. If you're not a US person, swap the US rules for your own and check three things with a tax adviser in your country of residence: (1) whether your home country taxes foreign rental income and capital gains; (2) whether you must report foreign assets or holding companies at home; and (3) whether your country has a double-taxation treaty with Panama that affects how that income is taxed. The S.A.-ownership warning still matters to everyone, but the reasons it's costly are different outside the US.

Is it worth it in 2026?

For an American who wants a dollar-denominated home in a stable, well-connected hub — and who does the title and structuring diligence properly — Panama is one of the most foreign-friendly markets anywhere. Buyer closing costs are modest, owner-occupier property tax can be zero up to $120,000 of value, capital gains are a flat 10%, and a purchase can double as a fast track to permanent residency (especially if you act before the $300,000 Qualified Investor threshold jumps to $500,000 on October 15, 2026). The US-dollar economy removes a tax trap that bites in most other markets.

The ways buyers get hurt are entirely avoidable: paying titled-land prices for untitled Rights of Possession claims, buying inside the 10 km border zone, and accepting a Panamanian S.A. structure that quietly creates a five-figure annual US compliance burden and forfeits valuable US exclusions. Get an independent attorney for the title and a US cross-border advisor for the structure, and the deal that looks easy at the front door stays easy all the way through.

Frequently asked questions

Can Americans own property in Panama?

Yes. Article 47 of Panama's Constitution gives foreigners the same property rights as citizens — to buy, sell, lease, mortgage, and inherit titled real estate, with no residency or visa required. The constitutional limits are that foreigners (and even companies with any foreign capital) cannot own land within 10 km of a national border or within the public coastal strip. The bigger practical issue is buying titled land rather than untitled Rights of Possession (ROP) land.

How much are closing costs when buying property in Panama?

For a buyer, budget roughly 2–5% of the price: legal fees of about 1–1.5%, notary fees, and the Public Registry inscription fee (a tiered scale). The 2% transfer tax and the 3% capital-gains advance are by law the seller's costs, though everything is negotiable and should be set out in the promise-to-buy contract. Panama uses the US dollar, so there are no currency-conversion costs.

What taxes do you pay on Panama property?

Annual property tax (IBI) on a primary residence or Family Tax Patrimony is 0% on the first US$120,000 of registered value, 0.5% from $120,001 to $700,000, and 0.7% above $700,000, under Law 66 of 2017. Second homes and investment property use a separate scale (exempt to $30,000, then 0.6%–1.0%). On sale, capital-gains tax is 10% of the net gain, collected via a 3% advance on the sale price that is credited or refunded.

Can buying property in Panama get you residency?

Yes. The Qualified Investor Visa grants permanent residency for a US$300,000 real-estate investment held five years — but that threshold rises to US$500,000 after October 15, 2026. The Friendly Nations Visa (Americans qualify) accepts a US$200,000 property purchase as the economic tie, granting two-year provisional then permanent residency. Retirees can instead use the Pensionado visa with US$1,000/month of lifetime pension income — no purchase required.

Should I hold Panama property in a corporation?

Usually not, if you're American. A Panamanian sociedad anónima (S.A.) is a "per se" foreign corporation to the IRS: it triggers an annual Form 5471 (minimum $10,000 penalty for non-filing), can create CFC, GILTI, or PFIC exposure, and forfeits the Section 121 exclusion and step-up in basis at death. It also cannot bypass the 10-kilometer border rule. Get US tax advice before choosing a structure.
